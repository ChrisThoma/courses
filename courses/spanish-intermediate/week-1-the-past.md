# Week 1: Talking About the Past

> **The thesis of this week:** the single biggest leap from beginner to intermediate is being able to narrate the past, and Spanish does it with two tenses, not one. The **preterite** is for completed actions ("I ate, she called, they arrived"). The **imperfect** is for ongoing, habitual, and background situations ("I used to eat, it was raining, she was happy"). Learning both, and learning to choose between them, is the central skill of this week and arguably of the whole course. We close the week with **object pronouns**, which stop your speech from sounding like a textbook by replacing repeated nouns with small words. Conjugate out loud and by hand every day.

All conjugation tables for these tenses are in the [Reference file](SUPPLEMENT-reference-and-vocabulary.md). Keep it open.

# Day 1: The Preterite of Regular Verbs

### Objectives
- Conjugate regular -ar, -er, and -ir verbs in the preterite.
- Use the preterite for completed past actions.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say hablar and comer in the preterite aloud, twice each: hablé, hablaste, habló, hablamos, hablasteis, hablaron; comí, comiste, comió, comimos, comisteis, comieron. Then: Ayer hablé con el jefe. Comí a la una. Ella llamó al cliente.

### Lecture

The **preterite** is the tense for actions you view as completed: they happened, they finished, they are done. "Yesterday I worked," "she called twice," "we finished the project." If you can put a box around the action in time, it is preterite.

**Regular endings** (Reference section 1). Drop the infinitive ending and add:

| | -ar: hablar | -er/-ir: comer/vivir |
|---|---|---|
| yo | habl**é** | com**í** |
| tú | habl**aste** | com**iste** |
| él/ella/usted | habl**ó** | com**ió** |
| nosotros | habl**amos** | com**imos** |
| vosotros | habl**asteis** | com**isteis** |
| ellos/ellas/ustedes | habl**aron** | com**ieron** |

Two things to notice. First, **-er and -ir verbs share the same preterite endings**, which cuts your work. Second, **the accents on the yo and él forms are doing heavy lifting**: hablo (I speak, present) vs. habló (he spoke, preterite); hablé (I spoke) is clearly past. Drop that accent and you have changed the meaning, so it is a real error, not a typo.

The preterite loves **time markers** of completion: *ayer* (yesterday), *anoche* (last night), *la semana pasada* (last week), *el año pasado* (last year), *una vez* (once). Drill the time-and-sequence words in Reference section 10; they are the scaffolding of every story you will tell.

- *Ayer trabajé ocho horas.* (Yesterday I worked eight hours.)
- *La semana pasada ella vendió el coche.* (Last week she sold the car.)
- *Anoche comimos en un restaurante.* (Last night we ate at a restaurant.)

### Write by hand (20 min)
1. Handwrite the full preterite of **trabajar**, **comer**, and **escribir**, twice each, saying every form aloud and putting in the accents.
2. Write five true sentences about what you did yesterday, each with a different regular verb and a time marker.

### Speak and record (10 min)
Record yourself narrating three things you did yesterday and two things a friend or coworker did, all in the preterite.

### Self-check
1. What does the preterite express?
2. Give the preterite endings for -ar verbs.
3. Which two verb families share preterite endings?
4. Why does the accent on "habló" matter?
5. Name three time markers that signal the preterite.

# Day 2: The Irregular Preterite

### Objectives
- Conjugate the most common irregular preterites.
- Use them in past narration.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say these aloud, twice each: fui, fuiste, fue, fuimos, fueron; tuve, tuviste, tuvo, tuvimos, tuvieron; hice, hiciste, hizo, hicimos, hicieron. Then: Fui al trabajo. Tuve una reunión. Ella hizo el informe.

### Lecture

Many of the most-used verbs are irregular in the preterite, so you memorize them. The good news is there is a pattern within the irregularity (Reference section 1): most of these irregulars use a **changed stem plus a shared set of endings** (-e, -iste, -o, -imos, -ieron), and crucially they take **no accents** on the yo and él forms. So it is *tuve* and *tuvo*, not "tuvé."

The high-frequency irregulars to drill this week:

- **ser and ir are identical**: fui, fuiste, fue, fuimos, fueron. Context decides whether you "were" or "went." *Fui médico* (I was a doctor) vs. *Fui al médico* (I went to the doctor).
- **hacer**: hice, hiciste, **hizo** (note the z), hicimos, hicieron.
- **tener**: tuve, tuviste, tuvo, tuvimos, tuvieron.
- **estar**: estuve, estuviste, estuvo, estuvimos, estuvieron.
- **poder**: pude, pudiste, pudo, pudimos, pudieron.
- **poner**: puse, pusiste, puso, pusimos, pusieron.
- **querer**: quise, quisiste, quiso, quisimos, quisieron.
- **venir**: vine, viniste, vino, vinimos, vinieron.
- **decir**: dije, dijiste, dijo, dijimos, **dijeron** (j-stems drop the i: dijeron, not dijieron).
- **dar** and **ver** are short: di, diste, dio, dimos, dieron; vi, viste, vio, vimos, vieron (no accents needed because they are one syllable).

These ten verbs cover a huge share of real past narration. Drill them until they come without thought, because you will reach for them in every story.

### Write by hand (20 min)
1. Handwrite the preterite of **ser/ir**, **tener**, **hacer**, and **estar**, twice each, saying each form aloud. Mark that there are no accents on yo and él.
2. Write six sentences about a past day using six different irregular preterites.

### Speak and record (10 min)
Record yourself telling where you went, what you did, what you had to deal with, and one thing you could not do yesterday, using irregular preterites.

### Self-check
1. Which two verbs share the exact same preterite forms?
2. Conjugate tener in the preterite.
3. What is unusual about the accents on irregular preterites?
4. What is the él form of hacer, and what spelling change does it have?
5. What happens to the i in the ellos form of decir?

# Day 3: The Imperfect

### Objectives
- Conjugate the imperfect.
- Use it for ongoing, habitual, and background past.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say hablar and comer in the imperfect aloud, twice each: hablaba, hablabas, hablaba, hablábamos, hablaban; comía, comías, comía, comíamos, comían. Then: Antes vivía en México. De niño comía mucho. Llovía y hacía frío.

### Lecture

The **imperfect** is the other past tense, and it has a completely different job from the preterite. Where the preterite boxes off a completed action, the imperfect paints a past that was **ongoing, habitual, or background**. It is the "used to," the "was ...ing," and the "would (repeatedly)" of English.

**Regular endings** (Reference section 2), and they are wonderfully regular:

| | -ar: hablar | -er/-ir: comer |
|---|---|---|
| yo | habl**aba** | com**ía** |
| tú | habl**abas** | com**ías** |
| él/ella/usted | habl**aba** | com**ía** |
| nosotros | habl**ábamos** | com**íamos** |
| ellos/ellas/ustedes | habl**aban** | com**ían** |

**There are only three irregular imperfects in the entire language**: ser (era, eras, era, éramos, eran), ir (iba, ibas, iba, íbamos, iban), and ver (veía, veías, veía, veíamos, veían). Everything else is regular. That is a gift; memorize those three and the imperfect holds no more surprises.

**The imperfect's jobs:**
- **Habitual / repeated past** ("used to," "would"): *Antes trabajaba los sábados.* (I used to work on Saturdays.) *De niño jugaba al fútbol.* (As a kid I would play soccer.)
- **Ongoing action in progress** ("was ...ing"): *Llovía cuando salí.* (It was raining when I left.)
- **Background description** (time, age, weather, feelings, physical and mental states): *Eran las tres. Tenía treinta años. Estaba cansado. Hacía sol.*

Time markers that signal the imperfect: *siempre, a veces, todos los días, generalmente, mientras, de niño/a, antes.* If the marker implies repetition or an unfinished backdrop, think imperfect.

### Write by hand (20 min)
1. Handwrite the imperfect of **trabajar** and **vivir**, twice each, plus the three irregulars **ser**, **ir**, **ver** once each.
2. Write six sentences about your childhood or a past routine ("when I was young / before...") using the imperfect.

### Speak and record (10 min)
Record yourself describing what your life used to be like a few years ago: where you lived, what you did regularly, what you were like, all in the imperfect.

### Self-check
1. What three kinds of past does the imperfect express?
2. Give the imperfect endings for -ar and for -er/-ir verbs.
3. Name the only three irregular imperfect verbs.
4. Which tense describes weather, age, and time in the past?
5. Name three time markers that signal the imperfect.

# Day 4: Preterite vs. Imperfect

### Objectives
- Choose correctly between preterite and imperfect when narrating.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say these contrast pairs aloud: Comía cuando llamaste. / Comí y luego salí. Llovía. / Llovió toda la noche. Eran las tres cuando llegó.

### Lecture

This is the most important lesson of the week, and one you will keep refining for months. Both tenses are "past," but they answer different questions, and Spanish forces you to choose.

**The core distinction:** the **preterite** moves the story forward with completed events ("and then this happened, and then that happened"). The **imperfect** sets the scene around those events ("it was night, she was tired, it was raining"). In one sentence: **preterite is the event, imperfect is the backdrop.**

**A practical way to choose, action by action, ask:**
- Did it happen and finish, a single completed event or a clear sequence? → **preterite.** *Llegué, hablé con ella, y me fui.* (I arrived, spoke with her, and left.)
- Was it ongoing, repeated, or describing the situation? → **imperfect.** *Mientras trabajaba, escuchaba música.* (While I worked, I would listen to music.)

**The classic combined sentence** uses both at once: an imperfect backdrop interrupted by a preterite event.
- *Comía cuando sonó el teléfono.* (I was eating when the phone rang.) The eating was ongoing (imperfect); the ringing was a completed interruption (preterite).
- *Dormíamos cuando llegaron.* (We were sleeping when they arrived.)

**Some verbs even shift meaning between the two tenses**, because "knowing" something as a state vs. "finding out" as an event are different ideas:
- *sabía* = I knew (state) vs. *supe* = I found out (event).
- *conocía* = I knew a person (state) vs. *conocí* = I met (event).
- *quería* = I wanted vs. *quise* = I tried (and often "no quise" = I refused).
- *podía* = I was able (in general) vs. *pude* = I managed to (and succeeded).

Do not expect this to be automatic yet. Expect to narrate, choose, and sometimes choose wrong, then see why. The choice becomes intuition only with many real stories, which is exactly what this week's project gives you.

### Write by hand (20 min)
1. For each verb in this mini-story, write P (preterite) or I (imperfect) and then the correct form, by hand: "It (ser) las ocho. I (estar) tired. I (trabajar, ongoing) when my boss (entrar). He (decir) something and then (salir)."
2. Write a five-sentence past anecdote of your own that uses at least three preterites and two imperfects, including one "was ...ing when..." sentence.

### Speak and record (10 min)
Record yourself telling a short true story about something that happened recently: set the scene in the imperfect (where you were, what was going on) and narrate the events in the preterite.

### Self-check
1. In one line, what is the difference between preterite and imperfect?
2. In "Comía cuando sonó el teléfono," which verb is which tense, and why?
3. Which tense carries the sequence of events forward?
4. Which tense sets the background scene?
5. Give the difference between "sabía" and "supe."

# Day 5: Direct Object Pronouns

### Objectives
- Use direct object pronouns to avoid repeating nouns.
- Place them correctly.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say: ¿Tienes el informe? Sí, lo tengo. ¿Ves a María? Sí, la veo. ¿Compraste los libros? Sí, los compré. Te ayudo.

### Lecture

When you keep repeating a noun, your speech sounds robotic: "Do you have the report? Yes, I have the report." Real speakers replace the noun with a **direct object pronoun**: "Yes, I have it." The direct object is the thing directly receiving the action (the report, María, the books).

**The forms** (Reference section 5): me, te, **lo / la**, nos, os, **los / las**. The third-person forms agree with the noun they replace in gender and number:
- *el informe* to **lo**: *¿Tienes el informe? Sí, lo tengo.*
- *la carta* to **la**: *La escribí ayer.*
- *los libros* to **los**: *Los compré.*
- *las llaves* to **las**: *No las encuentro.*
- people too: *¿Ves a María? La veo. ¿Me ayudas? Sí, te ayudo.*

**Placement** is the part to drill:
- **Before a conjugated verb:** *Lo tengo. No la veo.*
- **Attached to the end of an infinitive or gerund:** *Voy a hacerlo* (or *Lo voy a hacer*, both correct). *Estoy leyéndolo* (or *Lo estoy leyendo*).
- **Attached to an affirmative command** (Day 9): *Hazlo.* But **before a negative command**: *No lo hagas.*

The most common beginner habits to break: leaving the noun in ("Sí, tengo el informe" when "Sí, lo tengo" is more natural), and putting the pronoun after a conjugated verb (never "tengo lo").

### Write by hand (20 min)
1. Rewrite each answer using a direct object pronoun: ¿Tienes las llaves? / ¿Compraste el coche? / ¿Ves a tus compañeros? / ¿Necesitas la ayuda?
2. Write four of your own question-and-answer pairs that use lo, la, los, and las.

### Speak and record (10 min)
Record yourself answering ten quick questions about whether you have/see/need various things, replacing every noun with the correct direct object pronoun.

### Self-check
1. What is a direct object?
2. Give the six direct object pronouns.
3. What do lo/la/los/las agree with?
4. Where does the pronoun go with a conjugated verb? With an infinitive?
5. Rewrite "Tengo el informe" with a pronoun.

# Day 6: Indirect and Double Object Pronouns, and Quiz 1

### Objectives
- Use indirect object pronouns.
- Combine two pronouns correctly.
- Take Quiz 1.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say: Le doy el informe al jefe. Te escribo un correo. Nos explican el plan. Se lo doy. Me lo dijo.

### Lecture

The **indirect object** is the person to whom or for whom something is done: I give the report **to the boss**, I write an email **to you**. The indirect object pronouns (Reference section 5) are me, te, **le**, nos, os, **les**. Note that only the third person (le/les) differs from the direct object set.

- *Le doy el informe.* (I give the report to him/her.)
- *Les explico el plan.* (I explain the plan to them.)
- *Te mando el correo.* (I send you the email.)

Spanish often includes the indirect pronoun **even when the person is also named**, which sounds redundant to English ears but is normal and correct: *Le doy el informe **al jefe**.* The "a + person" part clarifies who "le" is.

**Double object pronouns:** when you replace both objects, two rules apply (Reference section 5):
1. **Indirect comes before direct:** *Me lo da.* (He gives it to me: "me" indirect, "lo" direct.)
2. **le / les become se before lo/la/los/las.** You cannot say "le lo"; it becomes **se lo**: *Se lo doy.* (I give it to him.) *Se las mandé.* (I sent them to her.)

This se-substitution trips up everyone at first, so drill it specifically: *¿El informe al jefe? Se lo doy.* (not "le lo").

### Write by hand (15 min)
1. Add the indirect pronoun: ___ doy el libro a ella. / ___ explico el plan a ustedes. / ___ mando el correo a ti.
2. Rewrite with double pronouns: Doy el informe al jefe. / Mando las cartas a María. / Explico el problema a ellos.

### Quiz 1
Take **Quiz 1** now (see `QUIZZES.md`), closed-book. Grade against `ANSWER-KEYS.md`. It covers the preterite (regular and irregular), the imperfect, the preterite/imperfect choice, and object pronouns, the whole Week 1 core. If you score below 90%, re-study the weak spot on tomorrow's review day.

### Self-check
1. What is an indirect object?
2. Give the indirect object pronouns; which two differ from the direct set?
3. Why is "Le doy el informe al jefe" not redundant in Spanish?
4. In double pronouns, which comes first, indirect or direct?
5. What do le and les become before lo/la? Give an example.

Week 1 gave you the **past**: two tenses and the art of choosing between them, plus the object pronouns that make speech concise. You can now tell what happened. **Week 2 opens up the rest of time and mood:** reflexive verbs, commands for giving instructions, the future and conditional for plans and politeness, the perfect and progressive tenses, comparisons, and the por/para distinction that beginners get wrong for years.
