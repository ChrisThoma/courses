# Week 2: Commands, the Future & More

> **The thesis of this week:** you can now handle the present and the past. This week opens up everything else: reflexive verbs for daily actions, commands for telling people what to do, the future and conditional for plans, predictions, politeness, and the hypothetical, the perfect and progressive tenses, and two notoriously tricky pieces, comparisons and the por/para split. None of these is as hard as the preterite/imperfect choice you already tackled. Each is a clear pattern with clear uses. Conjugate out loud and by hand, and keep the past tenses warm with daily review.

All conjugation tables are in the [Reference file](SUPPLEMENT-reference-and-vocabulary.md).

# Day 8: Reflexive Verbs and Daily Routine

### Objectives
- Conjugate and use reflexive verbs.
- Describe a daily routine.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say: Me levanto a las seis. Te despiertas tarde. Se ducha por la mañana. Nos acostamos temprano. Then: Me llamo... ¿Cómo te sientes?

### Lecture

A **reflexive verb** is one whose action falls back on the subject: you wash *yourself*, you get *yourself* up. Spanish marks these with a **reflexive pronoun** (me, te, se, nos, os, se) that matches the subject, and the dictionary form shows it with **-se** attached: *levantarse* (to get up), *ducharse* (to shower).

You conjugate the verb normally and put the matching pronoun in front:

| me levanto | I get up |
| te levantas | you get up |
| se levanta | he/she gets up, you (formal) get up |
| nos levantamos | we get up |
| se levantan | they get up, you all get up |

**Daily-routine verbs** to drill: despertarse (to wake up, stem-changes e to ie: me despierto), levantarse (to get up), ducharse (to shower), bañarse (to bathe), vestirse (to get dressed, e to i: me visto), peinarse (to comb one's hair), lavarse (to wash up), acostarse (to go to bed, o to ue: me acuesto), dormirse (to fall asleep).

Reflexives also cover a large set of everyday non-routine actions where English does not use "myself": *sentirse* (to feel: *me siento bien*), *llamarse* (to be called: *me llamo*), *acordarse de* (to remember), *olvidarse de* (to forget), *darse cuenta de* (to realize), *reunirse* (to meet), *preocuparse* (to worry), *quejarse* (to complain). These are extremely common in real speech, so treat the reflexive as a normal, frequent structure, not an exotic one.

Pronoun placement follows the same rules as object pronouns: before a conjugated verb (*me levanto*), or attached to an infinitive or gerund (*voy a levantarme*, *estoy levantándome*).

### Write by hand (20 min)
1. Handwrite the full conjugation of **levantarse** and **sentirse** (note the stem change), saying each form aloud.
2. Write your morning routine in six to eight sentences using reflexive verbs and time expressions.

### Speak and record (10 min)
Record yourself describing your daily routine from waking up to going to bed, using at least six reflexive verbs in order.

### Self-check
1. What makes a verb reflexive?
2. Give the reflexive pronouns for all persons.
3. Conjugate levantarse for yo, tú, and nosotros.
4. Name three non-routine reflexive verbs common in speech.
5. Where do the reflexive pronouns go relative to the verb?

# Day 9: Commands

### Objectives
- Form affirmative and negative commands for tú, usted, and ustedes.
- Use commands to give instructions and make requests.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say: Habla más despacio. No hables tan rápido. Hable con el jefe. No se preocupe. Pásenme el informe. Then the irregulars: di, haz, ve, pon, sal, sé, ten, ven.

### Lecture

Commands (the imperative) are how you tell someone to do or not do something. Spanish has different forms for tú (informal), usted (formal), and ustedes (you all), and different forms for affirmative vs. negative (Reference section 6).

**Affirmative tú** is the easy one: it is identical to the **third-person singular present**. hablar to **habla**, comer to **come**, vivir to **vive**: *Habla con ella. Come algo.*

**Eight irregular affirmative tú commands** must be memorized: decir to **di**, hacer to **haz**, ir to **ve**, poner to **pon**, salir to **sal**, ser to **sé**, tener to **ten**, venir to **ven**. *Haz el trabajo. Ven aquí. Ten cuidado.*

**Everything else uses a subjunctive form** (which you meet fully in Week 3, but the command forms are worth learning now):
- **Negative tú:** no + the subjunctive tú form: *No hables. No comas. No vengas.*
- **Usted (affirmative and negative):** the subjunctive usted form: *Hable. No hable. Venga. No venga.*
- **Ustedes:** the subjunctive ustedes form: *Hablen. No hablen.*

To build these subjunctive command forms quickly: take the yo form of the present, drop the -o, and add the opposite vowel endings (-e for -ar verbs, -a for -er/-ir verbs). hablo to hable; tengo to tenga; vengo to venga. That single trick gives you the usted, ustedes, and negative tú commands at once.

**Pronoun placement with commands** (this is where people slip): attach to the **end** of an affirmative command (and add an accent to keep the stress): *Hazlo. Dígame. Levántate. Pásenmelo.* Put **before** a negative command: *No lo hagas. No te levantes. No me lo pases.*

Softening: in real life, a bare command can sound brusque, so people often soften with *por favor* or rephrase as a question (*¿Puede pasarme el informe?*). Use the command when instruction is appropriate, and the question form when you want to be gentle.

### Write by hand (20 min)
1. Write the affirmative and negative tú command, and the usted command, of: hablar, comer, escribir, hacer, venir.
2. Write five instructions you might really give someone (use a mix of tú and usted), then rewrite two of them with a pronoun attached.

### Speak and record (10 min)
Record yourself giving someone directions or instructions for a simple task (five to seven commands), using both affirmative and negative forms, then say two of them more politely as questions.

### Self-check
1. How do you form the affirmative tú command of a regular verb?
2. Give five of the eight irregular affirmative tú commands.
3. How do you form usted and negative-tú commands (the quick trick)?
4. Where do pronouns go with affirmative vs. negative commands?
5. How can you soften a command in real conversation?

# Day 10: The Future Tense

### Objectives
- Conjugate the future tense.
- Use it for predictions, plans, and probability.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say hablar in the future, twice: hablaré, hablarás, hablará, hablaremos, hablarán. Then: Mañana hablaré con el cliente. ¿Qué harás? Tendremos una reunión. ¿Dónde estará el jefe?

### Lecture

You already know one way to talk about the future, **ir a + infinitive** (*voy a trabajar*), which is the most common in everyday speech. The **future tense** is the second way: a bit more formal, used for predictions and promises, and with one special extra use (probability).

**Formation** is unusually easy (Reference section 3): for regular verbs you add the endings to **the whole infinitive**, not a chopped stem. The endings are the same for all three families: **-é, -ás, -á, -emos, -éis, -án.**

- hablar to **hablaré, hablarás, hablará, hablaremos, hablarán** (I will speak...)
- comer to comeré; vivir to viviré.

**Irregular stems** (Reference section 3): a dozen common verbs use a modified stem, but the same endings: tener to **tendr-** (tendré), poder to **podr-** (podré), hacer to **har-** (haré), decir to **dir-** (diré), salir to **saldr-**, poner to **pondr-**, venir to **vendr-**, querer to **querr-**, saber to **sabr-**, haber to **habr-**. Learn the stem and the rest follows.

**Uses:**
- **Predictions and plans:** *El año que viene viviré en otra ciudad.* (Next year I will live in another city.)
- **Promises:** *Te llamaré mañana.* (I will call you tomorrow.)
- **The future of probability**, a use English does not have: the future tense can express a guess about the present, "probably / must be." *¿Dónde está el jefe? Estará en la reunión.* (Where is the boss? He is probably in the meeting.) *¿Qué hora es? Serán las tres.* (It must be about three.) This is common in speech and worth recognizing.

### Write by hand (20 min)
1. Handwrite the future of **hablar**, **tener** (irregular stem), and **hacer** (irregular stem), saying each form aloud.
2. Write five sentences about your real plans or predictions for next year using the future tense.
3. Write two "probability" sentences guessing about the present (where someone is, what time it is).

### Speak and record (10 min)
Record yourself making five predictions about the next year and two guesses about the present using the future of probability.

### Self-check
1. What do you add the future endings to, for regular verbs?
2. Give the future endings for all persons.
3. Give the irregular future stems of tener, hacer, and decir.
4. Besides plans, what special meaning can the future tense carry?
5. Translate: "He is probably in the office" using the future.

# Day 11: The Conditional

### Objectives
- Conjugate the conditional.
- Use it for politeness, hypotheticals, and "would."

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say hablar in the conditional, twice: hablaría, hablarías, hablaría, hablaríamos, hablarían. Then: Me gustaría un café. ¿Podrías ayudarme? Yo no haría eso. Sería mejor esperar.

### Lecture

The **conditional** is the "would" tense: what would happen, what you would do, and, very usefully, a way to be polite. It is the twin of the future: **same stems, different endings.**

**Formation** (Reference section 3): add to the infinitive (or the irregular stem) the endings **-ía, -ías, -ía, -íamos, -íais, -ían** (the same endings as the -er/-ir imperfect, but built on the infinitive).

- hablar to **hablaría, hablarías, hablaría...** (I would speak...)
- The irregular stems are identical to the future: tener to tendría, hacer to haría, poder to podría, decir to diría, and so on.

**Uses:**
- **Hypothetical "would":** *Yo no haría eso.* (I would not do that.) *Sería difícil.* (It would be difficult.)
- **Politeness, the everyday workhorse use:** the conditional softens a request or wish so it does not sound blunt. Compare *Quiero* (I want) with *Querría* / *Me gustaría* (I would like). *¿Podrías ayudarme?* (Could you help me?) is far gentler than *¿Puedes...?* *¿Le importaría esperar?* (Would you mind waiting?)
- **Future-in-the-past** (reported speech): *Dijo que llamaría.* (He said he would call.)

For real interaction, the politeness use is the one to lock in. *Me gustaría...*, *¿Podría...?*, and *¿Le importaría...?* make you sound considerate and competent, which matters when you are speaking with people regularly.

### Write by hand (20 min)
1. Handwrite the conditional of **hablar**, **poder** (irregular stem), and **gustar** (me gustaría, etc.).
2. Write five polite requests or wishes using the conditional (me gustaría, podría, querría).
3. Write three hypothetical "I would..." sentences.

### Speak and record (10 min)
Record yourself making five polite requests you might really make at work or in a service situation, using the conditional, then two hypothetical statements about what you would do in some situation.

### Self-check
1. What does the conditional express?
2. What endings does it use, and what does it build them on?
3. Are the irregular stems the same as the future? Give one.
4. How does the conditional make a request more polite? Give an example.
5. Translate: "I would like a coffee, please."

# Day 12: The Present Perfect and the Progressive

### Objectives
- Use the present perfect (have done).
- Use the present progressive (am doing) correctly, and know when not to.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say: He hablado con el jefe. ¿Has comido? Hemos terminado. Then: Estoy trabajando ahora. Están comiendo. ¿Qué estás haciendo?

### Lecture

**The present perfect** says that something **has happened**, usually recently or with present relevance. It is formed with the present of **haber** (he, has, ha, hemos, habéis, han) plus the **past participle** (Reference section 4):

- *He terminado el informe.* (I have finished the report.)
- *¿Has hablado con ella?* (Have you spoken with her?)
- *Hemos tenido muchos problemas.* (We have had many problems.)

**Past participles:** -ar verbs to **-ado** (hablado, trabajado), -er and -ir verbs to **-ido** (comido, vivido). The common irregulars must be memorized: hacer to **hecho**, ver to **visto**, escribir to **escrito**, decir to **dicho**, poner to **puesto**, volver to **vuelto**, abrir to **abierto**, romper to **roto**, morir to **muerto**. Two points learners get wrong: haber and the participle **cannot be split** (never put a word between "he" and "hablado"), and in this tense the participle **never changes for gender or number** (it always ends in -o: *ella ha hablado*, not "hablada").

In much of Latin America the simple preterite often does the job the present perfect does in Spain (*Ya comí* vs. *Ya he comido* for "I already ate"). Both are correct; recognize both and use whichever the people around you use.

**The present progressive** (estar + gerund) is for an action **genuinely in progress right now** (Reference section 4):
- Gerund: -ar to **-ando** (hablando), -er/-ir to **-iendo** (comiendo, viviendo).
- *Estoy escribiendo un correo.* (I am writing an email, right now.)

The trap, especially for English speakers: **do not overuse it.** English uses "I am ...ing" for habitual and even future actions ("I am working tomorrow"), but Spanish uses the **simple present** for those. *Trabajo aquí* (I work / I am working here, in general), not "estoy trabajando aquí." Reserve the progressive for what is literally happening at this moment.

### Write by hand (20 min)
1. Handwrite the present perfect of **terminar**, **hacer** (irregular participle), and **ver** (irregular participle) for yo, tú, and nosotros.
2. Write four sentences about what you have done today using the present perfect.
3. Write three sentences about what you are doing right now using the progressive, and one habitual sentence that should NOT use the progressive (write it in the simple present and note why).

### Speak and record (10 min)
Record yourself saying what you have already done today (present perfect) and what you are doing at this moment (progressive), keeping the two uses distinct.

### Self-check
1. How is the present perfect formed?
2. Give the past participles of hablar, comer, hacer, and ver.
3. Can you split "he" and "hablado," and does the participle change for gender?
4. How is the progressive formed?
5. Why is "estoy trabajando aquí" wrong for "I work here in general"?

# Day 13: Comparisons, Superlatives, and Por vs. Para, then Quiz 2

### Objectives
- Compare and rank things.
- Choose correctly between por and para.
- Take Quiz 2.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say: Ella es más alta que yo. Es tan rápido como tú. Es el mejor restaurante. Then: Gracias por tu ayuda. Es para el jefe. Salgo para Madrid. Caminé por el parque.

### Lecture

**Comparisons** (Reference section 9) are built from a few frames:
- **más ... que** (more than): *Este trabajo es más difícil que el otro.*
- **menos ... que** (less than): *Es menos caro que ese.*
- **tan ... como** (as ... as): *Soy tan alto como mi hermano.*
- **tanto/a/os/as ... como** (as much/many ... as), agreeing with the noun: *Tengo tantos clientes como tú.*
- **Superlative** (the most/least): *el/la/los/las + más/menos + adjective*: *Es el más grande. Es la mejor opción.*
- **Irregular comparatives:** mejor (better), peor (worse), mayor (older), menor (younger): *Este es mejor que ese.*

**Por vs. para** is the classic intermediate hurdle, because both translate as "for" but they are not interchangeable (Reference section 8). The reliable mental model: **para points forward to a destination, goal, or deadline; por points back to a cause, or describes a means, exchange, or route.**

**para** for goal, recipient, destination, deadline:
- *Estudio para hablar mejor.* (I study in order to speak better. Goal.)
- *Este informe es para el jefe.* (Recipient.)
- *Salgo para la oficina.* (Destination.)
- *Es para el viernes.* (Deadline.)

**por** for cause, duration, exchange, means, movement through:
- *Gracias por tu ayuda.* (Cause/reason.)
- *Trabajé por ocho horas.* (Duration.)
- *Pagué veinte dólares por esto.* (Exchange.)
- *Hablamos por teléfono.* (Means.)
- *Caminé por el centro.* (Through/along.)

When you are unsure, ask: is this about the **goal or destination** (para) or the **reason, route, or trade** (por)? It will not be automatic for a while, and that is normal; this is a distinction even good speakers keep refining.

### Write by hand (15 min)
1. Write three comparison sentences (one each of más...que, tan...como, and a superlative) about people or things you know.
2. Fill por or para and write the full sentence: Gracias ___ todo. / Es ___ ti. / Estudio ___ aprender. / Caminamos ___ la calle. / La tarea es ___ mañana. / Pagué diez dólares ___ el libro.

### Quiz 2
Take **Quiz 2** now (see `QUIZZES.md`), closed-book. Grade against `ANSWER-KEYS.md`. It covers reflexives, commands, the future, the conditional, the perfect and progressive, comparisons, and por/para, the whole Week 2 core. If you score below 90%, re-study the weak spot on tomorrow's review day.

### Self-check
1. Give the frames for "more than," "as ... as," and the superlative.
2. Give the irregular comparatives for good and bad.
3. In one line, what is the difference between por and para?
4. Which one for a goal or deadline? Which for a reason or duration?
5. por or para: "Thanks for your help" and "It is for the boss."
