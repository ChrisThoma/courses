# SPAN-20 Syllabus: Spanish II, Intermediate Conversation & Grammar

## Course description

A three-week intensive that takes a beginning speaker to functional intermediate Spanish. Students learn to narrate the past with the preterite and imperfect and to choose correctly between them, to make speech concise with object pronouns, to handle daily routine with reflexive verbs, to give instructions with commands, to talk about the future and the hypothetical, to use the perfect and progressive tenses, to compare things, to master por vs. para, and to express wishes, doubts, and emotions with the present subjunctive. The back of the course turns this grammar into natural, connected, real-world conversation, with attention to register and the strategies that keep a real exchange going. By the end, students can discuss past, present, and future events and opinions in a sustained conversation and write accurate connected prose.

This is a **speaking-and-writing course**: the bulk of the work is producing the language yourself, every day, out loud and on paper.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this course you will be able to:

1. **Conjugate and use the preterite**, including its common irregulars, to narrate completed past actions.
2. **Conjugate and use the imperfect** to describe ongoing, habitual, and background past situations.
3. **Choose correctly between preterite and imperfect** when narrating, the central intermediate skill.
4. **Use direct, indirect, and double object pronouns** in the correct order and position.
5. **Use reflexive verbs** for daily routine and other reflexive actions.
6. **Form and use commands**, affirmative and negative, with tú, usted, and ustedes.
7. **Use the future and conditional tenses** for plans, predictions, probability, and polite or hypothetical statements.
8. **Use the present perfect and the progressive** tenses correctly.
9. **Compare and rank things** with comparatives and superlatives, and **choose correctly between por and para**.
10. **Form the present subjunctive and use it** after expressions of wish, emotion, doubt, and the main triggering conjunctions, in real sentences.

## Daily structure (about 60 to 90 minutes)

| Block | Time | Activity |
|---|---|---|
| Warm up and review | 10 min | Spaced-repetition cards; redo one conjugation from an earlier tense from memory; the day's quick drill |
| Listen and say | 5 min | Hear the day's new structure in context and repeat each example out loud |
| Lecture | 15 to 20 min | Read the lecture; say every example aloud as you reach it |
| Write by hand | 15 to 20 min | Handwrite the day's conjugation tables and copy the examples, then write your own |
| Speak and record | 10 to 15 min | The day's speaking task; record it and listen back |
| Self-check | 5 min | Answer the self-check from memory; note what to review tomorrow |

Grade yourself against `RUBRIC.md`. **Mastery threshold: 90%.** The written work is graded on exact correctness, where accents and agreement are right or wrong; the spoken work is graded by recording and listening back honestly. A tense you can conjugate on paper but cannot produce in a flowing sentence is not done yet.

## Course policies

- **Out loud, always.** Intermediate grammar is only real when you can produce it in real time. Every example, drill, and answer gets said aloud.
- **By hand, always.** Conjugations across tenses and your own compositions are written by hand. The accent that separates "hablo" from "habló" is exactly the kind of thing the pen makes you see.
- **Review the old tenses every day.** The intermediate trap is not learning a new form but losing an old one. Keep the previous tenses warm with the spaced-repetition system.
- **Short and daily beats long and rare.** Memory consolidates between sessions; a language is built in many short sittings, not a few marathons.
- **Make mistakes on purpose.** Narrate, guess the tense, find out, fix it. The corrected error is what sticks. Closed-book quizzes exist to expose gaps.
- **Record yourself weekly.** You will hear your real pace and your tense errors, not the flattering version in your head.
- **Falling behind:** use the weekly review day to catch up. If you are more than a day behind, slow down rather than skip. The preterite/imperfect choice (Day 4) and the subjunctive (Week 3) are the load-bearing days; protect those.
