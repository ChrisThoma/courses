# Week 3: The Subjunctive & Real Fluency

> **The thesis of this week:** the subjunctive is the last major structure between you and being able to say almost anything, and it is far less frightening than its reputation. It is a verb mood used in specific, learnable situations: when you express a wish, an emotion, a doubt, an opinion, or use certain conjunctions. We learn to form it, then walk into its uses one trigger at a time. The back half of the week shifts from grammar to fluency: professional register, softening, connectors, and the survival strategies that keep a real conversation moving. Speak everything out loud; this is the week it all has to come together in real time.

The subjunctive conjugation tables and trigger lists are in the [Reference file](SUPPLEMENT-reference-and-vocabulary.md), sections 7 and 10.

# Day 15: Forming the Present Subjunctive

### Objectives
- Form the present subjunctive of regular and irregular verbs.
- Understand, at a high level, what the subjunctive is for.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say the subjunctive of hablar and comer, twice: hable, hables, hable, hablemos, hablen; coma, comas, coma, comamos, coman. Then: Quiero que hables con él. Es importante que comas. Espero que vengas.

### Lecture

First, what the subjunctive **is**. So far every verb you have used has been in the **indicative** mood, which states facts: what is, was, or will be. The **subjunctive** is a different mood, used not for facts but for things that are **wished, doubted, felt about, or hypothetical**, the things that live in someone's head rather than in the world. English barely has it ("I suggest that he *be* on time"), so it feels foreign, but Spanish uses it constantly.

Today is just the **forms**; tomorrow is when to use them. The formation is one you already met building usted commands (Reference section 7):

**Take the yo form of the present indicative, drop the -o, and add the opposite endings:**
- -ar verbs take **e-endings**: -e, -es, -e, -emos, -éis, -en. hablar (yo hablo) to **hable, hables, hable, hablemos, habléis, hablen.**
- -er and -ir verbs take **a-endings**: -a, -as, -a, -amos, -áis, -an. comer (yo como) to **coma, comas...**; vivir (yo vivo) to **viva, vivas...**

Because it is built from the **yo form**, every irregular yo carries straight in, which means most "irregular" verbs are actually predictable in the subjunctive:
- tener (tengo) to **tenga, tengas, tenga...**
- hacer (hago) to **haga**; decir (digo) to **diga**; salir (salgo) to **salga**; conocer (conozco) to **conozca**; ver (veo) to **vea.**
- Stem-changers carry their change: querer to **quiera**, poder to **pueda**, pensar to **piense.**

**Six verbs are fully irregular** (not from a yo form) and must be memorized: ser to **sea**, estar to **esté**, ir to **vaya**, haber to **haya**, saber to **sepa**, dar to **dé.** A memory hook some use is the nonsense word "DISHES": Dar, Ir, Saber, Haber, Estar, Ser.

### Write by hand (20 min)
1. Handwrite the present subjunctive of **hablar**, **comer**, and **vivir** fully, saying each form aloud.
2. Write the subjunctive yo and tú forms of: tener, hacer, salir, querer, poder (the carry-over irregulars).
3. Write the full subjunctive of **ser** and **ir** (the fully irregular ones).

### Speak and record (10 min)
Record yourself reciting the subjunctive of three regular verbs and the six fully irregular verbs from memory. No use yet, just clean forms.

### Self-check
1. What is the subjunctive mood for, in contrast to the indicative?
2. What is the formation rule (from which form, and which endings)?
3. What endings do -ar verbs take? What about -er/-ir?
4. Why are most "irregular" verbs predictable in the subjunctive?
5. Name the six fully irregular subjunctive verbs.

# Day 16: The Subjunctive with Wishes, Emotions, and Doubt

### Objectives
- Use the subjunctive after expressions of wish, request, emotion, and doubt.
- Recognize the two-clause structure that triggers it.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say: Quiero que vengas. Espero que estés bien. Me alegro de que estés aquí. Dudo que sea verdad. No creo que tenga razón.

### Lecture

The subjunctive almost always appears in a **two-part sentence** joined by **que**, where the first part triggers it. The pattern is:

> [main clause that expresses wish / emotion / doubt] + **que** + [second subject, verb in **subjunctive**]

The key condition is a **change of subject**: someone wants, hopes, or doubts that *someone else* do something. If there is no change of subject, you just use an infinitive (*Quiero salir*, I want to leave), but as soon as a second subject enters, you need que plus the subjunctive (*Quiero que tú salgas*, I want you to leave).

The big trigger categories (Reference section 10):

**Wishes, wants, and requests** (one person wanting another to do something):
- *Quiero que hables con el cliente.* (I want you to speak with the client.)
- *El jefe pide que terminemos hoy.* (The boss asks that we finish today.)
- *Necesito que me ayudes.* (I need you to help me.)
- *Te recomiendo que estudies.* (I recommend that you study.)

**Emotions** (reacting to something):
- *Me alegro de que estés aquí.* (I am glad you are here.)
- *Siento que no puedas venir.* (I am sorry you can't come.)
- *Tengo miedo de que sea tarde.* (I am afraid it is late.)

**Doubt, denial, and disbelief:**
- *Dudo que venga.* (I doubt he will come.)
- *No creo que tenga razón.* (I don't think he is right.) Note: *creo que tiene razón* (I think he is right) stays indicative, because believing is not doubting. The negative flips it to subjunctive.
- *No es verdad que sea fácil.* (It is not true that it is easy.)

**Ojalá** ("I hope / hopefully," from an Arabic phrase) always takes the subjunctive: *Ojalá llueva. Ojalá que todo salga bien.*

A useful memory aid for all the triggers is **WEIRDO**: Wishes, Emotions, Impersonal expressions, Recommendations/Requests, Doubt/Denial, Ojalá. If your main clause is one of those and there is a change of subject across "que," reach for the subjunctive.

### Write by hand (20 min)
1. Complete each with the correct subjunctive form, written out: Quiero que tú (hablar) con él. / Espero que ustedes (estar) bien. / Dudo que ella (venir). / Me alegro de que nosotros (poder) ir. / El jefe pide que yo (hacer) el informe.
2. Write five sentences of your own: a wish, a request to someone, an emotion, a doubt, and an "ojalá," each with a change of subject.

### Speak and record (10 min)
Record yourself saying five things you want other people to do (Quiero que...), two things you hope (Espero que... / Ojalá...), and two things you doubt (Dudo que...).

### Self-check
1. What two-clause structure triggers the subjunctive?
2. What condition across the two clauses is required (besides the trigger)?
3. Why is "Quiero salir" an infinitive but "Quiero que salgas" a subjunctive?
4. Why does "no creo que" take the subjunctive but "creo que" does not?
5. What does "ojalá" mean and what mood does it require?

# Day 17: The Subjunctive with Impersonal Expressions and Conjunctions

### Objectives
- Use the subjunctive after impersonal opinion expressions.
- Use it after the conjunctions that require it.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say: Es importante que llegues a tiempo. Es necesario que hablemos. Te llamo antes de que salgas. Trabajo para que tengamos más clientes.

### Lecture

Two more trigger types complete your working command of the subjunctive.

**Impersonal expressions of opinion or necessity** ("it is important/necessary/possible that...") trigger the subjunctive when a specific subject follows:
- *Es importante que llegues a tiempo.* (It is important that you arrive on time.)
- *Es necesario que el equipo entienda el plan.* (It is necessary that the team understand the plan.)
- *Es mejor que esperemos.* (It is better that we wait.)
- *Es posible que tengan razón.* (It is possible that they are right.)

But impersonal expressions of **certainty** stay indicative, because there is no doubt: *Es verdad que tiene razón. Es obvio que está cansado.* Certainty is indicative; everything short of certainty (importance, possibility, doubt) tends to the subjunctive.

**Certain conjunctions** always take the subjunctive because they introduce something not-yet-real, a purpose or a condition. The common ones to memorize:
- **para que** (so that), **antes de que** (before), **sin que** (without), **a menos que** (unless), **con tal de que** (provided that), **en caso de que** (in case).
- *Trabajo para que mi familia tenga una buena vida.* (I work so that my family has a good life.)
- *Llámame antes de que salgas.* (Call me before you leave.)
- *No voy a menos que tú vayas.* (I am not going unless you go.)

A separate group of time conjunctions (**cuando**, **hasta que**, **en cuanto**, "when / until / as soon as") takes the subjunctive **only when the event is still in the future**: *Cuando llegues, llámame* (When you arrive, call me, it has not happened yet, subjunctive). Compare the habitual *Cuando llego, llamo* (When I arrive, I call, a routine fact, indicative). This nuance takes time; for now, learn the always-subjunctive conjunctions solidly and recognize the future-time pattern.

### Write by hand (20 min)
1. Complete with the subjunctive: Es importante que tú (estar) preparado. / Es necesario que ellos (saber) la verdad. / Te lo explico para que (entender, tú). / Salgo antes de que (llegar) el jefe.
2. Write four sentences of your own: two with an impersonal expression (es importante/necesario que...) and two with a conjunction (para que, antes de que).

### Speak and record (10 min)
Record yourself saying three things that are important or necessary for someone to do (Es importante que...), and two purpose sentences with para que.

### Self-check
1. Give three impersonal expressions that trigger the subjunctive.
2. Why does "es verdad que" stay indicative?
3. Name four conjunctions that always take the subjunctive.
4. When does "cuando" take the subjunctive vs. the indicative?
5. Complete: "Te llamo antes de que tú (salir)."

# Day 18: Professional and Register-Aware Spanish

### Objectives
- Speak appropriately in formal and professional situations.
- Soften requests and disagreement.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say: ¿Sería posible...? Me gustaría comentar algo. Disculpe que lo interrumpa. Quedo a su disposición. Le agradezco su tiempo.

### Lecture

Knowing the grammar is half of communicating well with people regularly. The other half is **register**: matching how you speak to the situation, so you come across as competent and respectful. Today assembles the tools you already have into professional, register-aware speech.

**Default to usted in professional and first-contact situations**, and keep the verb forms consistent. Switching mid-conversation between tú and usted is a common slip; pick one and hold it until invited to change.

**Soften with the conditional and with questions** (Days 9 and 11). Bare imperatives and bald "quiero" can sound abrupt. Softer versions:
- *Querría / Me gustaría...* instead of *Quiero...*
- *¿Podría...? / ¿Sería posible...? / ¿Le importaría...?* instead of a flat command.
- *¿Me permite...?* (May I...?) for politeness when you need to do something.

**Soften disagreement and bad news**, which matters when you must keep a working relationship:
- *Entiendo su punto, pero...* (I understand your point, but...)
- *Tal vez sería mejor...* (Perhaps it would be better...)
- *No estoy seguro de que sea posible.* (I am not sure that it is possible.) Note the subjunctive after the doubt.
- *Lamento informarle que...* (I regret to inform you that...)

**Useful fixed professional phrases** (memorize as chunks): *Quedo a su disposición* (I remain at your disposal), *Le agradezco* (I thank you / I appreciate), *Con mucho gusto* (Gladly), *Por supuesto* (Of course), *Permítame un momento* (Allow me a moment), *¿En qué puedo ayudarle?* (How can I help you?), *Que tenga un buen día* (Have a good day).

**Idioms and set phrases** make you sound natural, but use them where they fit. A few high-utility ones: *vale la pena* (it is worth it), *en serio* (seriously / really), *de hecho* (in fact), *o sea* (in other words), *poco a poco* (little by little), *darse cuenta* (to realize), *tener en cuenta* (to bear in mind), *a lo mejor* (maybe).

### Write by hand (20 min)
1. Rewrite each blunt sentence in a softer, professional register: "Quiero hablar con el jefe." / "Repita." / "No, eso no es posible." / "Necesito el informe hoy."
2. Write a short formal message (five to six lines) to a coworker or client, using usted, a conditional softener, and at least one professional fixed phrase.

### Speak and record (10 min)
Record yourself handling a professional situation aloud: greet formally, make a softened request, express a polite disagreement, and close with a courteous phrase.

### Self-check
1. Which "you" do you default to in professional situations, and what is the common slip?
2. Give two ways to soften a request.
3. How do you politely disagree while keeping the relationship?
4. Why does "No estoy seguro de que sea posible" use the subjunctive?
5. Give three professional fixed phrases.

# Day 19: Connected Speech and Fluency Strategies

### Objectives
- Link ideas smoothly with discourse markers.
- Use reported speech and circumlocution to keep a conversation moving.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say these connectors aloud with a sample sentence each: sin embargo, además, por lo tanto, es decir, o sea, de hecho, la verdad es que, me parece que.

### Lecture

Intermediate speakers who still sound like beginners usually have the grammar but lack the **connective tissue**: the small words and strategies that turn a list of correct sentences into flowing speech.

**Discourse markers and connectors** (Reference section 10) link and signal your ideas:
- Contrast: **sin embargo** (however), **en cambio** (on the other hand), **aunque** (although).
- Addition: **además** (besides), **también** (also).
- Result: **por lo tanto** (therefore), **por eso** (that is why), **así que** (so).
- Clarifying: **es decir / o sea** (that is to say / in other words), **de hecho** (in fact).
- Opinion: **me parece que** (it seems to me), **la verdad es que** (the truth is), **creo que** (I think).
Using two or three of these in a turn instantly raises how fluent you sound.

**Fillers buy thinking time** without dead silence, exactly as "um, well, you know" do in English: **pues..., bueno..., a ver..., este..., o sea...** A natural filler is better than a long silent pause or switching to English.

**Reported speech** lets you relay what someone said, and it follows a tense logic you already have the pieces for: a present "dice que" keeps the tense (*Dice que viene*), while a past "dijo que" shifts forward in the past (present to imperfect, future to conditional): *Dijo que venía. Dijo que llamaría.* You do not need to master every shift now, but recognize the pattern, because real conversation is full of "he said that..."

**Circumlocution is your most important fluency tool**, and it never stops being useful. When you do not know a word, **talk around it** rather than stop:
- Forgot "wrench"? *la herramienta para los tornillos* (the tool for screws).
- Forgot "deadline"? *la fecha en que tengo que entregarlo* (the date I have to hand it in by).
- Forgot a verb? Describe the action: *la cosa que haces cuando...*
The willingness to keep going in Spanish, imperfectly, is what separates people who become conversational from people who stall. A speaker who can describe their way around any gap can have a real conversation with only a few hundred words.

### Write by hand (20 min)
1. Rewrite this choppy paragraph into flowing speech by adding connectors: "Trabajo mucho. No tengo tiempo. Quiero aprender español. Es difícil. Voy a seguir." (Add at least three connectors.)
2. Write three "circumlocution" definitions: describe three words in Spanish without naming them, as if you had forgotten the word.

### Speak and record (10 min)
Record yourself speaking for ninety seconds on a familiar topic, deliberately using at least four connectors and at least one filler, and at least one moment where you talk around a word instead of stopping.

### Self-check
1. Name two contrast connectors and two result connectors.
2. What are fillers for, and name three?
3. How does "dijo que" shift the tense of what follows?
4. What is circumlocution and why does it matter so much?
5. Describe "a stapler" in Spanish without naming it.

# Day 20: Putting It Together & Project 3

### Objectives
- Sustain a discussion that moves across tenses and moods.
- Begin Project 3.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Read the model exchange below aloud twice before studying it.

### Lecture

Everything in both courses exists to let you handle the kind of exchange below: a real discussion that moves freely across time and mood. Read it, then notice how many structures it uses.

> A: Buenos días. Le agradezco que me reciba. Quería comentarle algo sobre el proyecto.
> B: Por supuesto. Dígame.
> A: La semana pasada terminamos la primera parte, pero tuvimos algunos problemas con el horario. Mientras trabajábamos, el cliente cambió de idea.
> B: Entiendo. ¿Y qué recomienda usted?
> A: Me parece que sería mejor que hablemos con él antes de seguir. Es importante que entienda los costos. Si no, tendremos más problemas después.
> B: Tiene razón. ¿Podría preparar un resumen para mañana?
> A: Claro. Se lo mando esta tarde. Ojalá que todo salga bien.
> B: Gracias. Quedo a su disposición si necesita algo.

In that short exchange you can find: formal register and usted throughout, the preterite (terminamos, tuvimos, cambió) and imperfect (trabajábamos) working together, an object pronoun (se lo mando), the conditional for politeness (quería, sería, podría), the subjunctive after a recommendation and an impersonal expression and ojalá (que hablemos, que entienda, que salga), por/para, and connectors. That is the whole intermediate system in eight lines.

Read it again and label, in your notebook, each structure and which day it came from. That is the proof that you now have a working system across past, present, future, and the subjunctive.

**When you stall in a real discussion** (and you will): use the Day 19 tools. Buy time with a filler, ask for a repeat or a rephrase, talk around the missing word, and keep the turn alive in Spanish. Fluency is not never getting stuck; it is getting unstuck without leaving the language.

### Write by hand (15 min)
Write your own twelve-to-sixteen-line discussion, by hand, set in a real situation you face, that uses at least: one preterite and one imperfect, one object pronoun, one conditional softener, one subjunctive (after a wish, doubt, or impersonal expression), one por or para, and two connectors. Full accents and punctuation.

### Project 3
Begin **Project 3: Opinions and Hypotheticals** (see `PROJECTS.md`): you will record yourself discussing an opinion and a hypothetical, using the subjunctive, conditional, and connectors, and turn in the handwritten script. Due with the final materials.

### Self-check
1. Find three different tenses or moods in the model exchange and name each.
2. Which line uses the subjunctive, and what triggers it?
3. Which line uses an object pronoun?
4. What do you do when you stall mid-discussion?
5. Why is writing the discussion by hand before recording it useful?

Today you close the course. Two things.

### 1. Final Exam
Take the **Final Exam** (see `QUIZZES.md`), closed-book, and grade it honestly against `ANSWER-KEYS.md`. The mastery bar is 90%.

### 2. Final Interview Recording
Record and submit the **Final Interview** (see `PROJECTS.md`): a sustained conversation, ideally with a real partner, that ranges across past, present, future, opinion, and the hypothetical, plus the handwritten preparation and a short reflection. This is the capstone and the largest single piece of your grade.

### Where to go next
You now have the full structural core of the language: every common tense, the object and reflexive pronouns, commands, and a working subjunctive. To turn this into real fluency:
- **Use it constantly with real people.** At this level, real conversation does more than any exercise. If you regularly speak with Spanish speakers, push yourself to stay in Spanish through the hard moments, using the circumlocution and repair tools from Day 19. The stalls are where the growth is.
- **Add input you enjoy.** Podcasts, shows, and reading at your level feed your ear and vocabulary far faster than drills alone.
- **Keep a small daily habit.** Ten minutes of spaced-repetition review and one out-loud monologue keeps everything sharp between conversations.
- **Continue toward advanced study:** the past subjunctive and conditional-sentence patterns ("si tuviera tiempo, iría"), the more literary tenses, and the regional varieties of the people you speak with most. You have the foundation to absorb all of it through use.

Lo lograste. Ahora puedes hablar de casi todo. Sigue hablando. (You did it. Now you can talk about almost anything. Keep talking.)
