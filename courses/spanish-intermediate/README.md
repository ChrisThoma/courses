# SPAN-20: Spanish II, Intermediate Conversation & Grammar (A 3-Week Intensive)

**Format:** Self-paced intensive, modeled on a college January-term language course
**Time commitment:** about 60 to 90 minutes per day, 6 days per week, for 3 weeks (one lighter review day each week)
**Prerequisites:** A solid command of beginning Spanish, equivalent to **SPAN-10: Foundations for Speaking**. You should already be comfortable with pronunciation and stress, noun gender and agreement, ser vs. estar, the present tense of regular and common irregular verbs, questions and negation, gustar, and basic everyday conversation. If any of those are shaky, spend a few days in the SPAN-10 Reference file before starting. You also need a notebook and pen, a way to record and play back your voice, and a tolerance for getting tenses wrong out loud for a while.

> **The promise of this course:** in three weeks you will move from "I can talk about now" to "I can talk about anything, in any time frame." You will narrate the past, give instructions, talk about the future and the hypothetical, compare things, and express wishes, doubts, and emotions with the subjunctive. More than that, you will sound less like a textbook and more like a person, because the back half of the course is about connected, natural, real-world speech.

## What this course is, and what it is not

This course teaches **the structures that separate a beginner from a genuinely functional intermediate speaker**:

- The two past tenses, **preterite** and **imperfect**, and the single most important intermediate skill: choosing between them
- **Object pronouns** (direct, indirect, and the two together), which make speech concise and natural instead of repetitive
- **Reflexive verbs** for daily routine and for a large class of everyday actions
- **Commands**, affirmative and negative, formal and informal, for giving instructions and making requests
- The **future** and **conditional** tenses, for plans, predictions, probability, and politeness
- The **perfect tenses** (have done, had done) and the **progressive** (am doing)
- **Comparisons and superlatives**, and the full **por vs. para** distinction
- A working command of the **present subjunctive**: how to form it and the main situations that require it
- The register, connectors, and survival strategies of real professional and everyday conversation

It does not try to make you sound native or cover every tense and nuance. It leaves the past subjunctive, the more literary tenses, and fine regional detail for continued study. What it gives you is the structural core that lets you say almost anything you need to say, and the habits to keep improving.

## How this language is actually learned (still true at this level)

The same three forces from SPAN-10 still do the work, and they matter more now, not less:

1. **Speaking out loud, every day.** Intermediate grammar only becomes usable when you can produce it in real time. Every lesson has you say things aloud and record yourself.
2. **Writing by hand.** You will handwrite conjugations across multiple tenses and write your own compositions. By hand is slow on purpose: it forces you to notice the accent that distinguishes "hablo" (I speak) from "habló" (he spoke), and the agreement on a past participle, that the eye skips when reading. **Handwritten work is a required, graded part of this course.**
3. **Spaced repetition.** You now have several tenses competing for the same verbs. Scheduled review is what keeps them from blurring together. The Practice Menu gives you the system.

## How to use this course

1. Read `SYLLABUS.md` and `RUBRIC.md` first. Skim the Reference verb tables so you know what is coming; you will return to that file constantly.
2. Each day, work the lesson in order: **Listen and say, then Lecture, then Write by hand, then Speak and record, then Self-check.**
3. **Always out loud, always with your notebook open.** No silent reading, no typing the conjugations. The friction is the point.
4. **Review old tenses every day.** Once you have three or four tenses, the danger is no longer learning a new form but keeping the old ones sharp and distinct. The spaced-repetition system handles this; do not skip it.
5. Take the quizzes closed-book and grade yourself honestly against `ANSWER-KEYS.md`.
6. Do the projects. They are where a tense you can conjugate becomes a story you can tell.

## A note on pace and on the subjunctive

Three weeks is enough to **learn and begin using** the full intermediate system, including the subjunctive. It is not enough to make all of it automatic, and nobody expects that. The preterite/imperfect choice and the subjunctive in particular keep getting more natural for months of real use afterward. What this course gives you is correct rules, enough reps to start producing them, and the daily habit so that the months that follow compound.

A word on the subjunctive specifically: many learners fear it. It is not as hard as its reputation. It is a verb mood you use in specific, learnable situations (wishes, doubts, emotions, certain conjunctions), and Week 3 walks you into it one trigger at a time. Treat it as the natural next step it is.
