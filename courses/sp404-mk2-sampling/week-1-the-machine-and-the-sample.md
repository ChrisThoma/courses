# Week 1: The Machine & The Sample

> **The thesis of this week:** everything the SP-404 MK2 does starts with *one sound on one pad*. Before you can chop, sequence, or effect anything, you have to get a clean sample into the machine, trim it so it starts and ends where you want, and play it back in tune and in time. That is the whole craft in miniature. So Week 1 is about the loop from ear to pad: hear something, record it well, trim it, pitch it, play it. Get this solid and the rest of the course is building on rock. Rush it (record everything too quiet, leave sloppy edges on your samples) and every beat you make later will sound amateur for reasons you cannot find. Go slow and listen hard.

Before Day 1, read the [Field Guide](FIELD-GUIDE.md) sections on the layout, storage and backup, and signal flow, and **update your firmware** as it instructs. The SP saves your work automatically as you go; what it cannot do is undo your mistakes or survive a storage failure. Throughout the course, "back up" means the actual backup from Field Guide §2, done on schedule.

# Day 1: Meet the SP-404 MK2

### Objectives
- Identify the main parts of the machine and what each input and output is for.
- Power up, load a project, and understand that everything lives on the machine's internal storage.
- Run a backup and know why it, not a save button, is what protects your work.
- Know, in one paragraph, what a sampler is and where this one comes from.

### Listen (5 min)
Before you touch a button, **listen** to a beat made on this kind of machine. Search "Madlib beat tape" or "SP-404 beat" and play two minutes. Do not analyze yet. Just notice that a lot of what you hear is clearly *taken from somewhere else*: a snatch of an old song, a drum break, a voice, all rearranged into something new. That act of taking and rearranging is sampling, and it is what you are here to learn.

### Lecture

**A sampler is a machine that records short pieces of sound and lets you play them back from buttons.** That is the entire idea. A piano makes its own sound; a sampler plays back *recordings*, which means it can play anything: a drum hit, a horn stab, a word, a vinyl crackle. The SP-404 MK2 records sound onto its 16 pads and lets you trigger, chop, sequence, and mangle those recordings into music. Once you internalize "it is just recordings on buttons," the machine stops being mysterious.

**The layout in brief** (the full tour is in [Field Guide §1](FIELD-GUIDE.md)): 16 velocity-sensitive **pads** are your playing surface; the **bank buttons** beside them give you 10 banks (A through J) of 16 pads each, per project; **REC** records; **PATTERN SELECT** opens the sequencer; the **knob** and **screen** move you through menus; **SHIFT** reaches the second function on many buttons. On the back: **LINE IN** and **MIC/GUITAR IN** to get sound in, **LINE OUT** and **PHONES** to get sound out, **USB-C** for power and computer transfer, and an **SD card** slot for backups. Your work itself lives on the machine's internal storage.

**Everything lives on the machine's internal storage, organized into projects.** A **project** is one world: 10 banks of samples, its patterns, its tempo, its settings ([Field Guide §2](FIELD-GUIDE.md)). When you power on, the machine loads a project and lands in **sample mode** with the pads lit, ready to play. The SP **saves as you go**: record a sample or change a pattern and it is already written, no save button to forget. The catch is that your mistakes are written just as faithfully, and internal storage can still fail or fill. So the single most important habit you will build this week is the **backup**: copy your work out to an SD card or a computer every week. Lost work is the number-one reason beginners quit; do not be a statistic.

**One paragraph of history** (the long version is in [Field Guide §6](FIELD-GUIDE.md)): the SP line began in 1998 as a cheap, gritty "Dr. Sample" box, found its soul in the **SP-303** and its **Vinyl Sim** effect (the lo-fi filter Madlib built a sound on), became the standard sampler of the beat scene through the **SP-404** family (Flying Lotus, Knxwledge, Kaytranada), and arrived in 2021 as the **MK2**: the same lo-fi heart with a modern brain. You are joining a real lineage, and the people who defined it are exactly who you will go study when you want to get better.

### Do: today's assignment
1. **(10 min) Power up and look.** Plug in headphones, power on, and let it land in sample mode. Press a few pads and hear the factory samples. Identify by eye: the pads, the bank buttons, REC, SHIFT, the knob, the pitch knob, and the inputs/outputs on the back. Say each one's job out loud.
2. **(5 min) Switch banks.** Press the bank buttons and hear how each bank is a different set of 16 pads. Press a bank button a second time to reach the "F–J" banks (it flashes). Now you have felt all 160 pad slots of a project.
3. **(5 min) Back up.** Run your first backup (Field Guide §2): put an SD card in the slot and use the backup in the UTILITY menu (hold SHIFT and press pad 13), or connect USB-C and copy your projects to a computer with the SP-404 MK2 app. Confirm it landed. You now have a backup and a habit.

### Self-check
1. In one sentence, what is a sampler?
2. How many banks does a project have, and how many pads per bank? So how many pad slots total?
3. Where does everything you make get stored?
4. What does the SP do about saving as you work, and what habit actually protects that work?
5. Which effect and which producer are most associated with the SP sound?

# Day 2: Playing the Pads

### Objectives
- Play the factory samples fluently across pads and banks.
- Understand velocity (soft vs hard presses).
- Set a pad to gate, one-shot, or loop, and know when to use each.
- Use hold and the sub pad.

### Listen (3 min)
Replay a beat from Day 1. This time, count how many *different sounds* are going at once: a loop, a kick, a snare, a hat, maybe a vocal. Each of those is a pad being played. A beat is just pads triggered in time. Today you start triggering them.

### Lecture

In **sample mode** (where the machine boots), each pad plays its sample when you press it. The pads are **velocity-sensitive**: press softly for a quiet hit, hard for a loud one. This is not a gimmick; it is how you put dynamics and feeling into drums later, so start feeling it now. A machine-stiff beat where every hit is the same volume sounds dead, and velocity is the cure.

**The three ways a pad can play** ([Field Guide §5](FIELD-GUIDE.md)) are the most important setting you control:

- **One-shot (trigger):** one press plays the whole sample through; releasing does nothing. This is what you want for **drum hits** and one-off sounds.
- **Gate:** the sample plays only while you *hold* the pad and stops the instant you release. This is what you want for **chops** you play rhythmically, where how long you hold is part of the performance.
- **Loop:** the sample plays and repeats until you stop it. This is for **drum loops** and background beds you build over.

You set this per pad in its parameters. Get in the habit of asking, for every sample, "is this a one-shot, a gate, or a loop?" The answer shapes how the whole beat feels.

**Hold and the sub pad.** The **hold/remain** function lets a sample keep playing hands-free (hold the HOLD pad, then tap a sample, and it sustains), which frees your hands to play other pads on top. The **sub pad** is a 17th pad with special performance roles (rolls, re-triggering the last sound). You will use both later; today just meet them.

**Mono vs poly (re-trigger behavior).** When a pad is set so that pressing it again cuts off its own previous sound, that is mono behavior, and it is exactly right for a fast hi-hat or a stuttering vocal. When you want a sound to ring out and overlap with itself (a piano chord, a long pad), you want it to *not* cut itself off. This setting prevents a lot of confusion later, so know it exists.

### Do
1. **(8 min) Pad tour.** Play through several banks of factory samples. For each pad you like, notice: is it a drum, a loop, a stab, a vocal? Press soft and hard and hear the velocity.
2. **(12 min) Set play modes.** Pick one drum-like pad and set it to **one-shot**. Pick one loop-like pad and set it to **loop**. Pick one melodic stab and set it to **gate**. Play each and feel the difference in how it responds to your finger.
3. **(10 min) Finger groove.** With one loop running (loop mode) under your finger, tap a one-shot drum pad along with it in time. Congratulations, that is a beat. Keep it going for a minute without losing the time.

### Daily drill (start today)
**Eyes-closed pad-find, 3 min:** pick four pads in a bank. With your eyes closed, find and play them in a pattern (1, 2, 3, 4, then 1, 3, 2, 4). The goal is to stop *looking* at the pads, the way a typist stops looking at keys. This reflex is the foundation of finger drumming.

### Self-check
1. What does "velocity-sensitive" mean, and why does it matter for drums?
2. Name the three play modes and the best use of each.
3. Which play mode would you choose for a kick drum? For a four-bar background loop? For a horn stab you want to play rhythmically?
4. What does the hold/remain function let you do?
5. When would you want a pad to cut off its own previous sound?

# Day 3: Your First Sample

### Objectives
- Connect a sound source and record a sample onto a pad.
- Set a healthy recording level and understand why level matters.
- Use auto-trigger to start recording on the sound.
- Know what skip-back sampling is.

### Setup (5 min)
Get a source ready: the simplest is your **phone or laptop** playing a song, connected to the SP's **LINE IN** with the right cable (a 3.5mm or the appropriate adapter). Or set up a **microphone** in MIC/GUITAR IN (a guitar or bass plugs straight in there too). Or connect a **turntable** through a phono preamp into LINE IN. Have something playing that you would enjoy chopping.

### Lecture

Recording a sample is the heart of the machine, and there is one principle that matters more than all the others: **record at a healthy level.** Too quiet and your sample is buried in noise and weak when you turn it up later; too loud and it **clips** (distorts harshly and unrecoverably). The target is **loud but never clipping**: the level meter should bounce up near the top on the loudest part and never slam into the ceiling. Spend the ten seconds to set this every single time; a badly recorded sample cannot be fixed afterward, only re-recorded.

**The basic record flow** ([Field Guide §4](FIELD-GUIDE.md)):

1. Press **REC** to arm recording. The screen shows you are about to sample.
2. **Choose the source** (line in, mic, or USB) and set the **input level** so the meter is healthy with the source playing. This is the step beginners skip and regret.
3. **Choose dry or with input effects.** For now, record **dry** (no effects); you can always add them later. Recording effects in is a commitment, and commitments come after you can hear what you are doing.
4. **Start recording**, either by pressing start manually or by letting **auto-trigger** begin it for you (the SP waits and starts the instant sound crosses a threshold, so you do not capture a second of silence at the front).
5. **Stop** when you have what you need. The sample lands on the destination pad.

**Auto-trigger** is worth using from day one: set a threshold, hit start, and the recording begins exactly when the music does, giving you a clean front edge with no dead air. It saves you trimming work later.

**Skip-back sampling** is the SP's secret weapon and feels like magic. The machine is *always* quietly buffering the last several seconds of audio it heard. So when something great goes by (a perfect snare, a phrase you were not ready for) you can grab the past with the skip-back function instead of cursing that you missed it. Know it exists now; it will save you constantly.

> **A grounding habit: name your samples or at least remember their banks.** A project fills up fast. Decide now that, say, bank A is loops, bank B is drums, bank C is one-shots. Future-you, hunting for that snare during a beat, will be grateful.

### Do
1. **(10 min) Set the level.** With your source playing the loudest part of the song, press REC, choose the input, and set the level so the meter is strong but never clipping. Do this a few times with different parts of the song until reading the meter is second nature.
2. **(10 min) Record a sample.** Using auto-trigger, record a few seconds of the song (a section you like) onto an empty pad. Play it back. Is it clean? Loud enough? Re-record until it is.
3. **(10 min) Record three more.** Sample three different moments from the same song or others: a drum part, a melodic part, a vocal. You are stocking a parts bin for the rest of the week.

### Daily drill
**Level-set sprint, 3 min:** arm REC, set a healthy level on whatever is playing, disarm, repeat. The goal is to make "set the level" a five-second reflex you never skip, no matter how excited you are to grab a sound.

### Self-check
1. What is the one principle that matters most when recording, and what are the two ways to get it wrong?
2. What happens to a sample that was recorded clipping? Can you fix it after?
3. What does auto-trigger do for you?
4. What is skip-back sampling, and why is it useful?
5. Why record dry rather than with effects when you are starting out?

# Day 4: Trimming the Sample

### Objectives
- Set tight start and end points so a sample begins and ends exactly where you want.
- Make a sample loop cleanly with no click or gap.
- Understand mono vs stereo samples.
- Remove clicks and pops at the edges.

### Listen (3 min)
Play one of yesterday's raw samples. Notice the junk: maybe a bit of silence at the front, a chunk of the next phrase at the end, an abrupt cut. Trimming is the unglamorous skill that separates a clean-sounding beat from a sloppy one. Today you clean up.

### Lecture

A raw recording almost always has too much on both ends. **Trimming** means setting the sample's **start point** (where playback begins) and **end point** (where it stops) so only the part you want plays. On the SP this is the **START/END** controls ([Field Guide §4](FIELD-GUIDE.md)): you move the start point in to cut dead air or a stray sound at the front, and the end point in to cut whatever follows the part you want.

**Trimming for a loop is its own skill.** If you want a sample to **loop** seamlessly (a drum groove that repeats forever with no audible seam), the start and end points have to land on the same spot in the rhythm, almost always **right on a beat**. A loop that is a hair too long or too short will "hiccup" every time it comes around, and that hiccup will haunt your whole beat. The fix is patient: nudge the end point until the loop comes around smoothly and the downbeat lands where the downbeat should. Use your ears, not your eyes; let it loop and adjust until the seam disappears.

**Clicks and pops at the edges** happen when a sample starts or ends in the middle of a sound wave, creating a tiny jump the speaker reproduces as a click. Two fixes: move the start/end point to a **zero crossing** (a quiet moment where the wave is at rest), or apply a tiny bit of **attack and release** in the pad's amp envelope (a few milliseconds of fade-in and fade-out) to smooth the edges. A small attack/release also smooths the joins between chops next week, so learn the move now.

**Mono vs stereo.** A stereo sample carries left and right channels (width); a mono sample is a single channel. Stereo sounds wider but uses more of the machine's resources and can cause phase weirdness when you chop and layer. Many beatmakers record drums in **mono** on purpose for punch and simplicity, and keep loops in stereo for width. You do not have to decide a philosophy today; just know the difference exists and that mono is often the punchier choice for drums.

### Do
1. **(12 min) Trim for tightness.** Take one of yesterday's samples and set the start point to cut any dead air at the front and the end point to cut anything after the part you want. Play it. It should begin and end exactly on the sound, nothing extra.
2. **(15 min) Make a clean loop.** Take a sample that has a clear rhythm (a drum break or a groove) and set it to **loop** mode. Now adjust the start and end points until it loops *seamlessly*, the downbeat coming around right on time with no hiccup. This is fiddly and worth every minute; let it loop and nudge.
3. **(8 min) Kill a click.** Find a sample that clicks at the start or end. Fix it: move the point to a quieter spot, or add a touch of attack/release in the amp envelope. Confirm the click is gone.

### Daily drill
**Loop-the-seam, 4 min:** take any rhythmic sample, set it looping, and adjust the end point in tiny steps until the seam vanishes. Do this with a new sample each day this week. Hearing "is the loop clean?" is a skill you build by reps.

### Self-check
1. What do the start and end points control?
2. Why is trimming a *loop* harder than trimming a one-shot, and what must the start and end points line up with?
3. What causes a click at the edge of a sample, and name two ways to fix it?
4. What is the difference between a mono and a stereo sample, and why might you choose mono for drums?
5. What does a small amount of attack and release in the amp envelope do?

# Day 5: Pitch & Speed

### Objectives
- Change a sample's pitch and speed with the pitch knob.
- Understand the difference between repitch and time-stretch.
- Find a sample's key by ear well enough to make two samples fit together.
- Know the sped-up sampling trick and its slowed-down inverse.

### Listen (3 min)
Listen to a "slowed and reverbed" or "chopped and screwed" track (search either term). Notice how pitching a sample *down* makes it heavier, darker, and slower all at once. That is the most basic and most powerful sample transformation, and it is one knob on your machine.

### Lecture

The **pitch/speed knob** changes how fast a sample plays back. Like speeding up or slowing down a record, faster playback raises the pitch and shortens the sample; slower playback lowers the pitch and lengthens it. This is **repitch** (pitch and speed tied together), and it is the classic sampler sound. Pitching a vocal down a few steps gives you that thick, syrupy texture; pitching a loop up makes it brighter and more frantic.

**Repitch vs time-stretch.** Sometimes you want to change the **speed without changing the pitch** (fit a 90 BPM loop into a 100 BPM beat but keep it in the same key), or change the **pitch without changing the speed**. That is **time-stretching**. The SP does it well: its default mode is the vinyl-style **repitch**, and it also has Roland's **VariPhrase** stretch modes (one tuned for sounds with sharp attacks like drums, one for sustained sounds like strings and pads) that change pitch and tempo independently. Stretching always introduces some artifacts (a slightly "grainy" or "watery" sound) the more you push it. The rule of thumb: **repitch is free-sounding but ties pitch and tempo together; time-stretch decouples them but can sound processed if pushed hard.** For a lot of lo-fi beatmaking, repitch is preferred precisely because the artifacts are part of the charm.

**Finding the key by ear (no theory needed).** To make two samples sound good together they usually need to be in compatible keys, and you do not need to read music to get there. The method is purely your ear:

1. Play sample A (say, a loop) so its main note rings.
2. Play sample B (say, a bass note or a stab) on top.
3. If it sounds *wrong* (clashing, sour), nudge sample B's pitch up or down a little and listen again.
4. Keep nudging until it "locks" and sounds *right* (consonant, resolved). That is the key matching.

You are not naming notes; you are training the same instinct that tells you a singer is flat. It develops fast with reps, and it is worth more than any chart.

**The sped-up sampling trick** is a classic that costs nothing: play the source *faster* than normal while you sample it (a 33 rpm record spun at 45, or a file played fast), then pitch the sample back down to the original speed. The pitch-down thickens and darkens the sound, and back when samplers measured memory in seconds it also squeezed more music into the machine; a whole era of sped-up soul chops was built on this move. The inverse is just as famous: sample at normal speed and pitch down without correcting, which is the foundation of slowed and vaporwave styles. Experiment in both directions.

### Do
1. **(8 min) Pitch play.** Take a vocal or melodic sample and sweep the pitch knob from low to high. Find a setting you love. Notice how far down you can go before it gets muddy, and how far up before it gets thin.
2. **(12 min) Key matching by ear.** Load a loop on one pad and a single bass note or stab on another. Play them together, then pitch the bass note until it *locks* with the loop. Trust your ear: wrong is obvious once you listen for it. Do this with two more pairs.
3. **(10 min) Repitch vs stretch.** Take a loop that is the "wrong tempo" for a beat in your head. First repitch it to roughly the right speed (and hear the pitch move with it). Then switch to a VariPhrase stretch mode and fit it to the right speed *without* moving the pitch. Compare the two sounds and notice the stretch artifacts.

### Daily drill
**Lock-it-in, 3 min:** play any two pitched samples together and adjust one until it sounds right against the other. The faster you can hear "that is wrong, nudge it" the better. This single skill carries your whole musical ear.

### Self-check
1. What does the pitch/speed knob do, and what happens to length when you pitch down?
2. What is the difference between repitch and time-stretch, and what is the trade-off?
3. Describe, in steps, how to match two samples' keys by ear without knowing any note names.
4. What is the sped-up sampling trick, and what does pitching the sample back down do to the sound?
5. Why do many lo-fi producers prefer repitch even though it ties pitch and tempo together?

# Day 6: Your First Usable Loop (+ Quiz 1)

### Objectives
- Combine the week's skills into one clean, trimmed, in-tune, looping sample you would actually use.
- Take Quiz 1.

### Listen (5 min)
Listen to the intro of a beat you like, the first four to eight seconds before the drums come in, often just a looped sample. Notice it is clean, in time, and sits at a pitch that feels good. That is exactly what you are going to make today: not a beat yet, just one *usable* loop, the seed everything else grows from.

### Lecture

Today nothing is new; you assemble Week 1 into a single deliverable. A **usable loop** is a sample that is:

- **Recorded cleanly** at a healthy level (Day 3), so it is strong and noise-free.
- **Trimmed tight** with a seamless loop point (Day 4), so it repeats with no hiccup and lands on the beat.
- **Pitched to a comfortable key and speed** (Day 5), so it feels good and could fit other sounds later.
- **Set to loop mode** so it plays continuously under your finger.

This is the atom of sample-based music. A great many famous beats are, at their core, one well-chosen, well-prepared loop with drums on top. If you can reliably produce a clean usable loop, you have the hardest-won skill of the beginner stage. Everything in Weeks 2 and 3 (chopping, drums, effects, arrangement) is decoration and structure hung on loops like this one.

> **Why stop at a loop and not push for a whole beat today?** Because the loop is the foundation, and a wobbly foundation cannot be fixed by adding drums on top. Nail the loop. Tomorrow's project and all of Week 2 build the beat. A clean loop you are proud of is a real accomplishment and the right scope for the end of Week 1.

### Do
1. **(10 min) Hunt.** Sample three or four candidate sections from music you love, at a healthy level. Audition them. Pick the one with the most promise as a loop.
2. **(20 min) Prepare it.** Trim it tight, set a seamless loop point, kill any clicks, and pitch it to a key and speed that feels good. Take your time; this is the whole exam of the week, applied.
3. **(5 min) Live with it.** Let it loop for a minute. Does it still sound good, or does a flaw reveal itself on the tenth pass? Fix anything that nags.

### Quiz 1
Take **Quiz 1** now (see `QUIZZES.md`), closed-book. Grade against `ANSWER-KEYS.md`. It covers the machine, signal flow, playing pads, recording, trimming, and pitch: Week 1's whole technical core. If you score below 90%, re-study the weak spot on tomorrow's studio day.

### Self-check
1. List the four things that make a loop "usable."
2. Why must the loop point be seamless before you add anything on top?
3. Why is "one clean loop" a legitimate end goal for a week, rather than a whole beat?
4. Which Week 1 skill do you personally find hardest, and what will you drill on the studio day?
5. What is the very first thing to get right when you sample a candidate loop?

# Day 7: Studio Day + Project 1

Today is consolidation, not new material. "Studio day" means you stop taking in new ideas and spend the time making the Week 1 skills solid and finishing something. Three things:

### 1. Review the week
Reread the self-checks for Days 1–6 and answer every one from memory. Anything you fumble, reread that lecture's paragraph and do the example at the machine. The two skills everything later rests on are **recording at a healthy level (Day 3)** and **trimming a clean, seamless loop (Day 4)**, so spend the most time there. A beat is only as good as the samples under it.

### 2. The "five clean loops" drill (20 min)
Make five usable loops from five different sources, fast. Record clean, trim tight, loop seamless, pitch to taste. Do not get precious; the goal is *reps* at the full sample-prep pipeline until it is automatic. Speed and cleanliness here pay off every single day of the rest of the course.

### 3. Project 1: A Clean, In-Tune Loop
Do **Project 1** (see `PROJECTS.md`). It asks you to deliver one prepared loop and a short recording of it playing, the proof that Week 1's foundation is solid. It is due end of Day 7 (or carry into the Week-2 studio day if you genuinely need it, but do not let it slide; everything ahead stands on it).

### Looking ahead
Week 1 was the **foundation**: getting a clean, trimmed, in-tune sample onto a pad and playing it. Right now your machine can sample anything and loop it well. **Week 2 is where it becomes beatmaking**: chopping that loop into playable pieces, building a drum kit, programming the pattern sequencer, and learning to resample so you can keep stacking ideas. From here on, the sample-prep pipeline should fade into reflex so your attention is free for the music.
