# Week 2: Chopping & Drums

> **The thesis of this week:** a loop is a found object; a *beat* is something you build. The two skills that turn one into the other are **chopping** (cutting a sample into playable pieces you can rearrange) and **drums** (a kit and a groove of your own under the sample). Add the **pattern sequencer** to play it back hands-free and **resampling** to commit your moves, and you have the entire beatmaking loop. This is the most important week of the course. Week 1 got sound into the machine; this week you make music out of it. Expect to make a lot of bad beats. That is the job. Each one teaches your ears and your fingers, and good ones start arriving faster than you would believe.

Keep saving as a reflex, and keep listening with a sampler's ear all day. When you hear a song now, ask not just "would I sample this" but "where would I *chop* it."

# Day 8: Chopping a Sample

### Objectives
- Understand what chopping is and why it is the core sampling skill.
- Chop a sample by hand (lazy chop).
- Chop a sample automatically by transient and by even grid.
- Assign chops across the pads.

### Listen (3 min)
Listen to a classic chopped-soul beat (search "J Dilla chops" or "Kanye chipmunk soul"). Hear how a single phrase from an old record gets cut into pieces and reordered into a *new* melody that the original singer never sang. That re-composition is chopping, and it is the deepest well in sampling.

### Lecture

**Chopping** means slicing one long sample into several short pieces, each landing on its own pad, so you can **play them in any order**. A four-second vocal phrase becomes sixteen little pads you can re-sing into something new. This is what separates a sampler from a tape player: you do not just replay the source, you *recompose* it.

On the SP-404 MK2 you enter the chop function from sample edit ([Field Guide §4](FIELD-GUIDE.md)), and there are two ways to place the slice points (the "marks"):

**By hand (the lazy chop).** Play the sample and **tap a pad at each spot you want a new slice to begin**, in time with the music. The SP drops a mark wherever you tapped. This is the most musical way to chop because *you* decide where the pieces fall, by feel, on the beats and syllables that matter. It is called "lazy" chopping because it is loose and human, and that looseness is a feature: the chops fall where your ear wants them. Then you push to **assign** the marked pieces out across the pads.

**Automatically.** The SP can place marks for you two ways:
- **By transient:** it detects the attacks (the sharp onsets of drum hits or notes) and slices there. This is fast and great for drum breaks, where every hit gets its own pad.
- **By even grid:** it divides the sample into equal time slices (by number of pieces, or by musical division like 16ths). This is great for a steady loop with no clear transients, or when you want mechanical, even chops.

After auto-marking, the real power is **moving the marks**: nudge a slice point a little earlier or later until each chop starts cleanly on the sound, not in the middle of it. Auto gives you a starting grid; your ear makes it musical.

**A practical note on clicks.** Chopped pieces often click at the joins because each slice starts and ends mid-wave. The same fix as Day 4 applies: a touch of **attack and release** in the amp envelope smooths every chop at once. Set it once and your chops stop clicking.

### Do
1. **(12 min) Lazy chop.** Take a melodic or vocal loop. Play it and tap out slice points by hand on the beats and syllables. Assign the chops to pads. Play them back one by one; each should be a clean, playable piece.
2. **(10 min) Auto-chop a break.** Take a drum break (a few bars of just drums) and auto-chop by **transient**. You should get kicks, snares, and hats landing on their own pads. Nudge any mark that lands sloppily.
3. **(8 min) Grid-chop a loop.** Take a steady loop and auto-chop by **even grid** (say 16 pieces). Play across the pads in order and hear the original; then play them out of order and hear something new.

### Daily drill (start today)
**Chop-and-replay, 4 min:** chop any sample (hand or auto), then play the chops back in their *original* order in time, like replaying the source from the pieces. When you can do that smoothly, you truly own the chops. Do it with a new sample each day this week.

### Self-check
1. In one sentence, what is chopping and why is it more than replaying a sample?
2. Describe the lazy (by-hand) chop method. Why is its looseness a feature?
3. What are the two automatic chop methods, and what is each best for?
4. After auto-chopping, what is the single most important thing to do to the marks?
5. How do you stop chops from clicking at the joins?

# Day 9: Playing Chops Musically

### Objectives
- Re-arrange chops into a new phrase, not just replay the original.
- Use gate mode and choke so chops play tightly.
- Develop basic timing playing chops by finger.

### Listen (3 min)
Listen again to a chopped beat and focus only on the *reordering*: the producer plays the source phrase's pieces in a new sequence to make a new hook. Notice the rhythm of the chops, how some are short stabs and some ring out. That rhythmic re-performance is today's skill.

### Lecture

Yesterday you cut a sample into pads. Today you **play them as an instrument**, which is where chopping becomes composition. The move is simple to describe and takes a while to feel: instead of playing the chops in their original order, **play them in a new order, in time**, to make a phrase the original never contained. Pitch some chops, leave others, repeat one for emphasis. You are now writing melody out of someone else's recording.

Two settings make chops play tightly:

- **Gate mode** ([Field Guide §5](FIELD-GUIDE.md)): the chop sounds only while you hold the pad, so *how long you hold* shapes the rhythm. Short taps give stabs; longer holds let a chop ring. This is usually the right mode for performed chops.
- **Choke (mono re-trigger):** set the chop pads so that playing one **cuts off** the previous one. This makes a row of chops behave like a single instrument that can only play one note at a time, so your phrases come out clean instead of smearing into each other. For most melodic chopping, choke is what you want.

**Timing is the whole game.** A chop played a hair late or early sounds wrong even if it is the right note. Practice playing your chops **with a loop or a metronome running** so you are landing them *on the beat*, not just in sequence. Start slow. It is exactly like learning to type: accuracy first, speed arrives on its own. The pad-find drill from Week 1 pays off here, because you cannot watch your fingers and your timing at the same time.

> **Re-pitching chops as you play.** A powerful trick: take one chop and copy it across several pads, each at a different pitch, so you can "play" that one sound as a melody up and down the pads. A single sung "ah" becomes a playable instrument. This is the seed of the melody work in Week 3.

### Do
1. **(12 min) Reorder.** Take yesterday's chopped phrase and, with a slow loop or click running, play the chops in a *new* order to make a new phrase. Find one rearrangement you actually like and play it three times in time.
2. **(10 min) Tighten with choke.** Set your chop pads to choke so each cuts off the last. Replay your phrase and hear how much cleaner it is. Use gate mode and feel how hold-length changes the rhythm.
3. **(8 min) Pitch a chop into a melody.** Copy one chop to four pads, pitch each differently, and play a little melodic line across them in time.

### Daily drill
**New-phrase-a-day, 4 min:** from any set of chops, build one new in-time phrase you like and play it cleanly three times. The point is fluency at *recomposing*, the heart of the craft.

### Self-check
1. What turns chopping from "replaying a sample" into composition?
2. Why is gate mode usually right for performed chops?
3. What does choke (mono re-trigger) do, and why does it make melodic chopping cleaner?
4. Why must you practice chops with a loop or click running?
5. How do you turn one chop into a playable melodic instrument?

# Day 10: Drums 1, Building a Kit

### Objectives
- Know the anatomy of a basic drum kit and what each piece does.
- Load or sample one-shot drums onto pads in a usable layout.
- Layer two sounds into one fatter drum.
- Play a basic backbeat by finger.

### Listen (3 min)
Listen to any beat and ignore everything except the drums. Find the **kick** (the low thump, usually on beats 1 and 3), the **snare** (the crack, usually on 2 and 4, the "backbeat"), and the **hi-hats** (the fast tick keeping time). Almost every beat you have ever heard is built from those three roles. Today you build them.

### Lecture

A drum kit, in beatmaking, is a small set of one-shot samples laid out on pads so you can play a groove. The core roles:

- **Kick (bass drum):** the low, round thump that anchors the beat and lands on the strong beats. It is the floor everything stands on.
- **Snare (or clap):** the bright crack that lands on the **backbeat** (beats 2 and 4 in common time). The snare is what makes people nod; it is the answer to the kick's call.
- **Hi-hats:** the high, short ticks (closed hats for the tight "tss," open hats for a longer "tsss") that subdivide the beat and create momentum and swing.
- **Percussion and extras:** rimshots, claps, shakers, toms, and whatever you sample, used to taste.

**One-shots.** Drums should almost always be **one-shot** pads ([Field Guide §5](FIELD-GUIDE.md)): one press plays the whole hit, every time, instantly. Set your kick, snare, and hats to one-shot. Lay them out in a way your fingers can reach comfortably (a common choice: kick and snare on two big easy pads, hats nearby).

**Where drums come from.** Three sources: the **factory and downloadable kits** (fast and clean), **chopping a drum break** (Day 8, for character and that sampled feel), and **sampling individual hits** from records (the classic crate-digging approach). For now, get a kick, a snare, and a hat you like from any source onto pads.

**Layering** is the simplest way to make a drum hit better: stack two samples on the same beat so they sound as one fatter hit. A weak kick plus a deep sine thump becomes a strong kick; a thin snare plus a clap becomes a snare with body. On the SP you layer by triggering two pads together (in a pattern next lesson) or by resampling the two-hit stack into one pad (Day 13). Layering is how pros get drums that hit hard.

**The backbeat** is the most fundamental groove in popular music: **kick on 1 and 3, snare on 2 and 4, hats keeping time underneath.** Internalize it by playing it with your fingers before you ever sequence it. If you can tap kick-snare-kick-snare steadily, you can make a beat.

### Do
1. **(10 min) Build a kit.** Get a kick, a snare, and a closed hi-hat onto three pads, set to one-shot, in a comfortable layout. Add an open hat and a clap if you have them.
2. **(12 min) Play the backbeat.** With a click or simple loop at a slow tempo, finger-drum: kick on 1 and 3, snare on 2 and 4, hats on every beat (or every half-beat). Keep it steady for a full minute. Slow and steady beats fast and lurching.
3. **(8 min) Layer a drum.** Stack a second sample on your kick (a deeper thump) or your snare (a clap) by playing both together. Hear how it fattens up.

### Daily drill
**Backbeat hold, 4 min:** finger-drum a steady kick-snare backbeat with hats over a metronome. Every time you rush or drag, that is the rep; reset. Steady time is the single most valuable thing in a beat.

### Self-check
1. Name the three core drum roles and where each typically lands in the bar.
2. What play mode should drum hits use, and why?
3. What is the "backbeat"?
4. What is layering, and what does it do for a drum sound?
5. Name three sources you can get drum sounds from.

# Day 11: The Pattern Sequencer

### Objectives
- Open the pattern sequencer and understand patterns.
- Program a beat step-by-step with TR-REC.
- Record a beat in real time by finger.
- Use quantize, and know its trade-off.

### Listen (3 min)
Listen to a simple loop-based beat and notice it repeats *exactly* the same every bar, hands-free. A human is not re-playing it each time; a **sequencer** is playing it back. Today your hands stop having to play every hit live, and the machine starts holding the groove for you.

### Lecture

A **pattern** is a recorded loop of pad hits that the SP plays back for you, over and over, in time. You build a pattern once, and the machine performs it while your hands are free to tweak, play other pads, or just listen. Patterns are stored one per pad ([Field Guide §4](FIELD-GUIDE.md)), so a project holds many, and you can chain them into a song (Week 3). You open the sequencer with **PATTERN SELECT**, pick an empty pattern, and **REC** to start recording into it.

There are two ways to get hits into a pattern:

**TR-REC (step recording).** This is the classic drum-machine method, named for Roland's TR drum machines. The bar is divided into **steps** (usually 16 steps for one bar of sixteenth notes), and you **place a hit on each step** where you want that drum to sound, without playing in real time. Select your kick, light up steps 1 and 9 (beats 1 and 3); select your snare, light up steps 5 and 13 (beats 2 and 4); select your hat, light up every other step. Press play and there is your backbeat, perfectly in time. TR-REC is precise, forgiving, and the best way for a beginner to get a solid beat down, because timing is automatic.

**Real-time recording.** With the pattern looping, you **play the pads live** and the SP records what you play, when you play it. This is more expressive and more human, but your timing has to be good (or you lean on quantize, below). Real-time is how you capture feel; TR-REC is how you nail precision. Most producers use both, often real-time for the groove and TR-REC to fix or add specific hits.

**Quantize** snaps your played hits to the nearest step of a grid (16ths, 8ths, and so on), cleaning up timing automatically. It is a lifesaver for shaky real-time playing, but it has a cost: **quantize too hard and the beat becomes stiff and robotic**, because the tiny human timing variations that make a groove feel alive get erased. The SP offers grid options and shuffle; for now, use quantize to tidy up, but know that tomorrow's lesson is about deliberately *not* over-quantizing.

> **Start with TR-REC.** As a beginner, build your first beats with TR-REC so you get the satisfaction of a tight, working groove immediately and learn where hits belong in the bar. Add real-time playing once your finger timing is steadier.

### Do
1. **(15 min) TR-REC a backbeat.** Open a new pattern at a slow-to-medium tempo. Step-program kick on 1 and 3, snare on 2 and 4, closed hats on the eighths or sixteenths. Press play. You made a beat that plays itself. Sit with how good that feels.
2. **(10 min) Add to it.** Step in an open hat for lift, a couple of extra kick hits for bounce, a clap layered on the snare. Keep it simple and let it loop.
3. **(8 min) Real-time pass.** With the pattern looping, finger-play a small percussion part live, with quantize on. Then turn quantize off and play it again; hear the difference in feel.

### Daily drill
**One beat, one minute:** start a fresh pattern and TR-REC a basic working beat in under a few minutes. Speed at laying down a solid foundation beat means you spend your creative time on the interesting parts, not the plumbing.

### Self-check
1. What is a pattern, and what does the sequencer free your hands to do?
2. Describe TR-REC step recording. Why is it good for a beginner?
3. How does real-time recording differ, and what is its trade-off?
4. What does quantize do, and what is the danger of using too much of it?
5. For a one-bar backbeat in 16 steps, which steps get the kick, the snare, and the hats?

# Day 12: Groove

### Objectives
- Add swing/shuffle and understand what it does to feel.
- Use velocity and ghost notes to make a beat breathe.
- Diagnose why a stiff beat sounds dead and fix it.

### Listen (3 min)
Listen to a beat with obvious **swing** (a lot of hip-hop and almost all of J Dilla's work). Notice the hats and snares do not land on a rigid grid; they lean, slightly late or early, with a drunken, human bounce. That feel is not sloppiness, it is *groove*, and it is placed on purpose. Today you learn to place it.

### Lecture

A beat can be perfectly in time and still sound **dead**. The difference between a drum-machine pattern and a groove is the small, deliberate human imperfections layered on top. Three tools:

**Swing (shuffle).** Swing delays the off-beats (the "and" between beats) by a little, so pairs of notes go long-short instead of even, giving that rolling, bouncy feel. The SP has a **shuffle/swing** setting in the sequencer ([Field Guide §4](FIELD-GUIDE.md)); dial in a moderate amount and hear the beat start to bounce. Too much and it lurches; a little goes a long way. Swing lives mostly in the hats.

**Velocity and dynamics.** A real drummer does not hit every snare equally hard. Vary the **velocity** (Day 2) of your hits: accent the backbeat snares, let some hats be soft and some loud, make the kick on beat 1 a touch stronger. This is the single biggest upgrade you can make to a stiff beat. Flat velocity is the number-one reason a beat sounds programmed.

**Ghost notes.** These are very quiet extra hits (usually snares or hats) tucked between the main hits, felt more than heard. A soft snare ghost just before the backbeat, a couple of quiet hat taps, and suddenly the beat has a pulse and a forward lean. Ghost notes are the secret of grooves that feel alive.

**The Dilla feel.** Some of the most loved beats in history come from *deliberately* not quantizing: leaving the kick a hair late, the snare slightly off the grid, so the beat feels handmade and a little drunk. You do not have to chase that yet, but know that "perfectly on the grid" is a choice, not a requirement, and often not the best one. The groove lives in the gaps.

> **The diagnosis to remember:** if a beat sounds lifeless, the cause is almost always **flat velocity** and **too-hard quantize**. The fix is to vary your hit strengths and to loosen or turn off quantize on the parts that should breathe (usually hats and percussion). Try that before you add anything new.

### Do
1. **(10 min) Add swing.** Take yesterday's beat and add a moderate amount of shuffle. Sweep the amount and find where it bounces best. Notice the hats are where you feel it most.
2. **(12 min) Velocity pass.** Go through your beat and vary the velocity: accent the backbeat, soften some hats, make beat 1's kick stronger. Compare before and after. The difference should be obvious.
3. **(8 min) Ghost notes.** Add two or three very quiet ghost snares or hats between the main hits. Feel how the groove tightens and leans forward.

### Daily drill
**Dead-to-alive, 4 min:** take any stiff, flat beat and bring it to life using only swing, velocity, and ghost notes, no new sounds. Training yourself to hear "this is stiff" and fix it with feel is what makes your beats sound human.

### Self-check
1. What does swing/shuffle do to the timing, and where do you feel it most?
2. What is the single biggest fix for a beat that sounds programmed?
3. What are ghost notes and what do they add?
4. What are the two usual causes of a lifeless beat, and the fix for each?
5. Is "perfectly on the grid" always the goal? Explain.

# Day 13: Resampling (+ Quiz 2)

### Objectives
- Understand resampling and why it is central to the SP workflow.
- Resample a chop sequence or a layered drum into a single pad.
- Use resampling to commit effects and free up voices.
- Take Quiz 2.

### Listen (3 min)
Listen to a dense beat with many layers and ask: how does one machine with limited voices play all that at once? Often the answer is **resampling**, the producer bounced finished parts down to single pads to make room for more. Today you learn the move that lets a small machine make big music.

### Lecture

**Resampling means sampling the SP's own output back into a new sample** ([Field Guide §3](FIELD-GUIDE.md)). You set the record source to the machine's internal output instead of an external input, play something (a chop sequence, a layered drum, a loop running through effects), and record the result onto a fresh pad. The output of your machine becomes the input. It sounds almost too simple to be important, and it is one of the most important ideas in the whole course.

Why it is central:

- **It commits your work.** A clever sequence of chops, a perfectly layered kick-plus-thump, a loop drenched in Vinyl Sim, all of that is "live" and fragile until you resample it. Resample, and now it is **one solid sample on one pad**, exactly as you made it, that you can play, chop again, or build on. Production is a series of decisions made permanent; resampling is how you make them.
- **It frees up voices and pads.** The SP can only play so many sounds at once. If a fat drum took two pads and three layers, resample it to one pad and you have reclaimed your resources for the next idea. Big beats on small machines are built this way.
- **It is how you "print" an effect.** The effects bus changes the sound in real time but does not alter the underlying sample. Resample the effected output and the effect becomes baked in, so you can stack a *new* effect on top, or chop the gritty, vinyl-soaked result.
- **It adds character.** Each resample pass can add a touch of grit (especially at lower fidelity settings), which is part of how the lineage's lo-fi sound is built: sample, resample, resample again, until it has that worn, warm quality.

**The resample mindset.** Beginners cling to the source and tweak forever, afraid to commit. The SP rewards the opposite: get something sounding good, **resample it, and move on**, keeping the source on another pad or in another bank if you might want it. Deciding is the skill. The machine is built to reward decisions, and resampling is how you make them stick.

### Do
1. **(10 min) Resample a chop phrase.** Take a phrase you built from chops (Day 9), play it, and resample the performance into one pad. Now you can trigger the whole phrase with one finger. Chop *that* if you want, and recompose again.
2. **(10 min) Resample a layered drum.** Stack two samples into one fat kick or snare, resample them to a single pad, and you have a custom drum that takes one pad and one voice.
3. **(8 min) Commit a loop.** Take a loop, get it sounding how you want (pitch, trim), resample it to a clean pad, and save. Practice the habit of committing and moving on.

### Daily drill
**Commit-and-continue, 4 min:** take any in-progress sound, make one decision about it, resample it, and build the next thing on top. Training yourself to *decide and commit* instead of tweaking forever is the difference between finishing beats and drowning in options.

### Quiz 2
Take **Quiz 2** now (see `QUIZZES.md`), closed-book. Grade against `ANSWER-KEYS.md`. It covers chopping, playing chops, drums, the sequencer, groove, and resampling: Week 2's whole core. If you score below 90%, re-study the weak spot on tomorrow's studio day.

### Self-check
1. What is resampling, in one sentence?
2. Give two reasons resampling is central to the SP workflow.
3. How does resampling let you "print" an effect, and why is that useful?
4. What is the "resample mindset," and what beginner habit does it cure?
5. How does resampling help a small machine make a dense, layered beat?

# Day 14: Studio Day + Project 2

Today is consolidation, not new material. Three things:

### 1. Review the week
Reread the self-checks for Days 8–13 and answer every one from memory. Anything you fumble, reread that paragraph and do the example at the machine. The skills everything in Week 3 rests on are **chopping cleanly (Day 8)**, **a steady, grooving beat (Days 10–12)**, and **resampling to commit (Day 13)**. Spend the most time on whichever is shakiest.

### 2. The "loop to beat in fifteen minutes" drill (20 min)
Take one of your Week 1 loops and, in fifteen minutes, turn it into a beat: chop it (or loop it), build a drum kit under it, TR-REC a grooving pattern with swing and velocity, and resample the result. Do not aim for perfect; aim for *finished and grooving*. This is the whole Week 2 pipeline run end to end, and doing it fast and often is how it becomes second nature.

### 3. Project 2: A Chopped Loop + a Sequenced Beat
Do **Project 2** (see `PROJECTS.md`). It asks you to deliver a beat: a chopped sample, a drum groove sequenced with feel, and a short recording of it playing. This is the proof you can actually make a beat, the center of the whole course. It is due end of Day 14.

### Looking ahead
Week 2 made you a beatmaker: you can chop a sample, build drums, sequence a groove, and resample to keep building. **Week 3 is about turning a beat into a track**: the SP's signature effects and the lo-fi sound, bass and melody from samples, arranging patterns into a real song with structure, and a basic mix that translates to other speakers. The making is in your hands now; Week 3 is about making it *sound finished* and *say something*.
