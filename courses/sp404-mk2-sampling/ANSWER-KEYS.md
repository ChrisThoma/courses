# Answer Keys: MUS-45H

Grade honestly. For "name N things" questions, award partial credit per correct item. For the **at the machine** parts, grade against the stated standard; the bar is *clean, in time, and musical*, not clever or complicated. Bounce it to a file and listen on another speaker if you are unsure; the recording does not lie.

---

## Quiz 1: The Machine & The Sample

### Part A
1. A machine that **records short pieces of sound and plays them back from buttons (pads).**
2. **10 banks** (A–J), **16 pads** each.
3. The **microSD card** (in projects); the habit is **saving** (and backing up the card weekly).
4. **LINE IN** and **MIC IN** (USB audio also accepted).
5. The **effects bus** (the MFX effects).
6. **Sampling the SP's own output** back into a new sample/pad.
7. **Save the project.**
8. **Vinyl Sim** and **Madlib** (SP-303/404 lineage).

### Part B
1. Harder presses are **louder**; the pad responds to how hard you hit it.
2. **One-shot (trigger), gate, loop.**
3. **One-shot.**
4. **Loop.**
5. **Gate.**
6. Keep a sample **playing hands-free** after you let go, so your hands are free for other pads.
7. When you want it to **cut itself off** on re-trigger (a fast hi-hat, a stuttering vocal): mono behavior.
8. Flat, equal velocity sounds **dead/programmed**; varied velocity gives the beat life and feel.

### Part C
1. **Record at a healthy level: loud but never clipping.**
2. **Too quiet** (buried in noise, weak) or **clipping** (harsh, unrecoverable distortion).
3. **No.** It must be re-recorded.
4. **Starts the recording the instant sound crosses a threshold**, giving a clean front edge with no dead air.
5. The SP is **always buffering the last several seconds**, so you can grab a sound *after* it already played.
6. Effects are a **commitment**; record dry so you can hear what you are doing and add effects later non-destructively.
7. **Assign banks to categories** (e.g., loops in A, drums in B), or otherwise track where things are.
8. The **LINE IN.**

### Part D
1. **Where the sample begins (start) and ends (end)** on playback.
2. They must line up with the **rhythm / a beat** (the same point in the groove), so the loop comes around seamlessly.
3. The sample starts or ends **mid-waveform** (a jump the speaker clicks on). Fix: move to a **zero crossing**, or add a touch of **attack/release**.
4. **Repitch** ties pitch and speed together (faster = higher and shorter); **time-stretch** changes speed without changing pitch (or vice versa), at the cost of artifacts.
5. It gets **longer** (and lower).
6. Play them together and **nudge one's pitch until it locks / sounds right** (no theory, by ear).
7. For **punch and simplicity** (and to avoid phase issues when layering/chopping).
8. **Extra detail in the chops and a punchier, deeper result** when played back at full speed.

### Part E
1. **4 pts:** healthy level (no clip, no noise), tight trim, and a seam you cannot hear over several passes. Deduct for clipping, dead air, or a hiccup.
2. **4 pts:** the second sample sits **in tune** against the loop (consonant, not sour). Deduct for a clash left unfixed.

## Quiz 2: Chopping & Drums

### Part A
1. **Slicing one sample into pieces on separate pads so you can replay them in any order**: it lets you *recompose*, not just replay.
2. **Play the sample and tap a pad at each point you want a new slice**, in time, then assign the pieces to pads.
3. The chops fall **where your ear/hand wants them** (on the beats and syllables that matter), giving a human, musical result.
4. **By transient** (at the attacks) and **by even grid** (equal time/musical divisions).
5. **By transient**, because each drum hit has a sharp attack and lands on its own pad.
6. **By even grid.**
7. **Move/nudge the marks** so each slice starts cleanly on the sound, not mid-note.
8. A touch of **attack and release** in the amp envelope (smooths every join at once).
9. It makes each chop **cut off the previous one**, so phrases come out clean instead of smearing together.
10. Because **how long you hold** the pad shapes the rhythm (short = stab, long = ring); length becomes performance.

### Part B
1. **Kick, snare (or clap), hi-hats.**
2. Kick on the **strong beats (1 and 3)**; snare on the **backbeat (2 and 4)**.
3. **Snare on beats 2 and 4** (the off-beats that make you nod), against kick on 1 and 3.
4. **One-shot (trigger).**
5. **Stacking two samples on the same hit** so they sound as one fatter drum.
6. Any two: **factory/downloaded kits, chopping a drum break, sampling individual hits** from records.
7. Because real drummers vary their hits; **flat velocity** has no dynamics, so it sounds mechanical/lifeless.
8. **Kick on 1 and 3, snare on 2 and 4, hats keeping time underneath.**

### Part C
1. A **recorded loop of pad hits the SP plays back for you**; it frees your hands to tweak, play other pads, or listen.
2. It is **step recording** (drum-machine style): the bar is split into steps and you **place a hit on each step** where a drum should sound, not in real time.
3. Timing is **automatic/precise**, so a beginner gets a tight working beat and learns where hits belong.
4. You **play the pads live** and the SP records what and when you play; more expressive but needs good timing (or quantize).
5. **Snaps played hits to the nearest grid step**, cleaning up timing.
6. The beat becomes **stiff/robotic** because the human timing variations that make a groove feel alive get erased.
7. Kick on **steps 1 and 9**; snare on **steps 5 and 13** (hats on the eighths/sixteenths).
8. **One per pad**; you can **chain them into a song** (an arrangement).

### Part D
1. **Delays the off-beats** so notes go long-short (a bounce); felt most in the **hats**.
2. **Varying the velocity / dynamics** of the hits.
3. **Very quiet extra hits** (snares/hats) tucked between the main hits, felt more than heard, that add pulse and lean.
4. **Flat velocity** and **too-hard quantize.**
5. **Sampling the SP's own output to a new pad**; central because it **commits work / frees voices / prints effects / adds character** (any one).
6. **Resample the effected bus output**, baking the effect into the new sample.
7. **Clinging to the source and tweaking forever** (decision paralysis); commit and move on.
8. By **bouncing finished parts to single pads**, freeing voices/pads to add more layers.

### Part E
1. **4 pts:** chops are clean (no clicks), and the new order is **in time** and musical. Deduct for sloppy edges or wandering time.
2. **4 pts:** the beat **grooves** (audible swing and dynamics), steady time, makes you nod. Deduct for a flat grid.

## Final Exam

### Part A
1. **Records sound and plays it back from pads.** 2. **10.** 3. **One-shot, gate, loop.** 4. **Healthy level, no clipping.** 5. **Sampling the SP's own output.** 6. **Vinyl Sim.** 7. **2 and 4.** 8. **TR-REC.** 9. **The highs (high frequencies).** 10. **Saving.**

### Part B
1. **(2)** Press REC, choose the input and **set a healthy level (loud, no clip)**, then start manually or by **auto-trigger**; sample lands on a pad.
2. **(2)** Start and end on the **same rhythmic point (a beat)**; fix a hiccup by **nudging the end point** until the seam vanishes by ear.
3. **(2)** Repitch ties pitch and speed (free, natural, but coupled); time-stretch **decouples** them but adds **artifacts** when pushed.
4. **(2)** **Tap a pad at each slice point as the sample plays**; the looseness puts chops where your ear wants them (human, musical).
5. **(2)** **Transient** (best for drum breaks) and **even grid** (best for steady loops / mechanical chops).
6. **(2)** Play them together and **nudge one's pitch until it locks/sounds right.**

### Part C
1. **Kick (1 and 3), snare (2 and 4), hats (keeping time).**
2. **One-shot.**
3. **Stacking two samples on one hit** to make a fatter drum.
4. **Kick on 1 and 3, snare on 2 and 4, hats underneath.**
5. Place hits on the **steps** of a divided bar, not in real time.
6. The beat becomes **stiff/robotic** (loses human feel).
7. Delays off-beats for a **bounce**; felt most in the **hats**.
8. **Vary the velocity / dynamics.**
9. **Quiet extra hits** between the main ones that add pulse/lean.
10. Timing is **automatic**, so they get a tight beat and learn hit placement.

### Part D
1. **Sampling the SP's own output back to a new pad.**
2. Any two: **commits/prints work, frees voices and pads, bakes in effects, adds lo-fi character.**
3. **Resample the effected output** so the effect is baked into the new sample.
4. Adds **vinyl crackle/noise and rolls off the highs (and lows)** for a warm, old-record lo-fi sound.
5. Low-pass removes **highs** (darker/muffled); high-pass removes **lows** (thinner/distant).
6. Delay = **echoes/repeats**; reverb = a **room/space tail** around the sound.
7. **Washes everything out / muddies** the mix.
8. Makes the bass/music **duck when the kick hits**, so the kick punches through and the track pumps; cleans up the low end.
9. Effects applied **as you record** (baked into the sample); the trade-off is they are **committed** (cannot be removed later).
10. **Using too many at once until it is mud**; the cure is **restraint**: add one, ask if it helps, remove if not.

### Part E
1. **Copy one note across pads and tune each to a different pitch** (or use chromatic/keyboard mode).
2. With the loop playing, **find the note that locks by ear**, add a few more, keep what sounds right.
3. **Overcrowding the low end**; the fix is **fewer notes, one bass locked with the kick.**
4. **Intro (sparse), main section, variation, break.**
5. **Dropping elements out builds tension**, so their return hits hard (tension and release); excitement from subtraction, not just addition.
6. **Balancing levels so every element is clearly audible** and the whole thing sounds good anywhere.
7. **High-passing makes room** (stops two parts fighting) rather than just making everything louder and muddier.
8. Many speakers **sum to mono**, and different speakers reveal different problems; you want it to **translate everywhere.**
9. Deliberately lo-fi is a **chosen** warm/narrow/gritty aesthetic; accidentally muddy is an **unfixed** problem you did not intend.
10. A **finished** thing teaches the whole process (and finishing is the rare skill); a long unfinished one teaches only fragments.

### Part F
1. **(3)** A complete beat plays start to finish with a chopped sample, grooving drums, and a low/melodic element; steady and musical. Deduct for stalling, flat time, or unfinished feel.
2. **(2)** One or two effects give a **deliberate, clean** sound and it is **resampled/printed**. Deduct for mud or no intent.
3. **(3)** A real arrangement move (a break or build) is demonstrated and the **mix still reads in mono** (nothing vanishes).
