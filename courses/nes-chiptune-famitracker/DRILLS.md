# Practice Menu (Drills): MUS-8B

The lessons prescribe a default daily drill, but no single drill fits everyone every day. This is the full menu: **pick one drill per active skill and rotate every few days** so nothing goes stale. Two things need daily reps for as long as you write chip music: **listening (F)** and **finishing something (G)**.

**Building your daily ~30 minutes:** 5 min listening (F) · 5 min tracker speed (B) · 10 min the week's focus skill (A/C/D/E) · 10 min working on your actual track (G). Always with the tracker open. Always saving a new version at the end.

> **The one rule above all drills:** *a technique you have not typed into a pattern is trivia.* Reading about arpeggio teaches you nothing. Typing `047` and hearing a chord appear teaches you permanently.

---

## A. The chip: knowing your instrument (start Day 1)

| Drill | How | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **A1: Voice ID** | Play any NES track; name out loud which channel is producing each sound you hear | the foundational ear skill |
| **A2: Duty roulette** | Loop a 2-bar phrase, change duty every pass with `Vxx`, and name the role each duty suits | knowing your three timbres cold |
| **A3: Range hunt** | Enter the same note in every octave on a channel; find where it stops being usable | staying inside the hardware |
| **A4: Noise tour** | Play all 16 noise pitches in order; pick your hat, snare, and crash | drum sound selection by ear |
| **A5: Limit recall** | Without notes, write down all five channels and every limit each has | the Domain 1 test, repeated |

## B. Tracker speed (start Day 2)

| Drill | How | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **B1: Blind entry** | Enter a melody you know by ear, no reference, as fast as you can type | keyboard fluency |
| **B2: Block gymnastics** | Copy, paste, transpose by semitone and octave, and reverse a selection, without the mouse | the 80% of tracker speed |
| **B3: Frame sprint** | Build an eight-frame arrangement from three patterns in under five minutes | order-list thinking |
| **B4: Effect recall** | Name what `3xx`, `Qxy`, `Gxx`, `Sxx`, `Vxx`, `Zxx`, and `Bxx` do, from memory | the effect vocabulary |

## C. Instrument design (start Day 4)

| Drill | How | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **C1: One-note test** | Play a single held note with a new instrument for ten seconds; if it bores you, fix the envelope | the honest instrument test |
| **C2: Six in fifteen** | Build a pluck, pad, bass, kick, hat, and snare from scratch in fifteen minutes | speed, so sessions go to music not setup |
| **C3: Blip variations** | Take one lead and try five different attack arpeggios (`12 7 0`, `24 0`, `-12 0`, `5 0`, `0`) | the highest-value instrument trick |
| **C4: Envelope steal** | Pick a sound from an NES track and rebuild its envelope by ear | sound design by imitation |
| **C5: Duty sequence lab** | Build three pseudo-PWM duty sequences at different speeds | the chip's only "filter" |

## D. Arrangement and the voice budget (start Day 8)

| Drill | How | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **D1: Job map** | For any 8 bars you have written, write down each channel's job per bar; find the idle channel | seeing wasted voices |
| **D2: Mush test** | Play 8 bars on the worst speaker you own; can you find the melody in 2 seconds? | foreground/background |
| **D3: Interleave** | Rewrite two pulse lines so they never move at the same time | the anti-mush rule |
| **D4: Cut one thing** | Delete an entire channel from a section and decide whether the track got better | learning that less is usually more |
| **D5: Time-share** | Give one channel three different jobs across four bars | using channels fully |

## E. Harmony, groove, and expression (start Day 9)

| Drill | How | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **E1: Chord vocabulary** | From memory, produce major, minor, minor 7th, sus4, and augmented with `0xy` | arpeggio fluency |
| **E2: Arpeggio speed lab** | Run one arpeggio at speeds 3, 6, 10, and 16; listen for how many arpeggio steps fit inside one-row notes | arpeggio against the tracker grid |
| **E3: One-bar groove** | Take a flat drum bar and make it groove in three minutes, volume column and `Gxx` only | the highest-return production skill |
| **E4: Volume-only groove** | Give a flat 8th-note hat line a groove without adding a single note | dynamics as rhythm |
| **E5: One-note expression** | Make a single held note interesting five different ways with effects | phrasing |
| **E6: Fill-a-bar** | Turn one drum bar into a fill three ways: snare roll, tom run, dropout | transitions |
| **E7: Phrase punctuation** | End three phrases three different ways: a bend up, a fall, a hard cut | musical punctuation |

## F. Listening and analysis (start Day 1; every day, no exceptions)

| Drill | How | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **F1: Channel solo** | Follow exactly one channel through a whole track; narrate its job bar by bar | the core analytical skill |
| **F2: Arpeggio hunt** | Find every arpeggio in a track and name the chord | hearing harmony on a mono voice |
| **F3: Drum transcription** | Transcribe one bar of a track's drums into your tracker by ear | rhythm ears, and it gets fast |
| **F4: Seam listening** | Listen only to the four rows either side of every section change | transitions |
| **F5: Full teardown** | The seven-step Day 19 procedure on a track you love; one page of notes | the lifelong method |
| **F6: Chip ID** | Guess whether a Famicom track uses expansion audio, and which chip | timbre recognition |
| **F7: Steal two** | From any teardown, name two techniques you do not use and implement both | turning listening into writing |

## G. Finishing (start Day 7; this is the one people skip)

| Drill | How | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **G1: Three problems** | Listen to an old track cold; write the first three things that bother you | the production habit |
| **G2: Ten-loop test** | Set a track looping, leave for ten minutes, come back, try to hear the seam | invisible loops |
| **G3: Quiet test** | Turn the track down until barely audible; list what you can still hear, in order | mix hierarchy |
| **G4: Phone test** | Export to WAV, play on phone speakers, check the track still works with no bass | writing that survives bad speakers |
| **G5: Checklist run** | Run the Day 20 finish checklist end to end | catching the boring failures |
| **G6: Export and verify** | Export an NSF, load it in a different player, listen all the way through the loop | shipping |
| **G7: One a month** | A complete track with intro, two sections, and a loop, exported, every month | the only drill that actually makes you good |

## Rules that make any drill work

1. **Type it, do not read it.** Every idea in this course has a hands-on version. Do that one.
2. **Save versions.** Increment the filename every session. Yesterday's version is often better than today's and you will want it back.
3. **Listen every single day.** Ten focused minutes on one channel of one track beats an hour of passive background listening.
4. **Turn the tempo down to find mistakes.** Anything that is wrong is obvious at half speed and invisible at full.
5. **Finish things.** A folder of eight-bar loops teaches you almost nothing. The last 10% of a track is where the actual skill lives, and it is the part beginners never practice.
6. **Constraint first.** When a drill tempts you to add an expansion chip or bounce stems into a DAW, do the drill inside the five voices instead. That is the whole point.
7. **Listen cold.** Never judge a mix the same day you made it.
8. **When a drill gets easy, retire it** and pull the next one down the list. Easy drills are entertainment.
