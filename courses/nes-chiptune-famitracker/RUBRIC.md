# Mastery Rubric: MUS-8B

Grade yourself in each domain. **Passing this course = Level 3 in all ten domains, with Domains 1, 2, and 3 at Level 4.** Level 4 everywhere is the long-term target, not the three-week one; the note under the score sheet explains which domains realistically get there later and why. The honest test for most domains is the same: *export to WAV, wait a day, and listen cold on speakers you did not write on.* Levels:

- **1 (Novice):** Aware of the concept; can't do it without looking it up and getting lucky.
- **2 (Developing):** Can do it slowly, with reference material open, and the result is usually rough.
- **3 (Proficient):** Can do it reliably and quickly; the result holds up; rare mistakes.
- **4 (Solid):** Automatic. You do it while thinking about the music rather than about the technique.

---

## Domain 1: The chip

| Level | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 1 | Knows the NES has "a sound chip" |
| 2 | Can name the five channels |
| 3 | Knows each channel's capabilities and limits (duty settings, no triangle volume, 16 noise pitches, DPCM size) and writes within them without checking |
| 4 | Reasons *from* the hardware: predicts why a part will fail before writing it, and uses quirks (short-mode noise, ultrasonic triangle, the mixer groups) deliberately |

**Test:** From memory, state what each of the five channels can and cannot do, including volume resolution, timbre options, and the triangle's missing volume control.

## Domain 2: Tracker fluency

| Level | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 1 | Can enter notes one at a time, hunting for keys |
| 2 | Enters notes and effects; uses patterns; copies and pastes |
| 3 | Fluent with block select, transpose, frames, and the order list; builds arrangements without copy-paste duplication |
| 4 | The tool is invisible; ideas go from head to pattern with no friction, and files stay organized enough to edit a month later |

**Test:** Build an eight-frame arrangement with reused drum and bass patterns, in under ten minutes, without touching the mouse more than a few times.

## Domain 3: Instrument design

| Level | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 1 | Uses the default instrument for everything |
| 2 | Can write a volume sequence with a loop point |
| 3 | Builds plucks, pads, basses, and drums from volume, arpeggio, pitch, and duty sequences with correct loop and release points |
| 4 | Designs an instrument to fit a specific role in a specific arrangement; a single held note is already musical |

**Test:** In fifteen minutes, build from scratch a plucked lead with an attack blip, a soft pad, a triangle kick, a hi-hat, and a snare, all usable.

## Domain 4: The voice budget (arrangement)

| Level | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 1 | Runs out of channels and does not know what to cut |
| 2 | Fits melody, bass, and drums, but the pulses fight each other |
| 3 | Assigns every channel a clear job per section, time-shares channels, and keeps a legible foreground and background |
| 4 | Writes music that is *conceived* for five voices: the arrangement never sounds constrained, and channels change jobs fluidly |

**Test:** Play an eight-bar section for someone and have them identify the melody within two seconds on phone speakers.

## Domain 5: Harmony without chords (arpeggio)

| Level | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 1 | Does not know how NES chords work |
| 2 | Can type `047` and get a major chord |
| 3 | Uses both `0xy` and arpeggio sequences, picks major/minor/7th correctly, and understands how row speed affects how much of an arpeggio fits inside each note |
| 4 | Uses arpeggio as one texture among several: drops it out, varies it, and knows when a track is better without it |

**Test:** Write a four-bar chord progression on one pulse channel, with the arpeggio dropping out for one bar for effect.

## Domain 6: Expression and effects

| Level | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 1 | Notes are static; no effects used |
| 2 | Uses vibrato and portamento, sometimes in the wrong places |
| 3 | Uses vibrato, `3xx`, `Qxy`/`Rxy`, `Gxx`, `Sxx`, and duty changes musically, and knows which channel accepts which |
| 4 | Phrases sound played rather than entered; expression is built into instruments where it belongs, and effect state is never left running by accident |

**Test:** Take one held note and make it interesting five different ways using only effects and instrument sequences.

## Domain 7: Rhythm and groove

| Level | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 1 | Flat velocities, everything exactly on the grid |
| 2 | Drums are correct but stiff |
| 3 | Uses hat volume variation, ghost notes, fills, and swing; the beat feels played |
| 4 | Groove is a deliberate compositional choice; swing ratio, `Gxx` nudges, and rows-per-beat are all chosen for the piece |

**Test:** Take a flat one-bar drum pattern and make it groove in three minutes using only the volume column and `Gxx`.

## Domain 8: Song form and finish

| Level | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 1 | Writes eight-bar loops and stops |
| 2 | Has two sections but they are glued together with no transitions |
| 3 | Complete form: intro, contrasting sections, transitions at every seam, a loop point that returns correctly |
| 4 | The loop is inaudible, the form has a shape, and the track survives being heard twenty times |

**Test:** The ten-loop test. Set the track looping, leave for ten minutes, come back, and try to identify the seam without watching the screen.

## Domain 9: Mixing on the chip

| Level | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 1 | Everything at volume `F` |
| 2 | Some volume differentiation, but the mix is muddy or fatiguing |
| 3 | A clear volume hierarchy; separation achieved by register and duty; the mix holds up on bad speakers |
| 4 | Balance is part of the composition; uses `Zxx`, register, duty, and rhythmic interleaving as mixing tools; deliberately leaves space |

**Test:** The quiet test. Turn your track down until it is barely audible and confirm the melody is the last thing you can still hear.

## Domain 10: Listening and analysis

| Level | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 1 | Hears NES music as one undifferentiated sound |
| 2 | Can pick out the melody and the drums |
| 3 | Follows any individual channel through a track and names the techniques in it |
| 4 | Tears a track down systematically and transplants specific techniques into their own writing |

**Test:** Take a track you have not studied, produce a one-page teardown of its form and all five channels, and name two techniques you will steal.

Expected trajectory: end of Week 1 → Domains 1–3 at Level 3. End of Week 2 → Domains 4–6 at Level 3, Domains 1–3 climbing to 4. End of Week 3 → all domains at 3 or better, with Domains 1, 2, and 3 at 4.

Be realistic about the last three. **Domain 4 (the voice budget) and Domain 9 (mixing) reach a stable Level 4 only after you have finished a dozen tracks**, because both are judgment rather than knowledge, and judgment is built out of things that went wrong. **Domain 10 (listening) improves for as long as you keep doing teardowns**, which is to say forever. What this course locks in is the technical fluency and the working method, so those judgment domains have something to grow on.
