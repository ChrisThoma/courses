# Projects: MUS-8B

The four projects are one continuous piece of work. The 8-bar loop from Week 1 becomes the 32-bar tune in Week 2, which becomes the polished level theme in Week 3, which becomes the capstone. Nothing gets thrown away, and by Day 21 you have a finished track that took three weeks rather than one evening. That is deliberate: the reason most beginner chip music never gets good is that nobody spends three weeks on one track.

**Grade by exporting to WAV, waiting overnight, and listening cold on speakers you did not write on.** Music you just finished always sounds better than it is. "It's basically done" is a 70, not a 95.

> **Keep every export.** Project 1's WAV against the capstone's WAV is the single most encouraging thing you will hear all course.

---

## Project 1: The Four-Voice Loop *(Day 7 · 10% of grade)*

**Goal:** prove the Week 1 foundation. All four main channels, your own instruments, a loop that repeats without a bump. This is the Domain 1–3 test.

### Deliverable
One `.ftm` file plus a WAV export of an **8-bar loop**, played at least twice through, containing:
1. **Pulse 1:** a melody with a deliberate phrase shape, in octaves 4–5, using your own plucked-lead instrument (with an attack blip).
2. **Pulse 2:** something. Harmony, a counter-line, or a simple second voice. It does not have to be sophisticated; it has to be intentional and quieter than the lead.
3. **Triangle:** a bassline that moves at least every half bar and uses note length as its dynamics.
4. **Noise:** a drum pattern with a hat, a snare, and at least one varied bar.
5. **A kick**, on the triangle or the noise, and a note in your file saying which and why.
6. **At least four instruments you built yourself.** No default instruments.

### Rubric (10 pts)
| Pts | Criterion |
|---|---|
| 2 | Instruments are hand-built and musical; a single held note is not boring |
| 2 | The melody has a shape (question/answer, phrase endings, deliberate rests) |
| 2 | The bassline moves and uses note length for dynamics |
| 2 | The drum pattern has a hat/snare groove and at least one varied bar |
| 1 | Volume differentiation exists: the lead is clearly the foreground |
| 1 | The loop repeats without an audible bump |

**Pass bar:** somebody who has never seen a tracker hears it and says "that sounds like a video game," not "that sounds like a beginner's video game."

## Project 2: The Two-Part Tune *(Day 14 · 15% of grade)*

**Goal:** a real piece of music with two contrasting sections and harmony that is not there. This is the Domain 4–6 test.

### Deliverable
A **32-bar minimum** piece built from your Project 1 material, exported to WAV, containing:
1. **An A section and a B section**, with the B genuinely contrasting: at least **two** of key, register, density, or rhythm must change. A new melody over identical everything does not count.
2. **Arpeggiated harmony** on one channel for at least part of the piece, using an arpeggio sequence or `0xy`, that **drops out** for at least one section or phrase.
3. **A counter-melody or echo** on Pulse 2 somewhere, and evidence that Pulse 2 changes jobs at least once between sections.
4. **A drum pattern with fills** in bars 4 and 8 (or their equivalents).
5. **A working loop point** set with `Bxx`.
6. **An order list that reuses patterns.** If you copy-pasted your drums into every frame, you lose points here, and more importantly you will hate editing it next week.
7. **A one-paragraph written note:** what job each channel does in each section.

### Rubric (15 pts)
| Pts | Criterion |
|---|---|
| 3 | The B section genuinely contrasts on at least two dimensions |
| 3 | Arpeggiated harmony is present, correct, and *breathes* (drops out somewhere) |
| 2 | Pulse 2 does more than one job across the piece |
| 2 | Drums have fills and variation; the groove is not flat |
| 2 | The loop works and returns to the right place |
| 2 | The order list reuses patterns rather than duplicating them |
| 1 | The written channel-job note is accurate and specific |

**Pass bar:** you can listen to it four times in a row without wanting it to stop.

## Project 3: The Polished Level Theme *(Day 20 · 15% of grade)*

**Goal:** take Project 2 from "a piece of music" to "a finished production." Same material, dramatically better execution. This is the Domain 7–9 test.

### Deliverable
The Project 2 tune, revised and exported to **both NSF and WAV**, with:
1. **An intro** (2–8 bars) that plays once, with the loop returning after it.
2. **A transition at every single section boundary**, written in the last bar of the outgoing section.
3. **A groove pass:** deliberate volume on every note across every channel, plus either swing or `Gxx` nudges applied on purpose.
4. **A mix pass:** the volume hierarchy from Day 17 applied; nothing at `F` except the lead and snare; hats between `3` and `6`.
5. **At least one texture drop:** a moment where only two or three voices are playing.
6. **Two techniques transplanted from your Day 19 teardown**, named in a written note.
7. **A clean loop:** no persistent effects in the wrong state, nothing decaying across the seam, passes the ten-loop test.
8. **Filled-in title, author, and copyright metadata** in the NSF.

### Rubric (15 pts)
| Pts | Criterion |
|---|---|
| 3 | Every section boundary has a transition, and each one is in the outgoing bar |
| 3 | The mix has a real hierarchy; the melody is unmistakable on bad speakers |
| 3 | The groove is deliberate: volumes shaped, swing or nudges applied on purpose |
| 2 | The loop is clean and survives the ten-loop test |
| 2 | Intro present, plays once, loop returns correctly |
| 1 | At least one deliberate texture drop |
| 1 | Two transplanted techniques named, and audible in the track |

**Pass bar:** you would put it on the internet under your own name.

## Final Project: The Capstone Track *(Day 21 · 25% of grade)*

**Goal:** the graduation requirement. A complete NES chiptune, exported as a playable NSF, that you are genuinely willing to stand behind. This is the whole-course test.

### Specification
1. **At least 90 seconds** of music before the loop point (a typical NES theme is 45–90 seconds of unique material, so a loop that repeats to fill 90 seconds does not count; the *unique* material must reach 90 seconds).

   Do the arithmetic early, because this is the one requirement that ambushes people. At 150 BPM in 4/4, one bar is 1.6 seconds, so 90 seconds is about **56 bars**, and Project 2's 32-bar minimum is only about 51 seconds. You need roughly another 24 bars from somewhere: a C section, a longer B, a real intro and outro, or a second statement of A that is written out with variations rather than just repeated. Plan for that on Day 13 when you sketch your frames, and write the extra material during Days 15–19 as you go, not on Day 21. Day 21 is for finishing, not for composing a third of the track.
2. **Plain 2A03 only.** Five channels. No expansion audio, no stems bounced into a DAW, no post-processing. It must be a legitimate NSF that plays on hardware.
3. **Complete form:** an intro that plays once, at least two contrasting sections, transitions at every seam, and a loop point that returns to the top of the music rather than to the intro.
4. **All four synthesis channels used** (Pulse 1, Pulse 2, Triangle, Noise), with DPCM optional. If you do not use DPCM, say so in the reflection and explain what you did instead.
5. **A finished mix** that passes the phone test and the quiet test.
6. **An invisible loop** that passes the ten-loop test.
7. **Exported to NSF with metadata filled in**, and **verified** by loading it in a player other than the tracker.
8. **A WAV export** of two or three loops with a fade, for listening and sharing.
9. **A written reflection (½ page):** what the track is; which channel gave you the most trouble and why; the one arrangement decision you are proudest of; the one thing you would do differently; and what you will write next.

### Rubric (25 pts)
| Pts | Criterion |
|---|---|
| 5 | The arrangement uses five voices well: clear foreground and background, no mush, channels doing real work |
| 4 | The music is good: the melody is memorable and the form has a shape |
| 4 | The mix holds up on bad speakers and at low volume |
| 3 | Groove and expression: it sounds played, not entered |
| 3 | Transitions and an invisible loop |
| 3 | Instruments are hand-built and appropriate to their roles |
| 2 | The NSF exports, has metadata, and verifiably plays outside the tracker |
| 1 | The reflection is honest and specific |

**Graduation bar:** 23+/25, **and** you play it for someone who does not care about chiptune and they listen all the way through without asking you to skip ahead. If you hit that, you did not just complete a course; you wrote a chiptune. Post it somewhere. Then start the next one, and make that one a month-long habit.
