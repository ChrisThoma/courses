# Answer Keys: REC-24

Grade honestly. For "name N things" questions, award partial credit per correct item. For the **do** parts, grade against the stated standard; the bar is *clean, strong, and steady*, not "it sounded fine while I played." Record and listen back if unsure; the playback does not lie.

## Quiz 1: The Machine & Your First Track

### Part A
1. A **mixer (mixing console)** and a **four-track cassette recorder**.
2. **In (channel) → To tape (tracks) → Monitor (cue) → Out (mix)**.
3. Any of: track on **SAFE** but you expected playback; channel fader/master down; monitor/cue not routed; the track was never actually recorded (armed but not rolled); a bad cable. (Credit any reasonable point on the path.)
4. **Type II (high-bias / CrO₂)** cassette.
5. **Double speed (3¾ ips / 9.5 cm/s)**, twice normal cassette speed, for better fidelity (more tape per second).
6. Because a four-track uses the **entire tape width in one direction** for its four tracks; there is no "side B."
7. About **15 minutes**.
8. A four-track has **four tracks and no undo** (you must commit), versus unlimited tracks and infinite undo in a DAW.

### Part B
1. Park the **channel fader at its nominal mark** first (it's in the record path), then **trim, then EQ**; the fader becomes your mix-balance control at the end.
2. The meter shows the **actual signal strength**; the knob position tells you nothing about whether the level is healthy.
3. **Strong, near the top, never clipping** (peaks bounce high without slamming the ceiling).
4. Too quiet = **buried in hiss / weak**; too loud = **clipping / harsh distortion**.
5. **Boost the mid and sweep its center** until the bad frequency jumps out, then **cut** there.
6. The **1/4" jack**, with the **INPUT select on MIC/LINE**. There is no separate "LINE" position on that switch; the jack you use and the TRIM set the level range.
7. It **selects which buss** the channel feeds (left buss → tracks 1/3, right → 2/4).
8. Getting a **strong, clean level at every stage** of the path so you never fight noise or distortion.

### Part C
1. The **input loads down the high-impedance pickups**, losing treble (dull) and level (quiet): an impedance mismatch.
2. Line input is **5.6 kΩ**; a passive pickup wants to see a **very high impedance (around 1 MΩ)**.
3. A pedal is a **buffer**: high input impedance for the guitar, **low-impedance line-friendly output** to the 424.
4. **Active basses/guitars**, because they have a **built-in preamp (buffered, low-impedance output)**.
5. It **buffers** the high-impedance instrument to a clean, low-impedance output (the uncolored version of the pedal fix); most DIs put that out as a **balanced, mic-level XLR**, so it feeds a **MIC** input, not LINE.
6. Any buffered-bypass pedal, e.g., **a classic Boss pedal**, a compressor, a clean boost, a buffer.
7. The **trim/level**, set **by the meter**.
8. Pedals are both **tone and front-end buffer/DI** in one; the machine needs that buffered signal.

### Part D
1. **SAFE** = protected, cannot record; **DIRECT** = records from its same-numbered channel; **BUSS** = records from the stereo buss (L→1/3, R→2/4).
2. **Channel 2.**
3. Hold **RECORD** and press **PLAY**.
4. **Returns the tape to the counter zero point** (the top of the song).
5. Set it to **SAFE**, because there is **no undo on tape**; an armed track can be erased.
6. dbx **reduces tape hiss by companding** (compress on record, expand on playback).
7. It only **tracks correctly** if it compresses going in and expands coming back; on for only one side mis-tracks the level.
8. Saturation = a **strong level adds warmth/gentle compression** (pleasing); clipping = an **overloaded level distorts harshly** (bad, unrecoverable).

### Part E
1. **4 pts:** strong, clean level (no clip, not lost in hiss); no avoidable noise. Deduct for clipping or a buried, hissy take.
2. **4 pts:** part lands on track 3 via DIRECT; other tracks on SAFE; clean playback. Deduct for wrong routing or a noisy/clipped capture.

## Quiz 2: Multitrack & Time

### Part A
1. **No built-in metronome.** You **generate a click elsewhere (drum machine/phone/DAW) and record it to a track**, then play to it.
2. Because overdubbed parts recorded at different times must **agree on time**; there is no grid on tape to fix drift later.
3. **Track 4**, the natural utility/edge track.
4. **Yes** (for an ordinary audible click).
5. Only to record a **sync tone (FSK/SMPTE)** for tape sync, which companding would corrupt.
6. **Keep** = a drum machine you keep as the rhythm part; **scratch** = a bare metronome guide you mute/replace later.
7. The **foundation part (rhythm guitar or bass)**, because it sets the time and feel for everything else.
8. **Slow the tempo** until you can lock cleanly, record there, build the song at a tempo you own.

### Part B
1. Recording a **new part while listening to the parts already on tape**.
2. The **click, the parts already recorded, and the new part you are playing**.
3. If you cannot clearly hear the click/existing parts, your new part **drifts out of time/tune**.
4. **DIRECT** when one channel goes to one new track (most overdubs); **BUSS** to record a **blend** of channels (stereo/submix).
5. **Three.**
6. Whether the parts **lock to each other** (agree rhythmically), not just to the click.
7. Rush the **easy parts**, drag the **hard parts**.
8. So the player (you) can hear everything needed to **play in time and in tune**; fixing it mid-take ruins the pass.

### Part C
1. Replace a **short section of an already-recorded track** without redoing the whole take.
2. Runs the punch **without recording** so you can practice the entry and check timing safely.
3. In the **gaps**, a rest or a sustained note, so the seams are hidden.
4. So you are **already locked in time and tune** when the machine drops into record.
5. An **optional footswitch** to trigger punch in/out hands-free while playing.
6. **Play back across both seams**; you should not be able to hear where the repair starts/ends.
7. When the take needs **many punches**, the take is not good; re-record it.
8. The **in-point and out-point** counter numbers.

### Part D
1. **Combine several tracks onto one** to free up tracks; needed because you only have **four**.
2. The **balance, pan, and EQ** of the combined parts (you cannot un-blend them later).
3. A bounce is a **copy of a copy**, adding a little noise / losing a little clarity; keep it small with a **strong level and dbx on**.
4. Bounce the **parts you are surest about** (rhythm/background); keep the **important/uncertain part (lead/vocal)** live and record it after the bounce.
5. **Print** = record the effect onto the track (permanent); print the **tone-defining** effects (fuzz, defining modulation).
6. Send a channel out **EFFECT 1** to the effect, and return its output through the **STEREO IN (7–8) or SUB IN**.
7. A **clean DI** (instrument → buffer/pedal → 424): full, reliable, easy to mix; tape adds warmth.
8. It forces **decisive sounds** and the records sound finished; you spend effort getting tone right at the source.

### Part E
1. **4 pts:** the part locks to the click start to finish (check on playback). Deduct for drift/rushing.
2. **4 pts:** the overdub sits with the first part (good cue mix) and the bounce has a committed, sensible balance. Deduct for a drifting overdub or a bad/unusable bounce balance.

## Final Exam

### Part A
1. **In → to tape → monitor → out.** 2. **Type II.** 3. **SAFE / DIRECT / BUSS.** 4. **Channel 4.** 5. **Returns tape to counter zero.** 6. **On.** 7. **The impedance mismatch (buffers the signal: full tone, strong level).** 8. **LINE OUT (RCA L/R).** 9. **The four TAPE OUT jacks.** 10. **Trim (the input level).**

### Part B
1. **(2)** guitar → pedal/DI (buffer) → channel **INPUT/TRIM** → **EQ → sends → PAN → channel fader** → (DIRECT or BUSS) → **track / tape** → playback up the board → **master fader → LINE OUT**.
2. **(2)** Gain staging = a strong, clean level at every stage; target for tape = **strong, near the top, never clipping**; failure modes = **too quiet (hiss)** and **too hot (clipping)**.
3. **(2)** A passive pickup into the low (5.6 kΩ) input is **loaded down** (dull, quiet); fix = a **buffered pedal or DI** (high-Z in, low-Z out).
4. **(2)** A strong level adds **warmth/gentle compression**; use it by pushing the level up until it **sounds best** and backing off the instant it sounds bad (before clipping).
5. **(2)** **DIRECT** = channel N to track N (simple/one-to-one, can record all four at once); **BUSS** = record a **blend** to a track (bounce/submix; PAN picks L→1/3, R→2/4).
6. **(2)** Type II because the machine is **biased for it**; dbx on so hiss is reduced and it **tracks correctly** both ways; defeat dbx on **track 4 only for a sync tone**.

### Part C
1. **Generate a click elsewhere and record it to a track**, then monitor it while you play.
2. The **foundation (rhythm) part**; it sets time and feel.
3. **Click, recorded parts, and the live input.**
4. So parts recorded at **different times agree on time** (no grid on tape).
5. **Rehearse the punch without recording** to nail the entry safely.
6. In the **gaps (rests/sustains)** so the seams hide.
7. To be **locked in time/tune** before the machine drops in.
8. When the take needs **too many punches** (re-record it).
9. The **punch-in/out footswitch**.
10. **Rushing the easy parts (and dragging the hard ones).**

### Part D
1. **Combine tracks onto one to free up tracks** (fit a big arrangement on four).
2. **Balance/pan/EQ are baked in**; generation loss = a copy of a copy gains a little noise / loses a little clarity.
3. Bounce the **sure parts (rhythm/background)**; keep the **lead/vocal/uncertain part** live.
4. **Level (faders) → pan → EQ → effects (send/return).**
5. Because **relative volume is most of what makes parts sit together**; the rest is refinement.
6. **Rhythm guitar one side, another part the other, bass and lead up the middle** (or similar spread).
7. **Cutting** removes what fights and sounds more natural; boosting piles up energy and noise.
8. Because each part must sound right **in context (the whole)**; soloing hides how it fits.
9. A **shared reverb/delay** that glues parts into one space; adjustable without re-recording.
10. The tape plays in **real time**, so you **perform the mix** (and can ride faders) live.

### Part E
1. **Record the tape mix into the DAW as one stereo track** (in real time): one finished stereo file.
2. The **four tape tracks individually**, taken from the **TAPE OUT jacks**, straight off tape (before the mixer faders/EQ).
3. **−10 dBV (consumer line)**; set the interface to **line** and the DAW input by the meter, strong but not clipping.
4. **Wow and flutter / speed variation** make the tape **not stay locked to the grid**; the cassette's speed is not perfectly constant.
5. **Anchors the audio's timing to the grid** with markers (tames the drift where you need it).
6. When you want to **change the balance or process parts separately** in the box (tape tone, DAW control).
7. They come off the **same tape pass at once**, sharing the identical drift.
8. The **front-end sound (saturation/character) is recorded going in** and cannot be faked later; you record to tape for sound, finish in the box for control.
9. Send a clean/DAW part **out to the 424, record it to tape for character, and transfer it back**.
10. Any of: **no sample-accurate sync** (warp instead); **noise floor** (not silent); **only four tracks**; **tones/edits baked in (commitment)**.

### Part F
1. **(3)** Clean, strong level; no avoidable noise; correct routing. Deduct for clipping, hiss, or wrong track.
2. **(2)** A balance where every part is audible and parts do not fight, with a sensible pan and one purposeful EQ move; a completed mixdown pass.
3. **(3)** A clean, correctly-leveled transfer (no clipping), warped/handled to sit in time, and a finished exported file. Deduct for a clipped or badly-leveled transfer.
