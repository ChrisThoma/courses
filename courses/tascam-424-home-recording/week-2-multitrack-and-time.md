# Week 2: Multitrack & Time

> **The thesis of this week:** one good track is not a song. A song is several parts that lock together in time and stack into something bigger than any one of them. Week 2 is where you learn to build that, on a machine that gives you exactly four tracks and no metronome. You will make your own click and record it, play parts that lock to it, overdub one part against another, punch in to repair a single bad bar instead of redoing a whole take, and bounce tracks together so a full arrangement fits in four lanes. The two themes are **time** (everything locked to one click) and **economy** (making four tracks feel like more). Get these and you can record a real song.

This week assumes Week 1 is solid: you can capture a clean, strong part on tape by reflex. If level-setting still takes thought, drill Day 5 before continuing. Keep **Machine Reference §5 (recording), §6 (dbx/tape), and §10 (click & sync)** handy.

# Day 8: The Click-Track Problem

### Objectives
- Understand why a timing reference matters for multitrack recording.
- Generate a click (drum machine, phone, or DAW) and record it to a track.
- Decide which track the click lives on and whether dbx stays on.

### Setup (5 min)
Gather a click source: a **drum machine**, a **phone metronome app**, or a laptop running a metronome you can take out of a headphone/line jack. Have a cable to get it into the 424. Decide a tempo for a simple idea you want to record.

### Lecture

**The 424 MKIII has no built-in metronome.** There is no click button. So "playing to a click" means you *make* a click somewhere else and put it on tape, then play to it. This sounds like a limitation; it is actually a clean, flexible system once you set it up. ([Reference §10](SUPPLEMENT-machine-reference.md#10-click-track-sync))

**Why bother with a click at all?** Because the moment you overdub, every part has to agree on time. If your first guitar pass speeds up in the chorus, the bass you add later will fight it forever, and there is no grid to fix it on tape. A click is the shared clock that lets parts recorded minutes (or days) apart lock together. It is the single biggest upgrade to a beginner's recordings.

**The method:**
1. **Generate a click.** A drum machine playing a simple beat is the nicest option (musical *and* a timing reference). A bare metronome works fine too.
2. **Record it to a track.** Track 4 is the traditional home for the click (it sits at the edge and is the natural "utility" track). Because DIRECT records channel N onto track N, plug the click into **channel 4** and set track 4 to DIRECT, or plug into any channel and route it to track 4 through the **BUSS** (pan it hard right so it hits the right buss / track 4). Set a healthy level and record the click for the length of the song.
3. **Keep dbx on** for an ordinary audible click. (You only defeat dbx on track 4 for a *sync tone*, which is the advanced tape-sync path, not this.)
4. **Monitor it** while you record other parts (Day 10), so every overdub locks to the same clock.

> **A click is not the final drums.** Two honest paths: (1) use a *drum machine* as your click and keep it as the actual rhythm part, or (2) use a bare *metronome* as a scratch guide, record your parts to it, then record real drums/percussion over it and mute the click at mixdown. Decide which you are doing before you record, because it changes whether the click track is precious or disposable.

### Do
1. **(10 min) Get the click in.** Plug your click source into **channel 4** (INPUT select = MIC/LINE), park the fader at nominal, and set a healthy level by the meter. Confirm you hear it. (It has to enter on channel 4 because the next step arms track 4 DIRECT, and DIRECT records channel 4 onto track 4. If you would rather plug into another channel, route it to track 4 via the BUSS instead and pan hard right.)
2. **(12 min) Record the click to track 4.** Zero the counter. Arm track 4 **DIRECT** (it now records channel 4), set the others to SAFE, and record a steady click/beat for at least a minute (longer than your song idea). Play it back and confirm it is clean and steady.
3. **(5 min) Note the plan.** Write down: tempo, which track holds the click, and whether the click is "keep" (drum machine) or "scratch" (metronome). Set track 4 to SAFE so you do not erase your clock.

### Daily drill
**Click-in-a-minute, 3 min:** from nothing, get a click source in, leveled, and recorded to a track. Make setting up a timing reference fast and routine, not a project.

### Self-check
1. Does the 424 MKIII have a built-in metronome? So how do you "play to a click"?
2. Why does multitrack recording need a shared timing reference?
3. Which track traditionally holds the click, and why?
4. Do you keep dbx on for an audible click? When would you defeat it?
5. What is the difference between a "keep" click and a "scratch" click, and why decide first?

# Day 9: Tracking to the Click

### Objectives
- Record a foundation part locked tightly to the click.
- Monitor the click comfortably while you play.
- Judge timing objectively against the click on playback.

### Setup (5 min)
Click recorded on track 4 (SAFE). Your instrument-through-pedal front end into a channel. Arm the track you will record the foundation part on (say track 1, DIRECT). Headphones in, with the click audible.

### Lecture

**Lay the foundation first.** The first musical part you record sets the time and the feel for everything else, so it should be the steadiest, most rhythmic part of the song: usually **rhythm guitar or bass**. Record it locked to the click, and every later part has a solid floor.

**Hearing the click while you play.** Set your monitor so the click is clearly audible *under* your playing, not buried and not so loud you cannot hear yourself. You are playing a duet with the click; you must hear both. (The monitor/cue section, Day 10, is how you balance this.)

**Playing in time is a skill, and the click exposes it.** Most beginners rush the parts they find easy and drag the parts they find hard. The click makes this objective: on playback, you can hear exactly where you and the click part ways. The fix is not to play to your ragged ability and hope; it is to **slow the tempo** until you can lock to the click cleanly, record it there, and build the song at a tempo you actually own.

> **Lock, do not chase.** If you are constantly catching up to or pulling away from the click, you are chasing it. Slow down, simplify the part, and find the tempo where you and the click breathe together. A simple part perfectly in time beats a busy part that fights the clock, every single time.

### Do
1. **(10 min) Play along, no recording.** Roll the click and play your foundation part with it several times until you lock in. If you cannot lock, lower the tempo (re-record the click slower) and try again.
2. **(15 min) Record the foundation.** Arm your track, RECORD + PLAY, and record the part to the click. Do a few passes; keep the one that locks best.
3. **(7 min) Judge the timing.** Play the part back *with the click* and listen specifically for where you rush or drag. Mark those spots (by counter number) as candidates for a punch-in (Day 11) or a re-record. Set the keeper to SAFE.

### Daily drill
**Lock-in pass, 4 min:** play any simple riff to the click and record 20 seconds; play it back against the click and find every spot you drifted. Loop the drift spot slowly. This is how timing actually improves.

### Self-check
1. Which part do you usually record first, and why?
2. How loud should the click be in your monitor relative to your playing?
3. Where do beginners most commonly rush, and where do they drag?
4. What is the fix when you cannot lock to the click at the chosen tempo?
5. How do you judge your timing objectively after recording?

# Day 10: Overdubbing

### Objectives
- Overdub a second part while monitoring the click and the first part.
- Set a usable cue/monitor mix.
- Choose DIRECT vs BUSS for an overdub.

### Setup (5 min)
On tape: click (track 4) and your foundation part (track 1), both SAFE. Front end into a channel, ready to record a new part onto track 2 (DIRECT). Headphones in.

### Lecture

**Overdubbing** is recording a new part *while listening to* the parts already on tape. It is how a one-person band is built: foundation first, then bass, then a lead, then a vocal, each locked to the same click. ([Reference §2](SUPPLEMENT-machine-reference.md#2-the-signal-flow-the-mental-model))

**The cue mix is everything.** To overdub well you need a clear headphone blend of: the **click**, the **parts already recorded**, and the **new part you are playing**. You build that blend with the **TAPE CUE** system: set the master **EFFECT 2 / TAPE CUE** switch to **TAPE CUE**, set the monitor **EFFECT/CUE** switch to **EFFECT 2/CUE** (the switch that actually feeds the cue into your phones), set **MONITOR SELECT (LINE OUT)** to **L-R** for your live input, and raise the **TAPE CUE** knob for each recorded track (the click on 4, the foundation on 1); your live input comes through its channel fader on the main mix. Full recipe in [Reference §2, "Monitor and cue: a known-good setup"](SUPPLEMENT-machine-reference.md#monitor-and-cue-a-known-good-setup). If the cue mix is muddy or unbalanced (you cannot hear the click, or your new part is too quiet to play in tune), the overdub will drift. Spend a minute getting it right *before* you hit record. A good engineer sets a great cue mix for the player as a matter of course; here you are both.

**DIRECT vs BUSS for an overdub:**
- **DIRECT** (channel N → track N) is the simple choice when one input goes to one new track. Use it for most overdubs.
- **BUSS** lets you record a *blend* of channels to a track (for example, two mics on one amp, or a stereo source). PAN sets which buss/track. You will use BUSS mainly for bouncing (Day 12) and stereo submixes.

**Watch your track budget.** You have four tracks and one is the click. That leaves three for music, unless you bounce (Day 12). Before you start stacking, sketch a plan: what goes on tracks 1, 2, 3, and what you will bounce to make room.

> **Overdub to the part, not just the click.** Once a foundation is down, lock your new part to *it* (and the click underneath). Listen to how the parts lock together, not just whether each is individually on the grid. Music is parts agreeing with each other.

### Do
1. **(8 min) Build the cue mix.** With the master EFFECT 2 / TAPE CUE switch on **TAPE CUE**, the monitor **EFFECT/CUE** switch on **EFFECT 2/CUE**, and MONITOR SELECT (LINE OUT) on **L-R**, raise the **TAPE CUE** knobs for track 4 (click) and track 1, set **MONITOR LEVEL**, and balance them against your live input until you can comfortably hear all three. Adjust until it feels playable.
2. **(15 min) Overdub track 2.** Arm track 2 (DIRECT), others SAFE, and record a second part (bass under guitar, or a lead over rhythm) locked to track 1 and the click. Do a few passes; keep the best.
3. **(7 min) Listen to the lock.** Play tracks 1, 2, and 4 together. Do the parts agree rhythmically? Mark any drift. Set the keeper to SAFE.

### Daily drill
**Cue-mix set, 3 min:** with two parts on tape, set a clean cue mix from scratch (click + existing + input) fast. A great cue mix is half of a great overdub.

### Self-check
1. What is overdubbing?
2. What three things does a cue mix blend, and why does it matter?
3. When do you use DIRECT vs BUSS for an overdub?
4. With one track holding the click, how many are left for music before you bounce?
5. What should you listen for besides "is my new part on the click"?

# Day 11: Punch-In & Punch-Out

### Objectives
- Repair a section of a take without re-recording the whole thing.
- Use rehearsal mode to practice the punch safely.
- Set punch points and use the footswitch if you have one.

### Setup (5 min)
A take on a track with one fixable mistake (a wrong note in an otherwise good pass). Note the counter numbers just before and just after the mistake. The track armed for that section.

### Lecture

**Punching in** means dropping the machine into record on an *already-recorded* track for a short stretch, replacing just the bad part, then dropping back out. It is how you fix a single flubbed bar without losing the good performance around it. ([Reference §5](SUPPLEMENT-machine-reference.md#5-recording-the-record-function-switches-transport))

**The 424 MKIII does auto punch-in/out with rehearsal:**
- You set an **in-point** (where recording starts) and an **out-point** (where it stops), by the counter.
- **Rehearsal mode** lets you run the punch *without actually recording*, so you can practice coming in cleanly and check your timing, with no risk to the take.
- When the rehearsal is clean, you commit, and the machine punches in and out at exactly those points.
- An optional **RC-30P footswitch** plugs into the punch jack so you can trigger the punch with your foot while both hands play. Without it, you punch by hand (or have a friend do it).

**The technique that makes punches clean:**
1. **Pick punch points in the gaps.** Punch in and out during a rest or a sustained note, not in the middle of a fast phrase. The seams hide in the silences.
2. **Play along *before* the in-point.** Start playing a bar early, in time and in tune, so when the machine punches in you are already locked. Keep playing past the out-point.
3. **Rehearse first, always.** Use rehearsal mode until the entry is clean, then record for real.
4. **Listen across the seams.** Play back through both the punch-in and punch-out points; you should not be able to hear where the repair starts and ends.

> **Punching is repair, not a crutch.** It is for fixing one spot in a good take. If a take needs five punches, the take is not good; re-record it. Punching a bad performance into a good one rarely works and always takes longer than it should.

### Do
1. **(8 min) Set points and rehearse.** Note the in/out counter numbers around the mistake, then store the points and run it in **rehearsal** several times until your entry is clean and in time. The exact button sequence (cue, then REHEARSAL → PLAY → RECORD → PLAY to store START / IN / OUT, then AUTO PUNCH → PLAY to commit) is laid out step by step in [Reference §5](SUPPLEMENT-machine-reference.md#5-recording-the-record-function-switches-transport).
2. **(12 min) Punch it.** Commit the punch: play along starting a bar early, let the machine drop in and out at your points, fixing just the bad part. Repeat until the repair is invisible.
3. **(7 min) Check the seams.** Play back through both seams. If you can hear the punch, redo it with better points (move them into gaps) or a better lead-in.

### Daily drill
**Rehearse-then-punch, 4 min:** deliberately record a small flub, then fix it with a rehearsed punch-in and punch-out. Make rehearsing-first automatic so you never punch blind.

### Self-check
1. What does punching in let you do that re-recording does not?
2. What does rehearsal mode do, and why use it?
3. Where should you place punch points to hide the seams?
4. Why do you start playing a bar before the in-point?
5. When should you give up on punching and just re-record the take?

# Day 12: Bouncing (Ping-Pong)

### Objectives
- Combine several tracks down to one to free up space.
- Set a committed submix balance before you bounce.
- Plan track use to fit a full arrangement on four tracks.

### Setup (5 min)
At least two or three recorded parts on separate tracks, plus a free track to bounce to. Front faders and EQ on those channels ready to set a balance.

### Lecture

**Bouncing (also called ping-pong) is how four tracks become enough for a real song.** You play several recorded tracks back through the mixer, balance them, and record that *blend* onto a single free track. Now those parts live on one track and you have freed the others for new overdubs. ([Reference §5](SUPPLEMENT-machine-reference.md#5-recording-the-record-function-switches-transport))

**How it works on the 424:**
1. Set those channels' **INPUT select to TAPE** so the recorded tracks play back through the board, bring them up on their faders, and **set a balance** (levels, pan, EQ) you are happy with.
2. **Pan toward the buss that feeds your target track** (left buss → tracks 1/3, right buss → 2/4), set the destination track's **RECORD FUNCTION to BUSS** to arm it, and set the **master fader** (on a buss bounce the master fader sets the record level). To bounce tracks **1+2 down to the free track 3**: set channels 1 and 2 to TAPE, pan both left (track 3 sits on the left buss), arm track 3 to BUSS, and record. Then re-record over tracks 1 and 2. (The textbook ping-pong example bounces onto track 4, but in *this* course track 4 holds your click, so never bounce onto it; bounce onto whichever track is genuinely free.)
3. Record the bounce. Now your blend is on one track.

**The catch: a bounce is committed, and it costs a generation.** When you bounce, the **balance and EQ are baked in**: you cannot later turn the guitar down relative to the bass inside that bounce. And the bounced track is a *copy of a copy*, so it picks up a little more tape noise and a little less clarity (this is "generation loss"). dbx and a strong level keep it minimal, but it is real. So:
- **Commit the submix carefully**, because you cannot undo it.
- **Bounce the parts you are surest about** (rhythm section, background layers), and keep your most important or most uncertain part (lead, vocal) on its own track, recorded *after* the bounce.

**Plan the track map before you record a note.** A typical four-track plan for a one-person song:

```
  Stage 1 (record):     T1 rhythm gtr   T2 bass        T3 (free)      T4 click
  Stage 2 (bounce):     T1+T2 ─────────────────────►  T3 = "rhythm+bass" submix
  Stage 3 (re-use):     T1 lead gtr     T2 vocal       T3 submix      T4 click
  Mixdown:              fade T4 (click) out; mix T1, T2, T3
```

> **Plan, then commit.** The skill is not the button-press; it is deciding *which* parts to glue together and *when*, so the parts you might still want to change stay free. Sketch the map, bounce the safe stuff, keep the precious stuff live.

### Do
1. **(10 min) Set the submix.** Balance two or three recorded tracks on their channels (level, pan, a little EQ). Get it sounding right; this balance is about to be permanent.
2. **(12 min) Bounce.** Set those channels to **INPUT select = TAPE**, balance them, pan toward the buss feeding your target track, arm a free track to **BUSS**, set the master fader, and record the bounce. Play it back and check the balance survived.
3. **(5 min) Plan a map.** On paper, write a four-track plan for a song you want to record: what records first, what bounces to where, what stays live for last. You will use this in Project 2.

### Daily drill
**Submix-and-commit, 4 min:** balance two tracks and bounce them to a third. Practice committing to a balance fast and trusting it, because hesitation is the enemy of a four-track workflow.

### Self-check
1. What does bouncing let you do, and why is it necessary on a four-track?
2. What gets permanently baked in when you bounce?
3. What is generation loss, and how do you keep it small?
4. Which parts should you bounce, and which should you keep on their own track?
5. Why do you plan the track map before recording?

# Day 13: Pedals & Tone in Multitrack + Quiz 2

### Objectives
- Decide what to print (record) vs what to keep flexible.
- Use the effect send/return loop vs recording effects straight in.
- Handle bass and guitar tone choices for tape.
- Take Quiz 2.

### Setup (5 min)
Your pedalboard, your instrument, and a recorded part or two on tape. If you have an outboard effect (a reverb or delay pedal you can put on a send), have it ready.

### Lecture

You have been using pedals as your front end since Day 3. Now use them as *production* tools, and learn the two ways effects get into a recording.

**Printing effects (recording them onto the track).** When you record a guitar through a drive and a delay, those effects are *printed*: baked onto the tape, permanent. This is the four-track way, and it is good: it forces commitment and saves tracks. Print the effects that are part of the *sound* of the part (the fuzz on a fuzz riff, the modulation that defines a clean part). The cost is you cannot change them later, so dial them in and commit.

**The effect send/return loop (a shared effect over the mix).** The 424 has **EFFECT SEND** jacks and no dedicated return ([Reference §3](SUPPLEMENT-machine-reference.md#3-connections-cheat-sheet)). To use an effect this way: send a channel out **EFFECT 1** to a reverb/delay pedal, then bring the pedal's output **back in** through the **STEREO IN (ch 7–8)** or **SUB IN**. Now you can add that effect to any channel by turning up its send, and adjust the wet amount without re-recording. Use this for *mix* effects (a reverb that ties parts together) rather than tone-defining effects. This matters most at mixdown (Day 15).

**Bass and guitar choices for tape:**
- **Bass:** a clean **DI** (instrument → buffer/pedal → 424) is reliable, full, and easy to mix; tape adds pleasant warmth and roundness. You can also record a miked amp (dynamic mic, no phantom needed) or blend, but a clean DI is the dependable default.
- **Guitar:** print the tone you want. A miked amp (SM57 on the grille) into a MIC input, or a pedal/amp-sim straight into a LINE input, both work. Commit to a sound that *sits* in the track, not your favorite solo-in-the-bedroom sound, which is often too big to fit a mix.
- **Both:** a strong level to tape adds saturation that flatters guitar and bass. Push it to the sweet spot (Day 5).

> **Commit your tone.** On a four-track you decide the sound *going in*, not in a mix later. That is a feature: records made this way sound decisive. Spend your time getting the tone right at the source, then print it and trust it.

### Do
1. **(10 min) Print vs send.** Record a short part with its tone-defining effects printed. Then set up one effect on the EFFECT 1 send returning through the stereo/sub input, and hear how you can dial it onto a channel without re-recording.
2. **(10 min) Bass and guitar tones.** Record a bass DI and (if you can) a guitar through your committed tone. Play them back and judge whether each *sits* (sounds right in context), not just whether it sounds big alone.
3. Set keepers to SAFE.

### Quiz 2
Take **Quiz 2** now (see [QUIZZES.md](QUIZZES.md)), closed-book. Grade against [ANSWER-KEYS.md](ANSWER-KEYS.md). It covers the click track, tracking and overdubbing, the cue mix, punch-in, bouncing, and pedals/tone: Week 2's whole core. If you score below 90%, re-study the weak spot on tomorrow's studio day.

### Self-check
1. What does it mean to "print" an effect, and which effects should you print?
2. How do you set up an effect send/return on the 424, given there is no dedicated return?
3. Which kind of effects suit the send/return loop?
4. What is the dependable default for recording bass, and why?
5. Why is committing your tone at the source a feature, not a limitation?

# Day 14: Studio Day + Project 2

Today is consolidation, not new material. Three things:

### 1. Review the week
Reread the self-checks for Days 8–13 and answer every one from memory. The skills everything in Week 3 rests on are **playing to the click (Day 9)**, **overdubbing with a good cue mix (Day 10)**, and **planning and committing a bounce (Day 12)**. Spend the most time there.

### 2. The "build a section" drill (15 min)
Pick a short song section. Record a click, then a foundation part to it, then one overdub locked to the foundation, then bounce two parts down to free a track. The goal is to run the whole multitrack loop (click → track → overdub → bounce) smoothly, with clean levels and steady time throughout.

### 3. Project 2: A Multitrack Built to a Click
Begin **Project 2** (see [PROJECTS.md](PROJECTS.md)). It asks you to record a short, complete musical idea: a click, at least two musical parts locked to it, one overdub, and one bounce, with at least one repaired section (a punch-in). This is the Domain 6–9 test. It is due end of Day 14 (carry into the Week-3 studio day only if you must).

### Looking ahead
Week 2 turned tracks into a **song**: parts locked in time, stacked, repaired, and bounced to fit. Right now you can build a multitrack arrangement on four tracks. **Week 3 is finishing**: mixing those tracks on the 424's own board, bouncing a final stereo mixdown, and transferring the result into Ableton, both as a stereo bounce and as four separate stems, so you can polish and export a real, shareable track. The recording is done; now you make it sound like a record.
