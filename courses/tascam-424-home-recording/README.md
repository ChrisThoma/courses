# REC-24 Home Recording on the Tascam 424 MKIII: Guitar & Bass to Tape (A 3-Week Intensive)

**Format:** Self-paced intensive, modeled on a college studio/applied-recording course
**Time commitment:** ~45–75 min/day, 7 days/week, 3 weeks (the seventh day each week is a "studio day")
**Prerequisites:** A working **Tascam Portastudio 424 MKIII** (a 414/424/424 MKII will mostly work, with small differences), a **Type II (high-bias) cassette**, headphones, **cotton swabs with head-cleaning fluid/isopropyl alcohol for the heads and a rubber cleaner for the pinch roller**, a **guitar or bass and at least one pedal** (or a DI box), and, for Week 3, a way to get audio into a computer (an audio interface and **Ableton Live**, or another DAW). **No prior recording experience, no engineering background, and no music theory required.** If you can play a few bars of something, you can record it.

> **The promise of this course:** in three weeks you will go from never having recorded to capturing a real multitrack song on cassette, guitar and bass, played to a click, overdubbed, mixed on the machine's own board, and transferred into Ableton to finish. More than that, you will understand *signal flow* well enough to record cleanly on any gear for the rest of your life. This is not a gear-collecting course or a theory course. It is a course in **getting a good sound onto tape and out the other side.**

## What this course is (and isn't)

This course covers **everything you need to make finished recordings on a 424 MKIII and nothing more:**

- How the machine works as a **mixer plus a four-track tape deck**, and the **signal flow** that explains every "where did my sound go?" problem
- **Gain staging**: setting trim and levels by the meter so recordings are strong and clean, and using tape's natural **saturation** on purpose
- Getting **guitar, bass, and pedals** into the box cleanly (the impedance problem and why a pedal or DI solves it)
- **Recording to tape**: the record-function switches, DIRECT vs BUSS routing, arming tracks, the transport and counter, and **dbx** noise reduction
- **Keeping time with a click track** on a machine that has no built-in metronome
- **Multitrack craft**: overdubbing, the cue/monitor mix, **punching in** to fix mistakes, and **bouncing** (ping-pong) to fit a big arrangement onto four tracks
- **Mixing on the 424's board**: balance, pan, EQ, and an outboard effect via the send/return
- **Transferring to Ableton**: a stereo bounce *and* four separate stems via the tape outputs, plus how to deal with tape drift by warping

It deliberately **omits** what a home recordist does not need to start: deep mixing theory, mastering, microphone technique beyond the basics, MIDI tape sync, and the endless catalog of outboard gear. We name those doors and leave them for later. (Tape sync and full in-the-box mixing are the two biggest; the [Machine Reference](SUPPLEMENT-machine-reference.md) points the way.)

## A note on how recording is actually learned

Recording is a **craft learned at the controls**, not from reading. You learn gain staging by setting a level wrong, hearing the hiss or the distortion, and fixing it; you learn routing by recording to the wrong track once and never doing it again. So this course is built around **machine time**: every concept is paired with a task you do with your hands on the 424, and almost every day ends with you **listening back to what you recorded** and judging it honestly. Your ears, trained by listening to your own takes, are the real instrument here. A recording you only read about is trivia.

## How to use this course

1. Read `SYLLABUS.md` first, then `RUBRIC.md` so you know what "good" looks like. Then read the **Machine Reference** sections §1–§3 (the machine, the signal flow, the connections) before Day 1.
2. Each day, work the lesson in order: **Setup → Lecture → Do → Self-check.**
3. **Always at the machine.** Every concept here is learned by doing it (and doing it wrong, then right) at the controls.
4. **Listen back to everything you record.** The fastest feedback in recording is hearing your own take cold. Build the habit now.
5. **Set levels by the meter, every time.** It takes ten seconds and it is the difference between recordings you can use and recordings you cannot.
6. **Save your work by leaving finished tracks on SAFE** and labeling your cassettes. There is no undo on tape; protect what is good.
7. Take the quizzes closed-book and grade yourself honestly against `ANSWER-KEYS.md`. The applied "do it" portions are where the real grade lives.
8. Do the projects. They are where the skills consolidate into actual *songs you recorded.*

## A note on pace

Three weeks is enough to build **clean signal flow, confident tracking and overdubbing, a real multitrack workflow, and a finished song transferred to the computer.** What keeps unfolding for years is *taste*: the arrangement choices, the tones, the mix moves that make a recording sound like a record. What this course gives you is correct fundamentals and a working method, so those years compound instead of fighting noise and bad routing forever.

Load a fresh Type II tape. Let's record.
