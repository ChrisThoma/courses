# Week 3: Mix, Transfer & Finish

> **The thesis of this week:** a pile of recorded tracks is not a finished song. Finishing is two jobs: making the parts sound good *together* (the mix), and getting the result out of the machine and into a form you can share (the transfer). Week 3 does both. You will mix your four tracks on the 424's own board with level, pan, EQ, and an outboard effect; bounce a final stereo mixdown; and then transfer your work into Ableton Live two ways, as a single stereo bounce and as four separate stems, learning to warp past the tape's natural drift. By Friday you will have a song that started as a guitar through a pedal and ends as an exported file. That round trip, tape in, computer out, is a complete, modern home-recording workflow.

Keep **Machine Reference §3 (connections), §8 (transferring to Ableton), and §9 (gain staging)** open this week. You will need an audio interface and Ableton Live (or another DAW) for Days 17–20.

# Day 15: Mixing on the 424

### Objectives
- Build a mix with level, pan, and EQ on the 424's board.
- Add a shared effect via the send/return loop.
- Make parts sit together instead of fighting.

### Setup (5 min)
A multitrack recording on tape (your Project 2 or a fresh one). All tracks on SAFE. Set channels 1-4 **INPUT select to TAPE** so the four tape tracks come up on their own channel faders (this is the mixdown side of the switch you kept on MIC/LINE while tracking), and set **MONITOR SELECT** to **L-R**. Headphones, and monitor speakers if you have them. An outboard reverb/delay on the EFFECT 1 send, returning through the stereo/sub input, if you have one.

### Lecture

**Mixing is making the parts sound good together.** On the 424 you mix with four tools, in roughly this order:

1. **Level (faders).** The big one. Set the relative volumes so you can hear every part and nothing buries the others. Start with the most important part (usually the vocal or lead) and balance everything around it. Most of a good mix is just level.
2. **Pan.** Place parts across the stereo field so they are not all stacked in the center. Rhythm guitar to one side, a second part to the other, bass and lead up the middle is a classic spread. Panning creates space without touching volume.
3. **EQ.** Carve so parts stop competing. If guitar and bass are both muddy, cut some low-mids on the guitar so the bass owns the bottom. Use the **swept mid** to find and cut the offending frequency ([Reference §4](SUPPLEMENT-machine-reference.md#4-the-channel-strip)). Subtractive (cutting) usually beats boosting.
4. **Effects (send/return).** Add a shared reverb or delay over the EFFECT 1 send and return it through the stereo/sub input ([Reference §3](SUPPLEMENT-machine-reference.md#3-connections-cheat-sheet)). A touch of the same reverb on several parts glues them into one space.

**Listen to the whole, not the parts.** The beginner trap is soloing one track, making it sound huge, and discovering it does not fit. Mix with everything playing; judge each move by what it does to the *whole song*. A guitar that sounds thin alone often sits perfectly in the mix.

> **Mute the click.** If you used a scratch metronome on track 4, pull its fader all the way down for the mix (and beyond, if you do not need it). If your "click" was a drum machine you are keeping, mix it like any other part.

### Do
1. **(10 min) Level pass.** With everything playing, set the faders for a balance where you can hear every part clearly. Do nothing else yet. Get this right; it is most of the mix.
2. **(8 min) Pan pass.** Spread the parts across the stereo field. Listen on headphones for the placement.
3. **(10 min) EQ and effects.** Make one or two EQ moves to stop parts from fighting (cut, do not boost, first). Add a little shared reverb/delay via the send if you have one. Mute the scratch click.
4. **(5 min)** Listen to the whole mix start to finish and note what still bugs you.

### Daily drill
**Balance-first, 4 min:** on any multitrack, build a mix using *only* the faders, no EQ or effects. Train your ear to get most of the way there with level alone.

### Self-check
1. What are the four mixing tools on the 424, and in what order do you reach for them?
2. Why is level the most important?
3. What does panning buy you, and what is a classic spread?
4. Why is cutting EQ usually better than boosting in a mix?
5. Why must you mix with everything playing rather than soloing parts?

# Day 16: The Mixdown

### Objectives
- Understand what "mixdown" means on a tape machine.
- Bounce your final stereo mix out of the 424.
- Set mixdown output levels for a clean capture.

### Setup (5 min)
Your mix balanced from Day 15. A recorder or interface connected to the **LINE OUT (RCA L/R)**, or just be ready to capture into Ableton tomorrow. Master fader at a sensible level.

### Lecture

**Mixdown is performing the mix to a stereo recording.** On a four-track, you do not "export." You play the four tracks back, the board mixes them to stereo per your fader/pan/EQ/effect settings, and that stereo result goes out the **LINE OUT** to whatever is capturing it. Because the tape plays in real time, **a mixdown is a live performance of your mix**: you can even ride a fader during it (push the lead up in the solo, pull the rhythm down in the verse). That hands-on move is a real mixing technique, not a limitation. ([Reference §2](SUPPLEMENT-machine-reference.md#2-the-signal-flow-the-mental-model))

**Where the mix goes.** The 424's stereo mix leaves on the **LINE OUT (RCA, −10 dBV)**. Your options to capture it:
- Into an **audio interface** → a computer (Ableton), which is what Days 17–20 do.
- Into a standalone recorder (another cassette, a digital recorder, a phone with a line input). The principle is identical.

**Set the mixdown level.** The capture device has its own input level. Aim, again, for **strong but not clipping**: the loudest part of the song should read healthy on the *receiving* meter without going into the red. You are gain-staging the handoff, the same discipline as everywhere ([Reference §9](SUPPLEMENT-machine-reference.md#9-gain-staging-reference)).

> **A mixdown is a take.** You may do several mixdown passes (different balances, different fader rides) and pick the best, exactly like recording a part. Do not expect the first pass to be the one. Print a few, choose, move on.

### Do
1. **(8 min) Set the handoff.** Connect LINE OUT to your capture device. Play the loudest section of your song and set the *receiving* level to strong-but-not-clipping. Confirm both sides are clean.
2. **(12 min) Mixdown passes.** Do two or three full mixdown passes of your song, riding a fader where it helps (lead up in the solo, etc.). Capture each.
3. **(5 min) Choose.** Listen back to your passes and pick the best mixdown. That stereo file is your "tape mix."

### Daily drill
**Fader-ride pass, 3 min:** during a mixdown, practice one deliberate move (a part up for its moment, down after). Hands-on mixing is a skill; rehearse the move before the take.

### Self-check
1. What does "mixdown" mean on a tape machine, and why is it a performance?
2. Which output carries the stereo mix, and at what level?
3. What is the target level for the capturing device?
4. Name one fader-ride move and when you would use it.
5. Why might you do several mixdown passes?

# Day 17: Transferring to Ableton, Part 1 (The Stereo Bounce)

### Objectives
- Capture the stereo mixdown into Ableton at a correct level.
- Understand tape drift and why the audio will not sit perfectly on the grid.
- Warp the transfer so it lines up where it matters.

### Setup (5 min)
424 **LINE OUT (RCA L/R)** → two inputs of your audio interface → Ableton, set to record one **stereo audio track**. Interface inputs set to **line** level. A new Live set open.

### Lecture

**The simplest transfer is the stereo bounce:** record your finished tape mix into Ableton as one stereo track, in real time. One file, the whole song, exactly as it sounds off the tape. ([Reference §8](SUPPLEMENT-machine-reference.md#8-transferring-to-ableton))

**Level the handoff.** The 424's LINE OUT is **−10 dBV (consumer line level)**. Set your interface inputs to **line** (not mic, not instrument), arm the Ableton track, play the loudest part of your song, and set the input gain so Ableton's meter reads healthy and never clips. Same gain-staging rule, one more time. Then record the whole song through in real time.

**The drift caveat (this is important and honest).** A cassette's speed is not perfectly constant: **wow and flutter** and small speed variation mean the tape will *not* stay locked to Ableton's grid bar for bar across a whole song. Do not expect to drop the audio on bar 1 and have the last chorus land on the grid. This is normal and not a fault. ([Reference §8](SUPPLEMENT-machine-reference.md#8-transferring-to-ableton))

**Warp is the fix.** Ableton's **Warp** lets you anchor the audio's timing to the grid with markers. For a transferred tape mix:
- If you just want the song to *play* and maybe sit near a tempo, set the clip to a fixed tempo and add a few warp markers at obvious downbeats.
- If you need it tight to the grid (to add programmed parts in the box), warp more carefully, placing markers on strong beats through the song.
- For many home recordings, leaving it mostly unwarped (just trimmed and tempo-tagged) is fine; you are finishing a performance, not building a click-tight production.

> **You are capturing a performance, not importing a project.** The tape mix is a finished thing with its own feel, drift and all. Warp it enough to be useful, not so much that you iron the life out of it.

### Do
1. **(8 min) Level and arm.** Set the interface to line, arm the stereo track, and set Ableton's input level by the meter on the loudest part. No clipping.
2. **(12 min) Record the bounce.** Record your whole tape mix into Ableton in real time. Confirm the file is clean and full-level.
3. **(7 min) Warp it.** Trim the top, set a tempo, and add a few warp markers at downbeats so the song sits sensibly. Play it back against Ableton's metronome and notice the drift, then notice how a few markers tame it.

### Daily drill
**Clean handoff, 3 min:** set up the LINE OUT → interface → Ableton chain and get a clean, correctly-leveled input from scratch. The transfer should become routine.

### Self-check
1. What is a stereo bounce transfer, and what do you end up with?
2. At what level does the 424 output, and how do you set the Ableton input?
3. What is tape drift, and why does it happen?
4. What does Ableton's Warp do for a transferred tape mix?
5. Why might you *not* warp a tape mix tightly to the grid?

# Day 18: Transferring to Ableton, Part 2 (Four Stems)

### Objectives
- Capture all four tape tracks into Ableton as separate stems.
- Decide when stems beat a stereo bounce.
- Begin finishing the mix in the box.

### Setup (5 min)
424 **TAPE OUT 1–4 (RCA)** → four inputs of a multichannel interface → Ableton, set to record **four mono audio tracks**. Inputs at line level. (A **4-input interface** is what you want for stems, so all four capture in a single pass; a two-input interface forces two passes that will *not* stay aligned over the song — see *Aligning stems* below.)

### Lecture

**The four tape outputs let you transfer stems.** Each of the 424's four **TAPE OUT** jacks carries one tape track, straight off tape, *before* the mixer's faders and EQ ([Reference §3](SUPPLEMENT-machine-reference.md#3-connections-cheat-sheet)). Send all four into four interface inputs and record them into Ableton simultaneously as four separate tracks. Now each part lives on its own track in the computer, and you can re-balance, re-EQ, edit, and add plug-ins to each one independently. ([Reference §8](SUPPLEMENT-machine-reference.md#8-transferring-to-ableton))

**Stems vs stereo bounce, when to use each:**

| | Stereo bounce | Four stems |
|---|---|---|
| You get | one finished stereo file | four independent tracks |
| Mix is | already done on the 424 | finished in Ableton |
| Best when | you love the tape mix as is | you want to change the balance or process parts separately |
| Effort | low | higher |

Use the **stereo bounce** when the tape mix is the sound you want and you just need a file. Use **stems** when you want the tape's *tone* but the computer's *control* over the mix.

**Aligning stems.** Because all four come off the same tape pass at once, the stems are in sync with *each other*. They share the tape's wow and flutter identically. **This only holds within a single pass**, which is why you really want a **4-input interface** for true stem transfer: record all four at once and they line up perfectly. Two separate passes through a two-input interface will *not* stay aligned, because each pass has its own independent wow and flutter; even if you start both from the same counter point (RTZ), the two passes drift apart over the length of the song and no single nudge in Ableton can re-lock them. Treat two-pass transfer as acceptable only for short or rough material where a little divergence does not matter; otherwise use a 4-input interface, or fall back to the **stereo bounce** (Day 17), which keeps everything locked because it is one stereo pass.

**Finishing in the box.** With stems in Ableton you now have the best of both: tape-recorded guitar and bass, and a DAW to mix them. Set levels, pan, EQ, and add reverb/compression as you like. You are mixing again, with the Day 15 principles, just with more control.

> **Why bother recording to tape at all, then?** Because the *sound* going onto the stems, the saturation, the slight compression, the character, happened on the way in and cannot be faked by dropping a plug-in on a sterile DI. You record to tape for the front-end sound, and finish in the box for the control. That hybrid is the whole point of the workflow.

### Do
1. **(10 min) Capture stems.** Route TAPE OUT 1–4 to four interface inputs, level each by its Ableton meter, and record all four tracks in one pass. Confirm they are in sync and clean.
2. **(12 min) Rough mix in the box.** Set levels, pan, and a little EQ on the four stems in Ableton. Compare this to your Day 16 tape mix: what is easier, what did you lose?
3. **(5 min)** Save the Live set with both the stereo bounce (Day 17) and the stems, clearly labeled.

### Self-check
1. What do the four TAPE OUT jacks give you, and from where in the signal path?
2. When do stems beat a stereo bounce, and vice versa?
3. Why are the four stems automatically in sync with each other?
4. Why won't two passes through a two-input interface stay aligned over a whole song, and what should you do instead?
5. Why record to tape and finish in the box rather than just recording into the computer?

# Day 19: Finishing (and Back to Tape)

### Objectives
- Edit and polish the transferred audio to a finished state.
- Understand the optional "reamp / print back to tape" move and the hybrid loop.
- Be honest about the limits of the tape-and-DAW workflow.

### Setup (5 min)
Your stems (or stereo bounce) in Ableton from Day 18. Your 424 still connected.

### Lecture

**Finishing in Ableton.** With your audio in the box, finishing means the same judgments as mixing on the 424, plus what the computer makes easy:
- **Edit:** trim silence, fix an obvious clam by copying a clean bar over it, tighten an entrance.
- **Balance and process:** finalize levels, pan, EQ, and add light compression and reverb to taste.
- **Arrange:** if you transferred stems, you can copy a section, build an intro/outro, or rearrange. (You could not do this on tape.)
- **Export:** bounce the finished song to a WAV/MP3 you can share. That is the deliverable.

**The reamp / print-back move (optional).** The hybrid loop runs both directions. You can send a track *out of* Ableton, **into the 424**, record it onto a tape track to pick up tape character (saturation, compression, a little wobble), then transfer it *back* into Ableton. This is how you give a clean, computer-recorded or programmed part the tape sound after the fact. It is optional and a rabbit hole; know it exists.

```
  Ableton track ─► interface out ─► 424 input ─► record to tape ─► TAPE OUT ─► interface in ─► Ableton
        (clean part)                         (gains tape character)                  (back, with character)
```

**The honest limits.** This workflow is wonderful and it is not magic:
- **No sample-accurate sync** between tape and DAW without a sync box; you warp instead.
- **Noise floor:** tape and dbx are good, not silent; clean technique matters more than on a computer.
- **Four tracks** is a real constraint; bouncing costs a generation. Plan around it.
- **Commitment:** tones and many edits are baked in at the tape stage. That is the trade you make for the sound.

> **The point is the marriage, not the machine.** Tape gives you a front-end sound and a discipline; the computer gives you control and a finished file. Neither alone does what the pair does. You now own both halves.

### Do
1. **(15 min) Finish a song.** Take your transferred audio to a finished state: edit, balance, light processing, and a clean intro/outro. Export a WAV.
2. **(10 min, optional ★) Reamp.** Send one clean part out to the 424, record it to tape, and transfer it back. Compare before/after; hear the tape character.
3. **(5 min)** Listen to your exported file on two systems (headphones and a speaker). Note anything to fix in Project 3.

### Self-check
1. Name three things finishing in the box lets you do that tape did not.
2. What is the reamp / print-back move, and what does it give you?
3. What is the deliverable at the end of finishing?
4. Name two honest limits of the tape-and-DAW workflow.
5. In one sentence, what does each half (tape, computer) contribute?
