# Projects: HORT E-15

Projects are where understanding becomes a real plan for *your* garden. Each has a deliverable and a grading rubric. Every project is built around your actual yard (or, if you have none yet, a specific site you have access to: a relative's, a community-garden plot, or a detailed hypothetical lot you commit to). Grade yourself honestly; "I basically did it" is a 70, not a 95.

All four projects are cumulative: Project 1's site data feeds Project 2's plant choices, which feed Project 3's maintenance plan, which all roll into the Final Project's master plan. Do them in order and the final assembles almost by itself.

---

## Project 1: Site & Soil Assessment *(Day 6 · 15% of grade)*

**Goal:** produce the complete, honest profile of your site that every later decision depends on. You can't choose plants or fix soil you haven't measured.

### Deliverables
1. **Climate facts:** your hardiness zone (and whether you're in a heat-island 7a pocket), your average last-spring and first-fall frost dates, and your growing-season length. State your two anchor dates (~May 15, ~Oct 15).
2. **Site map** (hand sketch or printed aerial): mark north; outline beds, lawn, trees, and structures; and overlay **sun zones** (full sun / part / shade, from observation or off-season estimate), **slope/aspect**, and at least **three microclimates** (a hot wall, a wet low spot, a dry rain shadow, a windy edge).
3. **Soil texture:** results of a **ribbon test** (≥2 areas) and a **jar test** with estimated sand/silt/clay percentages. State your texture class (most will be clay/clay-loam).
4. **Drainage:** a **perc test** result (or, off-season, a mapped record of where water puddles after rain) with each bed rated slow/good/fast.
5. **Soil test:** either your actual Penn State soil-test report (pH + nutrients + recommendations) **or**, if results aren't back yet, proof you've submitted it plus your sampling plan, *and* a written interpretation of what each likely result would prescribe. Include your **lead-risk assessment** (distance to old structures/roads) and decision on lead testing / raised beds.
6. **Summary statement:** 3–5 sentences naming your site's biggest opportunities and biggest constraints (e.g., "heavy clay, acidic, fast-draining nowhere; best sun on the south slope; wet pocket by the downspout; possible lead near the 1920s foundation").

### Rubric (15 pts)
| Pts | Criterion |
|---|---|
| 3 | Climate facts correct and anchor dates stated |
| 4 | Site map complete with sun zones, slope, and ≥3 microclimates |
| 3 | Texture + drainage tested and correctly classified |
| 3 | Soil test submitted/interpreted incl. pH plan and lead-risk decision |
| 2 | Summary correctly identifies real opportunities and constraints |

## Project 2: Planting & Care Plan *(Day 13 · 15% of grade)*

**Goal:** demonstrate "right plant, right place" and correct care by designing the plantings for one or more real areas of your site, using your Project 1 data.

### Deliverables
1. **Plant selections for one full bed (minimum):** choose 6–10 plants matched to that spot's light/soil/drainage/space, **biased toward natives**, with mature sizes that fit. For each plant give: name, why it fits (cite the site condition it matches), mature size, light/water needs, and bloom time. At least **half must be perennials/woody** (the backbone), and your set must provide **bloom spring → fall**.
2. **Tag-reading proof:** for three of your chosen plants, copy out the key tag spec (zone, light, size, spacing, water) and state your yes/no reasoning against the site.
3. **Planting plan:** for each plant, the correct **planting window** (cool-season early spring / tender after ~May 15 / woody in fall) and a note on correct **hole and depth** and **spacing** (a simple to-scale layout earns full marks).
4. **Care plan** for the bed: watering rule (establishment vs. established), mulch plan, fertilizing decision (test-driven; justify *not* fertilizing if that's correct), and the pruning/deadheading approach per plant (including spring- vs. summer-bloomer timing for any shrubs).
5. **Avoid-list:** name any invasive/poor-choice plants you considered or already have, and their native alternatives.

### Rubric (15 pts)
| Pts | Criterion |
|---|---|
| 5 | Plant choices genuinely fit the site (light/soil/size/hardiness); native bias; season-long bloom |
| 3 | Tag reading correct and reasoning sound |
| 3 | Planting windows, depth, and spacing correct |
| 3 | Care plan correct and test-driven (watering, mulch, fertilizer restraint, pruning timing) |
| 1 | Avoid-list with native alternatives |

## Project 3: Weed & Maintenance Plan *(Day 20 · 15% of grade)*

**Goal:** prove you can keep the garden healthy over time: manage weeds by life cycle and run a year-round calendar.

### Deliverables
1. **Weed inventory:** identify the weeds actually present in your yard (target the Pittsburgh dozen). For each: name, **life cycle** (annual/biennial/perennial), and its **weak point / control method** (stop seed / remove root / smother / specific tactic). Flag any **poison ivy** and any **invasives** present.
2. **Invasive plan (if applicable):** for any knotweed, garlic mustard, or similar, a **multi-step, multi-season** control plan with methods, timing windows, and **disposal** (trash, not compost). If you have none, write the plan you *would* use for knotweed (to prove you know it).
3. **Cultural weed-prevention plan:** how you'll use mulch, dense planting/ground covers, and no-dig to suppress weeds and protect the seed bank; name your bare-soil hot spots and how you'll cover them.
4. **Month-by-month maintenance calendar** for *your* garden: every month, the specific tasks (planting, mulch, pruning by rule, watering, scouting, lawn care by season, fall renovation, put-to-bed, do-nothing winter). Must correctly place the season-critical items (no fall woody fertilizing, fall planting/lawn renovation, spring pre-emergent timing, ~May 15 tender planting).
5. **Do-nothing list:** at least four things you'll deliberately *not* do, with the reason for each.

### Rubric (15 pts)
| Pts | Criterion |
|---|---|
| 4 | Weed inventory correct with life cycles and matched controls; poison ivy/invasives flagged |
| 3 | Invasive plan is realistic, correctly timed, and disposes properly |
| 3 | Cultural prevention plan addresses real bare-soil/seed-bank risks |
| 4 | Calendar is complete and seasonally correct for Pittsburgh |
| 1 | Do-nothing list with sound reasons |

## Final Project: Your Garden Master Plan *(Days 20–21 · 20% of grade)*

**Goal:** the capstone. Integrate Projects 1–3 into a single, coherent master plan that would let anyone walk into your yard and build and maintain a healthy garden. This is the Domain-7 rubric test.

### Specification
A master plan document (written, with sketches/maps, neat is fine; this is horticulture, not graphic design) containing:
1. **Site profile** (from Project 1, refined): the conditions and constraints that drive everything.
2. **The vision + design:** what you want this garden to be and do, expressed as a design that applies **biodiversity, native pollinator support, layering, and plant communities** (Day 18). Show at least one **plant community** matched to a real spot, planted in drifts and layers, blooming across the season, with a **ground layer** that suppresses weeds. Include a **lawn decision** (keep/right-size/convert) with reasoning.
3. **Soil & water strategy:** how you'll build and maintain healthy clay soil (compost/organic matter, mulch, no-dig, lime per test) and water correctly, tying to your composting plan (Supplement Part 2).
4. **The integrated calendar:** your month-by-month plan (from Project 3) covering plant care, weeds, pests (IPM), lawn, and soil: the operating manual.
5. **The healthy-garden virtuous cycle:** a short section naming, for *your* yard, one concrete action at each stage (soil → right plants → diversity → pollinators/predators → pest & weed suppression → less work → organic matter back to soil), proving you understand the garden as a self-improving system, not a to-do list.
6. **The defense (the actual deliverable):** for every major choice (a plant, a design move, a soil action, a calendar item), be able to answer *why*, from the principles of this course. Write the three choices you're proudest of and defend each in 1–2 sentences (cite the principle: right plant/right place, feed the soil, life-cycle timing, etc.).

### Rubric (20 pts)
| Pts | Criterion |
|---|---|
| 4 | Site profile is accurate and actually drives the plan |
| 5 | Design applies diversity, natives/pollinators, layering, and plant communities correctly; lawn decision justified |
| 4 | Soil & water strategy is sound and Pittsburgh-appropriate (clay, compost, mulch, test-driven) |
| 4 | Integrated calendar is complete, seasonally correct, and covers all systems |
| 3 | The defense is *correct* and shows *intent*: choices explained from principle, not habit; virtuous cycle understood |

**Master's bar:** 18+/20, *and* you could hand this plan to a neighbor with a different yard and adapt it for their site without starting over, because you're applying principles, not copying a template.
