# Field Practice Menu — HORT E-15

Gardening fluency isn't built by daily recall drills like a musical instrument — it's built by **recurring observation and small, consistent actions** that train your eye and keep the garden ahead of trouble. This is the menu of those practices. **Pick a daily one and a few weekly ones; rotate seasonally.** The gardener who looks at the garden a little every day catches every problem while it's still small — that habit, more than any knowledge, is what separates a thriving garden from a struggling one.

**Building your routine:** one **daily** practice (the garden walk, ~10 min) + two or three **weekly** practices matched to the season + the **journal** every time something changes. Off-season, the observation and planning practices continue; the hands-on ones pause.

---

## A. Daily — the garden walk (all season, never stop)

| Practice | How | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **A1 — The morning walk** | Walk the whole garden slowly with coffee. No tools, no agenda — just *look*. What changed overnight? What's blooming, wilting, chewed, or sprouting? | the master habit; catches everything early |
| **A2 — Lens of the day** | Same walk, but each day pick one lens: water (who's thirsty/soggy?), pests (check leaf undersides), weeds (any new seedlings?), bloom (what's opening?) | training a specialist's eye one system at a time |
| **A3 — Touch test** | On the walk, finger-test soil moisture in 2–3 beds and containers before deciding to water anything | replaces the calendar with the plant's actual need |
| **A4 — One-minute fix** | Whatever small thing you notice (a weed seedling, a spent flower, a snapped stem), fix it *now* | "little and often" — stops small problems compounding |

## B. Weekly — scouting & upkeep (growing season)

| Practice | How | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **B1 — Pest patrol** | Once a week, deliberately inspect leaf tops *and undersides* on your most pest-prone plants (roses, squash, tomatoes, phlox). ID before reacting | catching infestations at threshold, not crisis |
| **B2 — Weed patrol** | Walk the beds pulling/hoeing weeds *while they're tiny* — and always before any weed flowers. Get the root on perennials; surface-slice annuals | draining the seed bank; "never let it seed" |
| **B3 — Deadhead round** | Snip spent blooms to push rebloom and tidy up (leave the ones you're saving for birds/winter) | extending the bloom season |
| **B4 — Water audit** | Check the rain gauge; only irrigate if the week fell short of ~1 inch, and then deep-soak in the morning | right-sized watering; disease prevention |
| **B5 — Mulch check** | Spot-check mulch depth (2–3 in) and top up thin areas, keeping it off stems/trunks | continuous weed suppression + moisture |

## C. Seasonal — the bigger moves (do in their window)

| Practice | How | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **C1 — Spring start** | Refresh mulch, plant cool-season crops as soil works, prune summer-bloomers, time crabgrass pre-emergent, plant tender crops after ~May 15 | the spring push, correctly sequenced |
| **C2 — Fall renovation** | Plant trees/shrubs/perennials, renovate the lawn (seed/aerate/feed), plant bulbs, treat knotweed, "leave the leaves" | using the best planting season |
| **C3 — Prune by the rule** | Dormant structural pruning of summer-bloomers in late winter; spring-bloomers right after they flower; 3 D's any time | never cutting off next year's flowers |
| **C4 — Put-to-bed** | Late fall: sanitation of diseased material, protect tender/young plants, store hoses, clean & oil tools | a garden that overwinters well |

## D. Skill-builders — train the eye (any time, incl. off-season)

| Practice | How | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **D1 — Plant ID reps** | Pick one plant a day (yours, a neighbor's, a park's) and identify it + its needs (light/water/size). Use an app or key to check | building the plant vocabulary a gardener needs |
| **D2 — Weed ID reps** | Same, for weeds — name it and its life cycle and control. Master the Pittsburgh dozen first | turning "is that a weed?" into instant tactics |
| **D3 — Diagnosis drill** | Find a struggling plant (yours, a neighbor's, online) and diagnose from principles: light? water? drainage? nutrient? pest? Walk the list before guessing | the core problem-solving skill |
| **D4 — Tag reading** | At any nursery or online, read three full plant tags and decide yes/no for a real spot in your yard | shopping without being upsold into failure |
| **D5 — Garden tour** | Visit Phipps Conservatory, the Pittsburgh Botanic Garden, a Master Gardener demo garden, or just walk a leafy neighborhood — note what thrives here and steal ideas | calibrating your eye to what works in Zone 6b |

## E. The journal (every time something happens — the compounding habit)

| Practice | How | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **E1 — Log the firsts** | Note first/last frost, first bloom of each plant, first pest sighting, first harvest. Build your own phenology | a personal calendar that beats any book |
| **E2 — Log the fails** | When something dies or struggles, write what and your best guess why. Patterns emerge over seasons | not repeating the same mistake |
| **E3 — Log the data** | Soil-test results and dates, what you planted where and when, what you fed and sprayed | continuity year over year |
| **E4 — Photo diary** | A monthly photo from the same spot. The year's arc — and your progress — becomes visible | motivation + a design record |

---

## Rules that make any practice work

1. **Look every day.** Ten minutes of daily observation beats a frantic monthly cleanup. The eye is the most important tool in the shed.
2. **Small and now beats big and later.** The one-minute fix you do today is the weekend you don't lose next month.
3. **Identify before you act.** Most "pests" are harmless and many "weeds" are tolerable. Diagnose, then decide.
4. **Write it down.** Memory fades; the journal compounds. Next season's gardener is you, and you'll be grateful.
5. **Honor the off-season.** When the garden rests, you plan, learn, and observe — then stop. Doing nothing in January is a legitimate practice.
