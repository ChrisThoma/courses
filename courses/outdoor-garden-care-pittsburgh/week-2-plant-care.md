# Week 2: Plant Care (Choosing, Planting, Feeding, Pruning, Protecting)

> **The thesis of this week:** with the site understood and the soil improving, plant care becomes a small set of well-timed, well-aimed actions. Choose plants that fit (Day 8), get them in the ground correctly (Day 9), water and feed them as they actually need (Day 10), prune with intent (Day 11), and protect them with the least force necessary (Day 12). Skill here is mostly knowing *when* and *how much*, and often when to do nothing.

# Day 8: Choosing Plants for Zone 6b (Natives, Perennials, and Reading the Tag)

### Objectives
- Choose plants that match your site and Zone 6b, biasing toward low-maintenance success.
- Understand why native plants are the smart default for a healthy Pittsburgh garden.
- Read a plant tag completely and shop without being upsold into failure.

### Lecture

**Plant selection is where the whole course pays off, or doesn't.** Every Week 1 assessment (zone, sun, soil, drainage, space) was preparation for this decision. Buy plants that fit your conditions and you've pre-solved most future problems; buy on impulse at the garden center and you'll spend the season nursing mismatches.

**Bias toward perennials, shrubs, and trees for structure; use annuals for seasonal color.** Perennials are the low-maintenance backbone: plant once, enjoy for years, and they develop deep roots that shrug off our weather. Annuals (planted fresh each May after frost) give long, bright bloom but are more work and water. A healthy Pittsburgh garden is mostly perennial bones with annual accents.

**Why native plants are the smart default.** A "native" plant evolved in this region (western PA / the Appalachian foothills) over millennia. Natives are the highest-leverage choice you can make:
- **Adapted to our exact conditions** (Zone 6b cold, clay soil, our rainfall and humidity), so they need *less* water, fertilizer, and fuss once established.
- **Pest- and disease-resistant** here, because they grew up with the local fauna.
- **The foundation of the food web**: native insects (especially pollinators and the caterpillars that feed baby birds) can use native plants in ways they often can't use exotics. A native garden is a living garden. (You'll build on this in Week 3's design lecture.)

Reliable, beautiful natives for Pittsburgh gardens:

| Sun perennials | Shade perennials | Shrubs / small trees |
|---|---|---|
| Purple coneflower (*Echinacea*) | Wild columbine (*Aquilegia*) | Serviceberry (*Amelanchier*) |
| Black-eyed Susan (*Rudbeckia*) | Virginia bluebells (*Mertensia*) | Eastern redbud (*Cercis*) |
| Bee balm (*Monarda*) | Foamflower (*Tiarella*) | Ninebark (*Physocarpus*) |
| Butterfly/swamp milkweed (*Asclepias*) | Wild geranium (*Geranium maculatum*) | Winterberry holly (*Ilex verticillata*) |
| New England aster, goldenrod | Christmas/ostrich ferns | Oakleaf hydrangea, summersweet |

(This isn't a memorization list; it's a shopping menu. Pick by your light and soil.)

**A word on invasives: the anti-list.** Some popular nursery plants are ecological problems here and should be *avoided*: **burning bush, Japanese barberry, callery/Bradford pear, English ivy, Norway maple, butterfly bush, periwinkle (vinca), and miscanthus** can all escape into our woods and waterways. Pennsylvania has begun restricting several. When in doubt, choose the native alternative.

**Reading the plant tag: the full decode.** Every tag is a spec sheet. Before buying, confirm all of it against your site:

| Tag item | What to check |
|---|---|
| **Hardiness zone** | Must include 6 (ideally lower) to survive winter. "Zones 7–10" = annual here. |
| **Light** | Full sun / part / shade; match your sun map exactly. |
| **Mature size (H × W)** | The #1 ignored line. That 1-gallon shrub may become 8 ft. Plan for the *adult*, not the cute baby. |
| **Spacing** | How far apart to plant; crowding now is disease and competition later. |
| **Water needs** | "Drought-tolerant" vs. "keep moist" tells you where it goes and how much work it is. |
| **Bloom time** | Stagger choices so something blooms spring → fall. |
| **Soil / pH notes** | e.g., acid-lovers (rhododendron) vs. average. |

### Examples

**Example 8.1: Matchmaking a real spot.** You have a bed: full sun, dry, lean clay, 4 ft wide. Good matches: coneflower, black-eyed Susan, butterfly milkweed, little bluestem grass, all natives that *want* sun and tolerate dry, lean soil, all top out under 4 ft, all feed pollinators. You've built a beautiful, near-zero-maintenance, ecologically rich bed entirely from your site data. Bad match for that spot: hostas (a shade plant that will scorch) or hydrangeas (which want moisture).

**Example 8.2: The mature-size trap.** A homeowner plants three "cute" dwarf Alberta spruces 2 ft apart and 1 ft from the foundation. Five years later they're a crowded, mildewed wall scraping the gutters. The tag said 10–12 ft tall, 3–4 ft wide. Reading one line would have prevented years of grief and a chainsaw. Plan for the adult.

### Lab

1. **(20 min)** Take **one real spot** in your yard and write its spec (light, soil/drainage, width, mature-height budget). Then choose 3–5 plants that fit; bias native. Use the menu above plus the Penn State / local nursery native lists.
2. **(15 min)** Practice **reading three real tags** (at a nursery, online, or on plants you own). For each, decide yes/no for your site and say why.
3. **(10 min)** Identify any **invasive** plants already in your yard (burning bush, barberry, vinca, etc.) and note them for eventual replacement.
4. **(15 min)** Sketch a **bloom-time calendar** for your chosen plants so you have flowers spring → fall (and ideally winter interest from berries/bark).
5. ★ Find one Pittsburgh-area native plant sale or nursery (Audubon, Penn State Master Gardeners, local natives nurseries) and note when they happen: spring/fall.

2. Give three concrete reasons natives are lower-maintenance and better for the ecosystem here.

# Day 9: Planting Done Right (Timing, Technique, Spacing, and Hardening Off)

### Objectives
- Plant at the right time of year for the right type of plant in Pittsburgh.
- Plant correctly (the right hole, the right depth) so plants establish instead of struggling.
- Harden off and transplant without shock.

### Lecture

**Timing: plant with the calendar, not the impulse.** What you plant determines *when*:
- **Cool-season vegetables & hardy annuals** (peas, lettuce, spinach, kale, pansies, onions): plant **early spring**, as soon as soil is workable (often late March–April here). They tolerate frost and bolt in summer heat.
- **Warm-season / tender crops & annuals** (tomatoes, peppers, squash, basil, marigolds, impatiens): plant **after the last frost, mid-May, after Mother's Day.** Earlier is a gamble with the weather as the house.
- **Perennials, shrubs, and trees:** plant in **spring or fall.** **Fall is often *best*** in Pittsburgh: warm soil and cool air let roots establish without heat stress, and fall rains do the watering. Aim to plant 6+ weeks before the ground freezes (so, by early-to-mid October). Spring is the close second. Avoid planting in the heat of midsummer if you can.

**The right hole: wide and shallow, not deep and narrow.** The old advice to dig a deep hole and backfill with rich compost actually *harms* trees and shrubs in clay (it makes a "bathtub" that fills with water, and roots circle in the cushy pocket rather than spreading). The modern, correct method:
- **Dig the hole 2–3× as wide as the rootball but no deeper than the rootball is tall.** Roots spread *out*, not down; loosen the wide perimeter so they can run.
- **Plant at the right depth: the top of the rootball level with (or slightly above) the surrounding soil.** Planting **too deep** is a top killer: it suffocates roots and rots stems/trunks. The trunk flare (where trunk widens into roots) must stay visible, never buried.
- **Backfill with the native soil you dug out** (amend the whole *bed*, not the single hole). Firm it gently to remove air pockets; don't stomp clay into concrete.
- **Tease apart circling/pot-bound roots** before planting, or score the rootball; otherwise roots keep strangling in their pot shape.
- **Water deeply right away** to settle the soil, then **mulch** (2–3 in, off the stem).

**Spacing: respect the tag.** Space plants by their *mature* width so air flows between them (disease prevention) and they don't compete. New beds look sparse; that's correct. Fill the temporary gaps with annuals or mulch, not with crowding.

**Hardening off: don't shock your transplants.** Seedlings raised indoors (or fresh from a greenhouse) are tender and will be scorched or wind-burned if planted straight out. **Harden off over 7–10 days:** set them outside in shade/shelter for a couple hours, then lengthen the time and increase sun/wind exposure daily before planting. Same idea for any plant moving from sheltered to exposed conditions.

**Transplanting established plants** (moving a perennial): do it in the **cool, dormant shoulders** (early spring or fall), take as much rootball as you can, replant immediately at the same depth, water in, and expect a week or two of sulking. Cutting back top growth reduces the water demand while roots re-establish.

### Examples

**Example 9.1: Tomato timing.** A gardener buys tomatoes on a warm April 20 and plants them. A May 5 frost (well within Pittsburgh's normal range) kills them. The neighbor who waited until May 18 plants healthy starts into warm soil and harvests earlier overall, because cold-shocked April tomatoes sulk for weeks even if they survive. **The calendar beats the thermometer in spring.**

**Example 9.2: The right tree hole.** Planting a serviceberry: hole 3 ft wide, only as deep as the 14-inch rootball, trunk flare just above grade, circling roots teased out, native clay backfilled and watered to settle, a wide flat mulch ring (no volcano). Result: roots spread into loosened soil and the tree establishes in one season. Compare the deep, narrow, compost-filled "bathtub" hole that drowns the same tree by August.

### Lab

1. **🌱/off-season (30 min)** **Plant something correctly** (even one perennial or a pot), practicing the wide-shallow hole, correct depth, root teasing, water-in, mulch. *Off-season:* do a full "dry run" on paper and, if you have houseplants/pot-bound plants, practice the root-teasing and repotting-at-correct-depth indoors.
2. **(15 min)** Build your **planting calendar**: list what you'll plant and write the target window (cool-season early spring / tender after May 15 / woody in fall) beside each.
3. **(10 min)** Practice spacing: take three plants' mature widths and lay out (with stakes, stones, or on paper) how far apart they truly go. Notice how "sparse" correct spacing looks.
4. **(10 min)** Write the **hardening-off schedule** (days 1–10) you'd use for spring seedlings.
5. ★ Find the trunk flare on an existing tree in your yard or street; check whether it's buried (too deep); a shockingly common problem on planted landscapes.

2. Describe the correct planting hole (shape, depth) and why deep-and-narrow is wrong in clay.

# Day 10: Watering and Feeding (How Much, How Often, Which Fertilizer)

### Objectives
- Water established and new plantings appropriately (building on Day 4).
- Decide whether, when, and what to fertilize: test-driven, not reflexive.
- Recognize the signs of over- and under-watering and of nutrient problems.

### Lecture

**Watering, refined for living plants.** Day 4 gave the soil principle (deep and infrequent, ~1 in/week, finger-test first). Two refinements for plant care:
- **New plantings are the exception:** they have tiny root systems and need consistent moisture for the first season while they establish. Water new trees/shrubs/perennials deeply 1–3× per week (more in heat) through their first summer, then taper to "only in drought." This first-year attention is the difference between a plant that establishes and one that limps.
- **Containers are their own world:** limited soil dries fast, and in summer heat a pot may need water *daily*. Water until it runs from the drainage holes. (No drainage holes = a drowned plant; never let a pot sit in a saucer of water.)

**The over/under-watering diagnosis (they look alike).** Both produce wilting and yellowing, which fools beginners into watering a *drowning* plant. Distinguish by checking the *soil*, not the leaves:

| Sign | Underwatered | Overwatered |
|---|---|---|
| Soil | Dry 2+ in down | Soggy, smells sour |
| Leaves | Dry, crispy, brown edges; wilts, recovers when watered | Soft, yellow, may drop; wilts but soil is wet |
| Roots | Intact, white-ish | Brown, mushy, rotting |
| Recovery | Quick after watering | Watering makes it worse |

In clay-heavy, well-watered Pittsburgh gardens, **overwatering and poor drainage kill more plants than drought.** When in doubt, check the soil and wait.

**Fertilizing: feed the soil first, supplement second, and test before you do either.** Recall Day 3: most established clay isn't short on P and K, and over-fertilizing wastes money, burns roots, drives weak floppy growth, and pollutes our rivers. The disciplined approach:
1. **Build soil with compost/organic matter** (top-dress beds annually). This alone feeds most ornamental and native plantings indefinitely. A native perennial border may *never* need fertilizer.
2. **Supplement only where a crop is hungry or a test shows a deficiency**: chiefly **vegetables** (heavy feeders), annual flowers in pots, and lawns.
3. **Match the fertilizer to the need** (Day 3's N-P-K): leafy growth/lawn → higher N; flowers/fruit → balanced or higher P; general → balanced like 10-10-10 or, better, compost.

**Organic vs. synthetic, briefly:** *Organic* sources (compost, manure, blood/bone/feather meal, fish emulsion) release slowly, feed the soil web, and are hard to overdose, the better default for a healthy home garden. *Synthetic* (the bagged numbers) act fast and precisely, useful for a diagnosed deficiency or container plants, but do nothing for soil life and are easy to over-apply. **Always follow label rates; "more" is not "better" and frequently does harm.**

**Timing fertilizer:** feed in the **growing season** (spring into early summer). **Don't fertilize woody plants in late summer/fall:** you'll push tender new growth that winter kills. Let plants harden for winter.

### Examples

**Example 10.1: The killed-with-kindness houseplant move, outdoors.** A new gardener sees a wilting newly planted shrub in wet clay and waters it daily "to help." It declines and dies of root rot; it was drowning, not thirsty. The finger test (soggy soil) would have said *stop watering and improve drainage*. Lesson: diagnose the soil before you reach for the hose.

**Example 10.2: Fertilizer discipline.** Two beds of coneflowers: one gets monthly 10-10-10, one gets a spring inch of compost and nothing else. The fertilized bed grows tall, floppy, soft, and aphid-prone and needs staking; the compost bed grows sturdy, blooms hard, and stands on its own. With natives in decent soil, **less is more.**

### Lab

1. **(15 min)** Establish your **watering plan**: separate rules for (a) established beds, (b) first-year plantings, (c) containers. Write the finger-test trigger for each.
2. **(15 min)** Practice the **over/under diagnosis** on a real or pictured struggling plant: check soil moisture *first*, then call it.
3. **(15 min)** Audit your fertilizing: list what you currently feed and re-decide each based on "soil first, test-driven." Cross off anything you were feeding out of habit.
4. **(10 min)** Read the label rate on any fertilizer and calculate the correct amount for one bed; note how easy it would be to over-apply by eyeballing.
5. ★ Set up one rain gauge (or the tuna can) and start logging weekly rainfall so you only irrigate when nature falls short of the ~1 inch.

### Self-check

# Day 11: Pruning, Deadheading, and Training

### Objectives
- Understand why and how plants respond to pruning, and prune without harming.
- Apply the universal rules (the 3 D's, the right cut) and the timing rules that prevent the classic "I cut off all the flowers" mistake.
- Deadhead and pinch to extend bloom and shape plants.

### Lecture

**Pruning is directed growth, not haircutting.** When you cut a stem, you redirect the plant's energy; it pushes new growth from buds below the cut. Done with intent, pruning improves health, shape, flowering, and fruiting; done randomly, it creates ugly, disease-prone, weakly attached growth. The skill is *what, when, and how much.*

**Why we prune (the legitimate reasons):**
- **Health**: remove the **3 D's: Dead, Damaged, Diseased** wood. This is always safe, in any season, on any plant: your default first cut.
- **Structure**: remove crossing/rubbing branches and crowded interior growth to open the plant to light and air (huge for disease prevention in our humid climate).
- **Shape/size**: keep a plant in bounds (better: plant the right size so you rarely need to).
- **Flowering/fruiting/rejuvenation**: strategic cuts to drive bloom or renew an old shrub.

**The right cut:**
- Cut just **above an outward-facing bud or back to a branch/the branch collar**: never leave a long stub (it dies back and invites rot) and never cut flush into the trunk (it can't heal).
- Use **sharp, clean tools** (bypass pruners, not anvil; loppers/saw for thick wood). Wipe blades with alcohol between diseased plants to avoid spreading infection.
- **Don't "top" trees** (cutting the leader/big limbs back to stubs): it's the most damaging common practice, producing weak regrowth and decay. Hire an arborist for anything overhead or structural.

**Timing: the rule that saves your flowers.** The #1 pruning mistake is cutting off this year's flowers by pruning at the wrong time. The rule for flowering shrubs:
- **Blooms in SPRING** (forsythia, lilac, azalea, rhododendron, oakleaf hydrangea, old-fashioned bigleaf hydrangea) → sets its buds the *previous* year on old wood. **Prune right *after* it blooms** (early summer). Prune it in winter/early spring and you cut off all the flower buds.
- **Blooms in SUMMER** (panicle/'Annabelle' hydrangea, butterfly-attracting summersweet, roses, spirea, potentilla) → blooms on *new* wood grown this year. **Prune in late winter / early spring** while dormant; it'll bloom on the fresh growth.

A handy mnemonic: *spring bloomers, prune after; summer bloomers, prune before.* When unsure, the safe move is to prune just after flowering.

**General timing:**
- **Most deciduous trees & shrubs:** structural pruning in **late winter / dormant** season: you can see the form, the plant isn't stressed, and disease/insect pressure is low. (Exception: the spring-bloomers above.)
- **Avoid heavy pruning in late summer/early fall**: it spurs tender growth that winter kills.
- **Dead/damaged wood:** remove **any time.**

**Deadheading and pinching, easy wins:**
- **Deadheading** = removing spent flowers. It redirects energy from seed-making back into more blooms (many annuals and perennials rebloom if deadheaded) and tidies the plant. *Exception:* leave some seedheads (coneflower, black-eyed Susan, grasses) into fall/winter; they feed birds and look great in frost (a Week 3 design idea).
- **Pinching** = nipping the growing tips of young plants (mums, basil, coleus, asters) to force bushier, fuller growth instead of one leggy stalk.

### Examples

**Example 11.1: The lilac tragedy.** A homeowner neatly prunes the lilac every March "to shape it." It never blooms. Why: lilac sets next spring's buds the previous summer; March pruning removes them all. Fix: prune **right after** the May bloom finishes, and the buds for next year form undisturbed. Same logic saves forsythia, azalea, and rhododendron.

**Example 11.2: Deadheading for a second act.** A bed of salvia and coreopsis is sheared back by a third after the first flush fades; within two weeks it pushes a fresh wave of bloom that carries into fall. Left alone, it would have set seed and quit by July. A few minutes with the shears doubled the season.

### Lab

1. **(20 min)** Walk your woody plants and **identify the 3 D's**: flag any dead, damaged, or diseased wood. Remove what's clearly dead (safe any time) with a proper cut.
2. **(15 min)** Make a **bloom-timing prune list:** for each flowering shrub you have, write "spring bloomer → prune after flowering" or "summer bloomer → prune late winter." This single chart prevents the lilac mistake forever.
3. **🌱/off-season (10 min)** **Deadhead or pinch** something in active growth. *Off-season:* identify which of your perennials you'll *leave* standing for winter bird food/interest vs. cut down, and write the plan.
4. **(10 min)** Inspect your pruning tools: are they sharp and clean? Practice a correct cut (above an outward bud, no stub) on a twig.
5. ★ Find an example of bad pruning in your neighborhood (a topped tree, a stub-cut shrub, a "mulch volcano") and articulate exactly what's wrong; diagnosing others' mistakes builds your eye.

# Day 12: Pests and Disease (IPM and the Pittsburgh Usual Suspects)

### Objectives
- Apply Integrated Pest Management (IPM): the least-toxic, most-effective escalation.
- Identify and respond to the region's most common pests and diseases.
- Prevent most problems through the healthy-garden practices you've already learned.

### Lecture

**Most "pest problems" are really stress problems.** A vigorous plant in the right place, in good soil, with airflow and proper water, shrugs off pests that flatten a stressed neighbor. **Prevention is 80% of pest control,** and you've already learned it: right plant/right place, healthy soil, good spacing/airflow, morning watering at the roots, diversity (Week 3). Pesticides are the *last* resort, not the first, and broad-spectrum sprays kill the pollinators and the beneficial predators (ladybugs, lacewings, parasitic wasps, birds) that were controlling pests for free.

**Integrated Pest Management (IPM): the escalation ladder.** Always climb from the bottom:
1. **Identify first.** Don't spray a mystery. Is it even a pest? (Most insects are harmless or beneficial.) Penn State Extension's diagnostic resources and Master Gardener hotline exist for exactly this.
2. **Set a threshold.** A few chewed leaves is not an emergency; a garden is an ecosystem, not an operating room. Act when damage threatens the plant's health or yield, not at the first nibble.
3. **Cultural controls:** right plant/place, healthy soil, sanitation (remove diseased debris), crop rotation, resistant varieties.
4. **Physical/mechanical:** hand-pick (drop Japanese beetles and hornworms in soapy water), row covers, barriers, traps, blast aphids off with a hose, fencing for deer.
5. **Biological:** protect and invite predators; use targeted products like *Bt* (a bacterium that kills only caterpillars) or beneficial nematodes for grubs.
6. **Chemical, last and targeted:** choose the least-toxic effective option (insecticidal soap, horticultural oil, neem) before synthetics; spot-treat, never blanket-spray; **never spray open flowers** (you'll kill bees); and **follow the label exactly; it is a legal document.**

**The Pittsburgh usual suspects:**

| Pest | Signs | First-line response |
|---|---|---|
| **Japanese beetles** | Metallic beetles skeletonizing leaves (roses, beans, grapes) in June–Aug; grubs chew lawn roots | Hand-pick into soapy water (morning); don't hang lure traps (they attract *more*); treat lawn grubs only if damaging |
| **Slugs & snails** | Ragged holes, slime trails; worst in wet clay/shade (very Pittsburgh) | Reduce moisture/mulch near victims, hand-pick at night, beer traps, iron-phosphate bait, copper barriers |
| **Aphids** | Clusters on new growth; sticky "honeydew," curled leaves | Hose off; insecticidal soap; invite ladybugs; usually self-corrects |
| **Tomato hornworm** | Big green caterpillars stripping tomato plants | Hand-pick; *Bt*; leave ones with white wasp cocoons (parasitized; free pest control) |
| **Squash bugs / vine borers** | Wilting squash/zucchini; eggs on leaf undersides | Row covers until flowering, hand-pick eggs, crop rotation |
| **Deer & groundhogs** | Whole plants/tops grazed overnight (suburbs especially) | Fencing (the only reliable fix), repellents, deer-resistant natives |
| **Spotted lanternfly** | Showy planthopper (gray/red wings); swarms on trees, esp. tree-of-heaven; honeydew + sooty mold | Scrape egg masses (fall–spring), squash adults; report; it's an invasive under PA quarantine in Allegheny Co. |

**Common diseases (mostly fungal; our humid summers love them):**

| Disease | Signs | Management |
|---|---|---|
| **Powdery mildew** | White powder on leaves (lilac, phlox, squash, bee balm) | Airflow/spacing, sun, resistant varieties, morning watering at roots; not usually fatal |
| **Black spot / leaf spots** | Black/brown spots, yellowing, leaf drop (roses) | Sanitation (remove fallen leaves), airflow, resistant varieties, avoid wetting foliage |
| **Late blight / early blight** | Spots, browning, collapse on tomatoes/potatoes | Rotate crops, space, mulch (stops soil splash), water at base, remove infected plants |
| **Root rot** | Wilting in *wet* soil, mushy brown roots | A drainage problem, not a spray problem; fix the soil/water (Day 4/10) |

**The prevention checklist that beats most disease:** space for airflow, water at the base in the morning, mulch to stop soil splash, clean up diseased debris (don't compost it), rotate annual veggies, and choose resistant/native varieties. Notice these are all things you already know; disease control is mostly just good gardening.

### Examples

**Example 12.1: IPM on Japanese beetles.** Beetles appear on the roses in late June. The panic move is a broad insecticide, which also kills the bees on the blooms and the predators. The IPM move: knock beetles into soapy water each morning (they're sluggish then), accept some leaf damage (threshold thinking), skip the pheromone traps (they lure beetles from the whole block into your yard), and keep the roses vigorous so they outgrow the damage. Free, effective, pollinator-safe.

**Example 12.2: Reading wilt as drainage, not pest.** A pepper wilts and yellows; a beginner suspects bugs and sprays. But the soil is soggy and the roots are brown; it's **root rot from overwatered clay**, and no insecticide will help. The fix is drainage and restraint with the hose. *Identify before you treat*; half of "pest" problems are really culture problems.

### Lab

1. **(15 min)** Do a **scouting walk**: inspect leaf tops *and undersides* on several plants. Note any pests/eggs/damage. Practice the IPM first step (*identify*) before deciding anything's wrong.
2. **(15 min)** Build your **prevention checklist** for your garden (spacing, base watering, mulch, sanitation, resistant varieties) and check off what you already do vs. need to start.
3. **(15 min)** Pick the **two pests/diseases most likely** for your plants (e.g., Japanese beetles on roses, powdery mildew on phlox, slugs in a shady clay bed) and write the IPM response ladder for each.
4. **🌱/off-season (10 min)** Hand-pick a real pest or scrape a lanternfly egg mass if present. *Off-season:* identify overwintering sites to clean up (diseased leaf litter, egg masses) and plan spring sanitation.
5. ★ Locate the Penn State Extension Master Gardener hotline / plant diagnostic clinic for Allegheny County and save the contact; your lifeline for "what is eating my plant?"
