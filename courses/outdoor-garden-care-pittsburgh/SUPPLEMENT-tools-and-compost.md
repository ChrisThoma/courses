# Part 1: Tools & Safety *(Day 7, ~45 min)*

## The short, high-quality kit

You need far fewer tools than the garden center wants to sell you. **Buy fewer, better tools and maintain them**: a good tool used for decades beats a drawer of cheap ones. The essential kit:

| Tool | Job | Buying note |
|---|---|---|
| **Hand trowel** | Planting, transplanting small plants | Get a forged one-piece stainless; cheap ones bend on clay |
| **Bypass pruners** | Cutting live stems up to ~½ in | *Bypass* (scissor-action), not anvil; keep them sharp. The most-used tool you own |
| **Loppers** | Branches ½–1½ in | Long handles = leverage for our shrubs |
| **Pruning saw** | Branches over 1½ in | A folding saw covers most homeowner needs |
| **Round-point shovel / spade** | Digging holes, edging, moving soil | A spade (flat) for edging/dividing; a round shovel for digging |
| **Digging/garden fork** | Loosening clay, turning compost, lifting perennials | Better than a tiller for clay: no seed-bank disturbance |
| **Steel rake & leaf rake** | Leveling soil / gathering leaves | The leaf rake feeds your compost pile |
| **Hoe** | Slicing off annual weed seedlings | A sharp stirrup/hoop hoe is the weeder's secret weapon |
| **Garden hose + nozzle / soaker hose** | Watering at the base | Soaker/drip beats overhead sprinklers (disease) |
| **Gloves** | Everything, especially weeds and thorns | Get a pair that fits; you'll wear them constantly |
| **Watering can** | Containers, new transplants, seedlings | One with a removable "rose" for a gentle shower |
| **Wheelbarrow / garden cart** | Moving soil, mulch, compost, debris | Pittsburgh slopes make a cart with two wheels safer |

*Nice-to-have later:* a soil knife (hori-hori), a kneeling pad (your knees on clay will thank you), a sturdy garden kneeler/bench, a rain gauge, and a quality long-handled bulb planter.

## Tool care (makes cheap tools last and good tools immortal)

1. **Keep cutting tools sharp.** A sharp pruner/hoe cuts cleanly (better for the plant, easier for you); a dull one crushes and tears, inviting disease. Touch up blades with a file or sharpening stone.
2. **Clean after use.** Knock off soil; wipe blades. **Disinfect pruners with alcohol** between diseased plants so you don't spread infection (recall Day 11).
3. **Prevent rust.** Dry metal after use; wipe with a lightly oiled rag. The classic trick: a bucket of sand with a little oil mixed in; plunge tools in a few times to clean and oil at once.
4. **Care for wood handles** with a wipe of linseed oil seasonally so they don't dry and splinter.
5. **Winterize** (Day 19 put-to-bed): clean, sharpen, and oil everything; drain and store hoses out of the freeze; store hand tools dry.

## Garden safety: non-negotiable

- **Body mechanics:** lift with your legs, not your back (soil and mulch bags are heavier than they look); kneel rather than stoop; alternate tasks to avoid repetitive strain. Pittsburgh's hills add a fall risk; mind your footing on slopes and wet clay.
- **Sun & heat:** hat, sunscreen, water; garden in the cooler morning/evening in summer.
- **Ticks:** real in our region; wear long pants in tall grass/brush, do a tick check after, and know the Lyme-disease symptoms.
- **Poison ivy** (full treatment Day 15): "leaves of three, let it be"; long sleeves and gloves to remove; wash skin with soap and cool water within hours; **never burn it.**
- **Tools:** carry sharp tools pointed down; never leave a rake tines-up; keep pruners locked when not in use; mind power equipment.
- **Chemicals:** store pesticides/fertilizers in original labeled containers, away from kids/pets; wear the protective equipment the label specifies; **the label is the law.**
- **Tetanus:** keep your shot current; you're working in soil with sharp objects.

**Lab (Day 7):** Inventory the tools you own against the essential kit; note gaps. Then **clean, dry, and oil** your cutting tools and **sharpen** at least your pruners and a hoe. Walk your property for tick habitat and poison-ivy risk and note both.

# Part 2: Composting Deep-Dive *(Day 14, ~45 min)*

## Why compost is the heart of this course

For two weeks you've heard one prescription over and over: **add organic matter.** It builds clay structure (Day 2), improves drainage *and* water-holding (Day 4), feeds the soil food web and supplies slow-release nutrients (Days 2–3), tops-dresses beds and lawns, and makes the best mulch and the best planting amendment. **Compost is all of that, and you can make it for free from material you currently throw away.** Composting closes the loop: your "waste" becomes the engine of your healthy garden, and you stop buying what your yard produces.

## How composting works (the simple science)

Composting is **managed decomposition**: you give soil microbes and decomposers the conditions to break organic matter into dark, crumbly, sweet-smelling **humus**. Get four things right and it's nearly automatic:

1. **Greens (Nitrogen):** fresh, wet, "alive" material: kitchen scraps (fruit/veg peels, coffee grounds, eggshells), fresh grass clippings, green plant trimmings.
2. **Browns (Carbon):** dry, dead material: fallen leaves (Pittsburgh's autumn gives you a *mountain* of these; hoard them), straw, shredded paper/cardboard, woody bits.
3. **Air (Oxygen):** turn the pile (or otherwise aerate it) so it decomposes *aerobically*: fast and odorless. A pile starved of air goes anaerobic: slow and smelly.
4. **Water:** keep it as moist as a **wrung-out sponge**: damp, not soggy. Our rainfall usually handles this; cover if it gets waterlogged.

**The ratio that matters: aim for roughly 2–3 parts browns to 1 part greens by volume.** Too many greens (all grass clippings) → wet, smelly, slimy. Too many browns (all leaves) → dry, just sits there for years. When in doubt, **add more browns**, which is exactly why you bank those autumn leaves.

## What goes in, and what stays out

**Yes:** fruit & vegetable scraps, coffee grounds + filters, tea, eggshells (crushed), fallen leaves, grass clippings (in moderation, and only if not recently herbicide-treated), straw, shredded plain paper/cardboard, plant trimmings, spent potting soil.

**No:** **meat, fish, dairy, fats/oils** (smell, pests, slow rot); **pet waste** (dog/cat: pathogens); **diseased plants** (you'd spread the disease; trash these, per Day 12); **weeds that have gone to seed** and **perennial/invasive weed roots, especially Japanese knotweed and garlic mustard** (a home pile rarely gets hot enough to kill them; you'd replant your weeds; recall Days 15–16); **anything treated with herbicide/persistent pesticide;** glossy/coated paper; and "compostable" plastics (they need industrial facilities).

## Methods: pick your scale

- **Cold (passive) pile/bin:** heap browns and greens, ignore it, turn occasionally. Easiest; takes ~6–12 months. Fine for most homeowners. A simple bin (wire, pallets, or a purchased enclosed bin; the latter is tidier and more critter-resistant for a city yard) keeps it neat.
- **Hot (active) composting:** a large pile (~3×3×3 ft) with a balanced mix, kept moist and **turned regularly**, heats up (the microbes generate heat) and finishes in weeks to a couple months. The heat can kill some weed seeds and pathogens. More effort, faster, better product.
- **Tumbler:** an enclosed barrel you spin to aerate: clean, fast-ish, rodent-proof, good for small city lots. Lower capacity.
- **Vermicomposting (worm bin):** red wigglers turn kitchen scraps into rich castings: perfect for **apartments, patios, or winter** (kept indoors/in a basement/garage). No yard required.
- **Leaf mold:** just pile fallen leaves (or bag them, moisten, poke holes) and wait 1–2 years → crumbly black gold, the best soil conditioner and seed-starting amendment there is. Effortless, given Pittsburgh's leaf supply.
- **"Leave the leaves" / mulch-mowing:** the lowest-effort option: mow leaves into the lawn (free fertilizer) and rake the rest onto beds as mulch (Day 18's "leave the leaves" for overwintering insects). Composting in place.

## Troubleshooting

| Problem | Cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| **Smells bad (rotten/ammonia)** | Too wet / too many greens / no air | Add browns, turn it, aerate |
| **Not breaking down** | Too dry / too many browns / pieces too big | Add greens + water, chop material smaller, turn |
| **Pests (rodents/flies)** | Meat/dairy in pile, or scraps exposed | Remove those, bury scraps in the center, use an enclosed bin/tumbler |
| **Slimy, matted** | Grass clippings clumped, anaerobic | Mix in browns, break up clumps, turn |

## Using finished compost

It's ready when it's **dark, crumbly, and smells like forest floor**: original ingredients no longer recognizable. Use it to:
- **Top-dress beds** (½–1 in annually): the durable clay-structure fix.
- **Amend planting areas** (work into the *bed*, not the single hole; Day 9).
- **Top-dress the lawn** after fall aeration (Day 17).
- **Brew compost or make a thin mulch** for containers and new transplants.

You will never buy what your own yard and kitchen can make, and your soil, your plants, and Pittsburgh's overflowing yard-waste stream are all better for it.

**Lab (Day 14):** Choose a composting method that fits your space (yard pile/bin, tumbler, or worm bin for no yard). Identify your **greens** and **browns** supply (and start hoarding leaves in fall). If in season, **start a pile** with a rough 2–3:1 browns:greens mix, moistened. *Off-season:* set up the bin and begin collecting kitchen scraps (freeze them, or start a worm bin indoors) so you have material ready. Write your method, location, and ingredient sources into your garden journal.
