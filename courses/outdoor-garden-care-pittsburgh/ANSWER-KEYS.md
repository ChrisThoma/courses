## Quiz 1: Foundations (40 pts)

### Part A: Site & climate (10)
1. **Zone 6b**; average annual minimum **−5 °F to 0 °F**. (Day 1)
2. The **urban heat island**: pavement and buildings store heat, so the core behaves like **7a**. (Day 1)
3. **Last spring frost (~May 15)** and **first fall frost (~Oct 15)**. (Day 1)
4. It is **not winter-hardy here**: grow it as an annual or bring it indoors; it won't survive a Pittsburgh winter outdoors. (Day 1)
5. **Full sun = 6+ hrs** direct; **part sun/shade = 3–6 hrs**; **full shade = under 3 hrs**. (Day 1)
6. **South/west** = warmer, sunnier, drier (bakes in summer); **north/east** = cooler, shadier, holds moisture, slower to warm. (Day 1)
7. Warm: a **south-facing wall** (or where snow melts first). Cold: a **low spot/frost pocket** (or where snow lingers). (Day 1)
8. A **late frost (normal into mid-May)** will kill tender plants; warm April days are a trap. (Day 1)
9. Any two: **heat tolerance, humidity, soil, rainfall, frost timing, sun.** (Day 1)
10. The bottom of a slope tends to be **wet**; under the eaves tends to be **dry** (rain shadow). (Day 1)

### Part B: Soil (12)
1. **Sand, silt, clay.** (Day 2)
2. **Clay.** (Day 2)
3. **Heavy/dense clay (often compacted, with construction fill);** consequence: slow drainage / soggy roots / late to warm / bakes hard (any one). (Day 2)
4. **Texture** = the particle-size mix (fixed); **structure** = how particles clump into crumbs with pore space (**changeable**). (Day 2)
5. **Organic matter (compost)**: it glues clay into crumbs, opening pore space for air and water. (Day 2)
6. Build healthy living soil with organic matter and let the soil food web feed the plant, rather than feeding the plant chemically. (Day 2)
7. pH controls **nutrient availability**; most plants want **6.0–7.0**. (Day 3)
8. Usually **acidic (low)**; add **lime** to raise it. (Day 3)
9. **N (nitrogen) – P (phosphorus) – K (potassium).** (Day 3)
10. Leafy growth = **N**; flowers/fruit/roots = **P**. (Day 3)
11. Any two: test for lead; use **raised beds with clean soil**; add organic matter; mulch to stop dust; wash produce/peel roots; favor fruiting over leafy/root crops; keep beds away from foundations; wear gloves. (Day 3)
12. They're **notoriously inaccurate**; use a proper lab test (Penn State). (Day 3)

### Part C: Water & mulch (10)
1. Clay holds lots of **water** but little **air** (waterlogged → suffocated roots), yet baked dry it sheds water and cracks. (Day 4)
2. Excess water moves **down and away within a few hours**, leaving soil **moist but aerated**. (Day 4)
3. Water **deeply** (to 6–8 in) and **infrequently** (let the top inch or two dry); target **~1 inch per week** (rain + irrigation). (Day 4)
4. Wet foliage overnight **breeds fungal disease**; base watering gets water to roots and keeps leaves dry. (Day 4)
5. **Finger test**: push a finger ~2 in into the soil; water only if it's dry. (Day 4)
6. Any four: conserves moisture, suppresses weeds, moderates soil temperature, prevents erosion/crusting, feeds the soil as it breaks down. (Day 4)
7. Mulch against stems/trunks **rots the bark and invites disease/pests** (the "mulch volcano"). (Day 4)
8. **Adding sand to clay** (makes it concrete-like) and **gravel in the bottom of a planting hole** (worsens drainage). (Day 4)
9. Working **wet clay smears it into dense clods** and destroys structure for a season. (Day 4)
10. **~38 inches/year**, fairly evenly spread; nature waters a lot, so **overwatering** is a common killer. (Day 4)

### Part D: How plants live (8)
1. The plant **makes its own food (sugars) via photosynthesis** from **sunlight + CO₂ + water.** (Day 5)
2. Light powers food production, which nothing replaces; **fertilizer only supplies mineral nutrients (like vitamins),** not food. (Day 5)
3. Roots **respire**: waterlogged soil starves them of oxygen and they rot. (Day 5)
4. **Transpiration** outruns root uptake in heat/wind; the plant recovers when it cools. (Day 5)
5. **Annual** = one season; **perennial** = many years (may die back and return); **biennial** = two-year cycle (leaves yr 1, flowers/seeds yr 2). (Day 5)
6. e.g., Annual: tomato/marigold. Perennial: coneflower/any tree. Biennial: foxglove/garlic mustard. (Day 5)
7. Match a plant's needs (light, water, soil, hardiness, size) to a spot that supplies them; it pre-solves most problems and is the master principle. (Day 5)
8. **Insufficient light**: too little sun to photosynthesize enough sugar to set fruit; the fix is relocation, not a product. (Day 5)

## Quiz 2: Plant Care (40 pts)

### Part A: Choosing & planting (10)
1. Perennials **return for years** with deep roots (low-maintenance structure); annuals give **long seasonal color** but more work, replanted yearly. (Day 8)
2. Any three: adapted to our **zone/clay/rainfall** (less water/fertilizer/fuss), **pest/disease resistant** here, and the **foundation of the food web** (support pollinators/caterpillars/birds). (Day 8)
3. **Mature size (H×W)**: ignoring it leads to crowded, mildewed, overgrown plants scraping the house. (Day 8)
4. Any two: burning bush, Japanese barberry, callery/Bradford pear, English ivy, Norway maple, butterfly bush, vinca, miscanthus. (Day 8)
5. Tender crops **after last frost (~May 15)**; trees best in **fall** (warm soil, cool air, fall rain). (Day 9)
6. **2–3× as wide as the rootball, no deeper than it is tall**, top of rootball at/just above grade; deep-and-narrow makes a water-collecting "bathtub" and lets roots circle. (Day 9)
7. Burying the rootball/trunk flare below grade: it **suffocates roots and rots the stem/trunk.** (Day 9)
8. Gradually acclimating indoor/greenhouse plants to outdoor sun/wind over **7–10 days** to avoid scorch/shock. (Day 9)
9. Correct spacing is by **mature width** for airflow (disease prevention) and no competition, so new beds look sparse. (Day 8/9)
10. **Early spring or fall** (cool, dormant): less heat stress while roots re-establish. (Day 9)

### Part B: Watering & feeding (10)
1. New plantings have **tiny roots** and need **consistent moisture** the first season; established plants are watered only in dry spells. (Day 10)
2. Limited soil **dries fast**: pots may need **daily** water in heat, until it runs from the drainage holes. (Day 10)
3. Check the **soil**, not the leaves: dry 2 in down = under; soggy/sour with mushy brown roots = over (and watering makes it worse). (Day 10)
4. **Overwatering/poor drainage**: our wet clay drowns roots more often than drought. (Day 10)
5. **Test the soil** (and build it with compost first), so you only feed what's actually missing and don't over-apply. (Day 10)
6. Any three: weak floppy growth, pest-prone (e.g., aphids), root burn, wasted money, **river/water pollution** from excess P/N. (Day 10)
7. **Clay holds P and K tightly** (high cation exchange), so they're rarely depleted. (Day 3/10)
8. **Organic:** slow-release, feeds the soil web, hard to overdose. **Synthetic:** fast, precise for a diagnosed deficiency/containers. (Day 10)
9. It **pushes tender new growth that winter kills**; plants need to harden off. (Day 10)
10. **Little or none**: compost/healthy soil feeds it indefinitely; less is more with natives. (Day 10)

### Part C: Pruning (10)
1. **Dead, Damaged, Diseased** wood, removable **any time, any plant.** (Day 11)
2. **Spring bloomers: prune *after* they flower; summer bloomers: prune *before* (late winter/early spring).** (Day 11)
3. Prune **right after it blooms** (early summer); pruning in early spring **removes the flower buds set last year** → no blooms. (Day 11)
4. **Late winter / early spring** while dormant (it blooms on new wood). (Day 11)
5. Cut **just above an outward-facing bud or back to a branch/the branch collar**: no long stub, no flush cut. (Day 11)
6. Any two: leave a **stub**, cut **flush** into the trunk, or **top** the tree. (Day 11)
7. To avoid **spreading disease** between plants. (Day 11)
8. Redirects energy from seed-making into **more blooms** (and tidies the plant). (Day 11)
9. When you want **seedheads for birds/winter interest** (coneflower, grasses, etc.). (Day 11)
10. Nipping growing tips of young plants to force **bushier, fuller growth.** (Day 11)

### Part D: Pests & disease / IPM (10)
1. **Identify → set a threshold → cultural → physical/mechanical → biological → chemical (last, targeted).** (Day 12)
2. They **kill pollinators and the beneficial predators** that control pests for free, often making things worse. (Day 12)
3. **Hand-pick into soapy water** (mornings); pheromone **traps lure more beetles** from the whole area into your yard. (Day 12)
4. Act on **damage that threatens health/yield**, not at the first nibble; a few chewed leaves is fine. (Day 12)
5. **Identify it** (it may be harmless or beneficial). (Day 12)
6. Any three: **spacing/airflow, water at the base in the morning, mulch to stop soil splash, sanitation, crop rotation, resistant/native varieties.** (Day 12)
7. **Culture problem (root rot from overwatered clay)**: fix drainage/watering; no insecticide helps. (Day 12)
8. An **invasive planthopper** (under PA quarantine in Allegheny Co.); **scrape and destroy egg masses** (fall–spring), squash adults, report it. (Day 12)
9. You'll **kill bees/pollinators** visiting the flowers. (Day 12)
10. The pesticide **label is legally binding**: follow its rates, timing, protective equipment, and approved uses exactly. (Day 12)

## Final Exam (60 pts)

### Part A: Foundations recall (10)
1. **Zone 6b, −5 to 0 °F.** 2. **~May 15 and ~Oct 15.** 3. **Structure**, with **organic matter/compost.** 4. Feed the living soil and let it feed the plant. 5. **6.0–7.0.** 6. Deep & infrequent, **~1 inch/week.** 7. Any four: conserves moisture, suppresses weeds, moderates temperature, prevents erosion, feeds soil. 8. It **powers photosynthesis** (the plant's only food source). 9. Lives **many years**; e.g., coneflower/any tree. 10. Match the plant's needs to a spot that supplies them.

### Part B: Plant care (10)
1. **Fall**: warm soil + cool air + fall rain let roots establish without heat stress. 2. **2–3× as wide as the rootball, no deeper than it is tall,** rootball at/just above grade. 3. Check the **soil**: dry = under, soggy with mushy roots = over. 4. **Test/build the soil** (feed only what's missing). 5. **Spring bloomers prune after flowering; summer bloomers prune before (late winter).** 6. **Right after it blooms**: early-spring pruning removes its flower buds. 7. Any three: adapted to our conditions, pest/disease resistant, support the food web/pollinators. 8. Any two: burning bush, barberry, callery pear, English ivy, Norway maple, butterfly bush, vinca. 9. Gradually acclimating tender plants to the outdoors over ~7–10 days. 10. It pushes tender growth that winter kills.

### Part C: Weeds & invasives (12)
1. **A plant where you don't want it**: it's a human value judgment, not a botanical category. 2. **Annuals: stop seed**; **perennials: remove the whole root/rhizome** (cutting tops isn't enough). 3. The soil's **reservoir of dormant seeds**; implies **don't dig/till more than necessary** and **never let weeds set seed.** 4. **seven.** 5. **"Leaves of three"** (compound, three leaflets), vine/shrub/ground form; never burn it because **inhaling urushiol smoke is dangerous.** 6. **Mulch**: it blocks the light seeds need and keeps the seed bank buried. 7. **Hand-pull perennials/taproots from moist soil** (to get the whole root); **hoe annual seedlings on a dry day.** 8. **Pre-emergent stops seeds from germinating; post-emergent kills growing weeds.** 9. **Selective spares grass/desirable plants; non-selective kills everything green.** 10. Fragments **resprout**; you'd spread it; a home pile won't kill it. 11. **Late summer/early fall**, when the plant moves resources **down to the roots**, so herbicide reaches the rhizome. 12. **Hand-pull before it seeds, bag and trash it, repeat a few years**: it's shallow-rooted and reproduces **only by seed**, so you can drain its seed bank. (Days 15–16)

### Part D: The garden system (12)
1. **Cool-season grass;** summer browning is usually **dormancy, not death.** 2. **Fall**: cool air + warm soil + rain make it prime for seeding/feeding/aerating. 3. **Mow high (3–3.5 in), feed modestly (mostly fall), and fix the cause of thin/bare spots.** 4. **Early-mid spring as soil warms, "when the forsythia blooms."** 5. Any two: less mowing/water/fertilizer, fewer chemicals in rivers, more pollinators/birds, handles un-mowable slopes, more interest. 6. Diversity makes the garden **resilient**: pests/diseases can't sweep through and predators always have habitat. 7. Any four: plant **natives**, **bloom spring→fall**, plant in **drifts**, include **host plants**, go **pesticide-free**, provide **water/shelter**. 8. Canopy → understory → shrubs → perennials/grasses → ground cover; the **dense ground layer shades the soil** (living mulch) so weed seeds get no light. 9. It's the **best planting/lawn-renovation season**; two of: plant trees/shrubs/perennials, renovate lawn, plant bulbs, treat knotweed, "leave the leaves." 10. Any two: **major woody pruning, fertilizing woody plants, scalping the lawn, tilling** (all spur tender growth or weeds at the wrong time). 11. **Keeping a garden journal.** 12. Good soil → right plants → diversity → pollinators/predators move in → pests & weeds suppressed without sprays → less work, more life → organic matter feeds the soil → back to good soil. (Days 17–19)

### Part E: Integrated diagnosis & planning (16)
1. **(3)** Two issues: the bed **puddles (poor clay drainage)** → improve with organic matter / **raised bed**, water deeply but check first; **AND** "tall, pale, fruitless" signals **too little sun** → move tomatoes to a full-sun (6+ hr) spot. 3. **(2)** Lilac is a **spring bloomer** that sets buds the previous year; it's likely being **pruned at the wrong time (winter/early spring), removing the buds**; prune **right after it flowers** instead (also check it has enough sun). (Day 11)
4. **(3)** **Japanese knotweed.** Plan: **repeated cutting through the season** to exhaust roots and/or **targeted herbicide in early fall**; **bag and trash** all material (never compost/yard waste); expect a **multi-year** effort, possibly a pro. (Day 16)
5. **(2)** **Test the soil for lead** (old house/foundation, 1925 lead paint era); if elevated, grow food in **raised beds with clean imported soil** away from the foundation, add organic matter, mulch, and favor fruiting crops / wash produce. (Day 3)
6. **(3)** Any three: **add organic matter/compost (annually, top-dress), mulch, avoid working/tilling it wet, build raised beds, lime per soil test, plant deep-rooted plants / don't compact it.** (Days 2–4)
