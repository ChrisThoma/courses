# Week 1: Foundations (Site, Soil, Water, and How Plants Live)

> **The thesis of this week:** a garden is a *place* before it's a collection of plants. Before you buy a single plant, you must know your light, your frost dates, your slope, and above all your soil. Ninety percent of "my plant died" in Pittsburgh traces back to a site-and-soil mismatch that was knowable in advance. This week you learn to know it.

---

# Day 1: Your Pittsburgh Garden (Zone, Frost, Sun, and Microclimates)

### Objectives
- State your USDA hardiness zone and what it does (and doesn't) tell you.
- Know Pittsburgh's average frost dates and growing-season length, and why you don't plant tomatoes in April here.
- Map the sun, slope, and microclimates of your own site.

### Lecture

**The number to know first: your hardiness zone.** The USDA Plant Hardiness Zone map divides the country by **average annual minimum winter temperature**, the coldest it typically gets. Pittsburgh and most of Allegheny County sit in **Zone 6b**, meaning average winter lows of **−5 °F to 0 °F**. The urban core and some sheltered river valleys, warmed by pavement and buildings (the "urban heat island"), behave like **Zone 7a** (0 to +5 °F). The 2023 update to the map nudged much of the region warmer than the old 6a designation many older books still cite.

What the zone tells you: **whether a perennial, shrub, or tree will survive your winter.** A plant rated "hardy to Zone 5" will sail through a Pittsburgh winter; one rated "Zone 7–10" (most "tropical" patio plants) will not survive outdoors and must be treated as an annual or brought inside.

What the zone does **not** tell you: heat tolerance, summer humidity, soil, rainfall, or *when* your frosts come. Zone is a floor, not a forecast. Two more numbers do the rest:

**Frost dates and the growing season.** Pittsburgh's frost-free window runs roughly:

```
Last spring frost (average):   ~late April – mid-May   (plan around MAY 15)
First fall frost (average):    ~mid-October            (plan around OCT 15)
Frost-free growing season:     ~155–180 days
```

The practical rule every Pittsburgh gardener lives by: **do not put out frost-tender plants (tomatoes, peppers, basil, most annual flowers) until after Mother's Day / mid-May.** A warm spell in April is a trap: a single late frost will kill tender transplants overnight. Cool-season crops (peas, lettuce, spinach, pansies) are different; they *want* the cool weather and go out weeks earlier.

**Sun: the second site number.** Plants are sold by light requirement, defined by hours of direct sun:

| Label | Direct sun per day |
|---|---|
| Full sun | 6+ hours |
| Part sun / part shade | 3–6 hours |
| Full shade | < 3 hours (or dappled all day) |

Most vegetables and most flowering perennials want **full sun.** Pittsburgh complicates this: we are a hilly, tree-rich city, and a north-facing slope or a neighbor's mature oak can cut a "sunny" yard to part shade. You must *measure*, not assume.

**Slope and aspect.** On Pittsburgh's hills, the direction a slope faces (its **aspect**) changes everything:
- **South- and west-facing** slopes are warmer, sunnier, and drier: earliest to thaw, best for sun-lovers, but they bake in July.
- **North- and east-facing** slopes are cooler, shadier, and hold moisture longer: kinder to shade plants, slower to warm in spring.
- **Slopes drain and erode.** Water (and topsoil) runs downhill; the bottom of a slope stays wet, the top stays dry.

**Microclimates: your yard is not one place.** A microclimate is a small area whose conditions differ from the general climate. Learn to spot yours:
- A **south-facing wall** stores heat and can push a spot a half-zone warmer: where you put the fig that "shouldn't" survive here.
- A **low spot or hollow** collects cold air ("frost pocket") and frosts first and last.
- **Under the eaves** stays bone dry (rain shadow); the **downspout outlet** stays soggy.
- The **windward** (usually west/northwest in Pittsburgh) side dries and stresses plants.

### Examples

**Example 1.1: Reading a plant tag against your site.** A tag says: *"Echinacea 'Magnus': Full sun · Zones 3–8 · Height 30–36 in."* Decode: survives your winter easily (3–8 includes 6b ✓); needs 6+ hrs sun (so not under that oak ✗); will end up knee-to-thigh high (put it mid-border, not the front edge). Match made before purchase.

**Example 1.2: A sun map.** On a sunny day, photograph or sketch a bed at 9 a.m., noon, and 4 p.m. Where is it in sun in all three? That's full sun. In sun at noon only? Part sun. Always shaded? Full shade; stop trying to grow tomatoes there.

### Lab

1. **(15 min)** Confirm your zone: look up "USDA hardiness zone" for your ZIP code. Write down your zone and its minimum-temperature range. Note whether you're in a likely 7a heat-island pocket.
2. **(15 min)** Write your frost dates and count your growing-season days. Put **"safe planting ~May 15"** and **"first frost ~Oct 15"** on your real calendar.
3. **🌱/off-season (20 min)** Make a **sun map.** On a clear day, observe one bed at three times (morning/noon/afternoon) and label it full sun / part / shade. *Off-season:* use the sun's lower winter arc plus knowledge of which trees are deciduous to estimate summer light, and note where snow melts first (warm microclimates) and last (cold ones).
4. **(10 min)** Walk your whole property and mark, on a rough sketch, at least three microclimates (a hot wall, a wet low spot, a dry rain-shadow strip, a windy edge).
5. ★ Find your nearest Penn State Extension office (Allegheny County) and the regional National Weather Service frost/freeze info. Bookmark both.

2. What single date should anchor your planting of tender crops, and why?

# Day 2: Soil, Part 1 (Texture, Structure, and the Soil Food Web)

### Objectives
- Identify soil texture (sand/silt/clay) by hand, and know why western PA clay behaves as it does.
- Distinguish soil *texture* (fixed) from soil *structure* (improvable).
- Understand the soil food web and why "feed the soil, not the plant" is the idea everything else rests on.

### Lecture

**Soil is the garden.** Everything above ground is downstream of what's below it. Get the soil right and most plants thrive on neglect; get it wrong and no amount of fertilizer or fussing saves them.

**Texture: the mineral skeleton.** Mineral soil is a mix of three particle sizes:

| Particle | Size | Behavior |
|---|---|---|
| **Sand** | Large, gritty | Drains fast, warms fast, holds little water or nutrients |
| **Silt** | Medium, floury | Moderate everything; smooth when wet |
| **Clay** | Tiny, sticky | Holds water and nutrients tightly, drains slowly, compacts easily |

The blend is your **texture**, and it is essentially permanent: you can't truck in enough sand to change a clay soil's class, and trying just makes concrete. **Loam** (a balanced mix, ~40/40/20 sand/silt/clay) is the ideal, and the thing we build *toward* with organic matter, not by changing the mineral ratio.

**The Pittsburgh reality: heavy clay.** Western Pennsylvania soils, weathered from shale and sandstone, are typically **clay-rich, dense, and slow-draining**, and urban lots add **compaction and construction fill** on top. Clay's tiny, flat particles pack together and squeeze out the air. The consequences you'll fight all course:
- **Slow drainage** → soggy roots, root rot, and standing water after rain.
- **Compaction** → roots can't penetrate; water runs off instead of soaking in.
- **Late to warm** in spring (wet soil warms slowly) → don't rush spring planting.
- **Bakes hard** in summer drought → cracks, then sheds water.

But clay has a gift: it is **nutrient-rich.** Those same sticky particles hold onto plant nutrients (high "cation exchange capacity") that sandy soils let wash away. A well-managed clay soil is one of the most productive there is. The job is to improve its *structure*, not to replace it.

**Structure vs. texture, the key distinction.** *Texture* is the particle sizes (fixed). *Structure* is how those particles clump into crumbs, with pore spaces between them for air and water. **Structure is what you can improve, and the tool is organic matter.** Decomposed organic matter (compost, leaf mold, aged manure) glues clay particles into crumbs, opening up pores. Good structure in clay means it drains yet still holds water, and roots can run through it. You will hear this again and again: **add organic matter.** It is the answer to almost every Pittsburgh soil problem.

**The soil food web: the living half of soil.** Healthy soil is alive: billions of bacteria, fungi, protozoa, nematodes, plus earthworms and arthropods, per handful. This web:
- **Decomposes** organic matter into nutrients plants can absorb.
- **Builds structure** (fungal threads and earthworm tunnels create pores; microbial glues form crumbs).
- **Protects roots** (beneficial fungi, *mycorrhizae*, extend roots and trade nutrients for sugars; good microbes outcompete pathogens).

This is why the gardener's mantra is **"feed the soil, not the plant."** Synthetic fertilizer feeds the plant directly but does nothing for the food web, and overused it can harm it. Organic matter feeds the web, and the web feeds the plant, builds structure, and stores water. Tend the living soil and it tends your plants.

### Examples

**Example 2.1: The ribbon test (texture by hand).** Take moist soil, work it into a ball, and squeeze it out between thumb and forefinger into a ribbon:
- Won't form a ball, falls apart, feels gritty → **sandy.**
- Forms a ball; ribbon breaks under ~1 inch; feels smooth/floury → **silty / loam.**
- Forms a strong ball; ribbons 2+ inches before breaking; feels sticky; takes a polish when rubbed → **clay** (the usual Pittsburgh result).

**Example 2.2: The jar test (texture by settling).** Fill a clear jar 1/3 with soil, top with water and a drop of dish soap, shake hard, let settle 24–48 hrs. Sand drops in seconds, silt in minutes, clay over hours/days, leaving visible layers you can measure as percentages.

### Lab

1. **(20 min)** Do the **ribbon test** on soil from two different parts of your yard. Record your texture call for each. (Most Pittsburgh readers will write "clay" or "clay loam.")
2. **(10 min, then wait)** Set up a **jar test** from your main bed. Read it tomorrow and estimate your sand/silt/clay percentages from the layer thicknesses.
3. **(15 min)** Dig a hole one spade deep in a bed. Note: How hard was it to dig? Do you see earthworms (a sign of life and decent structure)? Is the soil crumbly or a solid mass? Is there a pale, compacted layer? Write it down.
4. **(10 min)** List every source of **organic matter** you could realistically get for free or cheap (fallen leaves, grass clippings, kitchen scraps, municipal compost, coffee grounds). This is your structure-building supply chain.
5. ★ Read your county's published soil survey description (Web Soil Survey) for your address; compare its prediction to what your hands found.

2. What's the difference between soil texture and soil structure, and which can you change?

# Day 3: Soil, Part 2 (The Soil Test, pH, Nutrients, and Urban-Soil Safety)

### Objectives
- Run and interpret a proper soil test (pH and nutrients).
- Understand pH and why it controls nutrient availability, and where Pittsburgh soils usually sit.
- Read an N-P-K analysis and know the role of each major nutrient.
- Apply urban-soil safety, especially lead, before growing food.

### Lecture

**Stop guessing and test.** You cannot see pH or nutrient levels. A **soil test** is the cheapest, highest-leverage thing you can do; it turns soil care from superstition into prescription. Do **not** rely on cheap big-box probe meters (notoriously inaccurate). Use the **Penn State Extension soil test kit**: order it online or pick one up at the Allegheny County Extension office, mail in a sample, and get a lab report with pH, nutrient levels, and specific recommendations for what you're growing. Cost is modest (about the price of two plants) and it's good for ~3 years.

**Taking a good sample** (garbage in, garbage out): with a clean trowel, take small slices 4–6 inches deep from 8–10 spots across the bed, mix them in a clean plastic bucket, and submit a cup of that blend. Sample lawns, veggie beds, and ornamental beds *separately*; they have different targets.

**pH: the master variable.** pH measures acidity/alkalinity on a 0–14 scale (7 = neutral, lower = acidic, higher = alkaline). It matters because **pH controls whether nutrients are available to roots.** Even a perfectly fertilized soil starves plants if the pH locks nutrients into forms roots can't absorb. Most garden plants want **pH 6.0–7.0** (slightly acidic to neutral), where the widest range of nutrients is available.

**Pittsburgh's tendency: acidic.** Western PA soils, and especially soils under conifers or in long-rained-on lots, often run **acidic (pH ~5.0–6.5)**, and our acid rain legacy hasn't helped. The fix:
- **Too acidic (low pH)?** Add **lime** (ground limestone) to raise it. The test tells you how much.
- **Too alkaline (high pH)?** Add **elemental sulfur** or organic matter to lower it (less common here, but happens near old concrete/foundations where lime leaches out).
- A few plants *want* acid: **blueberries, rhododendrons, azaleas, hollies, mountain laurel** (Pennsylvania's state flower) thrive at pH 4.5–5.5, which is why they do so well in our region and why you acidify *for them* specifically.

Change pH **slowly and by test**, because lime takes months to act and overshooting is worse than the original problem.

**N-P-K: the three macronutrients.** Fertilizer labels show three numbers, always in this order, **N-P-K**:

| Symbol | Nutrient | Drives | Deficiency sign |
|---|---|---|---|
| **N** | Nitrogen | Leafy green growth | Older leaves yellow; weak, pale, stunted growth |
| **P** | Phosphorus | Roots, flowers, fruit | Poor flowering/rooting; sometimes purplish leaves |
| **K** | Potassium | Overall vigor, stress/disease resistance | Yellow/brown leaf edges; weak stems |

So **10-10-10** is a balanced general fertilizer; **a "bloom" food** (e.g., 5-10-5) leans on phosphorus for flowers; **a lawn food** (e.g., 24-0-4) leans hard on nitrogen for leaf. Plus secondary nutrients (calcium, magnesium, sulfur) and micronutrients (iron, etc.) the test will flag if low. **Don't fertilize blind:** most established Pittsburgh clay is *not* short on phosphorus and potassium (clay holds them), so dumping balanced fertilizer can over-apply P, which pollutes our rivers. Test, then feed what's actually missing, usually just nitrogen and organic matter.

**Urban-soil safety, read before growing food.** Pittsburgh's industrial history and old housing stock mean some city soils carry contaminants, chiefly **lead** (from pre-1978 lead paint near old houses and from a century of leaded gasoline near busy roads). Lead in soil is a real health concern for children and for root/leaf crops.
- **Test for lead** (Penn State and other labs offer it as an add-on) if you'll grow vegetables, especially within ~10 ft of an old house's foundation/drip line, near old painted structures, or beside a busy road.
- **If lead is elevated:** grow food in **raised beds with clean imported soil**, keep beds away from foundations, add organic matter (binds lead, lowers availability), mulch to stop dust, **wash produce well**, peel root crops, and favor fruiting crops (tomatoes, peppers, beans) over leafy/root crops in questionable ground. Always wash hands and garden in gloves.

### Examples

**Example 3.1: Reading a soil report.** A report shows *pH 5.4, low nitrogen, optimum phosphorus, optimum potassium, recommend 50 lb lime/1000 sq ft and 1 lb actual N/1000 sq ft.* Translation: your soil is too acidic (typical) → lime it to nudge toward 6.5; it has plenty of P and K (typical clay) → **do not** add balanced 10-10-10; it just needs nitrogen and the lime. Buying a high-N or organic source and skipping the P/K is both cheaper and better for the rivers.

**Example 3.2: Diagnosing by leaf.** Lower, older leaves yellowing while new growth stays green points to **nitrogen** deficiency (N is mobile; the plant robs old leaves to feed new ones). Yellowing *between the veins of new* leaves points instead to iron/micronutrient lockout, often from pH that's too high, a clue to test, not to keep fertilizing.

### Lab

1. **(20 min)** **Order or prepare a Penn State soil test.** If in season, take a proper composite sample today and mail it. *Off-season:* fill out the form, order the kit, and write your sampling plan (which beds, separately) so you sample the moment the ground is workable.
2. **(10 min)** Read the jar test you started yesterday; record your texture percentages.
3. **(15 min)** Decide whether you need a **lead test**: walk your intended food-growing spots and note distance to the house foundation, any old painted structures, and roads. Mark any spot that warrants testing or a raised bed.
4. **(10 min)** Find one fertilizer (or compost/manure) product and read its **N-P-K**. Say out loud what each number would do for a plant.
5. ★ Calculate "1 lb actual nitrogen per 1000 sq ft" for a 24-0-4 product (hint: 1 ÷ 0.24 ≈ 4.2 lb of product). This is how pros translate a recommendation into a bag.

# Day 4: Water, Drainage, and Mulch on Clay

### Objectives
- Understand how water moves through soil and what "well-drained" actually means.
- Diagnose drainage and apply fixes suited to clay.
- Water correctly (deeply and infrequently) and use mulch as your most powerful single tool.

### Lecture

**The water paradox of clay.** Plants need water *and* air at their roots. Clay holds tons of water but little air; when waterlogged, roots literally suffocate and rot. Yet the same clay, baked dry, sheds water and cracks. Both extremes kill. The target is a soil that is **moist but not soggy**, holding water in its crumbs while keeping air in its pores, which, you'll notice, is exactly what **good structure (organic matter)** delivers. Most of "watering" in Pittsburgh is really about *drainage and structure*, not about adding more water.

**How water moves.** Rain or irrigation soaks down through pore spaces (infiltration). In sandy soil it races through; in compacted clay it barely enters and runs off the surface instead. Once in, water clings to particles; plants pull it from the crumbs. **"Well-drained" means excess water moves down and away within a few hours, leaving moist-but-aerated soil**: the condition most plant tags demand, and the one Pittsburgh clay fails by default.

**Diagnosing drainage: the perc test.** Dig a hole ~12 in deep and wide, fill with water, let it drain, fill again, and time the second drain:
- **< 2 in/hr (slow):** typical compacted clay; amend heavily or choose tolerant plants / raised beds.
- **2–6 in/hr:** good.
- **> 6 in/hr (fast):** sandy or raised; will need more frequent water.

**Fixing drainage in clay (in order of leverage):**
1. **Organic matter, always.** Compost worked in, and especially used as a top layer year after year, is the durable fix; it builds the crumb structure that lets clay drain *and* hold water.
2. **Raised beds / berms.** The fastest win on bad clay: build *up* with good soil and you sidestep the drainage problem entirely. Ideal for vegetables.
3. **Don't work wet clay.** Tilling or digging clay when it's wet smears it into dense clods and destroys structure for a season. Squeeze a handful: if it ribbons and shines, it's too wet; wait.
4. **Choose the right plant.** For chronically wet spots, plant things that *want* wet feet (see Day 18) rather than fighting physics.
5. **Avoid the "sand in clay" trap and the "gravel at the bottom of the hole" myth**: both make drainage *worse*, not better.

**Watering, done right: deep and infrequent.** The most common watering mistake is a little water every day, which wets only the surface and trains roots to stay shallow and vulnerable. Instead:
- **Water deeply** so moisture reaches 6–8 in down, encouraging deep, drought-tough roots.
- **Water infrequently**: let the top inch or two dry between waterings. Most established Pittsburgh plantings need water only in dry spells; we average ~38 inches of rain a year, fairly spread out, so *overwatering* is at least as common a killer as underwatering.
- **The rule of thumb: ~1 inch of water per week** (rain + irrigation combined), delivered in one or two deep soaks. Put a tuna can under the sprinkler to measure an inch.
- **Water the soil, not the leaves, and water in the morning.** Wet foliage overnight breeds fungal disease (a big deal in our humid summers). Drip hoses and soaker hoses beat overhead sprinklers for exactly this reason.
- **Check before you water:** push a finger 2 in into the soil. Moist? Don't water. Dry? Soak it. Your finger is a better sensor than the calendar.

**Mulch: the highest-return habit in the garden.** A 2–3 inch layer of organic mulch (shredded leaves, wood chips, straw, bark) over the soil surface, kept *off* stems and trunks, does an astonishing amount of work at once:
- **Conserves moisture** (cuts evaporation, so you water far less).
- **Suppresses weeds** (blocks the light weed seeds need; your Week 3 ally).
- **Moderates soil temperature** (cooler in summer, insulated in winter).
- **Prevents erosion and crusting** on slopes and clay.
- **Feeds the soil** as it breaks down, continuously building structure.

If you do only one thing from this course, mulch your beds.

### Examples

**Example 4.1: Deep vs. shallow watering.** Ten minutes of sprinkler daily wets ~½ inch and evaporates by noon → roots crowd the surface. One long soak weekly (the tuna-can inch) wets 6+ inches → roots dive deep and the plant shrugs off a dry week. Same total water, opposite outcome.

**Example 4.2: The mulch ring.** A young tree mulched in a wide, flat 3-inch ring (pulled back from the trunk, never a "mulch volcano" against the bark) keeps its roots cool and moist and its trunk free of weed-whacker damage and rot. The volcano shape, by contrast, rots the bark and invites disease, a mistake you'll see on half the street trees in town.

### Lab

1. **🌱/off-season (20 min)** Run a **perc test** on one bed and record the drainage rate. *Off-season (frozen ground):* instead, observe after the next rain/thaw where water puddles and lingers, and map those wet spots.
2. **(15 min)** Calibrate an "inch": set a tuna/cat-food can in range of your sprinkler (or note your hose's flow) and time how long it takes to collect 1 inch. Write that number down; it's your weekly soak time.
3. **(15 min)** **Mulch a bed** (or top up an existing layer to 2–3 in), keeping it off stems and trunks. *Off-season:* price and source mulch (free municipal wood chips/leaf compost are common around Pittsburgh) and stage it for spring.
4. **(10 min)** Do the squeeze test to judge whether your clay is workable today; practice recognizing "too wet."
5. ★ Sketch a simple raised-bed plan for your worst-draining spot (size, depth, soil to fill it).

# Day 5: How Plants Live (Roots, Leaves, Light, and "Right Plant, Right Place")

### Objectives
- Explain in plain terms how a plant feeds itself and what it needs from you.
- Connect plant biology to the practical care you'll learn in Week 2.
- State and apply gardening's master principle: right plant, right place.

### Lecture

**A plant is a solar-powered, self-feeding organism; mostly it just needs you to get out of its way.** Understand the machinery and every care decision becomes obvious.

**Photosynthesis: plants make their own food.** Leaves capture **sunlight** and use it to combine **carbon dioxide** (from air) and **water** (from roots) into **sugars** (the plant's food), releasing oxygen. This is why **light is non-negotiable**: a sun plant in shade is a factory with the power cut, and no fertilizer can substitute. (Fertilizer is *not* plant food: the plant makes its own food from light; fertilizer just supplies a few mineral nutrients, like vitamins.) That one fact explains Day 1's obsession with sun mapping.

**The parts and their jobs:**
- **Roots** anchor the plant and absorb water and dissolved nutrients. They need **air** as much as water (back to drainage). Most feeder roots are in the top 6–12 inches and spread out at least as wide as the plant, which is why you mulch and water *wide*, not just at the stem.
- **Leaves** are the solar panels (photosynthesis) and the plant's lungs/sweat glands: they release water vapor through tiny pores (**transpiration**), which pulls water up from the roots like a wick. On a hot, dry, windy Pittsburgh July afternoon, a plant can wilt simply because it's transpiring faster than its roots can drink, and recover by evening. (Wilting that *doesn't* recover overnight is a real problem: root rot or disease.)
- **Stems** hold the leaves to the light and plumb water up and sugars down.
- **Flowers** are the reproductive organs, and the pollinator magnet you'll build a whole garden around in Week 3. **Fruit and seed** follow pollination.

**What a plant needs from you, the whole list:**
1. **Light** appropriate to the species (you can't add it; you place the plant to get it).
2. **Water**: moist, not soggy; deep and infrequent.
3. **Air at the roots**: i.e., good drainage/structure.
4. **Nutrients**: mostly from healthy, organic-matter-rich soil; supplemented only as a test directs.
5. **Appropriate temperature**: i.e., a plant hardy and adapted to Zone 6b.
6. **Space**: room for roots and air for leaves (crowding breeds disease).

Notice what's *not* on the list: constant fussing. A plant matched to its place and given decent soil largely runs itself.

**Annuals, perennials, biennials: life-cycle literacy.** This vocabulary governs everything in Week 2:
- **Annual:** completes its whole life (seed → flower → seed → death) in one season. Tomatoes, basil, marigolds, petunias. In Pittsburgh, anything not hardy to Zone 6 is grown as an annual whether it "wants" to be or not.
- **Perennial:** lives many years; the top may die back each winter and return from the roots in spring. Coneflower, hosta, daylily, peony, and all trees and shrubs. **The backbone of a low-maintenance garden**: plant once, enjoy for years.
- **Biennial:** two-year cycle: leaves the first year, flowers/seeds the second, then dies. Foxglove, parsley, and the weed garlic mustard (Week 3).

**The principle that ties Week 1 together: RIGHT PLANT, RIGHT PLACE.** Match each plant's needs (light, water, soil, hardiness, mature size) to a spot that supplies them, and the plant thrives with minimal help. Force a plant into the wrong place and you sign up for a lifetime of propping it up, and usually a funeral anyway. Every assessment you made this week (zone, frost, sun, slope, soil, drainage) exists to serve this one decision. Gardening, done well, is mostly *matchmaking*.

### Examples

**Example 5.1: Why the shade tomato failed.** A gardener plants tomatoes in a bed that gets 3 hours of morning sun. The plants grow tall, pale, and leggy and set almost no fruit. Diagnosis: not a pest, not a nutrient: *insufficient light*. The photosynthetic "factory" can't make enough sugar to build fruit. The fix isn't a product; it's relocation. Right plant, wrong place.

**Example 5.2: Reading temporary wilt.** Hostas flop dramatically at 3 p.m. in a July heat wave, then stand back up by morning. That's transpiration outrunning uptake, normal, not an emergency. Watering at 3 p.m. in a panic mainly wets the leaves (disease risk). The right move: a deep morning soak and mulch, then let the plant do its afternoon thing.

2. **(15 min)** Classify ten plants you own or want as **annual / perennial / biennial.** Note which "perennials" elsewhere are only annuals here (not hardy to Zone 6).
3. **(15 min)** Find a wilted or struggling plant (yours, a neighbor's, or a photo) and practice diagnosing: light? water? drainage? Walk the list before reaching for a product.
4. **(10 min)** Write your own one-sentence definition of "right plant, right place" and three site conditions in your yard that will constrain plant choice (e.g., "deep shade under the maple," "soggy clay by the downspout," "baking south wall").
5. ★ Look up the mature size of one plant you already have; measure whether it actually has that much room. Crowding now is disease later.
