# HORT E-15: The Home Garden (Soil, Plants, and Weeds in Pittsburgh)

**Format:** Self-paced intensive, modeled on a college extension-school course
**Time commitment:** ~2 hours/day, 6 days/week, 3 weeks (one rest/review day per week)
**Prerequisites:** A patch of ground (or a few containers/raised beds), a willingness to dig in it, and curiosity. No prior gardening experience and no botany background assumed.

## What this course is (and isn't)

This course teaches you to **build and maintain a healthy home garden in Pittsburgh and the surrounding Allegheny County region**, and the *reasoning* behind every practice, so you can solve problems you've never seen before. You will learn:

- How to **read your own site**: hardiness zone, frost dates, sun, slope, and microclimates; on Pittsburgh's hills, no two yards are alike.
- **Soil**: why western Pennsylvania clay behaves the way it does, how to test it, and how to fix it.
- **Plants**: how they actually live, how to choose the right ones for Zone 6b, and how to plant, water, feed, and prune them.
- **Pests and disease**: an integrated approach to the usual Pittsburgh suspects: Japanese beetles, slugs, deer, groundhogs, spotted lanternfly, powdery mildew.
- **Weeds**: what they are, how they win, and how to beat the "Pittsburgh dozen", including invasive monsters like Japanese knotweed and garlic mustard.
- **The system**: lawn care, garden design for resilience and pollinators, and a month-by-month maintenance calendar so your garden is never neglected and never overwhelming.

It deliberately **omits** what a homeowner rarely needs: commercial production agronomy, greenhouse engineering, plant pathology at the lab level, and Latin-binomial memorization for its own sake. You'll meet botanical names where they prevent costly confusion, and nowhere else.

## A note on seasons (important: read this)

You can take this course **any time of year.** Gardening is seasonal, but learning to garden is not. Throughout the lessons, hands-on tasks that genuinely require a growing season are marked **🌱 (season-dependent)**, and every one of them comes with an **off-season alternative** you can do today: planning on paper, observing, soil testing indoors, or scouting a neighbor's beds. The understanding installs now; the muscle memory arrives when your window opens. Many core labs (site mapping, soil testing, weed ID, designing, building a calendar) work in January as well as June.

## How to use this course

1. Read `SYLLABUS.md` first, then `RUBRIC.md` so you know what mastery looks like.
2. Each day, work the lesson in order: **Lecture → Examples → Lab (do it) → Self-check.**
3. **Always with the garden in mind.** Knowledge you never apply is trivia. Wherever you can, take the lesson *outside*, to your beds, your soil, your weeds. Where the season won't allow it, do the off-season version and bank the rest.
4. Take quizzes closed-book. Grade yourself honestly against `ANSWER-KEYS.md` and the rubric.
5. Do the projects. A garden is the only final exam that matters; the projects are how the learning consolidates into a real plan for *your* yard.

## A note on pace

Three weeks is enough to *understand* everything here and to produce a concrete, correct plan and routine for your own Pittsburgh garden. Mastery (the kind where you glance at a plant and know what it needs) comes from the recurring field practices in `DRILLS.md` carried across a full season. Start them now; keep them going after the course ends. A garden rewards the gardener who shows up.
