# Week 2 — Light & Composition

> **This week in one line:** the camera should be close to reflex by now, so we turn to seeing. Photography means "drawing with light," and two things mostly separate a snapshot from a photograph: the quality of the light your subject sits in, and what you choose to put in the frame and where. Both are learnable. This week hands you the vocabulary and the rules. The eye itself comes later, with practice.

---

# Day 8 — The Qualities of Light

### Objectives
- Describe any light by its three physical qualities: **direction, hardness, intensity.**
- Predict how light will render a subject before shooting.
- Position subject or self to use the light you have.

### Lecture

Beginners think about *subjects*. Photographers think about *light*. The same face, the same building, the same street looks ordinary or extraordinary depending entirely on the light falling on it. You usually can't change the light itself, but you can choose where to stand, when to shoot, and where to put your subject. Most of photography lives in those three choices.

Describe any light by three properties:

**1. Direction** — where the light comes from *relative to the camera-subject line*:
- **Front light** (behind you, hitting the subject's front): even, flattering-but-flat, hides texture and shape. Safe, a little boring.
- **Side light** (from the left/right): reveals **texture and form** through shadow — the most sculptural, dramatic light. The workhorse of good portraits and landscapes.
- **Back light** (behind the subject, toward camera): creates rim/halo light, silhouettes, glow. Gorgeous and tricky (meter for the subject, +1 to +2 stops, or expose for a silhouette on purpose).
- **Top light** (overhead, e.g. noon sun): unflattering on faces — raccoon-eye shadows under brows and nose. Avoid for portraits; can work for graphic overhead scenes.

> The most useful habit you can build: when you see a subject, **walk around it** and watch how it changes as the light goes from front to side to back. Then choose.

**2. Hardness (quality)** — how sharp the shadow edges are. This is controlled by the *apparent size of the light source* relative to the subject:
- **Hard light:** small source (bare midday sun, a bare bulb, direct flash). **Sharp-edged, dark shadows**, high contrast, punchy and harsh. Dramatic; unforgiving of skin and clutter.
- **Soft light:** large source (overcast sky, light through a big window, sun through a curtain, open shade). **Soft, gradual shadows**, low contrast, gentle and flattering. The classic "good light" for portraits.

The trick: **a small source can be made soft by enlarging it** — clouds turn the tiny sun into a sky-sized softbox; a curtain turns a window into a wall of soft light. The *closer* a source is, the *bigger* (softer) it is relative to the subject. This single principle explains nearly all lighting.

**3. Intensity** — how bright. Mostly an exposure problem (Week 1), but it also drives mood: bright/high-key feels open and cheerful; dim/low-key feels intimate or somber. **Contrast** — the *range* from brightest to darkest — is the expressive cousin of intensity: high contrast = drama, low contrast = calm.

**Reading a scene's light** becomes second nature: glance at the shadows. *Sharp, dark shadows?* Hard light — move your subject to shade or wait for a cloud. *Soft or no shadows?* Soft light — great, use it. *Which way do shadows point?* That tells you the direction; move so the light rakes across your subject for texture, or behind for glow.

> 🟩 **X100VI:** With a fixed 35mm-equivalent lens you can't zoom to flatter a face from across the room, which makes **light position even more important** — you'll move your feet and your subject constantly, which is exactly the skill this week builds. The X100VI's EVF previews exposure live, so you can *see* a backlit subject going to silhouette and dial +EV until the face reads, all before pressing the shutter. The little pop-up/clip-on flash, used as **fill**, can open up harsh shadows in a pinch — but available light is the focus this week.

### Look
- Study three portraits you admire and name the light direction and hardness in each. Notice how often "good light" is *soft and from the side*. Find a dramatic photo and notice it's usually *hard, directional* light with deep shadows.

### Shoot
1. **(20 min) The 360° subject.** Put one subject (a person, a plant, a statue) in directional light (low sun or a bright window). Shoot it front-lit, side-lit, and back-lit by *moving yourself* around it. Compare how form and mood change with nothing but your position.
2. **(15 min) Hard vs. soft.** Shoot the same subject in direct hard sun, then in open shade (or wait for a cloud). Watch the shadows go from sharp and harsh to soft and gentle.
3. **(10 min) Window light study.** Place a subject beside a window (no direct sun). Shoot at 90° to the window (side light) and facing it (front light). This is the most useful portrait light in the world; learn to find it indoors.

### Daily drill (start today)
**Light-spotting, 3 min:** wherever you are, name the **direction** and **hardness** of the main light, and where you'd place a subject to use it best.

### Self-check

2. What physical property of a source makes light hard vs. soft?
3. How does an overcast sky soften the sun?
4. Why is overhead noon sun bad for portraits?
5. You have a small hard light. Name two ways to make it softer.

# Day 9 — Color of Light & White Balance

### Objectives
- Understand color temperature and the Kelvin scale.
- Set white balance by scene and use it creatively.
- Recognize and correct color casts.

### Lecture

Light isn't just bright or dim — it has **color**, and our brains hide this from us. A white shirt looks white under warm tungsten bulbs *and* under blue shade, because the brain auto-corrects. The camera doesn't (unless told to), so you must understand color of light.

**Color temperature** is measured in **kelvin (K)**, and the scale is counterintuitive: **low K = warm/orange, high K = cool/blue.**

| Light source | Approx. temperature | Look |
|---|---|---|
| Candle / fire | ~1800 K | very warm, orange |
| Tungsten bulb (old incandescent) | ~2800–3200 K | warm, orange |
| Sunrise / sunset ("golden hour") | ~3000–4000 K | warm, golden |
| Midday sun | ~5200–5500 K | neutral |
| Electronic flash | ~5500 K | neutral |
| Overcast / cloudy | ~6500–7500 K | cool, slightly blue |
| Open shade / blue hour | ~8000–10000 K | cool, blue |

**White balance (WB)** is the camera's correction: you tell it "this is the color of my light," and it neutralizes that cast so whites look white. Set WB *too warm* for the scene and the photo goes blue; *too cool* and it goes orange. The presets are just named Kelvin values:
- **Auto (AWB):** the camera guesses. Good most of the time; struggles in mixed light and dim warm light (it tends to over-neutralize a sunset's lovely warmth — sometimes you *don't* want it "corrected").
- **Daylight (~5500K), Cloudy (~6500K), Shade (~7500K):** progressively warm up the image to counter progressively cooler light.
- **Tungsten/Incandescent (~3200K):** adds strong blue to counter orange bulbs.
- **Fluorescent:** counters green casts.
- **Custom/Kelvin:** dial the exact value, or set off a gray/white card for perfect neutrality.

**The creative twist:** WB is also a mood tool. Warming a sunset (Cloudy/Shade preset) intensifies the golden glow. Cooling a winter scene deepens the chill. "Correct" and "expressive" are different goals — choose on purpose.

**The RAW lifeline (preview of Week 3):** if you shoot **RAW**, white balance is **fully adjustable after the fact with zero quality loss** — the camera records the actual sensor data and WB is just a tag. So if you shoot RAW, WB in-camera matters far less (set it to taste for the preview). If you shoot **JPEG**, WB is baked in and must be right in-camera. This is one of the biggest reasons to shoot RAW.

> 🟩 **X100VI:** White balance lives in the **IQ menu** (or assign to a Fn button / the Q menu). The X100VI's **Film Simulations** interact with color and mood — *Provia* (standard), *Astia* (soft), *Velvia* (punchy, saturated landscapes), *Classic Chrome* (muted, documentary), *Acros* (superb B&W), and the *Classic Neg* / *Nostalgic Neg* looks. These are Fujifilm's beloved JPEG color science. **Key workflow tip:** set the camera to **RAW + JPEG** — you get a gorgeous film-sim JPEG to enjoy immediately *and* a flexible RAW with adjustable WB to edit later. You give up nothing, and it's very on-brand for the X100VI.

### Look
- Find a photo shot under household bulbs with the wrong WB (orange) and one shot in shade left on Daylight (blue). Then find a sunset where the photographer *kept* the warmth vs. one where Auto WB neutralized it to a dull gray. Decide which you prefer — that's a WB judgment.

### Shoot
1. **(15 min) WB tour.** Indoors under artificial light, shoot the same scene on Auto, Daylight, Tungsten, Cloudy, and Shade. See the color swing from blue to orange. Identify which is actually neutral.
2. **(15 min) Mixed light.** Find a scene with two light colors (a warm lamp near a cool window). Notice no single WB fixes both — one will always be off. This is why mixed light is hard; learn to choose which light to favor.
3. **(10 min) Creative WB.** At golden hour (or with a warm lamp), deliberately set Cloudy/Shade to *exaggerate* the warmth. Make a cozy, warm frame on purpose.
4. ★ If shooting RAW already: shoot one frame, then change its WB across the full range in your editor and watch quality stay perfect. That's the RAW advantage made visible.

### Daily drill
**Kelvin sense, 3 min:** name warm/cool and rough Kelvin for a flashed source ("candlelight?" → ~1800K, warm; "open shade?" → ~8000K, cool) and the WB preset that neutralizes it.

### Self-check
1. Low Kelvin = warm or cool?
2. What does white balance do?
3. You leave WB on Tungsten but shoot outdoors at noon — what happens to the color?
4. Why does shooting RAW make in-camera WB less critical?
5. Name one creative (non-corrective) use of white balance.

# Day 10 — Composition I: The Frame & the Subject

### Objectives
- Identify and strengthen the subject of every frame.
- Use the rule of thirds, balance, and negative space deliberately.
- Control the background and the frame edges.

### Lecture

Composition is **the arrangement of elements within the frame** — deciding what to include, what to exclude, and where to put things. The camera is a *subtraction* tool: from the infinite chaos of the world, you cut a rectangle. Good composition is mostly good subtraction.

**Rule 1: Have one clear subject.** The single most common beginner failure is a photo *of nothing in particular* — the eye lands and finds no anchor. Before every frame, answer: *"What is this a photo of?"* If you can't answer in a few words, the photo will fail. Everything else serves the subject.

**Rule 2: The rule of thirds.** Divide the frame into a 3×3 grid:

```
+-----+-----+-----+
|     |     |     |
+-----●-----●-----+   ← place subjects on the lines,
|     |     |     |      key points on the intersections (●)
+-----●-----●-----+
|     |     |     |
+-----+-----+-----+
```

Placing your subject (or a horizon, or a person's eyes) **on a third-line or intersection** instead of dead center creates a more dynamic, balanced image and gives the subject "room." It's a starting heuristic, not a law — but it cures the reflexive bullseye centering that weakens beginner shots. (Centering is powerful too — *when chosen* for symmetry or confrontation, not defaulted to.)

**Rule 3: Balance and visual weight.** Every element has visual "weight" — bright things, faces, sharp things, and large things pull the eye. A photo feels balanced when weights are distributed, not all dumped on one side. A lone subject on the right can be balanced by **negative space** (empty area) on the left, which also gives a sense of calm or scale.

**Rule 4: Negative space.** Empty, simple areas (sky, wall, water) aren't wasted space — they isolate the subject, set a mood, and give the eye somewhere to rest. Beginners tend to cram the frame; experienced shooters often empty it out.

**Rule 5: Control the background.** A great subject is ruined by a pole "growing" out of their head or a cluttered, bright background stealing attention. *Look at the whole frame, not just the subject,* before shooting. Fixes: move yourself (change the background angle), open the aperture (blur it, Week 1), or move the subject. **Check your edges** — what's touching or cut awkwardly by the four borders? Tidy the edges and half your frames improve.

**Rule 6: Fill the frame / get closer.** Robert Capa: "If your photos aren't good enough, you're not close enough." Beginners stand too far back and include too much. Step in. Cut the clutter. Let the subject dominate.

> 🟩 **X100VI:** The fixed 35mm-equivalent lens is a *composition teacher in disguise*. You can't zoom, so to fill the frame you must **walk closer** — which changes perspective and forces real compositional decisions instead of lazy zooming. Turn on the **framing grid** (a thirds grid in the EVF/LCD: menu → Screen Set-Up → Framing Guideline). Use the **EVF** rather than the LCD when you can — composing with the camera to your eye, edges visible, is more deliberate and steadier than holding it at arm's length.

### Look
- Take five photos you love (yours or famous ones) and for each: name the subject in three words, find where it sits on the thirds grid, and identify the negative space. Then take five weak photos and diagnose: no clear subject? cluttered background? dead-center default? edges a mess?

### Shoot
1. **(15 min) One subject, thirds.** Photograph 10 different subjects, each placed deliberately on a third-line/intersection, each with a clean background. Name the subject before each frame.
2. **(15 min) Negative space.** Make 5 frames where the subject occupies a small part of the frame and simple empty space dominates. Feel how isolation and calm increase.
3. **(15 min) Background patrol.** Pick one subject. Without moving it, find three *different backgrounds* for it just by changing your own position and height. Pick the cleanest.
4. ★ **(10 min) Get closer.** Take a photo you think is "done," then physically halve your distance and reshoot. Usually better.

### Daily drill
**Name-the-subject, 2 min:** scroll your recent shots; for each, state the subject in ≤3 words. Any photo where you can't is a lesson in what went wrong.

### Self-check
1. What's the first question to ask before any frame?
2. What does the rule of thirds help you avoid?
3. What is negative space good for?
4. Name two ways to fix a cluttered background.
5. Why "get closer"?

# Day 11 — Composition II: Line, Shape, Depth & Perspective

### Objectives
- Use leading lines, shapes, and patterns to guide the eye.
- Create a sense of depth in a 2D image.
- Use camera height and perspective deliberately.

### Lecture

Day 10 was *what to include and where*. Day 11 is *how to move the viewer's eye through the frame* and *how to make a flat photo feel three-dimensional*.

**Leading lines.** The eye follows lines. Roads, fences, rivers, shadows, railings, a row of columns — all *lead* the viewer's gaze. Point them **toward your subject** (or toward a vanishing point) and you create flow and depth. A line entering from a corner is especially strong. Diagonal lines feel dynamic; horizontals feel calm; verticals feel strong/stable.

**Shape, form, pattern, texture.**
- **Shape** (2D outline) dominates in flat/backlit light — think silhouettes.
- **Form** (3D volume) appears with side light and shadow (Day 8).
- **Pattern** (repetition) is satisfying; a **break in a pattern** is even stronger — the odd one out becomes the subject.
- **Texture** is revealed by raking side light and invites a sense of touch.

**Depth — making 3D from 2D.** A photo is flat; these tricks restore depth:
1. **Foreground / midground / background layers** — include something near, something middle, something far. Instant depth.
2. **Leading lines** receding into the distance.
3. **Overlap** — objects partly hiding others read as "in front," establishing space.
4. **Atmospheric perspective** — distant things are lighter, hazier, bluer (use it in landscapes).
5. **Selective focus** — a sharp subject against a blurred fore/background (Week 1 aperture) separates planes.
6. **Scale cues** — a person for size against a mountain.

**Framing (the compositional kind).** Use foreground elements — a doorway, an arch, branches, a window — to **frame** your subject. It adds depth, directs attention, and gives context.

**Perspective and camera height.** *Where you put the camera* changes the entire relationship of elements — and beginners almost always shoot from standing eye level, the most boring option.
- **Low angle** (camera low, looking up): makes subjects powerful, looming, heroic; great for kids/pets (get to their level), tall structures.
- **High angle** (looking down): makes subjects smaller, vulnerable; good for overviews, flat-lays, patterns.
- **Eye level of the subject:** the honest, connected view for portraits and animals — kneel for the dog.

> Moving your feet and changing your height are *free* and transform photos more than any gear. Crouch, climb, lie down. Work the scene from multiple positions before settling.

> 🟩 **X100VI:** Small and light, the X100VI *wants* to be moved to weird heights — drop it to the ground for a low-angle street shot, hold it overhead. The **tilting LCD** makes waist-level (candid, unintimidating) and high-overhead shooting easy without contorting. The 35mm-equivalent field of view is wide enough to gather strong foreground-to-background layers for depth, but normal enough to avoid wide-angle distortion — ideal for the layering and leading-line work in today's lesson.

### Look
- Find a photo with strong leading lines and trace with your finger how your eye travels. Find a landscape with clear foreground/midground/background layers. Find a portrait shot from a low vs. high angle and feel how the subject's "power" changes. Notice a "framed" shot (subject seen through a doorway/branches).

### Shoot
1. **(15 min) Leading lines.** Find three scenes with strong lines (a path, a fence, a staircase, shadows) and compose each so the lines lead to a subject or vanishing point.
2. **(15 min) Three layers.** Make 5 frames that each have a clear foreground, midground, and background. Build depth deliberately.
3. **(15 min) Height study.** Pick one subject. Shoot it from a low angle, eye level, and a high angle. Pick which tells the story best and articulate why.
4. ★ **(10 min) Natural frame.** Find something to shoot your subject *through* (doorway, window, foliage) for a framing effect.

### Daily drill
**Eye-path, 2 min:** on any photo, identify the entry point and trace where the composition leads the eye. If it leads nowhere or off the edge, note the fix.

### Self-check
1. What do leading lines do, and where should they point?
2. Name three ways to create a sense of depth.
3. What does a break in a pattern become?
4. How does a low camera angle change how a subject feels?
5. Why is shooting from standing eye level usually the weakest choice?

# Day 12 — Lenses, Focal Length & the Discipline of a Prime

### Objectives
- Understand focal length and how it changes framing and "look."
- Bust the "lenses compress" myth (it's distance, not the lens).
- Embrace the one-focal-length constraint as a skill-builder.

### Lecture

**Focal length** (in millimeters) determines a lens's **angle of view** — how much of the scene it captures — and thus how "zoomed in" it is. (Because angle of view depends on sensor size, we quote **full-frame-equivalent** focal lengths to compare across cameras.)

| Equivalent focal length | Type | Angle of view | Typical use |
|---|---|---|---|
| 14–24mm | Ultra-wide | very wide | dramatic landscapes, interiors, cramped spaces |
| 24–35mm | Wide | wide | environmental, street, documentary, "you are there" |
| **35mm** | Wide-normal | natural, slightly wide | **street, reportage, everyday — the X100VI** |
| 50mm | Normal | ~human central vision | classic, versatile, portraits + general |
| 85–135mm | Short telephoto | narrow | portraits (flattering), isolation |
| 200mm+ | Telephoto | very narrow | sports, wildlife, distant subjects |

**What focal length changes:**
- **Framing:** longer = more magnified / narrower view.
- **Apparent depth of field:** longer lenses *appear* to blur backgrounds more for the same subject size.
- **Working distance:** longer lenses let you shoot from farther away.

**The "compression" myth, corrected.** People say telephotos "compress" perspective (make backgrounds look big and close) and wide lenses "expand" it (make near things huge, far things tiny). **The lens isn't doing that — your distance is.** Perspective — the relative size of near vs. far objects — is determined **only by where you stand.** What actually happens:
- To fill the frame with a face using a **wide** lens, you must get *very close* → the nose looms, background shrinks ("expansion," really just close perspective → unflattering for portraits).
- To fill the frame with a face using a **telephoto**, you stand *far back* → features flatten pleasingly, background looks large and close ("compression," really just distant perspective → flattering).

So: **the focal length forces a distance, and the distance creates the perspective.** This is why 85–135mm flatters faces (you're far) and why ultra-wides distort them (you're close). Internalize this and you'll choose focal length and distance with intent.

**Prime vs. zoom.** A **prime** (one fixed focal length, like the X100VI's) is "limiting" — and that's the point. You can't lazily zoom; you **zoom with your feet**, which forces you to consider perspective and composition actively. Primes are usually sharper, lighter, and faster (wider max aperture) for the money. Working within one focal length for this whole course will make you a *much* stronger composer than hopping around a zoom range — you'll learn to *see* in 35mm.

> 🟩 **X100VI:** You have one lens: **23mm f/2 = 35mm-equivalent.** Lean in. The X100VI also offers **Digital Teleconverter** modes (35mm / 50mm / 70mm equivalent) that crop the 40MP sensor — useful in a pinch and still high-resolution, but treat them as a crop, not a true zoom. Optional **conversion lenses** (WCL wider ~28mm, TCL tighter ~50mm) screw on if you ever need them. But the real lesson: **spend the course at native 35mm.** Constraint breeds creativity, and 35mm is the most versatile single focal length ever made — it's why the X100 series is a cult classic.

### Look
- Find two portraits, one shot wide-and-close (distorted, big nose, tiny background) and one shot long-and-far (flattering, big background). Then notice it's the *distance* doing it. Look at famous 35mm street work (Cartier-Bresson, Winogrand-era) to calibrate your eye to the focal length you're using.

### Shoot
1. **(20 min) Distance ≠ lens, proven.** If you have a zoom, frame a friend's head the same size at the widest and a longer setting (changing your distance to keep head size constant). Compare nose size and background — that's perspective from distance. *If you only have the X100VI prime:* photograph a face from very close (filling frame) then from far back with the digital teleconverter at 50mm — same demonstration.
2. **(20 min) See in 35mm.** Spend 20 minutes shooting only at your native focal length, *predicting the frame before you raise the camera.* The goal is to know what 35mm "sees" without looking.
3. ★ **(10 min)** Make one photo where being forced to step back (no zoom) gave you a *better* composition than zooming would have. Note what including more context did.

### Daily drill
**Focal-length call, 2 min:** for a flashed shot type, name the ideal equivalent focal length ("flattering head-and-shoulders portrait?" → 85–135mm; "tight interior?" → 16–24mm; "street?" → 35mm).

### Self-check
1. What actually determines perspective — the lens or your distance?
2. Why do telephotos flatter faces?
3. Why does an ultra-wide distort a close-up face?
4. What does "zoom with your feet" force you to do?
5. What is the 35mm-equivalent good for, and why is it the X100VI's choice?

# Day 13 — Working With Available Light, then Quiz 2

### Objectives
- Recognize and exploit the best natural-light windows of the day.
- Handle the hard cases: harsh midday, low light, mixed light.
- Take Quiz 2.

### Lecture

You now know how to *describe* light (Day 8) and its *color* (Day 9). Today: the practical playbook for the light the day actually gives you.

**The golden hours** (roughly the hour after sunrise and the hour before sunset): the sun is **low** (side/back light → texture, long shadows, rim light), **soft** (filtered through more atmosphere), and **warm** (golden color). This is the most universally flattering light there is. *Plan around it.* Most great outdoor photos happen in these two windows.

**Blue hour** (the ~20–30 min before sunrise / after sunset, sun below horizon): soft, even, deep blue light. Magic for cityscapes (when sky and artificial lights balance) and moody scenes. Needs slower shutters / higher ISO — bring support.

**Midday sun (the hard case).** High, hard, top-down — harsh shadows, squinting subjects, raccoon eyes. Strategies:
1. **Move to open shade** (under a tree, awning, building) — instantly soft, even light. The #1 fix.
2. **Use backlight** — turn the subject so the sun is behind them (rim light), exposing for the face (+EV) or adding fill.
3. **Embrace the hardness** — high-contrast, graphic, deep-shadow images *want* hard light (street, architecture, B&W).
4. **Wait** — or shoot subjects that suit hard light (textures, patterns) until the light improves.

**Overcast = a giant softbox.** Beginners think gray days are bad; they're *ideal* for portraits, flowers, anything wanting soft, even, shadowless light. Just keep the (boring white) sky out of the frame or let it go bright.

**Window light (indoors).** A window with no direct sun gives you a soft, directional light for free — it's the light the old portrait painters worked by. Place the subject *beside* it; the further from the window, the more dramatic the shadow falloff. A white wall/card opposite the window acts as a **reflector**, filling shadows.

**Low light.** As light drops: open up (wider aperture), then slow the shutter to your handheld limit (Day 3), then raise ISO (Day 4) — in that order — and accept that a sharp, slightly noisy frame beats a clean blurry one. Find and use *any* available light source (a lamp, a sign, a phone). Brace the camera. Consider Auto-ISO with a sensible max.

**Fill light.** When shadows are too deep (harsh sun, strong side light), *fill* them: a reflector (or white wall, or even a sheet of paper) bounces light back into the shadows; a touch of flash does the same. Fill reduces contrast to taste — you control how much shadow you keep.

> 🟩 **X100VI:** A perfect golden-hour and street companion — pocketable, quiet, quick. Set **Auto-ISO (max ~6400, min shutter ~1/125)** for low light so you only think about aperture and moment. For backlit golden-hour portraits, use the **live EVF preview + exposure comp** to keep the face bright while preserving the rim glow. The **built-in ND filter** lets you shoot wide-open f/2 portraits in bright sun (shallow DoF without blowing out). The little **fill flash** (leaf shutter syncs at *any* speed) is great for a subtle shadow-fill in harsh midday. *Acros* film simulation turns harsh midday contrast into a feature for striking B&W.

### Look
- Compare a portrait shot at noon in open sun (squinting, harsh shadows) with one in open shade or golden hour. The difference is *entirely* light, not subject or gear. Find a blue-hour cityscape and a window-light portrait and note how little equipment each needed.

### Shoot
1. **(30 min) Golden hour.** Shoot at sunrise or sunset. Make one back-lit (rim/glow) frame, one side-lit (texture/long shadow) frame, and one frame using the warm color for mood. This is the headline shoot of the week.
2. **(15 min) Tame midday** (do this on a separate bright day if needed): take a harsh-sun portrait, then fix it three ways — move to shade, turn to backlight, and add fill (reflector/paper/flash).
3. **(15 min) Window light.** Make a soft window-light portrait or still life indoors, with a white surface as a reflector on the shadow side. Move the subject nearer/farther from the window to control drama.

### Daily drill
**Light-window planning, 2 min:** given a shot you want, name the *best time of day and light setup* for it ("dramatic landscape?" → golden hour, side light; "soft headshot, no gear?" → overcast or window light).
