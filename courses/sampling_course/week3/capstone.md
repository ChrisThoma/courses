# Capstone: The Finished Track

**Due:** end of Week 3 · **Weight:** 30% · **Rubric dimensions:** D6, D7, D8 (your *Artist* evidence): built on everything from D1–D5

This is what the whole course has been building toward. Not a loop, not a beat, but a **finished, mixed, exported, documented piece of music made from samples** that you would actually play for someone. Everything you've built (Project 1's flip, Project 2's beat, your Week-3 sound design, arrangement, and mix) converges here. If this course gives you one thing, it's the experience of *finishing,* and this is where you get it.

---

## What you're delivering

A complete sample-based track:

- **Length:** 2:00–3:00. Long enough to have a real structure; short enough to actually finish.
- **Source:** built primarily from samples (chopped, played, processed). At least **one element must be from a source you recorded or sampled yourself** (proving D8 / the "sample yourself" path from Lecture 14). The rest can be anything for this learning context, but your log documents a real release plan for each source.
- **Structure:** distinct sections with genuine contrast: at minimum an intro, the main groove appearing at least twice, one real breakdown, and an outro (Lecture 12).
- **Production:** designed sound (not just raw samples, Lecture 11), a coherent mix that translates across systems (Lecture 13), and a competent finish/export (Lecture 14).

You may build directly on your Week-2 beat and Week-3 labs; in fact you should. The capstone is the *integration* of the course, not a from-scratch sprint.

## Required, and demonstrated (not just present)

| # | Component | Demonstrated by |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **A real flip** (D4/D5) | a source transformed so it's musical and not obviously the original: chopped, re-sequenced, re-pitched, or played as an instrument |
| 2 | **Drums with a pocket** (D4) | chopped break and/or built+layered kit, grooving (swing/velocity/micro-timing), not flat |
| 3 | **The resampling loop** (D3/D6) | at least one element made by committing audio and re-transforming it; documented in the log |
| 4 | **Designed sound** (D6) | at least one signature sound built with a Rack/processing chain: junk-to-gold |
| 5 | **A self-recorded source** (D8) | one element from audio you captured yourself |
| 6 | **An arrangement** (D7) | sections with contrast; ≥3 automation moves; a built transition (riser/impact) |
| 7 | **A translating mix** (D6/D7) | high-passed/EQ-carved elements, one sidechain, shared reverb space, mono-checked |
| 8 | **A finished export** (D7) | 16-bit/44.1 distribution WAV (dithered) + 24-bit master, tails rendered, sane loudness |

### PRODUCTION_LOG.md (this is half the grade-feel)

A real producer can *explain* their track. Write:

1. **The concept**: one paragraph: what is this track, what's the vibe, what were you going for?
2. **Source inventory**: every sample, where it came from, and its **release plan** (cleared / royalty-free / public domain / self-recorded / would-replace). The self-recorded source flagged.
3. **The signature moves**: the 5–8 decisions that define the track: the key flip, the drum approach, the resampling chain, the standout sound design, the arrangement's turning point. One short paragraph each, *attributing the result to the technique* ("the pad is the vocal 'oohs' stretched 8× in Complex Pro, resampled, then high-passed and sidechained: Lectures 9, 11, 13").
4. **Arrangement map**: your sections with timestamps and what changes at each boundary.
5. **Mix notes**: your three biggest mix decisions and what problem each solved; what changed when you checked it on phone/mono.
6. **Honest self-critique**: what works, what you'd fix with another week, what you learned about *finishing.*
7. **Full rubric self-assessment**: all eight dimensions, claimed level + one artifact-pointing sentence of evidence each. This is your final rubric checkpoint.

## The bar, restated

Don't aim for a masterpiece; aim for **finished and honest.** A complete 2:30 track at 80% quality that you can explain end to end is a pass with distinction; a gorgeous 16-bar loop that never became a song is not. The whole course was a runway to this: prove you can take sound, hear music in it, transform it, arrange it, mix it, and *let it go* as a finished thing.

When it's done, do the real final test: play it for someone who doesn't know how it was made, and see if they ask: **"wait, what's that sample?"**
