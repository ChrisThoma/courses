# MUS-44S: The Art of Sampling in Ableton Live

**An intensive 3-week course on sample-based music production**
*Self-paced edition · ~3–5 hours/day · June 2026*

---

## Course Description

Almost every genre you love (hip-hop, house, jungle, lo-fi, pop, ambient) was built by people who took recorded sound and turned it into something new. That practice is **sampling**: treating audio as raw material rather than a finished performance. A two-second drum break becomes a rhythm section. A scratchy soul record becomes a chord progression. A field recording of rain becomes a pad. The skill isn't really about stealing a loop. It's *hearing the music inside a sound that was never meant to be that music*, and having the tools and craft to extract it.

This course teaches that craft from first principles, entirely inside **Ableton Live**, the DAW that more or less defines modern sample-based production. You will learn how Live thinks about audio (warping, the two views, the instruments), then build real material: you will flip a sample into a beat, slice a drum break, multisample an instrument, design sound by resampling, arrange loops into a finished song, mix it, and learn how to release it without getting sued.

By the end you will be able to drop any sound into Live and know (concretely, with technique, not vibes) how to turn it into music: how to chop it, tune it, time-stretch it, layer it, process it, arrange it, and finish it.

## Who this is for

- You have Ableton Live installed (**Standard or Suite recommended**; see *A note on editions* below) and have opened it at least once.
- You can find your way around your computer's files and audio.
- You do **not** need to read music, play an instrument, or know any theory; we build everything from sound. A little rhythmic feel helps; we'll develop the rest.
- You want to *make tracks*, not just collect knowledge. Every week ends with something you can play for a friend.

## What you need

| Thing | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Ableton Live 12** | Standard or Suite ideally. Intro/Lite works for ~80% of the course with noted limits (no full **Sampler**, capped track count). Where an instrument is Suite-only, the lecture says so and gives a workaround. |
| **Headphones or monitors** | You cannot make decisions about low end on laptop speakers. Anything honest beats anything loud. |
| **A folder of source material** | A few songs you own, some one-shots, a couple of full tracks/stems, and at least one **drum break** (we'll point you at legal sources in `RESOURCES.md`). ~20 sounds is plenty to start. |
| **A way to capture audio (optional)** | Phone voice memos or a cheap interface: for recording your own samples, the most slept-on technique in the course. |

> **A note on editions.** Live comes as Intro, Standard, and Suite. The two instruments at the heart of this course are **Simpler** (in *all* editions, including Intro) and **Sampler** (Suite, or a paid add-on). We teach Simpler as the core tool precisely because everyone has it; Simpler does ~90% of sampling work. Lecture 7 covers Sampler; if you don't have it, you'll still understand multisampling conceptually and can do the lab in Simpler with a documented compromise.

## How to study this course (read this)

The lectures (`weekN/lectures.md`) are written to be **read and worked through at the computer**: Live open, sound playing. They are not a reference manual; they are a studio session in text. Reading a lecture without doing the moves in Live is like reading about swimming.

Paired with each lecture is a curated set of videos and articles in **`RESOURCES.md`**: Ableton's own tutorials, the producers who teach this best on YouTube, and the few articles worth reading on sampling law and theory. A ⭐ marks the one resource per lecture you shouldn't skip.

**The recommended rhythm for each lecture:**
1. Read the lecture with Live open; **do every move as you read it.** Don't bank them for later.
2. Answer the **Listening & section questions** at the end, out loud or in writing. They train your ears, which is the actual bottleneck.
3. Do the **Lab.** Save the project; you will reuse these.
4. Watch that lecture's ⭐ resource to see someone fast do the same moves.

You learn this by *making bad music quickly and a lot of it.* Finish things at low quality rather than perfecting fragments. Every working producer's hard drive is a graveyard of loops; the skill that separates people is **finishing**, and this course is built around it.

## Course Structure (Harvard model, studio edition)

Each study day follows the same rhythm:

| Block | Time | What |
|---|---|---|
| **Lecture** | ~45–75 min | Read at the computer, doing every move in Live |
| **Listening** | ~15 min | The section questions: ear training and reflection |
| **Lab** | ~1.5–3 hrs | Hands-on production work; you keep the project file |
| **Reference** | ~20–40 min | That lecture's resources: watch a pro do it faster |

End of each week: a quiz (closed-Live, then self-grade) and a project milestone.

## A note on the law (we mean it)

Sampling other people's recordings without permission is, legally, copyright infringement of both the composition and the master recording, full stop, in most jurisdictions, regardless of how short the sample is or how much you changed it. This course teaches sampling as a **craft and a practice**; for everything you intend to *release*, Lecture 1 and Lecture 14 cover your real options: clearing samples, using royalty-free/Creative-Commons material, sampling your own recordings, and the practical risk landscape. Learn on whatever you want; release responsibly. This is education, not legal advice.
