# Mastery Rubric: The Art of Sampling in Ableton Live

Self-assess against this at the end of each week. Four levels per dimension:
**Beginner → Competent → Proficient → Artist**

**Passing the course:** Proficient+ on all eight dimensions.
**Mastery:** Artist on at least three, including at least one of D7 (Arrangement & Finishing) or D8 (Taste & Voice), the dimensions that separate people who *know* sampling from people who *make* music with it.

---

## D1: Live Fluency

| Level | You can… |
|---|---|
| Beginner | Find the browser, drop a sample on a track, hit play, and not get lost between Session and Arrangement view |
| Competent | Navigate Live by keyboard for the common moves (record-enable, loop a clip, consolidate, duplicate, group), and explain when to work in Session vs. Arrangement |
| Proficient | Work fluidly without breaking flow: resample, freeze/flatten, route audio between tracks, manage a CPU-heavy set, and recover when something breaks |
| Artist | Live disappears: the tool stops being in the way of the idea; you build templates and a workflow that lets you go sound→finished-loop in minutes |

## D2: The Sample as Material (Listening)

| Level | You can… |
|---|---|
| Beginner | Tell when a sample is in time and roughly in tune |
| Competent | Hear a source and identify what's *usable* in it (a chord, a drum hit, a texture) and what to discard |
| Proficient | Listen to a finished track and reverse-engineer the sampling: "that's a chop, pitched up a 4th, with the transient gated"; identify keys/tempos by ear well enough to work |
| Artist | Hear music *that isn't there yet* (the loop hiding in a 4-second clip nobody else would have flipped) and you choose sources for their potential, not their obviousness |

## D3: Warping & Time

| Level | You can… |
|---|---|
| Beginner | Get a loop to play in sync by setting its loop length and BPM |
| Competent | Warp a clip to the grid by setting Warp Markers, and pick a sensible Warp mode for a given material (Beats vs. Complex vs. Tones) |
| Proficient | Warp anything cleanly: fix drifting live recordings, preserve transients on breaks, choose modes by ear, and know when *not* to warp |
| Artist | Use warping *creatively* as sound design (deliberate artifacts, extreme stretches, Complex-mode smear as a texture) and treat time as something you compose, not just correct |

## D4: Chopping & Slicing

| Level | You can… |
|---|---|
| Beginner | Slice a loop to a Drum Rack / Simpler and trigger the pieces |
| Competent | Chop a melodic sample into musically useful pieces and re-sequence them into a new phrase; slice a drum break to transient |
| Proficient | Flip a sample so it's unrecognizable-yet-musical: re-pitch chops, re-order, gap and gate them, build a new groove from someone else's drums |
| Artist | Chopping is a compositional voice: you build melodies, harmonies, and rhythms from fragments with intention, and your chops have a recognizable feel |

## D5: Instruments from Samples (Simpler & Sampler)

| Level | You can… |
|---|---|
| Beginner | Load a one-shot into Simpler and play it across the keyboard |
| Competent | Set loop points, root note, and the amp envelope so a sample plays as a clean, tuned instrument; build a basic Drum Rack kit |
| Proficient | Multisample across zones (Sampler), use filter/envelope/LFO modulation to make a sample *expressive*, and design velocity- and round-robin behavior |
| Artist | Build playable, characterful instruments from raw recordings that feel designed (a sampled "synth," a Frankenstein kit, a tuned-percussion patch) that you'd reach for in real productions |

## D6: Sound Design & Processing

| Level | You can… |
|---|---|
| Beginner | Put an EQ and reverb on a sample and hear the change |
| Competent | Use Audio Effect Racks, chains, and the core effects (EQ, compression, saturation, delay, reverb) with intent rather than by accident |
| Proficient | Resample processing chains into new source material, build signal flows that transform a sound's character, and use modulation/automation to make processing move |
| Artist | Develop signature sounds (a processing voice someone could recognize) and turn unusable junk audio into the best element in the track |

## D7: Arrangement & Finishing

| Level | You can… |
|---|---|
| Beginner | Lay a loop across the Arrangement timeline so it plays for a few minutes |
| Competent | Build a track with distinct sections (intro/verse/chorus/break/outro), using arrangement to create change |
| Proficient | Create tension and release with automation, filtering, drops, and dynamics; mix to a clean balance; export a correctly-leveled file |
| Artist | Arrange and finish so the *track* is the art: your structures pull a listener through, your mixes serve the song, and you **finish things** instead of hoarding loops |

## D8: Taste & Voice

| Level | You can… |
|---|---|
| Beginner | Tell whether you like something |
| Competent | Articulate *why* a sample choice or a flip works or doesn't, in the language of the craft |
| Proficient | Make consistent aesthetic decisions across a track; critique your own work honestly and revise toward an intention |
| Artist | You have a recognizable point of view (source choices, processing, and arrangement that add up to *your* sound) and you can defend every decision in a finished piece |

---

## Self-Assessment Protocol

At each weekly checkpoint, for every dimension write **2–3 sentences of evidence pointing at an actual artifact**: a project file, a bounced loop, a quiz score, an annotated transcript of decisions. *"I claim Proficient on D4 because in Project 2 I sliced the Think break, re-sequenced it into a half-time groove, and pitched three chops to build the bassline; see `beat_v3.als`, track 'Drums' and 'Bass'."* No artifact, no level. "I think I'm good at this" is not evidence.

| Checkpoint | Expected floor |
|---|---|
| End of Week 1 | Competent on D1–D3, Beginner+ elsewhere |
| End of Week 2 | Proficient on D3–D5, Competent on D1–D2, D6 |
| End of Week 3 | Proficient on all; Artist on ≥3 (incl. D7 or D8) |

The honest measure of this course is not the rubric. It's whether, three weeks from now, someone hears your capstone and asks *"wait, what's that sample?"*
