**Q1.** A commercial record you want to sample carries how many copyrights you'd need to deal with, and what are they?

- A) One — the song
- B) One — the recording
- C) Two — the **composition** (the song) and the **master recording** (that performance)
- D) None, if the sample is under one second

**Q2.** You drop an 8-bar loop into Live and it plays in sync for the first bar but has drifted noticeably out of time by bar 8. The *first* thing to check is:

- A) The Warp Mode (switch to Texture)
- B) The **Seg. BPM** — Live likely guessed the tempo wrong (often by a factor of 2); try the :2 / \*2 buttons
- C) The master output level
- D) The sample's bit depth

**Q3.** You load one sustained piano note into Simpler and want to play *chords* with it across the keyboard. Which playback mode, and what one setting must you get right first?

- A) One-Shot; set the Release
- B) Slice; set the number of slices
- C) Classic; set the **root/Transpose** so it's in tune
- D) One-Shot; turn Warp off

**Q4.** Which is the correct description of the difference between Session and Arrangement view?

- A) Session is for audio, Arrangement is for MIDI
- B) Session is a non-linear loop sketchpad; Arrangement is the linear timeline where the song is committed
- C) Session is Suite-only; Arrangement is in all editions
- D) They are identical; it's just a color preference

**Q5.** A transient is:

- A) The reverb tail of a sound
- B) The sharp onset/attack of a sound — what Live's slicing uses to find chop points and what your ear hears as "the hit"
- C) A sample that is only used once
- D) The stereo width of a recording

**Q6.** "Lo-fi" production often deliberately uses:

- A) Higher sample rate and bit depth than normal
- B) *Lower* sample rate and bit depth (e.g., the 12-bit grit of classic samplers) as an aesthetic
- C) Only mono samples, never stereo
- D) Warp mode set to Complex Pro

**Q7. (short answer)** A beginner says: "I changed the pitch and chopped it up, so it's legally a new work and I can release it." In two sentences, explain why that reasoning is unreliable, and name two ways they *could* release sample-based music safely.

**Q8. (short answer)** You're comparing two samples and one sounds obviously "better." What should you do before trusting that judgment, and why? (One or two sentences.)

**Q9. (short answer)** Name Simpler's three playback modes and, for each, one kind of sample you'd use it on.

**Q10. (short answer)** Explain in your own words why warping is the feature that makes Ableton Live especially suited to sample-based music. What does it let you do that a "fixed audio lump" DAW makes hard?

## Answer Key

**Q1 — C.** The composition and the master are separate rights; sampling a record uses both, and there is no length-based exemption. (D is the myth the course explicitly debunks.)

**Q2 — B.** Drift over the length of a loop is the signature of a wrong tempo guess — almost always a half/double error. Fix Seg. BPM (:2 / \*2) before touching warp modes.

**Q3 — C.** Classic mode plays the sample pitched by the keyboard; if the root/Transpose is wrong, every chord is in the wrong key. Tune first.

**Q4 — B.** Session = non-linear sketchpad (clips/scenes, no fixed time); Arrangement = linear song timeline. Both hold audio and MIDI; both ship in all editions.

**Q5 — B.** The attack onset. Where you place a slice *relative* to the transient decides whether a chopped drum hits hard or sounds weak.

**Q6 — B.** Lo-fi often *reduces* fidelity on purpose — the 12-bit/low-sample-rate grit of vintage samplers. You add limitation back as an aesthetic (Lecture 11).

**Q7.** There is no "I changed it enough" rule — even short, transformed samples can infringe both the composition and master copyrights; fair use is narrow, fact-specific, and not a reliable plan. Safe routes (any two): clear/license the sample, use royalty-free/Creative-Commons/public-domain material, official sample packs, or sample your own recordings.

**Q8.** Match their levels (Utility/clip gain) and judge them at equal loudness — louder reliably sounds "better" for a few seconds, so an unmatched comparison is biased. Then decide based on the music, not the volume.

**Q9.** **Classic** — sustained/melodic material (a chord, a held note, a pad → playable instrument). **One-Shot** — single hits (kick, snare, stab, vocal chop, SFX). **Slice** — phrases you want to chop and re-sequence (drum break, melodic loop).

**Q10.** Live auto-analyzes every clip and stretches it to any tempo independent of pitch (warping), so samples from different sources and tempos all snap to one grid and can be re-timed, re-pitched, and looped freely. A DAW that treats audio as a fixed block makes matching tempos, chopping to grid, and re-timing laborious. (Grade generously if they capture "elastic audio locked to a common grid.")
