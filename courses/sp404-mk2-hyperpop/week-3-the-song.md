# Week 3: The Song

> **The thesis of this week:** a drop is thirty seconds; a song is a shape. This week you learn the pop form hyperpop compresses (verse, pre-chorus, chorus), build the quiet sections that make your loud one land, stitch the seams with the transition craft you already own, balance maximalism against space, and mix the whole thing loud and bright on purpose. The week points at one deliverable: on Day 21 you bounce a complete, original hyperpop song, under two and a half minutes, that a stranger could follow and a phone speaker can carry. Finishing it, flaws included, is the entire point of the three weeks behind you.

Back up at the start of this week and after Day 20. Everything in the project is now irreplaceable.

# Day 15: Pop Structure vs the Beat Loop

### Objectives
- Learn the standard pop form and how hyperpop compresses it.
- Map your existing material onto a written section plan.
- Understand patterns-as-sections and the chain as the song.

### Listen (5 min)
One canon track with a timer visible. Write a timestamp at every section change. Most will land at 15–30 second intervals, and the whole song will likely run under two and a half minutes. Compressed form is not laziness; it is the genre's respect for your attention span.

### Lecture

**The standard pop form**, which every listener alive has internalized whether they know it or not:

```
intro → verse → pre-chorus → CHORUS → verse 2 → pre-chorus → CHORUS → bridge → CHORUS (out)
```

Each section has a *job*. The **verse** tells the story at low energy and makes you want the chorus. The **pre-chorus** is a ramp: it audibly aims at the chorus (your Day 13 build is a pre-chorus wearing EDM clothes). The **chorus** is the hook at full palette (your drop). The **bridge** is the one moment of genuinely different material that makes the final chorus feel new again.

**Hyperpop compresses this.** Sections of 4–8 bars at 160 BPM go by in 6–12 seconds; whole songs run 1:30–2:30; some tracks cut the second verse, or end cold after two choruses, or are all-chorus. The compression rule: **every section earns its bars or gets cut.** When in doubt, halve it. A course-recommended first-song form, totalling roughly 1:20–1:30 at 160:

| Section | Bars | Built from |
|---|---|---|
| Intro | 4 | One element (filtered stabs, or a lone vocal chop) |
| Verse 1 | 8 | Thinned loop + a new low-energy vocal (today's lecture, tomorrow's work) |
| Pre-chorus | 4–8 | The Day 13 build |
| Chorus | 8 | The Day 13 drop |
| Verse 2 / breakdown | 8 | Verse variation, or chops-led section |
| Pre-chorus | 4 | Shortened build |
| Chorus ×2 | 16 | Drop + drop variation |
| Outro | 2–4 | One element decaying, or a cold stop |

**On the machine**, a section is a pattern (or two chained), and the song is the chain of patterns, exactly the MUS-45H Day 18 mechanics at bigger scale. Patterns are cheap: build each section as its own pattern, then chain. Keep a written map (paper is fine) of pattern number → section, because by Day 20 you will have a dozen patterns and no memory of which is which.

> **Steal the skeleton, not the paint.** Map your two reference tracks' structures today, to the bar. Then take the *skeleton* (section lengths, where the first chorus lands, how it ends) for your own song. Structure is the most stealable thing in music; nobody can hear it, and everybody does it.

### Do
1. **(15 min) Map both references** with timestamps and bar counts. Note where the first chorus arrives (it is probably shockingly early).
2. **(15 min) Write your song map.** The table above, adjusted to your references' skeleton, on paper. Assign existing material to each row and mark what does not exist yet (typically: verse vocal, intro, outro).
3. **(10 min) Rough the chain.** Create the pattern slots per the map and chain what exists (intro sketch → build → drop). Play the chain; a song with holes in it is now audibly *a song with holes*, which is exactly the motivation Week 3 runs on.

### Daily drill
**Skeleton-a-day, 3 min (daily this week):** map one canon track's structure by ear with timestamps. By Day 21 you will have internalized the genre's forms, which is how future songs get written without a table.

### Self-check
1. Name the standard pop sections and each one's job in one phrase.
2. How does hyperpop compress the form, and what is the compression rule?
3. What is a section on the SP, and what is the song?
4. Why keep a written pattern map?
5. What does "steal the skeleton" mean and why is it legitimate?

# Day 16: Verses and the Pre-Chorus Lift

### Objectives
- Build a verse: the thinned palette and the low-energy vocal.
- Write and record the verse vocal fast, using the Week 2 chain.
- Sharpen the pre-chorus so it aims, not just rises.

### Listen (5 min)
One canon track: listen to what the *verse* is made of. Usually shockingly little: a bass, a thin drum pattern, one texture, and the voice much drier and closer than in the chorus. The verse is where the vocal is a person; the chorus is where it becomes a machine.

### Lecture

**The verse palette is subtraction.** Build verse patterns from the chorus loop by removing: no stabs (or filtered to a whisper), 808 sparse or replaced by a plain bass note, drums half as dense, one texture (the Day 11 vocal-synth pad is perfect here). The verse should feel like the same song with the ceiling lowered; if it feels like a different song, you removed the wrong things (keep the sounds, thin the *activity*).

**The verse vocal.** Same writing method as Day 8 (blunt phrase, small melody, rhythm first), two differences. First, verses hold more words than hooks, but at 160 BPM keep lines short and leave air; you are conversational here, not rapping double-time. Second, **process it *less***: same KEY, same chain, but consider AT PITCH slightly lower, no octave-up layer, fewer doubles, drier. The contrast between a close, human-ish verse voice and the chrome chorus stack is a huge, free arrangement tool: the *voice itself* tells the listener which section they are in.

**The pre-chorus lift.** Your Day 13 build rises; a good pre-chorus also *aims*. Three aiming devices, use at least two: a vocal line that ends on a held, unresolved note (sing into the chorus, then cut); drums that double their rate in the last two bars; the filter opening across the section (Filter+Drive sweeping bright exactly into the downbeat). And keep the gap. Always the gap.

> **Write the verse today, not perfectly.** The verse vocal is the most commonly *unwritten* part of a beginner song; people loop the hook and call it done because writing is scarier than producing. Two blunt lines, recorded committed, processed with your existing chain, beats an imaginary verse every time. You can rewrite tomorrow; you cannot arrange a blank.

### Do
1. **(10 min) Build verse patterns:** two 4-bar variants per the subtraction rules, chained as verse 1's eight bars.
2. **(20 min) Write and record the verse vocal:** two-to-four short lines, recorded through the Week 2 chain with the lighter settings, doubled once. Into bank D, into the verse patterns.
3. **(10 min) Sharpen the pre-chorus:** add two aiming devices, confirm the gap, and play intro → verse → pre → chorus end to end. The song's whole first minute now exists.

### Daily drill
**Thin-it, 3 min:** take any full pattern and make a verse of it by removing four things. Judging what survives subtraction is arrangement's core skill.

### Self-check
1. What does a verse remove, and what must it keep?
2. How does the verse vocal's processing differ from the chorus's, and what does that contrast buy?
3. Name the three aiming devices of a pre-chorus.
4. Why is the verse the most commonly unwritten section, and what is the rule against that?
5. What tells the listener which section they are in, besides the instruments?

# Day 17: Transitions and Glitch Craft

### Objectives
- Dress every seam in the song map with the right-sized transition.
- Use stutters, tape stops, silence, and fills as section punctuation.
- Build the bridge (or second-drop variation) from glitch material.

### Listen (5 min)
Your favorite canon track, seams only, again (Day 12's exercise, wiser ears). This time rank the seams by size: the biggest gesture in the song is almost always into the final chorus. Transitions have a hierarchy, and today you build yours.

### Lecture

You own all the parts already (bank F, Day 11's gestures, Day 12's candy); today is placement, and the craft is **matching the transition's size to the seam's importance**:

| Seam | Size | Typical dressing |
|---|---|---|
| Intro → verse | Small | A fill, or one reversed hit |
| Verse → pre | Small–medium | Riser starts, a drum change announces |
| Pre → chorus | Big | The full Day 13 apparatus: riser, gap, impact |
| Chorus → verse 2 | Medium, *deflating* | Downlifter, exhale, tape stop into the thinner world |
| Into final chorus | Biggest | Everything you have: the longest riser, the deepest gap, or the one trick you saved |
| Ending | A statement | Cold stop, tape stop, or one element decaying |

Two placement rules. **Announce exits, not just entrances:** the bar before a section ends should already be leaving (a fill, a stutter, the candy starting), or the change feels like a splice. **Save one trick:** whatever your most striking gesture is (the pitch-ramp stutter, the Back Spin, the ROBOT line alone in silence), use it exactly once, at the biggest seam. Signature moves spend like currency; spent everywhere, they buy nothing.

**The bridge, the glitch way.** Your song map has one section of genuinely different material. The cheapest good bridge in this genre is **the song eating itself**: take the printed hook or chorus loop, and wreck it beautifully for four-to-eight bars: chopped and re-ordered (Day 11), through Scatter or the DJFX Looper resampled, half-time with the pitch dropped, or the vocal synth playing the hook melody alone. The listener hears familiar material made strange, which is the bridge's whole job, and it costs no new writing.

> **Transitions are heard once but felt every time.** A listener cannot name a single one of these gestures, but remove them and the song feels cheap; add too many and it feels nervous. The test is always the end-to-end playthrough, never the seam in isolation.

### Do
1. **(20 min) Dress every seam** in the map per the size table, working start to end. Play across each seam, then the full chain.
2. **(15 min) Build the bridge:** wreck the hook by one of the recipes, four-to-eight bars, into its pattern slot. Make sure it *deflates* first (the bridge sits low so the last chorus towers).
3. **(5 min) The playthrough.** Full song, eyes closed. Note the two worst moments; they are tomorrow's targets.

### Daily drill
**Seam-size call, 3 min:** in one canon track, name each seam's size and dressing. Checking your hierarchy against real records calibrates placement taste fast.

### Self-check
1. Why do transitions have a hierarchy, and which seam gets the biggest?
2. What does "announce exits" mean?
3. What is the save-one-trick rule?
4. Give two recipes for a bridge that requires no new writing.
5. Why judge transitions only in the full playthrough?

# Day 18: Maximalism and Space

### Objectives
- Understand dynamics as the genre's engine: loud is a relationship, not a level.
- Audit the song's energy curve and fix its flat spots.
- Layer the chorus to maximal without mud.

### Listen (5 min)
The loudest canon track you know, watching for the quietest moment in it. It is there, and it is placed *just before* the loudest one. Then notice how few simultaneous elements the loudest bar actually holds. Maximalism is stage-managed. Today you manage yours.

### Lecture

**The energy curve.** Draw your song as a line: height = how much is happening (element count × activity × brightness). The pop-form curve rises and falls in waves, each chorus a peak, each verse a valley, the final chorus the highest point. Two failure shapes, both common: **the plateau** (everything 80% loud always, so nothing is loud; the beginner-maximalist trap) and **the sawtooth of accidents** (energy jumping randomly because sections were built on different days at different enthusiasm levels; the fix is auditing sections *against each other*, exactly today).

**Loud is a relationship.** The chorus is only as loud as the verse is quiet. If the song plateaus, the fix is nearly always to make the quiet parts quieter (thin the verses further, lower the intro's ceiling, deepen the bridge's deflation), not the loud parts louder. You learned this at drop scale (Day 13); it governs the whole song.

**Maximal without mud**, for the chorus layers, three rules:

- **One king per range.** Low: the 808 rules, kick punches through it, nothing else lives there. Mid: the vocal rules; stabs interlock around it (Day 6's hole is now occupied; if stabs fight the voice, re-voice the stab or duck its level). High: hats and candy, and they take turns.
- **Add by role, not by pile.** Every chorus layer needs a nameable job (width, shimmer, thickness-under-lead). A layer whose job you cannot name is mud on retainer. The Day 10 stack discipline, applied to the whole mix.
- **Motion beats addition.** If the chorus feels static, before adding a layer, *move* one: a filter opening across eight bars, the octave layer entering at bar 5, one chop going feral in the last bar. Motion reads as "more" without costing space.

> **The mute test.** For every layer in the chorus: mute it. If the section does not miss it, it never comes back. Run this ruthlessly today; every survivor earns Day 19's mixing attention, and mud dies here, a day before the mix, where it is cheap to kill.

### Do
1. **(10 min) Draw the curve.** Play the song, draw the energy line, mark plateaus and accidents against the pop-form wave shape.
2. **(15 min) Fix by widening the range:** quiet parts quieter first, then one motion added to any static peak. Replay, redraw.
3. **(15 min) The mute test** on every chorus layer, then the range check (one king per range) on the survivors. Print any newly-final stacks.

### Daily drill
**Curve-a-track, 3 min:** draw a canon track's energy curve from one listen. Hearing shape as shape, at speed, is the arranger's whole ear.

### Self-check
1. What are the two failure shapes of an energy curve?
2. When a song plateaus, what is the fix, and why?
3. Name the three maximal-without-mud rules.
4. What is the mute test and why run it before mix day?
5. What does "motion beats addition" mean, with an example?

# Day 19: The Loud, Bright Mix

### Objectives
- Balance the finished arrangement: levels first, then carve, then glue.
- Distinguish deliberate distortion from accidental clipping, and keep headroom at the master.
- Verify translation: mono, phone speaker, and the playlist test.

### Listen (5 min)
Your sound-reference track at low volume, then loud. Notice it survives both: nothing disappears quiet, nothing shreds loud. That robustness is the mix. Then play your song after it, once, and just note the first difference you hear; that is where today starts.

### Lecture

Everything from MUS-45H Day 19 (levels first, carve with filters, mono check, multi-speaker check) still applies word for word. What hyperpop adds is a harder version of the loudness question, because this genre's records are *hot*, and the beginner conclusion ("so I should slam everything into the red") produces the accidental-clipping mush that marks amateur hyperpop from the first bar.

**The distinction that runs today: distortion on *elements* is an aesthetic; clipping at the *master* is an accident.** Your 808 is distorted, your snare is clipped, your vocal may be crushed: all chosen, all printed, all yours. The master output, the final sum, stays clean with real headroom, exactly the MUS-45H rule. Records get their final loudness from mastering, done with dedicated tools after mixing; on this machine, your job is a clean, balanced, bright mix, and it will be plenty loud from the element-level violence alone. If the master meter hits red, something comes *down*; you never fix the sum by definition.

**The order of operations:**

1. **Levels, chorus first.** Mix the loudest section first (drums → 808 → vocal stack → stabs → candy, the Day 6 order plus voice on top); every other section inherits and thins from it. The vocal sits *on top of* the wall, always audible: this is pop.
2. **Carve.** The Equalizer where two kings fight (kick/808 low-end from Day 4; stab-vs-vocal mids from Day 18). High-pass anything that does not need lows; a dozen layers each contributing inaudible rumble is a muddy master with no visible culprit.
3. **Glue.** The Compressor across the drum bus for punch, gently; and taming, at last, Week 1's deliberate over-brightness with the Equalizer where it genuinely hurts (harshness fatigues; brightness excites; the line between them is where your ears wince).
4. **Verify.** Mono: nothing vanishes. Phone: 808 reads, vocal reads, snare cracks. Quiet listen: still all there. Then **the playlist test**: bounce, put it in a playlist between your two references, walk away for an hour, come back and listen cold. Note every wince; wince notes are tomorrow's punch list.

> **Mix toward your reference, not toward louder.** After every change, thirty seconds of the reference. Ears acclimate to whatever they marinate in; the reference is the fixed star. If matching the reference means turning your favorite layer down, that is the job.

### Do
1. **(20 min) Mix the chorus** by the order of operations, referencing every few minutes.
2. **(10 min) Inherit outward:** verses, pre, bridge inherit the chorus balances, adjusted for their thinner palettes. Full playthrough for level jumps between sections.
3. **(10 min) Verify:** mono, phone, quiet, then bounce and set up the playlist test. Write the punch list cold, later today.

### Daily drill
**Reference thirty, every session from now on:** thirty seconds of the reference before and after any mix decision. Not optional today or ever.

### Self-check
1. What is the element-vs-master distortion distinction, and why is it the day's spine?
2. Why mix the chorus first?
3. What are the four order-of-operations stages?
4. What is the playlist test and why the hour away?
5. When the master clips, what is the only legitimate fix?

# Day 20: Final Workshop + Project 3

Consolidation and polish; no new material. Three jobs:

### 1. The punch list
Work yesterday's cold-listen notes, worst first. Small fixes only: a level, a seam, one re-recorded vocal line if one truly fails. Day 20 is not the day to rewrite the bridge; a finished song with a serviceable bridge beats an unfinished one with a brilliant plan.

### 2. Project 3: The Arranged Demo
Do **Project 3** (see `PROJECTS.md`): the full song, start to end, bounced, graded on structure, contrast, transitions, and mix translation. This bounce should be within polish-distance of the final.

### 3. The finisher's checklist
Run the whole song against it, fixing what fails:

- **Form:** can a stranger tell verse from chorus with their eyes closed? Does the first chorus arrive early enough?
- **The voice:** hook audible and on top in every chorus; verse voice human and close; no line buried that you meant to be heard.
- **Contrast:** is the quietest moment genuinely quiet? Does the final chorus tower over the first?
- **Seams:** every section change announced; the one saved trick spent at the biggest seam; no splice-feel anywhere.
- **Low end:** 808 tuned and phone-audible; kick and 808 taking turns, never wrestling.
- **Candy:** marks structure without hiding it (mute-test the candy; the form must survive bare).
- **Mix:** every layer passes the mute test; master clean with headroom; survives mono, phone, and quiet.
- **The playlist test:** cold, between the references. Wince count acceptably low.
- **Finish:** would you show a friend without narrating excuses first?

### Looking ahead
Tomorrow you take the final exam and bounce the song. The song will not be perfect. Its job was never perfection; its job was to be *finished*, to force every skill in the chain (design, writing, recording, processing, structure, mix) through one complete rep. The second rep is faster. That is the entire theory of this course.

### A final word
Three weeks ago hyperpop was a word and your voice was something you avoided hearing recorded. Now there is a song that exists because you designed every drum in it, found its key by ear, wrote something true enough to say into a microphone, built the machine-voice that says it, and made a shape out of loud and quiet that a stranger can follow. That is not a small thing. That is the whole job, done once.

The genre you now speak was built by people who did exactly this on similar budgets and posted the results the same week, rough edges out for everyone to hear, and got great in public.
