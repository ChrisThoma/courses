# Week 1: The Hyperpop Sound

> **The thesis of this week:** hyperpop is not a vibe, it is a buildable palette. Under the chaos, nearly every track in the genre stands on the same three legs: hard bright drums, a distorted 808, and a wall of detuned synth. This week you learn where the sound came from, then you build all three legs on the SP, from raw sources, with your own hands. By Day 7 you have an eight-bar loop at full energy that is unmistakably *this genre*, and a palette of sounds you designed that the rest of the course is built from. The voice comes next week; the voice needs a world to live in first.

A housekeeping note before Day 1: this course lives in **its own project** on the SP. Make one, name it, and put nothing else in it. You will fill most of its banks by Day 21.

# Day 1: What Hyperpop Is

### Objectives
- Trace the genre's short history: PC Music, SOPHIE, 100 gecs, the online wave.
- Name the defining elements of the sound and hear them in a real track.
- Start the daily habit of analytical listening with a concrete checklist.

### Listen (10 min)
Three tracks, in order, from the [Field Guide §1](FIELD-GUIDE.md) canon: one early PC Music track (Hannah Diamond or A. G. Cook), one SOPHIE track, and one from 100 gecs' *1000 gecs*. Do not multitask. Listen for one thing: **what has been exaggerated?** Every hyperpop move is a normal pop move pushed past taste on purpose.

### Lecture

Hyperpop has an unusually traceable origin story for a genre. In **2013**, London producer **A. G. Cook** founded **PC Music**, a label and art collective whose releases treated pop music as a material to exaggerate: vocals higher and glossier than any human, synths brighter than any instrument, sentiment sweeter than any real feeling. Critics called it **bubblegum bass**. Around the same label orbit, **SOPHIE** was building the sound's engineering wing: metallic, physical synthesis, enormous sub-bass, and pop hooks fused to genuinely abrasive texture. SOPHIE's production on **Charli XCX's *Vroom Vroom* EP (2016)** pushed the sound toward the mainstream, and Charli has carried it there ever since.

Then in **2019**, two Americans, Dylan Brady and Laura Les, released ***1000 gecs*** as **100 gecs**: twenty-three minutes of bubblegum bass collided with nightcore, ska, metal, and trance, and it detonated online. That August, **a Spotify editor created a playlist called "hyperpop"** to hold this music, and the playlist named the genre. A wave of very young artists on SoundCloud and Discord (the **digicore** scene: glaive, ericdoa, osquinn, and hundreds more) took the sound somewhere faster, sadder, and cheaper to make, often on laptops in bedrooms, often finished in a night. That last fact matters to you: this genre's founding documents were made quickly, with modest tools, by people who learned in public. An SP-404 and a cheap mic is not a handicap here. It is roughly the budget the scene started on.

**The sound itself** decomposes into a checklist, and this checklist is the course syllabus in miniature:

| Element | The treatment | Where you learn it |
|---|---|---|
| Drums | Hard, bright, clipped; metallic; stuttering | Day 3 |
| Bass | Distorted 808, audible on a phone | Day 4 |
| Synths | Bright detuned walls, trance DNA | Day 5 |
| Vocals | Pitched, hard-tuned, stacked; a synth made of voice | Days 8–11 |
| Ear candy | Risers, impacts, tape stops, glitch | Day 12 |
| Form | Compressed pop structure with EDM drops | Days 13, 15–17 |
| Dynamics | Violent contrast between sparse and maximal | Day 18 |

One more defining trait, harder to put in a table: **sincerity and irony at full volume simultaneously.** The genre is emotionally direct (loneliness, love, the internet) and aesthetically absurd at the same time, and neither cancels the other. Keep that in mind when you write your hook next week; blank cool does not work in this genre. Committed does.

> **The exaggeration test.** From today on, when you hear any hyperpop track, ask of each element: "what is the normal pop version of this, and how far past normal has it been pushed?" A tuned vocal becomes a chrome robot. A clap becomes a gunshot. A saw chord becomes a wall. This one question is most of the genre ear the Rubric grades.

### Do
1. **(15 min) Analyze two tracks with the checklist.** Pick two songs from the canon and fill in the table from the lecture for each: what are the drums, the bass, the synths, the vocal treatment, the structure? Write it down; keep these notes, they become your reference targets.
2. **(10 min) Pick your references.** Choose **two reference tracks** for the song you will make this course: one for *sound* (the palette you want) and one for *energy* (the shape you want). You will A/B against these for three weeks, so choose tracks you can stand to hear fifty times.
3. **(5 min) Set up the project.** New project on the SP, named for this course. Set the tempo to **160 BPM** as a starting home (you can change it, but start fast; the genre lives above standard pop tempo).

### Daily drill
**One-move-a-day listening, 5 min:** one hyperpop track, and name one production move (a vocal treatment, a drum choice, a transition) precisely enough that you could attempt it. This drill runs every day for the rest of the course.

### Self-check
1. What was PC Music, who founded it, and roughly when?
2. What did SOPHIE contribute to the sound, and which Charli XCX release carried it toward the mainstream?
3. How did the genre get its name?
4. Name the three instrumental legs of the palette and the vocal treatment that sits on top.
5. What is the "exaggeration test"?

# Day 2: The Machine, Through a Hyperpop Lens

### Objectives
- Confirm (or crash-refresh) the MUS-45H workflow: record, trim, chop, pattern, resample.
- Meet the specific effects this course lives on and where they sit.
- Set up the project's banks so three weeks of material stays findable.

### Listen (5 min)
One 100 gecs track. Count the distinct sounds you hear in the drop. It is fewer than it seems, usually well under ten. Maximalism is a few sounds pushed hard, arranged violently. Your machine holds 160 pads per project; you will not run out.

### Lecture

Today is the bridge day between the beatmaking course and this one. Everything below should read as review; where it does not, stop and drill that move today, because every later day assumes it.

**The five moves this course assumes cold** (all taught in [MUS-45H](#/sp404-mk2-sampling), day references in parentheses):

1. **Record a clean sample** from line, USB, or mic, level healthy, never clipping (Day 3 there).
2. **Trim it tight**: start point on the transient, no dead air, no clicks (Day 4).
3. **Chop it**: markers by hand or auto, assigned across pads (Day 8).
4. **Program a pattern**: TR-REC for grids, real-time for feel, quantize and swing (Day 11).
5. **Resample**: print pads-plus-effects to a new pad and keep building (Day 13). This is the spine here even more than there, because hyperpop sounds are *stacks*, and stacks eat voices until you print them.

**The effects shelf.** Skim the table in [Field Guide §3](FIELD-GUIDE.md) with the machine on, and turn each of these on for ten seconds against any sample so your ear files it: **Crusher** (digital grit), **Distortion** (violence), **Filter+Drive** (movement), **Compressor** (punch), **DJFX Looper** and **Scatter** (glitch), **Stopper** (tape stop). Also confirm where **Auto Pitch** lives in the input-FX list; you will learn it with the mic in Week 2. That shelf, plus delay and reverb, is ninety percent of the course's sound design.

**Bank discipline.** A three-week song project sprawls. Adopt this map today and keep to it:

| Bank | Contents |
|---|---|
| A | Drum kit (kicks, snares/claps, hats, percussion) |
| B | 808s and bass notes |
| C | Synth notes, chords, stabs |
| D | Vocal takes (leads, doubles, safeties) |
| E | Vocal chops and processed vocal layers |
| F | Ear candy: risers, impacts, transitions |
| G–I | Resampled sections, loops, works-in-progress |
| J | Scratch: experiments, happy accidents |

> **Speed is the point of today.** In this genre the distance between an idea and a pad has to be short, because the ideas are small and fast (a stutter, a pitch jump, a one-bar joke). If any of the five moves still requires thinking, drill it now. Menu time is where hooks die.

### Do
1. **(10 min) The cold-start test.** From power-on: record any sound off your phone, trim it, chop it into four pieces, put two of them in a one-bar pattern at 160 BPM, and resample the result to a new pad. Time yourself. Under ten minutes is passing; under five is where you want to be by Week 3.
2. **(10 min) The effects tour.** Run one drum loop through each shelf effect from the lecture, ten seconds each. No tweaking, just filing the flavors.
3. **(10 min) Set up the banks** per the map, and back up the empty-but-organized project so your backup habit starts on a clean state.

### Daily drill
**Cold-start reps, 5 min:** one sound from the world to a trimmed, playable pad, as fast as clean allows. Speed drill, run daily this week.

### Self-check
1. What are the five assumed moves, and which one is the spine of this course's workflow?
2. Why does hyperpop production punish slow menu navigation more than lo-fi beatmaking does?
3. Which effect is the hard-tune? Which two are the glitch performers? Which is the tape stop?
4. What lives in banks A through F in this course's map?
5. Why does resampling matter more when your sounds are stacks?

# Day 3: Drums 1, The Hyperpop Kit

### Objectives
- Understand what separates a hyperpop drum palette from a lo-fi or boom-bap one.
- Build a four-piece kit: hard kick, clipped snare/clap, bright hat, one metallic percussion.
- Use Crusher, Distortion, and Compressor as drum design tools, printed by resampling.

### Listen (5 min)
A SOPHIE track, drums only in your attention. Notice the percussion barely sounds like drums: it sounds like metal, latex, water, glass. Then a 100 gecs drop: the snare is so clipped it is almost a square wave. Neither palette came out of a sample pack sounding like that.

### Lecture

In MUS-45H you built kits that sat *behind* a sample: warm, dusty, a little soft. Hyperpop inverts every one of those choices. The drums sit **in front**, and they are **hard, bright, and short**.

**The four-piece and its treatment:**

- **Kick:** short, punchy, more click than boom (the boom is the 808's job, and they must not fight; that is tomorrow's whole lesson). Take any clean kick and make it harder: **Distortion** or **Overdrive** to bring up the mids, then trim the tail short. If the kick carries a long sub tail, it will smear against the 808.
- **Snare/clap:** the genre's signature violence. Layer a snare and a clap on one pad region, then **clip it**: Distortion pushed until the transient flattens into a slap. Crusher instead of (or after) Distortion gives the digital, bit-torn version. Both are period-correct; pick per song.
- **Hi-hat:** bright and tight. Closed hats, short, often crushed slightly so they spit. Rolls and stutters (a run of 1/16 or 1/32 hits, often with rising pitch) are a genre signature; you will program them with TR-REC today and perform them with the DJFX Looper later.
- **The metallic one:** the SOPHIE inheritance. One percussion voice that sounds like an *object*: a pan hit, a can, a knuckle on glass, keys dropped on a table, recorded with your mic, pitched and clipped. This single self-made sound will do more for your palette's identity than any downloaded pack.

**The design loop, which you will run dozens of times this course:** pick a raw source → put an effect on its bus → push it further than taste → back off ten percent → **resample** → trim the print. The printed pad is your new drum. Keep the raw source; you will want variants.

> **Hard is a transient, not a volume.** Beginners make "hard" drums by turning them up, and get a loud mush. Hardness lives in the attack: distortion flattening a transient, a trim that starts exactly on the hit, a Compressor snapping the front. If your kit only sounds hard when it is loud, it is not hard yet.

### Do
1. **(10 min) Source the raw four.** From royalty-free packs, the SP's included content, or your own recordings: a kick, a snare, a clap, a closed hat, plus record one metallic object with the mic. Into bank A, trimmed.
2. **(20 min) Design the kit.** Run the design loop on each piece: kick through Overdrive (short tail), snare+clap layered and clipped with Distortion, hat through a touch of Crusher, metal hit pitched to taste and clipped. Resample each print to a clean pad. A/B each against your sound-reference track.
3. **(10 min) Program the proof.** A one-bar pattern at 160: kick pattern, snare on the backbeat, a hat run with one 1/16-note roll. It should already sound like the genre with no other elements.

### Daily drill
**One-object percussion, 3 min:** record one household sound, clip it, and judge: keep or toss. A personal percussion library grows three minutes at a time.

### Self-check
1. Name three ways a hyperpop kit inverts a lo-fi kit.
2. Why should the kick be short and mid-forward in this genre?
3. What is the snare/clap treatment that defines the sound, and which two effects get you there?
4. What is "the metallic one" and why does it matter for identity?
5. Where does hardness actually live, if not in volume?

# Day 4: The 808

### Objectives
- Understand what an 808 is and why hyperpop distorts it.
- Design a distorted 808 from a single note and tune it by ear.
- Play a bassline in chromatic mode and lock it with the kick.

### Listen (5 min)
Any modern hyperpop or digicore track on a **phone speaker**. The bass is still there. A clean sine bass would vanish on that speaker; the distortion is what puts harmonics up where a tiny speaker can reproduce them. That is the whole reason this sound exists, and it came into hyperpop from trap and SoundCloud rap.

### Lecture

The **808** (named for the Roland TR-808 drum machine's kick) is a long, pitched, sine-heavy boom used as a bass instrument. Hyperpop borrowed the **distorted** 808 from rap production and pushed it, as usual, further.

**Designing one from a single note:**

1. **Source a sine-ish low note:** a royalty-free 808 one-shot, a bass note from any synth or app, the SP's Sound Generator, or the purest low tone you can find. One clean note is enough.
2. **Trim** so the start is exactly on the attack, and let it sustain long.
3. **Drive it:** Overdrive for a warm, round saturation; Distortion for the aggressive, square-ish version; Crusher for the digital scream. Push until the note is audibly harmonic on a small speaker, then back off ten percent.
4. **Resample the print.** This printed 808 is now bank B's foundation.
5. **Tune it:** enter chromatic mode with the 808, play against your Day 3 pattern, and find the note that feels like home. That home note is, for practical purposes, **your song's key**, and you will feed it to Auto Pitch's KEY parameter next week. Write it down by pad position if you have not learned note names; the machine does not care.

**Playing the bassline.** In chromatic mode, sequence a pattern (the SP records patterns in chromatic mode since firmware 2.00). Genre-typical basslines are simple: the home note held long, a jump up or down at the turn of the phrase, maybe an octave stab. **Simple and tuned beats busy and sour, every time.**

**The kick relationship.** The kick and the 808 want the same low territory, and two things can share it only by taking turns. Your options, best first: (a) **arrange around it**, kick hits at the 808 note's start, not during its sustain; (b) **shorten** one of them (usually the kick, per Day 3); (c) **carve** with Equalizer, rolling lows off the kick. If kick-plus-808 sounds like one fat punch, you got it; if it sounds like mud or a flam, it is fighting.

> **The phone test is the 808 test.** After every bass decision this course, play the loop on your phone speaker. If the bassline reads there, it reads everywhere. If it vanishes, you need more drive or a different octave, not more volume.

### Do
1. **(15 min) Build two 808s.** One Overdrive (round), one Distortion or Crusher (violent), from the same source note. Resample both prints to bank B. Phone-test both.
2. **(15 min) Tune and write.** Chromatic mode, find home against your drum pattern, then sequence a one-bar bassline: long home note, one move. Record the pattern in chromatic mode.
3. **(10 min) Lock the kick.** Play kick plus 808 alone, no other elements. Fix any fight by arrangement first, then length, then EQ. Confirm on the phone.

### Daily drill
**Find-home fast, 3 min:** any loop, chromatic 808, find the home note by ear as fast as you can. The same MUS-45H ear drill, now with consequences: this note becomes your Auto Pitch KEY.

### Self-check
1. What is an 808, and where does the name come from?
2. Why does distorting an 808 make it *more* audible on small speakers?
3. List the five steps of the 808 design loop.
4. How do you find the song's key with no music theory?
5. Name the three fixes for a kick/808 fight, in preference order.

# Day 5: Synths, The Wall of Saw

### Objectives
- Understand the supersaw sound and its trance lineage.
- Build a playable synth from one or two sampled notes.
- Fatten sampled synths with layering, detune-by-repitch, JUNO Chorus, and resampling.
- Sequence a chord stab pattern and a simple lead.

### Listen (5 min)
Charli XCX's *Vroom Vroom* EP, or any A. G. Cook production: the huge, bright, slightly-sour chord stabs. That is the **supersaw** inheritance, many detuned sawtooth waves stacked into one chord, imported into hyperpop straight from 1990s trance and eurodance. It is the genre's sound of joy.

### Lecture

A **sawtooth wave** is the buzzy, bright waveform that most dance-music synth sounds start from. A **supersaw** stacks several saws, each detuned slightly from the others, so they shimmer and beat against each other into a wall. Current SP firmware includes a Sound Generator, but this course leans on the workflow that matters most for the genre: **a sampler, chromatic mode, and the resample loop.**

**The sampled-synth workflow:**

1. **Source one clean sustained saw note** (and ideally the same note an octave up). Roland Cloud content, the SP's Sound Generator, any royalty-free synth pack, or a note recorded from any keyboard app. Into bank C.
2. **Chromatic mode makes it an instrument.** Play it against your loop; the same "find home" ear from yesterday applies, and home should agree with the 808's home.
3. **Fatten it, the sampler way.** True supersaws detune oscillators; you fake it convincingly by **stacking repitched copies**: resample the note layered with a copy of itself pitched a few cents-equivalent off (use the pitch knob's finest steps, or Chromatic PS on a parallel layer), then run **JUNO Chorus** over the stack. Chorus is the honest shortcut: it *is* detune-and-stack in a box. Print with resample.
4. **Chords by layering pitched copies.** Chromatic mode is monophonic, so do not try to play chords inside it. Instead, duplicate the saw note to two or three regular pads, tune those copies to different notes with Pitch/Speed or Chromatic PS, trigger the regular pads together, and resample the result: that print is a **chord stab**, one pad, one voice. Build two or three stabs that sound good in sequence against your bassline (ears, not theory: keep what locks, toss what sours), and you have the harmony of your song.
5. **Sequence.** Stabs on off-beats or driving 1/8s for energy; a simple lead line on the single-note instrument on top. In this genre the stab rhythm *is* the hook's engine.

> **Bright now, tamed later.** Sampled saws through Chorus can get harsh. Leave them bright this week; the mix day (Day 19) tames harshness with the Equalizer once everything else is in. Designing timid sounds now to avoid harshness is how tracks end up small.

### Do
1. **(10 min) Make the instrument.** One saw note sourced, trimmed, fattened per the workflow, printed. Test it chromatically against yesterday's loop.
2. **(15 min) Build three stabs.** Three chord prints that sound good in a row over your bassline. Name their pads so you can find them.
3. **(15 min) Sequence the synth layer.** Add to your pattern: a stab rhythm plus, if it wants one, a four-note lead. Keep the loop to these elements: drums, 808, stabs, maybe lead. A/B with your energy reference.

### Daily drill
**Stab-lock, 3 min:** duplicate your saw to a few regular pads, tune the copies to different notes, trigger two- and three-pad combinations over the bassline, and keep any combination that locks as a new stab candidate. Harmony by ear, built daily.

### Self-check
1. What is a supersaw, and what genre did hyperpop inherit it from?
2. How do you fake oscillator detune on a sampler?
3. What does JUNO Chorus do to a stacked saw, and why is it the honest shortcut?
4. How do you build a chord stab with no theory?
5. Why keep sounds bright this week instead of taming them now?

# Day 6: The First Hyperpop Loop, then Quiz 1

### Objectives
- Assemble drums, 808, and synths into one eight-bar loop at full energy.
- Balance the three legs so each is audible and the loop reads on a phone.
- Take Quiz 1.

### Listen (5 min)
Your two reference tracks, then nothing else. Today you A/B against them repeatedly, and your ear needs their scale of energy fresh.

### Lecture

Assembly day. Everything you built this week goes into **one eight-bar loop** that could sit under a chorus. The assembly order matters and is the same order you will use for every section of the song:

1. **Drums first, alone,** until the pattern slaps by itself. If the drums do not carry energy alone, more layers will bury the problem, not fix it.
2. **808 in.** Kick relationship checked (Day 4 rules). Phone test.
3. **Stabs in.** The stab rhythm should interlock with the drums, not shadow them; if the stab hits everywhere the kick hits, offset one of them.
4. **Lead last, and optional.** Space is being saved for the vocal; a loop that is already full has no room for next week.

Two eight-bar craft points. **Variation:** bars 1–4 and 5–8 should differ by one small thing (a fill, a bass move, a dropped hat) so the loop breathes. **The reference check:** play four bars of the reference, then your loop, back to back, and ask three questions: is mine as *fast-feeling*? As *bright*? As *hard*? Fix the worst gap, not all three, then check again.

> **Leave a hole where the voice goes.** Professional instrumentals sound slightly empty in the mid-range, on purpose; that is the vocal's parking spot. If your loop sounds complete with no vocal, it is overfull. The best check: hum any melody over it. If humming feels crowded out, thin the stabs or drop the lead.

### Do
1. **(25 min) Assemble the loop** in the order above, with one variation between halves. Resample the finished loop to a pad in bank G, and also keep the pattern (you will rearrange its parts in Week 3; the print is for reference and layering).
2. **(5 min) The three-question reference check.** Fix the worst gap.
3. **(30–40 min) Quiz 1** in `QUIZZES.md`, closed book. Grade against `ANSWER-KEYS.md`. Today may run longer than the normal daily block; the quiz is the assessment.

### Daily drill
Skip today; the quiz is the drill.

### Self-check
Covered by Quiz 1.

# Day 7: Studio Day + Project 1

A consolidation day, exactly like MUS-45H's. Three jobs, in order:

### 1. Catch up
Any day this week that is shaky, redo its Do-block. Priority order if time is short: Day 4 (the 808 and the key; Week 2 depends on it), then Day 3, then Day 5. Day 2's cold-start speed can keep improving through drills.

### 2. Project 1: The Palette + An Eight-Bar Loop
Do **Project 1** (see `PROJECTS.md`). It formalizes this week: a designed kit, two 808s, a synth instrument and stabs, and the assembled eight-bar loop, graded on hardness, tuning, phone-survival, and honesty of the A/B against your reference.

### 3. Back up
The weekly backup, and from now on your project contains real design work. Do it before you power off.

### Looking ahead
You now have the world your song lives in: drums, low end, harmony, at the right speed and the right violence. What it does not have is the thing every listener will actually remember, the voice. Next week you write a hook, record yourself saying it, and learn the processing chain (Auto Pitch, formant, pitch-up stacks) that turns a normal human voice into the genre's signature instrument. Charge the mic. Warn the housemates.
