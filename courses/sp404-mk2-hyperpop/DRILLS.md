# Practice Menu (Drills): MUS-47H

The lessons prescribe a default daily drill, but no single drill fits everyone every day. This is the full menu: **pick one drill per active skill and rotate every few days** so nothing goes stale. Two things need *daily, lifelong* reps: the **genre ear** (A) and the **mic habit** (D); keep those going forever, or at least until the album is done.

**Building your daily ~30 minutes:** 5 min power up + a cold-start or pad warm-up (B) · 5 min the week's focus skill (C/D/E/F) · 5 min genre ear (A) · 10–15 min working on the actual song · a reference A/B before you stop.

> **The one rule above all drills:** *record and commit.* The failure modes of this course are silence (never recording the voice) and dithering (never printing a decision). Every drill below ends with either a take banked or a resample printed. Deciding is the skill.

---

## A. The genre ear (start Day 1; every day, no exceptions)

| Drill | How | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **A1: One-move-a-day** | One hyperpop track; name one production move precisely enough to attempt it | the whole genre vocabulary |
| **A2: Exaggeration test** | For each element of a track, name the normal pop version and how far it was pushed | hearing the genre's logic, not just its surface |
| **A3: Skeleton-a-day** | Map a track's structure with timestamps and bar counts from one listen | internalizing the forms (Week 3's core) |
| **A4: Curve-a-track** | Draw a track's energy curve from one listen | hearing shape as shape |
| **A5: Seam-size call** | Name each section seam's size and its transition dressing | calibrating transitions against real records |

## B. The machine: speed (start Day 2)

| Drill | How | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **B1: Cold-start reps** | A sound from the world to a trimmed, playable pad, fast | idea-to-pad speed, where hooks live or die |
| **B2: Ten-minute pipeline** | Record, trim, chop, pattern, resample, timed | keeping the MUS-45H loop automatic |
| **B3: Weekly backup** | Back up to SD or computer; confirm it exists | the habit that protects your voice takes |
| **B4: Bank hygiene** | Five minutes returning strays to the bank map | finding anything in a 160-pad project |

## C. Palette design (start Day 3)

| Drill | How | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **C1: One-object percussion** | Record a household sound, clip it, keep or toss | a personal, clean drum library |
| **C2: Design-loop rep** | Raw source → effect pushed too far → back off 10% → resample → trim | the course's core sound-design move |
| **C3: Find-home fast** | Any loop, chromatic 808, find the home note by ear, timed | the key-finding ear everything depends on |
| **C4: Stab-lock** | Duplicate one synth note to regular pads, tune the copies, trigger two- and three-pad combos over the bassline; keep what locks | harmony by ear |
| **C5: Phone-test pass** | Play the current loop on the phone speaker; name what vanishes | the 808 and mix reality check |

## D. The voice (start Day 8; daily once started)

| Drill | How | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **D1: One-take warm-up** | One throwaway take of anything before the day's work | keeping the mic ordinary and commitment warm |
| **D2: Play the tuner** | Input Auto Pitch on; hold notes, slide between them, feel the snap | learning hard-tune as an instrument |
| **D3: Double-tightness rep** | Record a line, then a double matching its rhythm; listen for flams | the stack's foundation |
| **D4: One-layer audition** | Mute/unmute each stack layer; name each one's job | stack hygiene |
| **D5: Blunt-phrase five** | Write five true, plain hook candidates in five minutes | the writing muscle, kept warm |

## E. Chops, candy & glitch (start Day 11)

| Drill | How | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **E1: One-syllable jam** | One vocal chop, one drum pattern, find usable one-bar gestures | performed glitch |
| **E2: One-candy-a-day** | Make one riser, impact, or texture from existing banks | the self-made transition library |
| **E3: Gesture timing** | Stopper/Back Spin at bar boundaries until musical; resample the best | transitions that land in time |
| **E4: Ramp-stutter rep** | Build one pitch-ramp stutter from any chop | the genre's watermark move, made reflex |

## F. Arrangement & finishing (start Day 15)

| Drill | How | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **F1: Thin-it** | Make a verse of any full pattern by removing four things | subtraction, the arranger's core skill |
| **F2: Mute test** | Mute each layer of a section; cut what is not missed | mud prevention |
| **F3: Reference thirty** | 30 s of the reference before and after any mix decision | the fixed star for drifting ears |
| **F4: Finish-something** | Take any sketch to a bounced file | the rarest and most valuable skill |

## Rules that make any drill work

1. **Record even when you do not want to.** Especially then. The voice drills only work if the mic stays ordinary.
2. **Commit your moves.** When something sounds good, resample it and keep going. Endless tweaking is not production.
3. **Back up weekly, and after vocal sessions.** Your takes are the only unrecoverable data you have.
4. **Phone-test the low end.** Your monitors and your memory both flatter the 808; the phone does not.
5. **Reference before you trust your ears.** An hour inside your own track recalibrates you to it; the reference resets the scale.
6. **Keep one number.** Finished sections, banked takes, or your cold-start time. The line going up is the motivation.
7. **When a drill gets easy, retire it** and pull the next one. Easy drills are entertainment, not practice.
8. **Short and daily beats long and rare.** Twenty focused minutes a day, with sleep between, wires this in faster than weekend marathons.
