## Quiz 1 Key: The Genre & The Palette

### Part A: The genre
1. A. G. Cook, in London, in 2013.
2. The early PC Music-orbit style: pop exaggerated into gloss and unreality (high processed vocals, ultra-bright synths, plastic textures); the name critics used before "hyperpop."
3. SOPHIE; the *Vroom Vroom* EP (2016).
4. *1000 gecs*; a Spotify editor created the "hyperpop" playlist in August 2019 and the playlist's name stuck to the scene.
5. The younger SoundCloud/Discord wave (glaive, ericdoa, osquinn and others): fast, emotional, heavily pitched bedroom tracks descended from hyperpop.
6. Hard bright drums, distorted 808 bass, supersaw-style synths.
7. Roughly 150–180 BPM (the course home is 160); faster than standard pop.
8. For each element, ask what the normal pop version is and how far past normal it has been pushed; every hyperpop move is an exaggerated pop move.

### Part B: Drums
1. Any three of: in front instead of behind; hard instead of soft; bright instead of dusty; short instead of roomy; clipped instead of warm.
2. Because the boom is the 808's job; a long, subby kick smears against the 808's sustain.
3. Snare and clap layered, then clipped with Distortion (analog violence) or Crusher (digital bit-torn version) until the transient flattens into a slap.
4. One self-recorded object percussion (metal, glass, keys); it gives the palette an identity no downloaded pack has (the SOPHIE inheritance).
5. In the attack/transient: distortion flattening it, a trim starting exactly on the hit, compression snapping the front. If it is only hard when loud, it is not hard.
6. Pick a raw source → effect on its bus → push past taste → back off ten percent → resample the print (then trim it).
7. A run of 1/16 or 1/32 hat hits; program it in TR-REC, or perform it with the DJFX Looper and resample.
8. For variants: the raw source can be re-designed into alternate versions later.

### Part C: The 808
1. A long, pitched, sine-heavy boom used as a bass instrument; named for the Roland TR-808 drum machine's kick.
2. Distortion adds harmonics above the fundamental, up where small speakers can actually reproduce them.
3. Overdrive (warm, round), Distortion (aggressive, square-ish), Crusher (digital scream).
4. Chromatic mode over the loop, walk pads until one note locks as home; the Auto Pitch KEY and the harmony/stab choices both depend on it.
5. Enter chromatic mode with the 808 selected and record the pattern while playing pads (supported since firmware 2.00).
6. Hard-tuned, fast music exposes sour notes instantly; a long home note with one move reads as confident, and busy lines invite clashes.
7. Arrange around it (kick at the 808's start, not during sustain); shorten one (usually the kick); carve with the Equalizer.
8. Play the loop on a phone speaker; if the 808 vanishes, it needs more drive or a different octave, not more volume.

### Part D: Synths
1. The bright, buzzy basic dance-music waveform; a supersaw is several saws detuned against each other into a shimmering wall.
2. 1990s trance and eurodance.
3. Stack repitched copies of the same note (fine pitch offsets or Chromatic PS on a parallel layer) and resample; JUNO Chorus over the stack completes the illusion.
4. Chorus is detune-and-stack in a box: it widens and shimmers the sound (the honest supersaw shortcut).
5. Duplicate the synth note to two or three regular pads, tune the copies with Pitch/Speed or Chromatic PS, trigger the pads together, keep combinations that lock by ear, and resample the good one to a single pad.
6. Interlocking creates groove and leaves space; shadowing the kick doubles energy nowhere and crowds everything.
7. Harshness gets tamed once, at mix time, in context; designing timid sounds early makes small tracks.
8. A slightly empty mid-range; it is the vocal's parking spot for Week 2.

### Part E: At the machine
1. Full marks: loop at genre tempo, kit audibly designed, 808 tuned and phone-audible, one variation between halves. Deduct for mud, sour tuning, or a phone-invisible bassline.
2. Full marks: home found inside a minute and it locks (no sourness when held against the loop).

## Quiz 2 Key: The Voice

### Part A: Writing & recording
1. Say a true blunt thing; melody by ear in a small range; rhythm placement first.
2. The genre's engine is direct feeling in an absurd costume; plain phrases survive repetition and processing, clever ones age badly at speed.
3. Correction snaps to nearby scale notes; small steps snap cleanly, leaps invite wrong-note snaps and audible smearing.
4. Chant the phrase rhythmically on one or two notes; hard-tune turns a chant into a melody.
5. Mic into MIC/GUITAR IN; set level during a full-commitment take with the loudest word out of the red; monitor the beat in headphones; record in a soft quiet room.
6. Anything the mic hears is printed into the take forever; a beat in the room can never be removed from the vocal.
7. A second performance of the same line; it must match the lead's rhythm/timing; pitch imperfections are fine because the tuner reconciles them.
8. Processing amplifies the energy in the take; a shy take processed is still shy.
9. Outtakes become chops, ad-libs, and one-shots (Day 11); recorded voice is never waste.
10. Takes are the only unrecoverable data in the project; samples can be re-sourced, your Tuesday voice cannot.

### Part B: Auto Pitch
1. It measures pitch and pulls it toward the nearest note of a chosen key; set to maximum strength the pull becomes an instant audible snap: hard-tune.
2. Which notes the voice snaps to; the song's key from the Day 4 find-home test.
3. CHROMA snaps to all twelve semitones: usable for tight melodies, but risks wrong-note snaps; a set key is the beginner default.
4. Correction strength, from gentle polish to the hard snap.
5. Shifts the whole voice up or down, up to an octave either way (-100–100).
6. The voice's character/timbre (vocal-tract size), independent of pitch.
7. Flattens all inflection to one monotone pitch; use on a line or a background layer, not a whole song.
8. The dry/wet ratio between original voice and effect.
9. A dry safety stays reversible; input FX prints the effect at record time, which commits the sound but lets you perform into the tuner. Course default: keep dry safeties, record short input-FX tests to find settings, then re-record the keeper through those settings.
10. The KEY does not match the song; re-run find-home and update KEY (and anything else keyed to it).

### Part C: Pitch-up, stacks & chops
1. A new Auto Pitch input-FX take with PITCH raised (in-time lead shifts); FORMANT up (glossy character without pitch change); speed/repitch (nightcore chipmunk with time-warp, for chops and layers).
2. Fan-made sped-up remixes (rising pitch and tempo together); hyperpop inherited the sped-up chipmunk vocal aesthetic from it.
3. Lead at full level; double tucked clearly below; octave or +3/+4 layer quiet; Harmony or a wildcard (ROBOT, Vocoder, whisper) quieter still. Everything under the lead is audibly too quiet solo.
4. Adds pitched voices in a chosen key in one move; behind real takes, not instead of them.
5. Printing makes the hook portable (one pad to arrange, chop, and effect); the layers stay available for remixing the stack later.
6. Syllable chop; the stutter; the pitch-ramp stutter; the vocal synth.
7. TR-REC for surgical/gridded stutters; the Looper for performed, alive ones (resampled to keep).
8. Chop the syllable, enter chromatic mode with it, walk adjacent pads up (or down) on a rhythmic grid.
9. Record one sung note, tune if needed, make it a chromatic instrument, thicken with Chorus, print; your voice is always yours to release.
10. Glitching the hook so constantly it never plays straight; chop around the clean statements (intros, fills, pre-drop bars): glitch is a spice, the hook is the meal.

### Part D: Candy & the drop
1. It marks structure: tells the listener a section is ending, landing, or falling away.
2. Riser: a sung "ahh" repitched upward with reverb, resampled while riding the pitch up (or an accelerating hat roll). Impact: kick + metal hit + reverb printed as one slam (or a dense moment resampled and crushed).
3. Stopper: tape stop. Downer: pitch-fall. Back Spin: vinyl rewind.
4. The build (thinned, rising), the gap (near-silence before the one), the downbeat (impact + everything + hook), the drop body (with a halfway variation).
5. The silence makes the landing violent by contrast; a weak drop is almost always a build that gave too much away, so empty the build first.

### Part E: At the machine
1. Full marks: the build audibly withholds, the gap is programmed (not wished for), and the downbeat lands everything together with an impact.
2. Full marks: layers mute down to a lead that still carries, and the full stack reads as one big voice, not a crowd.

## Final Exam Key

### Part A: Speed fundamentals
1. A. G. Cook, 2013 (London). 2. A Spotify editor's August 2019 "hyperpop" playlist named the scene. 3. Hard bright drums, distorted 808, supersaw-style synths. 4. ~150–180 BPM. 5. KEY, AT PITCH, PITCH, FORMANT, ROBOT, BALANCE. 6. PITCH parameter, FORMANT shift, speed/repitch. 7. Source → effect → too far → back off 10% → resample. 8. Arrange, shorten, carve (EQ). 9. Build, gap, downbeat, drop body. 10. Element distortion is the aesthetic; master clipping is the accident.

### Part B: The palette
1. Short mid-forward kick (Overdrive, trimmed); layered clipped snare/clap (Distortion/Crusher); tight bright hat (touch of Crusher), rolls; one self-recorded metallic object, pitched and clipped. (2 pts: all four with treatments; 1 pt: pieces without treatments.)
2. Source a sine-ish note; trim to the attack, long sustain; drive it (Overdrive/Distortion/Crusher) until harmonic on a small speaker, back off ten percent; resample the print; tune it via chromatic-mode find-home.
3. Chromatic mode over the loop until a note locks as home. Downstream: Auto Pitch KEY, the Harmony effect's key, stab choices, the bassline's home, and any pitched vocal layers.
4. One clean sustained saw note (ideally plus an octave) → chromatic mode instrument → fatten by stacking repitched copies and JUNO Chorus → resample; chords by duplicating the note to regular pads, tuning the copies, triggering a combination, and printing the lock.
5. Distortion adds harmonics above the sine fundamental; small speakers cannot reproduce the fundamental but can reproduce the harmonics, so the bass reads everywhere.
6. Drums alone until they carry, then 808 (kick relationship checked), then stabs interlocking, lead last and optional. Drums first because layers bury rather than fix a weak foundation.

### Part C: The voice
1. Blunt true phrase; small-range melody by ear; rhythm first. Commitment beats range because processing amplifies the take's energy, and the tuner supplies the pitch accuracy.
2. KEY: notes snapped to. AT PITCH: snap strength. PITCH: whole-voice shift (±1 octave). FORMANT: timbre/character. ROBOT: monotone flattening. BALANCE: dry/wet.
3. PITCH: shifted, in time, controlled. FORMANT: character shrinks/grows, pitch intact, glossy. Speed: pitch and tempo rise together, chipmunk artifacts, time-warped (nightcore road).
4. Lead + rhythm-matched double under it + quiet octave/parallel layer + Harmony or wildcard, each support layer too quiet solo; the rule is nothing competes with the lead.
5. Verse: lighter tune (lower AT PITCH), fewer layers, drier, closer, no octave-up. Buys section signaling from the voice alone: human verse, chrome chorus.
6. Any two of syllable chop / stutter (TR-REC or Looper) / pitch-ramp / vocal synth, described mechanically; the rule: chops serve the hook, which must still play straight when it counts.

### Part D: Structure & dynamics
1. Intro (draw in), verse (story, low energy), pre-chorus (ramp that aims), chorus (hook at full palette), bridge (new material so the last chorus feels new), outro (deliberate end).
2. Short sections (4–8 bars), songs ~1:30–2:30, cut verses or all-chorus forms; the rule: every section earns its bars or gets cut (when in doubt, halve).
3. A section is a pattern (or two chained); the song is the chain.
4. Copy a reference's structure (lengths, first-chorus timing, ending) while writing your own material; structure is inaudible as theft and universal as practice.
5. Removes activity: stabs, 808 density, drum density; keeps the song's sounds and tempo so it stays the same song with a lower ceiling.
6. A vocal ending on a held unresolved note; drums doubling their rate at the end; a filter opening into the downbeat (plus: always the gap).
7. Because seams differ in importance; the biggest gesture goes to the entry of the final chorus.
8. Your most striking gesture appears exactly once, at the biggest seam; spent everywhere it buys nothing.
9. Wreck your own hook/chorus: chopped and reordered, Scatter/Looper resampled, half-time pitched down, or the vocal synth playing the melody alone.
10. The plateau (everything 80% loud, nothing loud) and the sawtooth of accidents (random energy from sections built on different days).
11. Make the quiet parts quieter; loud is a relationship, and the loud parts are already near the ceiling.
12. One king per frequency range; add by nameable role, not pile; motion beats addition.
13. Mute each layer and cut what is not missed; the day before mixing, so mud dies while it is cheap.

### Part E: The mix & the finish
1. It is the loudest, densest section; every other section inherits and thins from its balances.
2. Levels (chorus first), carve (EQ the fights, high-pass the needless lows), glue (Compressor on drums, tame real harshness), verify (mono, phone, quiet, playlist).
3. On elements (printed drums, 808, vocal crush): aesthetic. At the master: never; the sum stays clean with headroom.
4. Something comes down. The sum is never fixed by pushing it.
5. A dozen inaudible low-end contributions sum to a muddy master with no single culprit.
6. Bounce into a playlist between the two references, leave for an hour, listen cold, note every wince; the hour resets the acclimated ear.
7. Ears drift toward whatever they marinate in; the reference is the fixed scale.
8. Mono, the phone speaker, and a quiet listen (plus the playlist test as the final verdict).

### Part F: At the machine / applied
1. Full marks: form reads without narration, the final chorus is audibly the peak, and the ending is a choice. Deduct one for each miss, honestly.
2. Full marks: verdict delivered honestly, whatever it is. This item grades the honesty, not the song; a truthful "not yet, and here is why" scores full.
