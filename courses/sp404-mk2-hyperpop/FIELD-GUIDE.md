# Field Guide: The Hyperpop Sound on the SP-404 MK2 (MUS-47H)

Read §1 (the genre map), §3 (the effects this course lives on), and §6 (clean sources) *before Day 1*. The rest is reference you will return to all course. Exact menu wording can shift between firmware versions; the logic does not. The official Roland reference manual (§7) is the final word on any button.

> **First thing, before anything else: update the firmware.** This course is built on effects that arrived in updates (Auto Pitch, Vocoder, Harmony, and several delays and reverbs among them). Download the latest firmware from Roland's site, run the updater, and only then start Day 1. An out-of-the-box unit may be missing the exact tools you are told to use.

## 1. The genre map (a short, factual history)

- **2013, London: PC Music.** Producer **A. G. Cook** founds the PC Music label and collective. Its early releases (Hannah Diamond, GFOTY, Cook's own tracks) exaggerate pop until it is uncanny: ultra-glossy synths, high, processed vocals, plastic textures, and a knowing embrace of consumer-culture kitsch. The style gets called **bubblegum bass**.
- **2013 onward: SOPHIE.** The producer SOPHIE (first single "Bipp," 2013) pushes the sound's design language furthest: latex-squeak synthesis, metallic percussion, huge sub-bass, and pop hooks welded to abrasive texture. SOPHIE's production on **Charli XCX's *Vroom Vroom* EP (2016)** carries the sound toward the pop mainstream. SOPHIE died in January 2021; the influence is everywhere in this genre.
- **2019: 100 gecs.** Dylan Brady and Laura Les release ***1000 gecs***, a short, chaotic album that mashes bubblegum bass with nightcore, ska, metal, and trance, and it blows up online. In **August 2019 a Spotify editor creates the "hyperpop" playlist**, and the name sticks to the whole scene.
- **2020 onward: the online wave.** A generation of teenagers on SoundCloud and Discord (often tagged **digicore** or glitchcore: glaive, ericdoa, osquinn, and many others) makes fast, emotional, heavily pitched tracks in bedrooms. Charli XCX's lockdown album *how i'm feeling now* (2020, executive-produced by A. G. Cook) cements the sound's critical standing. The scene keeps mutating; the name is contested by nearly everyone it is applied to, which is itself very pop.

**What defines the sound** (the checklist your ear is being trained on):

| Element | The hyperpop treatment |
|---|---|
| Tempo | Fast for pop; roughly 150–180 BPM is a comfortable home base (plenty of exceptions) |
| Vocals | Pitched up and/or hard-tuned, formant-shifted, doubled and stacked; the voice as a synthesizer |
| Bass | Distorted 808: a sine-based boom pushed into overdrive so it reads on small speakers |
| Drums | Hard, bright, clipped; metallic percussion; stutters and rolls; often sparse but violent |
| Synths | Bright, detuned, supersaw-style walls; trance and eurodance DNA |
| Structure | Compressed pop form; songs commonly 1:30–2:30; drops borrowed from EDM |
| Dynamics | Extreme contrast: near-silence into maximal walls; the loud parts hit because the quiet parts exist |
| Attitude | Maximal, sincere and ironic at once, extremely online, finished fast and released often |

**Core listening** (the course's canon; stream them all): A. G. Cook, Hannah Diamond, and the PC Music compilations; SOPHIE, *PRODUCT* and *Oil of Every Pearl's Un-Insides*; Charli XCX, *Vroom Vroom* EP, *Pop 2*, *how i'm feeling now*; 100 gecs, *1000 gecs* and *10,000 gecs*; glaive and ericdoa's early tapes; and the current Spotify "hyperpop" playlist for what the scene sounds like this month.

## 2. The instrument choice (why an SP for this genre)

Hyperpop is normally made in a DAW, and no honest course pretends otherwise. The SP-404 MK2 works for it because the genre is, under the gloss, **collage music**: samples, resamples, chopped vocals, and effects stacked until they become a texture. That is exactly the SP's native workflow. What the SP gives you: a vocal chain in hardware (Auto Pitch and Harmony as input FX), chromatic mode as a playable synth, a resampling loop that forces commitment, and pads that make glitch edits a performance instead of a mouse chore. What it costs you: no piano-roll editing, limited undo, and none of a DAW's surgical mixing. This course treats those limits as the aesthetic: commit, resample, keep moving. When you outgrow them, the workflow transfers to any DAW almost one-to-one.

## 3. The effects this course lives on

The SP-404 MK2's effects run on buses, non-destructively, exactly as in MUS-45H (its [Field Guide](#/sp404-mk2-sampling) §3 has the signal-flow diagrams; they are not repeated here). Many can also run as **INPUT FX**, printing the effect into the sample as you record; the vocal processors in this course are taught as input effects. This course uses a specific subset, and you should know it cold:

| Effect | What it does | Where this course uses it |
|---|---|---|
| **Auto Pitch** | Hard-tune: corrects incoming pitch to a KEY, with PITCH (±1 octave), FORMANT, AT PITCH (correction strength), ROBOT (flattens all inflection), BALANCE | The hyperpop vocal, Days 8–10; the single most important effect in this course |
| **Harmony** | Adds pitched voices to the input in a chosen key | Vocal stacks, Day 10 |
| **Vocoder** | Robot-voice processing for the mic input; pitch comes from the NOTE parameter or external MIDI, not from the sung pitch alone | An optional color, Day 10 |
| **Crusher** | Bit/sample-rate reduction; digital grit | Drums and vocal chops |
| **Overdrive / Distortion** | Soft and hard clipping drive | The 808 (Day 4), drums, whole-bus violence |
| **Filter+Drive / Super Filter** | Resonant filters, with drive | Builds, drops, verse contrast |
| **Compressor** | Level squeeze; glue and punch | Drum bus and the final mix, Day 19 |
| **Equalizer** | Tone shaping | Carving the 808/kick relationship, mix day |
| **Sync Delay / TimeCtrlDly / Tape Echo** | Tempo-synced and character delays | Vocal throws, ear candy |
| **Reverb / SX Reverb / Cloud Delay** | Space, from rooms to washes | Depth behind the wall of sound |
| **DJFX Looper** | Live capture-and-stutter of the output | Stutter edits, fills, glitch performance |
| **Scatter** | Rhythmic glitch-slicing of the output | Instant glitch texture; use sparingly |
| **Stopper / Downer / Back Spin** | Tape-stop, pitch-fall, and rewind gestures | Transitions, Day 12 and 17 |
| **Chromatic PS** | Pitch shift in semitones | Pitched layers without changing speed |
| **JUNO Chorus / Chorus** | Width and shimmer | Fattening sampled synths, Day 5 |

> **The one-move rule from MUS-45H still applies, doubled.** Hyperpop stacks effects hard, but every layer in a real record is a decision. Add one effect, ask what it is for, resample, then add the next. A stack of ten deliberate moves is a sound; ten simultaneous maybes is mud.

## 4. Chromatic mode (your synthesizer)

Chromatic mode turns the 16 pads into a keyboard for one sample: **hold [SHIFT] and press pad [4]** to enter it, then choose the sample; the pads now play it in semitone steps. Chromatic mode is monophonic, so use it for basslines, leads, and one-note vocal-synth parts, not for playing chords directly. Since firmware 2.00 you can **record patterns while in chromatic mode**, which is how every bassline and synth line in this course is sequenced. Practical notes the lessons rely on:

- **Sample one clean, sustained note** (a synth key, a sung "ah," a bass note) and it becomes an instrument. The farther a pad is from the original pitch, the more artifact you hear; keep parts within roughly an octave of the source, or sample two notes an octave apart and switch.
- **Repitching changes character.** A note pitched far up gets short and chirpy, far down gets long and smeared. In this genre both artifacts are usable on purpose.
- **Find the key by ear, the MUS-45H way:** play your loop, walk the chromatic pads until one note locks as "home," and build from there. No theory needed; the KEY you find here is also the KEY you set in Auto Pitch (Day 9).

## 5. The vocal chain (the course's centerpiece, on one page)

The standard chain this course teaches, front to back:

```
  your voice → mic → MIC/GUITAR IN, level set healthy (loud, never clipping)
      → INPUT FX: Auto Pitch (KEY = song key, AT PITCH high, BALANCE full wet)   [printed]
      → recorded to a pad
      → double it: record the same line again, same settings → second pad, panned/level under the lead
      → color layers: pitch-up copy (octave up, or +3/+4 semitones), Harmony stack, chopped stutters
      → bus FX while mixing: delay throw, reverb, Crusher to taste
      → resample the stack to one pad when it is right
```

Decisions you make per song, not per take: how hard the tune is (AT PITCH), how unnatural the timbre is (FORMANT, PITCH), how thick the stack is (doubles and harmonies), and how buried or forward the voice sits. For Auto Pitch and Harmony, audition by recording short input-FX tests, compare them to your dry safety, then re-record the keeper through the found settings. Keep one **dry safety take** of every line so no decision is fatal.

**Mic practicalities:** any dynamic mic into MIC/GUITAR IN works; set the input level so your loudest word stays out of the red, stay a consistent fist-distance off the mic, and record in a soft room (closet, blankets) because reverb cannot be removed later. Condenser mics need an external preamp or interface with phantom/plugin power; the SP does not provide it and has no built-in mic.

## 6. Clean sources for every sound in this course

Everything in a hyperpop song can be sourced legally, most of it for free:

- **Your voice:** the lead vocal, chops, ad-libs, risers (a sung "ahh" pitched and stretched), even percussion (mouth clicks, breaths). Always yours to release.
- **Synth notes:** Roland Cloud and the SP-404 MK2 app's included content; the SP's Sound Generator; any royalty-free synth sample pack (read the license: "free to download" is not "free to release"); or record a few sustained notes from any synth, keyboard app, or instrument you can access. One good sawtooth note is a whole course of material.
- **Drums:** royalty-free one-shot packs, or design your own: distort a knock, crush a clap of your hands, resample the SP's own effects feedback. Self-made drums are both clean and distinctive.
- **808s:** royalty-free 808 one-shots are everywhere, or make one from a sine/bass note, including the SP's Sound Generator, and Overdrive (Day 4 does exactly this).
- **What to avoid releasing:** samples of copyrighted records, and re-sung melodies of existing songs (interpolation is also a rights question). For practice, anything goes; for release, keep it clean.
