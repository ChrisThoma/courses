# Week 2: The Voice

> **The thesis of this week:** the hyperpop voice is an instrument you build, and the raw material is your own ordinary speaking-and-singing voice, which is good enough, today, exactly as it is. The genre's entire vocal aesthetic exists because its founders were producers first and singers second: hard tuning, pitch-shifting, formant games, and thick stacks turn *any* committed take into that chrome, emotional, not-quite-human sound. This week you write a hook, record it, learn every parameter of Auto Pitch, build pitch-up doubles and stacks, chop your own voice into an instrument, make your ear candy, and assemble the loudest thirty seconds of your song: the drop. The only way to fail this week is not recording.

Every day this week starts with the mic already plugged in and a level already set. Reduce the friction to zero, because the friction is the failure mode.

# Day 8: Writing the Hook, and Getting It Recorded

### Objectives
- Write a hook: short, blunt, singable, and honest about a beginner's range.
- Record clean vocal takes into the SP at a healthy level.
- Bank a lead take, a double, and a dry safety of each.

### Listen (5 min)
Three choruses from your canon, lyrics only in your attention. Notice how *few words* there are, how plain they are, and how much repetition: a phrase, said again, said again with one word changed. The sophistication is all in the delivery and processing. Nobody in this genre is writing verses of poetry, and you are not either.

### Lecture

**Writing the hook.** A hyperpop hook is one or two short phrases, usually under ten words total, repeated. The craft constraints that actually help:

- **Say a true, blunt thing.** The genre runs on direct feeling wearing an absurd costume: "I don't want to be alone," "you're so cool," "I can't sleep." Write five candidate phrases about something you actually feel this week. Plain beats clever; clever ages badly at 160 BPM.
- **Melody by ear, small range.** Hum over your Week 1 loop until a shape sticks. Keep it within about five notes; Auto Pitch will snap it to the key (your Day 4 home note), and small melodies survive snapping better than leaps. If you cannot find a melody, *say* the phrase rhythmically on one or two notes; half this genre's hooks are chanted, and hard-tune turns a chant into a melody anyway.
- **Rhythm is the hook.** Where the words land against the kick matters more than which pitches they hit. Try the phrase starting on the one, then starting a half-beat early; keep the one that makes you nod.

**Recording into the SP.** Mic into MIC/GUITAR IN. Set the input level while performing at *full* commitment (the take you set levels on should be as loud as the real one): loudest word out of the red. Consistent fist-distance from the mic, soft room, phone silenced. Record with the loop in your headphones, not on speakers (the mic must not hear the beat). Details in [Field Guide §5](FIELD-GUIDE.md).

**What to bank today** (bank D, and label by pad position):

1. **The lead take:** your best committed performance of the hook, dry (processing starts tomorrow; today's job is the raw material).
2. **A double:** the same line performed again, matching the lead's timing as exactly as you can. Doubles are what make one voice sound like a record; match the *rhythm*, do not worry about matching pitch perfectly, the tuner will reconcile them.
3. Keep every take. Failed takes become chop fodder on Day 11. In this genre there is no such thing as a wasted recording of your own voice.

> **Commit to the take.** A shy, careful vocal processed to the moon still sounds shy; the processing amplifies whatever energy is in the take. Perform it bigger than feels reasonable alone in your room. The chrome robot on the record was recorded by someone doing something embarrassing into a microphone, every single time.

### Do
1. **(10 min) Write.** Five blunt phrases, pick one, find its melody or chant over the loop.
2. **(20 min) Record.** Levels set properly, then takes until you have a committed lead and one tight double. Expect ten-plus takes; that is normal forever, not just for beginners.
3. **(10 min) Bank and back up.** Lead, double, and the best outtakes into bank D, trimmed. Back up now; this is the first irreplaceable data in the project.

### Daily drill
**One-take warm-up, 3 min (daily for the rest of the course):** record one throwaway take of anything (the hook, a hum, an ad-lib) before the day's real work. It keeps the mic ordinary and your commitment warm.

### Self-check
1. What are the three writing constraints, and why does plain beat clever here?
2. Why does a small melodic range survive hard-tuning better than leaps?
3. Walk through the recording setup: input, level-setting, monitoring, room.
4. What is a double, what must it match, and what can it fudge?
5. Why record bigger than feels reasonable?

# Day 9: Auto Pitch, Every Parameter

### Objectives
- Understand pitch correction and why hyperpop sets it to maximum.
- Learn all six Auto Pitch parameters and what each is for.
- Choose dry safeties vs printed input FX, and process your hook take.

### Listen (5 min)
One Charli XCX chorus and one glaive or ericdoa chorus. Both voices are hard-tuned, but differently: one polished chrome, one raw and cracking *through* the tune. Hard-tune is not one sound; the parameters you learn today are why.

### Lecture

**Pitch correction** measures the pitch of a voice and pulls it toward the nearest note of a chosen key. Set gently, it is invisible polish. Set to maximum, the pull becomes instant and the voice *snaps* between notes with an audible synthetic slur between them: the hard-tune sound, T-Pain to trap to every corner of hyperpop. The genre uses it as a timbre, an instrument choice, almost never as subtle repair.

The SP's version is the **Auto Pitch** effect, and its six parameters ([Field Guide §7](FIELD-GUIDE.md) links the official page) are the whole game:

| Parameter | Range | What it does, in practice |
|---|---|---|
| **KEY** | CHROMA, or a key (C, D, E...) | Which notes the voice snaps to. Set it to **your song's key from Day 4**. CHROMA snaps to all twelve semitones: tighter melodies survive it, but wrong-note snaps are more likely; a set key is the beginner default |
| **AT PITCH** | 0–100 | Correction strength. Low = gentle polish; **high = the hard snap**. This is the "how hyperpop is it" knob |
| **PITCH** | -100–100 | Shifts the whole voice up or down as much as an octave. The pitched-up voice, tomorrow's whole lesson, lives here |
| **FORMANT** | -100–100 | The voice's *character* independent of pitch: lower reads as a bigger/deeper vocal tract, higher as a smaller/brighter one. Chipmunk-without-speeding-up and cartoon-bright doubles live here |
| **ROBOT** | OFF/ON | Flattens all inflection to a single monotone pitch. Instant android; great on one line or a background layer, exhausting on a whole song |
| **BALANCE** | dry↔wet | How much original voice remains. Full wet for the classic sound; partial for a tuned "shadow" behind a dry lead |

**Where to put it.** Auto Pitch is taught here as **INPUT FX**: printed into the sample as you record. The sound is committed, and your monitoring *is* the result, which helps performance enormously, since singing into the tuner teaches you to play it. The course default: **keep yesterday's dry takes as safeties, record short input-FX test takes while you dial settings, then re-record the lead and double through the found settings.** You get safety first and the committed print after.

**Finding your settings today:** KEY = your song's key. AT PITCH high (start maximal; this genre's default). PITCH and FORMANT at zero for now. ROBOT off. BALANCE full wet. Then sing *slightly slower and straighter* than feels musical: hard-tune rewards held, confident notes and punishes scoops and wobbles.

> **If the tuner outputs wrong notes, the KEY is wrong.** The most common Day 9 failure: Auto Pitch snapping to sour notes because the set key does not match the song. Re-run the Day 4 find-home test; if home disagrees with your written-down key, trust the test and update everything (KEY, and next week the harmony stabs too).

### Do
1. **(15 min) The parameter tour.** Auto Pitch as input FX, mic on, recording short throwaway test phrases. Sweep each parameter one at a time, extremes first: AT PITCH low→high, PITCH up an octave and down, FORMANT both ends, ROBOT on, BALANCE across. Say out loud what each does; you are building vocabulary, not settings, on this pass.
2. **(10 min) Dial the song's chain.** KEY, AT PITCH high, taste on the rest, printing short test takes as needed. A/B against your dry safety and your reference vocal until the *category* of sound matches.
3. **(15 min) Re-record through input FX** with those settings: lead and double, monitoring the tuned result while performing. Bank the prints beside the dry takes.

### Daily drill
**Play the tuner, 3 min:** with input FX Auto Pitch on, hold single notes and slide slowly between them, hearing where the snap catches. You are learning the instrument's response the way a guitarist learns bends.

### Self-check
1. What does pitch correction do, and what turns it into the hard-tune sound?
2. Name all six Auto Pitch parameters and one use for each.
3. When would you choose CHROMA vs a set KEY?
4. What is the dry-safety vs printed-input-FX trade-off, and this course's default sequence?
5. What performance style survives hard-tuning best, and what is the most common wrong-notes cause?

# Day 10: The Pitch-Up, Doubles, and Stacks

### Objectives
- Build the pitched-up hyperpop lead: PITCH vs FORMANT vs speed, and what each artifact sounds like.
- Stack doubles, octave layers, and Harmony into a thick vocal.
- Print the stack with resampling, keeping the layers.

### Listen (5 min)
100 gecs, then an osquinn or early digicore track: voices pitched far above natural. Then one PC Music track: pitched less far, but formant-raised into glossy unreality. Three different roads to "not quite human"; today you learn all three.

### Lecture

**The three roads up.** The pitched-up voice is the genre's flag, and there are three mechanically different ways to fly it:

1. **A new Auto Pitch input-FX take with PITCH raised:** shifts pitch while keeping the performance in time. The controlled, modern road: re-record the hook three-to-seven semitones up (or a full octave for a chipmunk lead), still in time with the beat.
2. **FORMANT up (pitch unchanged or modestly up):** the voice keeps its notes but the *body* shrinks: glossy, doll-like, PC Music's road. Combining a modest PITCH up with a further FORMANT up is the classic bubblegum lead.
3. **Speed, the sampler's own road:** repitch the recorded take with the pitch/speed knob, so it rises *and* speeds up, artifacts and all. This is the nightcore inheritance (fan-made sped-up anime-adjacent remixes, a direct hyperpop ancestor) and the cheapest, most characterful road: chipmunk timing chaos included. Chops of sped-up vocal are a Day 11 staple.

Rule of thumb: road 1 for the lead you need in time and are willing to re-record, road 2 for character on top of it, road 3 for chops and layers where the time-warp is a feature.

**The stack.** One tuned voice is a demo; a record is a **stack**:

- **Lead** (tuned, from yesterday) at full level.
- **Double** tucked under it (same line, second take): the two takes' tiny differences fuse into thickness. Level the double clearly below the lead.
- **Octave or pitched layer:** a freshly recorded Auto Pitch input-FX take with PITCH raised (road 1), or a repitched copy of the lead (road 3), quiet, adds shimmer; or +3/+4 semitones for a parallel-harmony effect (ears decide which; sour means try the other).
- **Harmony effect:** the SP's Harmony input effect adds pitched voices in a chosen key in one move; set its key to the song's and audition with short test takes. It is the fast road to a choir; stack it *behind* real takes rather than instead of them.
- **One wildcard:** a ROBOT-mode layer, a Vocoder pass with its NOTE/SCALE set deliberately, or a whispered take, very quiet. The layer nobody consciously hears but everybody misses when muted.

Then **print it**: balance the stack across pads, resample to one pad (bank E), and keep the layer pads. The print is your portable hook, one pad you can drop anywhere in the arrangement, chop on Day 11, and slam with bus effects on drop day.

> **Stacks are quiet layer by layer.** Every layer under the lead should be audibly *too quiet* on its own. If any single layer competes with the lead, the stack stops sounding like one big voice and starts sounding like a crowd of small ones. Big = one lead plus supporting shadows.

### Do
1. **(10 min) Three roads.** Compare the roads with short prints: a new input-FX take with PITCH raised, a formant-raised take, and a speed/repitch copy. A/B the artifacts and file which road your song's lead wants.
2. **(20 min) Build the stack.** Lead + double + one pitched layer + Harmony or a wildcard, leveled per the lecture. A/B the stack against a reference chorus.
3. **(10 min) Print.** Resample the balanced stack to one pad. Label it. This pad is Project 2's centerpiece. Back up.

### Daily drill
**One-layer audition, 3 min:** mute and unmute each layer of your current stack, asking of each: what does this add, and is it under the lead? Stack hygiene, daily.

### Self-check
1. Name the three roads to a pitched-up voice and one situation for each.
2. What is nightcore, and what did hyperpop inherit from it?
3. List a full stack's layers and their level relationships.
4. What does the Harmony effect do, and where should it sit relative to real takes?
5. Why print the stack, and why keep the layer pads too?

# Day 11: Vocal Chops, The Voice as Instrument

### Objectives
- Chop your printed hook and your outtakes into playable material.
- Build stutter edits by pattern and by performance (DJFX Looper).
- Make a chromatic vocal-synth from one sung note.

### Listen (5 min)
Any A. G. Cook or 100 gecs track's back half: hear the voice syllables repeating, stuttering, turned into melodic percussion. Half the "synths" you have been hearing all week are voices. Including, soon, yours.

### Lecture

Everything MUS-45H taught about chopping applies unchanged; today's material is just *you*. Four moves, in escalating order:

- **The syllable chop.** Chop the printed hook (Day 10's pad) on its syllable transients and assign across pads. Now the hook is playable percussion: re-order it, double a syllable, leave one hanging. A hook that also exists as chops is two instruments for one recording.
- **The stutter.** One syllable repeated on a rhythmic grid ("lo-lo-lo-lonely"): program it in TR-REC (1/16 repeats of one chop pad, mono/choke on so re-triggers cut cleanly) or perform it with the **DJFX Looper** over the playing hook and resample the performance. TR-REC for surgical, Looper for alive; you want both in the toolkit.
- **The pitch-ramp stutter.** A stutter where each repeat steps up (or down) in pitch: chop → chromatic mode → walk adjacent pads on the grid. This one gesture is practically a genre watermark.
- **The vocal synth.** One clean sung note ("ah" or "oo," recorded today, tuned by input Auto Pitch if pitch is shaky) becomes a chromatic-mode instrument like Day 5's saw. Voices-as-pads through JUNO Chorus, stacked and printed, is the genre's signature pad/choir texture, and it is one hundred percent yours to release.

**Outtakes are gold.** Yesterday's failed takes: chop the cracked word, the laugh, the breath. Pitched and placed, these become the ad-libs and one-shot jokes that make a track feel alive and personal. Bank E is for exactly this.

> **Chops must serve the hook, not bury it.** The failure mode is a track where the hook never plays straight because it is always being glitched. The record plays the hook clean when it counts and chops it *around* those moments: intros, fills, the bars before a drop. Glitch is a spice; the hook is the meal.

### Do
1. **(10 min) Syllable-chop the hook** and play re-ordered variations until one is keepable. Resample it.
2. **(15 min) Stutters.** One TR-REC stutter, one performed Looper stutter, one pitch-ramp. Resample each as a one-bar gesture into bank F (they are transitions as much as vocals).
3. **(15 min) The vocal synth.** Record the sung note, build the chromatic instrument, print one pad-texture with Chorus. Try it under the Week 1 loop as a background layer.

### Daily drill
**One-syllable jam, 3 min:** one vocal chop pad, one drum pattern, and your fingers: how many usable one-bar gestures can you find? Performance chops, daily.

### Self-check
1. What are the four vocal-chop moves, in escalating order?
2. When TR-REC a stutter, and when perform it with the Looper?
3. How do you build the pitch-ramp stutter?
4. How does one sung note become a choir texture, and why is it automatically releasable?
5. What is the chops-vs-hook failure mode and the rule that avoids it?

# Day 12: Ear Candy and Transitions

### Objectives
- Understand what ear candy is for: marking structure so the listener always knows where they are.
- Make your own riser, impact, and downlifter by resampling.
- Use Stopper, Downer, and Back Spin as transition gestures.

### Listen (5 min)
One track from your canon, listening *only* for the seams: the sweep into the chorus, the hit on its first beat, the stutter before a section change, the tape-stop into silence. Count them. A produced track marks nearly every seam; that marking is today's craft.

### Lecture

**Ear candy** is the production layer that carries no melody and no groove but tells the listener *where they are*: something rising means a section is ending; an impact means a section has landed; a tape stop means the floor is about to drop out. Three staples, all makeable from what is already in your banks:

- **The riser:** anything with rising pitch and/or intensity across one-to-four bars. Recipes: your sung "ahh" repitched upward and drenched in reverb, resampled while you ride the pitch knob up; a hat roll accelerating from 1/8s to 1/32s; the DJFX Looper tightening its loop over the playing track. Print several lengths.
- **The impact:** one big hit for the first beat of a section. Recipes: your kick + a metal hit + reverb, printed; or any dense moment of the track resampled and crushed into a single slam.
- **The downlifter/exhale:** the riser's opposite for the moment *after* a landing: a fall in pitch, a reversed riser (the pad reverse setting is free candy: reversed vocals and reversed cymbals are two prints away), a filtered wash decaying.

**The gesture effects.** Three MFX are pre-built transition moves: **Stopper** (tape stop: the track grinds to a halt), **Downer** (a pitch-fall on the audio), **Back Spin** (the vinyl rewind). Used live on the bus at a section boundary, resampled if you want them printed. One per seam, maximum; they are exclamation points, and a paragraph of exclamation points reads as noise.

> **Candy marks structure; it never is the structure.** If you mute all ear candy and the arrangement stops making sense, the arrangement was broken and the candy was hiding it. Build sections that work bare (next week's job), then mark the seams.

### Do
1. **(20 min) Make the kit:** two risers (different lengths), one impact, one downlifter, at least one reversed print. All from your own material, all into bank F, labeled.
2. **(10 min) Gesture practice.** Play the Week 1 loop and practice Stopper, Downer, and Back Spin at bar boundaries until the timing is musical. Resample your best tape-stop as a printed gesture.
3. **(10 min) Dress one seam.** Loop A (four bars sparse) into Loop B (your full loop): riser across A's last bar, impact on B's downbeat, exhale after. Hear how the seam now *announces itself*.

### Daily drill
**One-candy-a-day, 3 min:** make one new riser, impact, or texture from anything in your banks. Ear candy libraries are built by accretion, and yours is entirely self-made and clean.

### Self-check
1. What job does ear candy do, in one sentence?
2. Give a recipe each for a riser, an impact, and a downlifter from your existing material.
3. What do Stopper, Downer, and Back Spin each do?
4. Why one gesture per seam?
5. What does "candy marks structure, never is the structure" mean in practice?

# Day 13: The Drop, then Quiz 2

### Objectives
- Understand the drop as hyperpop's borrowed EDM engine: setup, arrival, payoff.
- Assemble your hook + full palette into the song's loudest section.
- Take Quiz 2.

### Listen (5 min)
Two drops from the canon, and thirty seconds *before* each. Notice the drop is loud partly because of what it contains, but mostly because of what the previous section withheld. The drop is a contrast machine, and both sides of the contrast are yours to build.

### Lecture

Hyperpop took the **drop** from EDM: a build section that rises and *withholds* (often thinning to just vocal and riser at the last bar), then the downbeat where everything lands at once. In this genre the drop and the chorus are usually the same event: the hook sings *over* the full palette, or the hook's chops *become* the drop's lead line.

**The anatomy, in your material:**

| Piece | What it is | You have it from |
|---|---|---|
| The build | 4–8 bars: thinned beat, rising tension, riser over the last bars, maybe the hook dry/filtered as a tease | Days 3–6 loop, thinned; Day 12 risers |
| The gap | The last half-beat before the drop: near-silence, or a lone vocal stutter | Day 11 stutters |
| The downbeat | Impact + full palette + hook stack, all at once, exactly on the one | Everything |
| The drop body | 8 bars: full loop + hook, with one variation at bar 5 (the Week 1 rule at section scale) | Loop + Day 10 print |

**The gap is the secret.** That instant of nothing before the downbeat is what makes the landing violent; the ear falls into the hole and the drop catches it. Program it deliberately: everything out for the last one or two 1/16s of the build. Silence is the loudest sound in this genre.

**Assembly today:** build pattern → gap → drop pattern, chained. The bar-5 variation keeps eight bars of maximal from flatlining: mute the 808 for two beats, swap the stab, let a chop run loose. Maximal sections need *internal* contrast most of all.

> **If the drop feels weak, fix the build first.** Nine times out of ten a limp drop is a build that gave too much away: too many elements, no thinning, no gap. Empty the build until it almost embarrasses you, and the same drop pattern doubles in force.

### Do
1. **(15 min) The build:** thin your loop hard (drums sparse, no 808 until halfway, stabs filtered), riser over the last two bars, hook teased once, gap programmed.
2. **(15 min) The drop:** impact + loop + hook stack on the downbeat, eight bars, bar-5 variation. Chain build → drop and play it end to end. Phone test. A/B against a reference drop and fix the worst gap.
3. **(30–40 min) Quiz 2** in `QUIZZES.md`, closed book. Grade against `ANSWER-KEYS.md`. Today may run longer than the normal daily block; the quiz is the assessment.

### Daily drill
Skip today; the quiz is the drill.

### Self-check
Covered by Quiz 2.

# Day 14: Studio Day + Project 2

The Week 2 consolidation. Three jobs:

### 1. Catch up
Priority if short on time: Day 9 (Auto Pitch settings correct for your key; everything downstream depends on it), then Day 10's stack, then Day 13's build-and-drop. Days 11 and 12 can be thinner and refined next week.

### 2. Project 2: A Hook and a Drop
Do **Project 2** (see `PROJECTS.md`): the written hook, the printed vocal stack, and a chained build → gap → drop, bounced and graded against the spec. This is the emotional core of your final song; treat it that way.

### 3. Back up
Non-negotiable this week: the project now contains your voice.

### Looking ahead
You have a world (Week 1) and a voice with a hook living in it (this week), and one complete high-energy passage: the build and drop. What you do not yet have is a *song*: the verses that give the drop something to be louder than, the pre-chorus that aims at it, the transitions that stitch it, and the mix that makes it read as one loud bright record instead of a stack of loud parts. That is Week 3, and it starts with the least glamorous, most powerful tool in pop: structure.
