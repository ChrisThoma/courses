# MUS-47H Syllabus: Making a Hyperpop Song on the SP-404 MK2

## Course description

A three-week intensive in genre production on the Roland SP-404 MK2, ending in one complete, original hyperpop song. Students first learn the genre itself (its history from PC Music through 100 gecs to the digicore scene, and the sound that defines it) and build its palette on the sampler: hard, bright, clipped drums; a distorted 808 bass played in chromatic mode; and supersaw-style synth parts built from sampled notes. The middle week is the voice: writing a short, singable hook, recording it cleanly into the SP, and processing it with Auto Pitch, formant shift, pitched-up doubles, and stacked harmonies, then turning that hook into vocal chops, glitch edits, and a drop. The final week is the song: pop structure on the pattern sequencer (verse, pre-chorus, chorus), builds and transitions, maximalism balanced with space, and a loud, bright mix that translates. By the end, students own a repeatable song-building workflow, not just one track.

This is a **studio (applied production) course**: most of the work is making sound, every day, with the machine on and a microphone plugged in. It assumes the sampling fundamentals of MUS-45H (recording, trimming, chopping, patterns, resampling); Day 2 compresses them into a refresher.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this course you will be able to:

1. **Describe hyperpop as a genre**: its lineage (PC Music, SOPHIE, 100 gecs, the online digicore wave), its defining sonic traits, and what separates it from the pop it exaggerates.
2. **Analyze a hyperpop track by ear**: identify the drum palette, bass treatment, vocal processing, structure, and at least one signature production move.
3. **Build a hyperpop drum kit** on the SP (hard kick, clipped snare/clap, bright metallic percussion, hi-hat rolls) and make it hit with Crusher, Distortion, and Compressor.
4. **Design a distorted 808 bass** from a single sampled note, play basslines in chromatic mode, and lock it with the kick.
5. **Build supersaw-style synth parts** from sampled single notes, sequence single-note parts in chromatic mode, and print chord stabs by layering pitched copies.
6. **Write a hook** that is short, blunt, and singable, and record your voice into the SP at a clean level.
7. **Process a vocal into the hyperpop voice**: Auto Pitch (key, correction strength, formant, robot), pitched-up doubles, and stacked harmonies through the SP's input-effect vocal workflow.
8. **Turn a vocal into an instrument**: chop it, stutter it, glitch it, and play it chromatically.
9. **Arrange a pop structure** (intro, verse, pre-chorus, chorus, bridge or drop-two, outro) from patterns, with builds, tape stops, and transitions that create real contrast.
10. **Mix loud and bright on purpose**: balanced levels, deliberate distortion vs accidental clipping, and a bounce that survives a phone speaker and mono.

## Daily structure (~45–75 minutes)

| Block | Time | Activity |
|---|---|---|
| Power up & warm up | 5 min | Turn on, load your project, the day's quick drill from the Practice Menu |
| Listen | 5–10 min | The day's reference track(s); listen for the specific move being taught |
| Lecture | 10–15 min | Read the lecture; do each small example at the machine as you read it |
| Make | 20–40 min | The day's work: sound design, recording, sequencing, arranging |
| Review | 5 min | Answer the self-check from memory; note what to fix tomorrow |

| Component | Weight |

| Project 1: The Palette + An Eight-Bar Loop | 10% |
| Project 2: A Hook and a Drop | 15% |

| The Song (final capstone) | 25% |
| Final Exam | 15% |

Grade yourself against `RUBRIC.md`. **Mastery threshold: 90%.** The honest grading here is in the bounce: record your work to a file, play it on a phone speaker and in headphones, and compare it directly against a reference track you love. Hyperpop is unforgiving this way; next to a real record, a weak 808 or a mistimed vocal chop is instantly audible. That comparison, called referencing, is not discouragement, it is the fastest teacher you have.

## Course policies

- **Back up weekly, and after every vocal session.** Samples can be re-downloaded; the take where your voice cracked in exactly the right way cannot. Your voice recordings are the only irreplaceable data in this course.
- **Record vocals even though you hate your voice.** This is the single most common quitting point. The processed result sounds nothing like the dry take, and nobody hears the dry take but you. A recorded bad take beats an imagined perfect one every day of this course.
- **Loud is a decision, not a default.** This genre distorts on purpose, which makes it easy to hide sloppiness under grit. The rule: you must be able to say which distortion is a choice. If you cannot, it is mud, and it fails the mix check.
- **Finish ugly, then fix.** Hyperpop culture ships fast and rough. Get the whole song standing (all sections, all parts) before polishing any one bar. A polished loop is not a song.
- **Reference constantly.** Every make-block ends with thirty seconds of A/B against a real track. Your ears drift toward whatever you have been hearing for an hour; the reference resets them.
- **Closed-book quizzes.** The point is to find the gaps, not to feel good.
- **Falling behind:** use the studio day to catch up. If you are more than a day behind, slow down rather than skip, and never skip a vocal day; the voice work is cumulative and it is the part of the course you cannot cram.
