# MUS-47H Making a Hyperpop Song on the SP-404 MK2 (A 3-Week Intensive)

**Format:** Self-paced intensive, modeled on a college January-term studio/production course
**Time commitment:** ~45–75 min/day, 6 days/week, 3 weeks (one "studio day" per week to catch up and consolidate)
**Prerequisites:** A Roland SP-404 MK2 on current firmware, headphones or speakers, and **a dynamic microphone you can plug into the MIC/GUITAR input** (a cheap dynamic mic is fine). Condenser mics need an external preamp or interface with the required power; the SP does not supply phantom power and has no built-in mic. You should already be able to record a sample, trim it, chop it, and program a basic pattern on the SP. The sister course [MUS-45H: Sampling & Beatmaking on the SP-404 MK2](#/sp404-mk2-sampling) teaches exactly that; take it first, or lean on Day 2's compressed refresher and expect to work harder in Week 1. **No singing ability and no music theory required.** If you can talk, you can make hyperpop vocals; the machine does the tuning.

> **The promise of this course:** in three weeks you will write, produce, and finish a complete hyperpop song, start to end, entirely on the SP-404 MK2: hard bright drums, a distorted 808, a supersaw-style synth built from samples, a hook you wrote and recorded yourself, pitched and hard-tuned into that unmistakable hyperpop voice, glitch edits and ear candy, a real pop arrangement (verse, pre-chorus, chorus), and a loud, bright mix. Along the way you will learn the genre's history, its sound design logic, and a repeatable song-building workflow, so the second song comes faster than the first.

## What this course is (and isn't)

This is a **genre production course**. Where MUS-45H taught you to make beats on the SP, this course teaches you to make *a specific kind of finished song*, and uses hyperpop to force every skill a beat course never touches: writing and recording a vocal, designing a sound palette on purpose, building pop structure, and mixing something loud and bright instead of warm and dusty. It covers:

- What hyperpop actually is: where it came from (PC Music, SOPHIE, 100 gecs), what defines the sound, and how to listen to it like a producer
- Building the **hyperpop palette** on a sampler: hard clipped drums, the distorted 808, and supersaw-style synths made from sampled notes and chromatic mode
- **Writing a hook**: short, blunt, singable, and honest about what a beginner can pull off
- **Recording your voice** into the SP at a clean level, and processing it with **Auto Pitch** (the SP's hard-tune), formant shift, pitch-up doubles, and stacked harmonies
- **Vocal chops**: turning your own hook into an instrument, stutters, and glitch edits
- **Ear candy and transitions**: risers, impacts, tape stops, and the drop
- **Pop song structure** on a pattern sequencer: verse, pre-chorus, chorus, and hyperpop's compressed forms
- **Maximalism done right**: layering without mud, and using silence so the loud parts actually hit
- A **loud, bright mix** that translates, and the difference between deliberate distortion and an accident

It deliberately **omits** what you do not need for your first finished song: music theory beyond finding a key by ear, DAW production, serious vocal technique, mastering, and synthesis from scratch on other hardware. Those doors stay open (a DAW version of this workflow, real sound design, vocal lessons) and this course names them when you pass them. The machine itself is covered only as deep as the song requires; [MUS-40H: Operating the SP-404 MK2](#/sp404-mk2-operation) is the full machine tour.

## A note on how this genre is actually learned

Hyperpop is a producer's genre born on laptops and internet forums, made by people who taught themselves by imitation: steal the drum sound from one song, the vocal move from another, the structure from a third, and something new falls out. That is exactly how this course works. Every week starts from **reference listening**, and every technique is taught as "here is the move, here is a song that uses it, now do it to your own material." The other thing the genre demands is **finishing**. Hyperpop songs are short, direct, and numerous; the culture rewards putting things out over polishing forever. You will finish a song in three weeks because finishing *is* the skill.

One more honest note: **you will hear your own voice a lot, processed and not.** Everyone hates their recorded voice at first. The genre's entire vocal aesthetic (pitched up, hard-tuned, doubled, buried in the mix when you want) exists to turn an ordinary voice into an instrument. Nobody is grading your singing. Record anyway.

## How to use this course

1. Read `SYLLABUS.md` first, then `RUBRIC.md` so you know what "good" looks like. Then read the **Field Guide** sections on the genre map, the effects this course uses, and clean sources before Day 1, and update your firmware (the vocal effects this course is built on arrived in updates).
2. Each day, work the lesson in order: **listen to the reference, read the lecture, do the work at the machine, then self-check from memory.**
3. **Always with the machine on and the mic within reach.** From Day 8 onward you record your voice most days. Set up so that takes thirty seconds, not ten minutes, or you will skip it.
4. **Back up weekly.** By Week 2 your project contains your own voice, which you cannot re-download. Protect it.
5. **Listen to hyperpop every day**, from the Field Guide listening list or your own finds, and name one production move per song. The genre is young and online; the whole curriculum is streaming for free.
6. Take the quizzes closed-book and grade yourself honestly against `ANSWER-KEYS.md`.
7. Do the projects. Each one is a section of the final song in disguise; if you do them properly, Day 21 is assembly, not panic.

## A legal and ethical note (read this once, now)

The same rules from MUS-45H apply: sampling other people's recordings without permission is, in most cases, copyright infringement, however hard you mangle it. Hyperpop makes this easy to handle, because the genre's core sources are **your own voice** (always yours) and **synth sounds**, which you can get royalty-free or record from any instrument you have access to. Learn from whatever you love; release only what is clean. The [Field Guide §6](FIELD-GUIDE.md) lists clean sources for every sound this course uses. One genre-specific note: interpolating a melody (re-singing someone else's hook) is also a copyright question, so for anything you release, write your own hooks. You will anyway; that is Day 8.

## A note on pace

Three weeks gets you one finished song and, more importantly, the **workflow**: palette, hook, vocal chain, structure, mix. That loop is the course's real deliverable. What three weeks cannot give you is a developed artistic identity or a trained voice; those come from making the next ten songs, which the workflow makes fast. Hyperpop's founding artists put out music constantly, and much of it was rough. Yours can be too. Finish it, post it or don't, and start the next one.

Plug in the mic. Let's make something loud.
