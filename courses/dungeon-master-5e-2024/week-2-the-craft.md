# Week 2: The Craft (Prep, Encounters, Adventures, Worlds, NPCs & Treasure)

> **The thesis of this week:** prep less, prepare better. The new DM's instinct is to write a novel the players will never read and then panic when they walk the other way. The craft is the opposite: prepare *situations*, not scripts, build only what the table will actually touch, and make every piece (an encounter, an NPC, a faction, a treasure) double as a tool you can reuse. By the end of this week you can prep a runnable session in about an hour, build a fight that's tuned and alive, structure an adventure that survives the players, and stand up a world and a cast sized for play.

---

# Day 8: Prep That Matters (The One-Hour Session)

### Objectives
- Prep a session by preparing situations, not a plot.
- Build a strong start, a handful of scenes, and a bank of secrets and clues.
- Use a one-page template so prep takes about an hour.
- Know what *not* to prep.

### Lecture

Most new DMs prep the wrong things, slowly. They write boxed text, plan exact dialogue, and chart the "correct" path through the adventure, and then the players ignore all of it and talk to a goblin instead. The fix is a shift in what prep *is*.

**Prepare situations, not plots.** A plot is a sequence of events you expect to happen ("the party finds the letter, goes to the docks, fights the smugglers, confronts the baron"). The moment players do something unexpected, a plot breaks. A **situation** is a set of pieces in tension that will *react* to whatever the players do: a baron with a secret, smugglers with a deadline, a missing shipment everyone wants. Drop the players in and let it play. Situations bend; plots snap.

**The one-hour prep method.** A reliable, low-prep checklist (adapted from the widely used "Lazy DM" approach; see [Field Guide Part 6](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#part-6-curated-free-resources-verified-real)) fits on one page (the template is in [Field Guide Part 4](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#part-4-prep-templates-copy-these)). In rough priority order:

1. **A strong start.** The first scene, already in motion, that drops the players straight into something requiring a decision. Never open with "you're all in a tavern, what do you do?" Open with the tavern *on fire*, or the contact already dead, or the deal going wrong. The strong start sets the energy for the whole session.
2. **Potential scenes.** Three to five likely scenes, as bullets, not scripts: "confront the harbormaster," "explore the flooded warehouse," "the ambush on the pier." You won't run all of them in order, and that's fine; they're a menu.
3. **Secrets and clues.** This is the highest-leverage prep there is. Write ~10 discrete **facts** the party could discover, with no fixed order and no fixed source. "The baron is being blackmailed." "The shipments stopped the week the priest vanished." "There's a tunnel from the chapel to the docks." Because they're unattached to a specific scene, you can drop any clue wherever the players happen to look, which is what keeps the game from stalling on a missed roll. We go deeper on clues on Day 10.
4. **Fantastic locations.** Two or three places with one evocative sentence each: a lighthouse whose lamp burns with cold blue fire, a market built in the ribs of a dead leviathan. A single strong image makes a location memorable; you fill in the rest live.
5. **NPCs.** A couple, with the quick-build (name, want, attitude, voice tag). Reuse from your stock.
6. **Monsters / encounters.** What might fight, with XP budget noted (Day 9).
7. **Rewards.** Treasure, an item, information, advancement (Day 12).

**What NOT to prep:** exact dialogue, boxed read-aloud text for every room (a few key ones, maybe), the "right" solution to anything, contingencies for things the players probably won't do, or any scene three steps deep that depends on two earlier scenes going exactly as planned. If you find yourself prepping branch four of decision tree C, stop. You're writing a novel.

> The test of good prep is not "did it all happen?" It's "was I never stuck?" Prep that gives you a strong start, a pile of unattached clues, and a few NPCs and locations will let you run almost any direction the players go. Prep that scripts a path will fail the first time they surprise you, which is always.

### Examples

**Example 8.1: Plot vs. situation.** *Plot prep:* "Scene 1: party meets informant Sela, who tells them about the warehouse. Scene 2: they go to the warehouse and find the ledger. Scene 3:..." If the players never find Sela, the whole thing collapses. *Situation prep:* "The smugglers must move the cursed cargo before the new moon (3 days). Sela knows the warehouse but is scared. The harbormaster is bribed. The cargo is in warehouse 7." Now whether the players start with Sela, the harbormaster, or by staking out the docks, the situation responds.

**Example 8.2: Unattached clues save the session.** You planned for the players to learn "the priest is the cultist" by finding his journal in the chapel. They skip the chapel entirely and interrogate a captured cultist instead. Because "the priest is the cultist" is a *clue*, not a *chapel-only fact*, you simply have the cultist let it slip. The session never stalls. This only works if you prepped clues as portable facts.

### Lab

1. **(15 min)** Take a one-line premise ("a mining town's children are sleepwalking into the woods at night") and write a strong start that drops the players into a decision in the first 60 seconds.
2. **(20 min)** Using the [one-page template](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#the-one-page-session-template), prep that premise into a runnable session: strong start, 3–5 scenes, **10 secrets/clues**, 2 NPCs, 2 locations, a likely encounter, a reward. Time yourself; aim for under an hour.
3. **(10 min)** Audit your clue list: for each clue, could the party get it from at least two different directions? Rewrite any clue chained to a single scene.
4. **(10 min)** Write your personal "do not prep" list: three things you're tempted to over-prepare and will stop preparing.
5. ★ Re-prep the same premise as if the players will go for the woods *first*; notice how little of your prep you'd have to change. That's the sign it's situation-based.

### Self-check
1. What's the difference between prepping a plot and prepping a situation, and why does it matter?
2. What is a "strong start," and why never open in a quiet tavern?
3. Why are secrets and clues the highest-leverage prep, and what makes a clue "portable"?
4. List the one-hour prep checklist from memory.
5. Name four things you should not prep.

# Day 9: Building Encounters (The 2024 XP Budget and Dynamic Fights)

### Objectives
- Build an encounter of a chosen difficulty with the 2024 XP budget.
- Account for the action economy, not just the math.
- Design encounters with terrain, objectives, and variety.
- Adjust difficulty fairly, on the fly.

### Lecture

A combat encounter has two layers: the **math** (is it the right size?) and the **design** (is it any good?). New DMs obsess over the first and skip the second. You need both.

**The math: the 2024 XP budget.** The full method and table are in [Field Guide Part 2](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#encounter-xp-budget-per-character). In short:

```
1. Pick a difficulty:  Low / Moderate / High
2. Read the per-character budget for the party's level from the table
3. Multiply by the number of characters  ->  your XP budget
4. Spend it: add up the XP value of each monster (from its stat block), up to the budget
```

There is **no encounter multiplier** in 2024 (unlike 2014): you just sum the monsters' XP. Example: a Moderate fight for four level-3 characters is 225 x 4 = 900 XP to spend. *Low* drains some resources, *Moderate* is a real fight, *High* could go badly. Most encounters in a session should be Low to Moderate, with a High one saved for a climax; a string of High fights with no rest will grind a party down (which is sometimes exactly what you want).

**The math's blind spot: action economy.** The budget treats "one monster worth 900 XP" and "nine monsters worth 100 XP each" as equal. They are not. Remember Day 5: power scales with the number of turns a side gets. A single big solo monster often *underperforms* its budget (the party's four-to-one action advantage shreds it, and one stun shuts it down for a round), while a swarm of weak monsters often *overperforms* (lots of attacks, lots of dice, hard to stop all at once). Practical rules of thumb:

- For a memorable boss, give the solo monster **help** (minions, lair actions, or the monster's own multi-action features) so it isn't outnumbered into irrelevance.
- A pile of low-CR monsters is deadlier than its XP suggests; respect it.
- The budget gets you in the ballpark. Then trust your read of the table.

**The design: make the fight a place and a problem, not a math rock.** A correctly-budgeted fight in an empty room is still boring. Add:

- **Terrain:** elevation, cover, difficult ground, hazards (a brazier to shove people into, a rickety bridge, deep water). Terrain creates choices.
- **An objective beyond "kill them":** protect someone, hold a position, reach a lever, escape, disrupt a ritual on a clock. (Recall Day 5.) Objectives are what make a fight a story.
- **Variety of roles:** mix a bruiser, some ranged attackers, and a controller or a leader, rather than five identical orcs. Different threats force different responses.
- **A reason to be there:** the fight should connect to the situation, not be a random speed bump.

**Adjusting on the fly, honestly.** You will sometimes misjudge a fight. It's fine to adjust *invisibly* and *fairly*: have reinforcements that were "coming" simply not arrive; let a monster flee when it's clearly lost (monsters value their lives); add a wave if the party is bored. What you should not do is fudge dice or silently inflate HP every round to "win"; players can feel when their choices don't matter, and it poisons the trust the whole game runs on. Adjust the *situation*, not the *outcome*.

> Difficulty is not the same as fun. A perfectly "balanced" fight in a featureless box is forgettable; a slightly-too-easy fight on a crumbling tower in a thunderstorm, racing a villain to the top, is the one they'll retell for years. Spend your prep time on the place and the problem, not on shaving the XP to the exact budget.

### Examples

**Example 9.1: Budgeting a fight.** Party: five level-5 characters. You want a Moderate fight. From the table, level-5 Moderate is 750 per character: 750 x 5 = 3,750 XP. You spend it on, say, a small cult cell: one leader and a handful of cultists and a guard beast, summing their stat-block XP up toward (not over) 3,750. Done.

**Example 9.2: The solo-boss trap.** You build a "High" fight as a single big monster worth the whole budget. In play it loses initiative, eats a Stun, and is dead before it acts twice; anticlimax. Rebuilt: the same boss at a lower XP *plus* three minions and a lair feature that lets it act on a second initiative. Now it threatens the whole party every round and the four-to-one action math doesn't trivialize it. Same budget, real fight.

**Example 9.3: Same monsters, better encounter.** "Six goblins in a cave" versus "six goblins defending a rope bridge over an underground river, with two of them ready to cut the ropes and a captive tied at the far end." Identical XP. The second has terrain, an objective (save the captive / reach the far side), and a ticking threat. Always build the second one.

### Lab

1. **(15 min)** Build three encounters for a named party (pick a level and size): one Low, one Moderate, one High, each using the [XP budget table](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#encounter-xp-budget-per-character). Note the budget and the monsters that fill it.
2. **(10 min)** Take your Moderate encounter and redesign it twice as a solo-plus-minions version and as a swarm version. Predict which will feel harder and why.
3. **(10 min)** Add terrain, an objective, and a reason-to-be-there to one of your fights. Sketch the battlefield as simple ASCII or boxes.
4. **(10 min)** Write your "fair adjustments" toolkit: three ways you'll make a fight harder mid-combat and three ways you'll make it easier, all without fudging dice.
5. ★ Look up three monsters you've never used and note their XP and one tactical quirk each, to expand your roster.

### Self-check
1. State the four steps of building an encounter with the 2024 budget.
2. What changed from 2014: is there still an encounter multiplier?
3. Why does a single big solo monster often underperform its budget, and how do you fix it?
4. Name three design elements that make a fight more than a math problem.
5. What's the difference between adjusting the situation and fudging the outcome, and why does it matter?

# Day 10: Adventure Design (Structure That Survives the Players)

### Objectives
- Choose between location-based and event-based adventure structures.
- Use the five-room dungeon as a reliable one-session skeleton.
- Design a mystery that doesn't dead-end, using the three-clue rule.
- Write hooks players actually bite.

### Lecture

An adventure is a session or few of connected play with a beginning, a middle, and a payoff. The trick is structuring it so it holds together *without* forcing the players down a single track.

**Two reliable structures:**

- **Location-based:** the adventure is a *place* (a dungeon, a haunted manor, a ruined city) that the party explores in whatever order they choose. The structure lives in the map and the rooms. Great for exploration, easy to run, naturally non-linear.
- **Event-based:** the adventure is a *situation in motion* (a heist, a festival that goes wrong, a siege, a murder to solve) where things happen over time and the party reacts. The structure lives in the timeline and the factions. Great for intrigue and drama.

Most adventures blend both: a location to explore *and* a clock ticking while you do it. You already prep the situation (Day 8); these are two shapes that situation can take.

**The five-room dungeon: a one-session skeleton.** A famous, flexible template (by Johnn Four) for a satisfying short adventure. The "rooms" are beats, not literal rooms:

```
1. ENTRANCE & GUARDIAN     A way in with an obstacle (a fight, a barrier, a gatekeeper) that signals "the adventure starts here."
2. PUZZLE OR ROLEPLAY       A non-combat challenge: a riddle, a negotiation, a trap to bypass.
3. TRICK OR SETBACK         A twist: things aren't what they seemed, a complication, a reversal.
4. CLIMAX / BIG BATTLE       The hardest challenge and the emotional peak (often, but not always, a fight).
5. REWARD / REVELATION       The payoff: treasure, the rescue, and a revelation or hook to what's next.
```

It works because it guarantees pillar variety (combat, exploration, social), an arc, and a payoff in one sitting. Use it as a checklist when an adventure feels flat: which beat is missing?

**Mysteries and the three-clue rule.** New DMs love a mystery and then watch in horror as the players miss the one clue and the adventure dies. The fix is the **three-clue rule** (Justin Alexander; see [Field Guide Part 6](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#part-6-curated-free-resources-verified-real)): *for any conclusion you need the players to reach, include at least three clues that point to it.* Players are creative and will misread, ignore, or destroy clues; with three, they can flub two and still get there. Better still, the third clue is your safety net when they go somewhere you didn't plan. This pairs exactly with the portable-clues idea from Day 8.

**Hooks: why the party engages at all.** A hook is the reason the players choose to start this adventure. Weak hooks ("a mysterious stranger gives you a quest") work once and feel hollow. Strong hooks tie to **what the characters already want or fear**: their backstory, their faction, their bonds, a threat to something they value, a reward they need. The best hook is one a player would chase even if you didn't dangle it. Ask in session zero what each character wants, then build hooks out of those answers. A few reliable hook types are in [Field Guide Part 5](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#part-5-random-tables-the-improv-safety-net).

> Linearity is not the enemy; *forced* linearity is. A dungeon can be linear (one path) and still feel free because the players choose how to face each room. A mystery can have a fixed solution and still feel open because there are many roads to it. Design the destination if you must, but never the single road; give the players three roads and let them pick.

### Examples

**Example 10.1: One premise, two structures.** "A cult is summoning something under the old observatory." *Location-based:* the observatory is a five-room dungeon the party clears at their pace. *Event-based:* the summoning happens at the eclipse in two nights, and the party races around town gathering what they need to stop it, with the cult acting each night. Same premise, two very different sessions; pick the one that fits your table.

**Example 10.2: Three clues to one killer.** You need the party to suspect the steward. Clue 1: the murder weapon is from the kitchen, which only staff access. Clue 2: a servant saw the steward washing his hands at an odd hour. Clue 3: the steward's ledger has a payment from the victim's rival. Miss any one and two remain. None is tied to a single room, so you can surface them wherever the players investigate.

### Lab

1. **(15 min)** Design a five-room dungeon for a one-session adventure: one line per beat, hitting all five. Make sure beats 2 and 4 aren't both combat.
2. **(15 min)** Take a mystery ("who poisoned the baron?") and write the solution plus *three* portable clues that point to it, each discoverable in at least two ways.
3. **(10 min)** Write the same adventure premise as a location-based outline and an event-based timeline. Decide which you'd run and why.
4. **(10 min)** Write three strong hooks for one adventure, each tied to a different character want or fear (invent the characters if needed).
5. ★ Take a published one-shot you can find free online and identify its structure and its clue redundancy; note where it would dead-end on a missed roll.

### Self-check
1. Contrast location-based and event-based adventures; where does each one's structure "live"?
2. List the five beats of the five-room dungeon.
3. State the three-clue rule and the two problems it solves.
4. What makes a hook strong rather than hollow?
5. What's the difference between linearity and forced linearity?

# Day 11: Worldbuilding for Play (Build Only What You Use)

### Objectives
- Choose top-down or bottom-up worldbuilding for your situation.
- Build a "home base" that anchors the campaign.
- Make a world feel lived-in without writing an encyclopedia.
- Write a three-sentence campaign premise.

### Lecture

Worldbuilding is the most fun part for many new DMs and the biggest trap. You can spend months drawing maps and inventing the genealogy of elven kings and never run a single session. The discipline: **build only what the players will touch, just before they touch it, and let the rest stay fog.**

**Top-down vs. bottom-up.** Two valid directions:

- **Top-down:** start big (the world, the continent, the major powers) and zoom in. Good if you have a grand vision or are using a published setting (the 2024 DMG ships with a gazetteer of **Greyhawk**, the game's classic default setting, precisely so you can borrow a ready-made world instead of inventing one). Risk: you build a lot the party never sees.
- **Bottom-up:** start small (the starting town, the local problem, the dungeon down the road) and grow outward as play demands. Good for new DMs and most campaigns. You invent the kingdom's politics only once the players care about them. Risk: less coherence up front, which you backfill as you go.

For your first campaign, **build bottom-up around a home base.** It is far easier to grow a world from one good town than to populate a continent you'll never visit.

**The home base.** A single anchoring location the party returns to: a town, a city ward, a frontier outpost, a ship. Give it just enough to feel real and to generate play:

```
HOME BASE STARTER KIT
- A name and a one-line identity ("a fog-bound fishing town that's seen better days").
- 3-5 key locations the party will use (the inn, the smith, the temple, the dock, the ruin nearby).
- 3-5 NPCs with wants (the worried mayor, the smith who knows secrets, the suspicious priest).
- 2-3 factions with agendas (Day 12).
- 1 looming problem (the hook engine: the disappearances, the failing harvest, the new lord).
```

That's a campaign's worth of starting material, and you can prep it in an evening. Everything beyond the home base, you sketch in a sentence and detail only when the party heads that way.

**Making a world feel lived-in.** A world feels real not from page count but from a few cheap techniques:

- **Consequences:** what the party does changes things. The bandits they cleared mean the road is safe now and a merchant thanks them by name next session. Reactivity beats lore.
- **Offscreen life:** NPCs and factions pursue goals when the party isn't watching (Day 12's clocks). The world should move whether or not they're there.
- **Specific, sensory details:** the local bread, the festival nobody outside town has heard of, the slang, the weather. One concrete detail implies a whole culture.
- **Restraint:** trust the players' imagination to fill gaps. You don't need to describe the entire pantheon; you need the one god this village fears.

**The campaign premise in three sentences.** Before you build, name what the campaign *is*, tightly enough to pitch to your players: the setting and tone, the central situation or threat, and what the party will be doing about it. "A waterlogged frontier where an old flood-god is stirring. Towns along the river are vanishing one by one. You're the only ones who've noticed, and the only ones willing to go upriver to find out why." That's enough to start, and it tells your players what kind of game to build characters for.

> The single most freeing realization in worldbuilding: the players cannot tell the difference between the world you built and the world you invent on the spot, as long as you're consistent. So build the minimum, stay consistent, and write down what you improvise so it becomes canon. Your campaign bible is mostly written *after* play, not before.

### Examples

**Example 11.1: Bottom-up in action.** You start with one town and a haunted mine. The players ask who rules the region; you didn't know, so you invent a distant duke in one sentence and write it down. Three sessions later they want to petition the duke, so *now* you build the duke's court, because now it's load-bearing. You never wasted prep on a court they might have ignored.

**Example 11.2: Cheap lived-in detail.** Instead of three pages on the town's economy, you decide: they eat a lot of eel, distrust outsiders from upriver, and ring the temple bell at dusk to "keep the river quiet." Three details, and the town feels like a place with a culture and a fear, which doubles as foreshadowing for your flood-god plot.

### Lab

1. **(10 min)** Write a three-sentence campaign premise (setting/tone, central situation, what the party does). Make it pitchable to real players.
2. **(20 min)** Build a home base with the starter kit: name and identity, 3–5 locations, 3–5 NPCs with wants, and the looming problem. (Factions come tomorrow.)
3. **(10 min)** Add three cheap "lived-in" details to your home base (a food, a custom, a fear or piece of slang) and note how at least one foreshadows your premise.
4. **(10 min)** List five things about your world you are deliberately *not* building yet, and the one-sentence fog version of each.
5. ★ Decide whether you'll use a published setting (e.g., Greyhawk from the 2024 DMG) or homebrew, and write one sentence on why that fits your table.

### Self-check
1. Contrast top-down and bottom-up worldbuilding; which is recommended for a first campaign and why?
2. What goes in a home-base starter kit?
3. Name three cheap techniques that make a world feel lived-in.
4. What three things does a campaign premise state?
5. Why is your campaign bible mostly written after play, not before?

# Day 12: NPCs, Factions, and the Reward Economy

### Objectives
- Make a memorable NPC fast and keep a reusable stock.
- Build factions with wants, methods, and advancing clocks.
- Run the reward economy: treasure, magic items, and leveling.
- Tie rewards to the fiction so they mean something.

### Lecture

This day turns your static world into a moving one and gives the party something to chase.

**NPCs, fast and reusable.** You met the quick-build on Day 4 (name, look, want, attitude, voice tag; see [Field Guide Part 4](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#npc-quick-build-run-cold-in-15-seconds)). The campaign-level skills:

- **Keep a stock.** Maintain a short list of pre-built NPCs you can drop in anywhere. When the players talk to someone you didn't plan, you pull from stock instead of freezing.
- **Reskin stat blocks.** You almost never need a custom stat block. The shopkeeper is a Commoner; the bodyguard is a Guard or Knight; the cult leader is a Cult Fanatic; the assassin is a Bandit Captain with a different name. Change the description, keep the numbers.
- **Promote the keepers.** When the table loves an NPC (and you can't predict which one they will), give that NPC more depth and bring them back. Recurring NPCs are how a world earns affection.

**Factions: the engine of a living world.** A faction is any group with a goal: a thieves' guild, a noble house, a cult, a town council, a monster tribe. Factions are what make the world move on its own. Build each one with the [faction sheet](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#faction-sheet): a **want**, a **method**, an **asset**, a **weakness**, a **face** (the NPC the party meets), and a **clock**.

**Clocks make factions act offscreen.** A clock is a short list of the next moves a faction will make *whether or not the party intervenes*:

```
FACTION: The Ashen Hand (cult)
  WANTS: to wake the flood-god and "cleanse" the valley
  CLOCK:  [ ] 1. Abduct the temple bell-ringer (silences the wards)
          [ ] 2. Flood the lower town as a "sign"
          [ ] 3. Perform the waking ritual at the dam
```

Each session (or downtime period), if the party doesn't stop them, the cult advances its clock and the world visibly changes. This is the secret to a campaign that feels alive: threats that *progress* instead of politely waiting for the party. Multiple factions with their own clocks, sometimes in conflict, generate endless play without you scripting a plot. (Clocks come from the Apocalypse World / Blades in the Dark tradition; see [Field Guide Part 6](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#part-6-curated-free-resources-verified-real).)

**The reward economy.** Rewards are how the party feels their effort mattered. Three currencies:

- **Treasure.** Coins, gems, and art objects, plus the occasional consumable. Hand out treasure hoards at meaningful moments, scaled to the party's tier (see [Field Guide Part 2](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#treasure-2024-structure)). Money matters most at low levels; give the party enough that gold *decisions* exist (gear, bribes, a base) without drowning them.
- **Magic items.** The exciting reward. Use rarity and attunement to pace them (see [Field Guide Part 2](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#magic-items-rarity-attunement-and-pacing)): a character attunes to at most three at once, which is your throttle. Prefer items with character: a blade that hums near undead beats a generic +1 sword. Give items that fit what a character loves to do, and you'll see their eyes light up.
- **Advancement (leveling).** Choose **milestone** (level up at story beats you set) or **XP** (track points). For a first campaign, milestone is simpler and lets you pace the story; see [Field Guide Part 2](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#rewards-xp-vs-milestone). Either way, leveling is itself a reward; time it to land on a high note, like just after a hard-won victory.

**Tie rewards to the fiction.** A chest of gold in room 6 is forgettable. The drowned merchant's signet ring, still on the skeleton's hand, that opens the harbor vault, is a reward *and* a hook *and* a story. Every reward can pull double duty if it comes from somewhere and points somewhere.

> A campaign's momentum comes from two engines running together: factions advancing clocks (pressure to act) and rewards that matter (reasons to act). Keep both running and the players will always have something to push against and something to chase. Let either stall and the campaign drifts.

### Examples

**Example 12.1: Reskinning instead of building.** The party demands a fight with the harbormaster's enforcer, who you never statted. You grab the **Bandit Captain** stat block, rename it, describe a scarred woman with a boarding axe, and you're running a balanced fight in ten seconds. The players never know (or care) it's a stock block.

**Example 12.2: A clock changing the world.** The party spends a session in the next town over and ignores the cult. Per the cult's clock, step 1 fires: they return to find the bell-ringer gone and the wards failing, townsfolk frightened. The party's *inaction* had consequences, which makes their future choices feel weighty. The world didn't wait for them.

**Example 12.3: A reward that's also a hook.** Instead of "you find 500 gp," they find a waterlogged strongbox holding 300 gp and a sealed letter ordering the cult's next abduction, signed with a symbol they don't recognize yet. Treasure, clue, and foreshadowing in one object.

### Lab

1. **(15 min)** Build two factions for your home base with the [faction sheet](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#faction-sheet): want, method, asset, weakness, face, and a three-step clock each. Make their goals capable of conflict.
2. **(10 min)** Build a stock of five reusable NPCs (quick-build) and note which stat block you'd reskin for each if a fight broke out.
3. **(10 min)** Plan rewards for your Project-2 adventure: a treasure hoard appropriate to the tier, one magic item with character, and your leveling choice (milestone or XP) with the trigger.
4. **(10 min)** Turn one plain reward into a fiction-tied one that doubles as a clue or hook.
5. ★ Decide how often each faction's clock advances (per session? per downtime?) and write the first tick for each.

### Self-check
1. How do you avoid freezing when players talk to an unplanned NPC?
2. What's on a faction sheet, and what is a clock for?
3. Why do clocks make a world feel alive?
4. Name the three reward currencies and how you pace magic items.
5. Why tie a reward to the fiction, and give an example.

# Day 14: Rest / Review + Bastions, Downtime & the Long Campaign

Light day, three pieces:

1. A couple of warm-up drills from [DRILLS.md](DRILLS.md), slow and for fun.
2. **Read [Field Guide Part 3](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#part-3-bastions-downtime-the-long-campaign-read-day-14):** downtime, the campaign calendar, and the 2024 **bastion** system. You won't use bastions until the party hits level 5, but understanding them now shapes how you build a home base worth defending and what the party does between adventures. Note how a bastion's facilities and the faction clocks can advance in the same downtime period; that's your "world moves while they rest" engine.
3. Re-do any quiz sections you missed and polish Project 2. If you have 20 spare minutes, read one free article from [Field Guide Part 6](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#part-6-curated-free-resources-verified-real) (the Lazy DM prep notes or the three-clue rule) to deepen this week's ideas.

**Looking ahead.** You can now build the game: prep fast, tune fights, structure adventures, stand up a world and a cast, and reward a party. Week 3 is the hardest and most important skill of all: running it *live* in front of real people, when the prep meets the players and the players do something you never imagined. We'll cover pacing, rulings on the fly, the human side of the table, sustaining a campaign over months, and then you'll prep and actually run Session 1.
