# Week 3: Running & Sustaining (At the Table and Over Time)

> **The thesis of this week:** prep is a rehearsal; the performance is live, in front of real people, when your careful plan meets five players who immediately do something you never imagined. This is the week you learn to run *that*: to pace a session, share the spotlight, make a fair ruling in two seconds instead of stopping to read, improvise when the map runs out, handle the human side of a real table, and steer a campaign across months so it pays off instead of fizzling. Then you do the only thing that actually makes a DM: you sit down and run a game.

# Day 15: Running the Session Live (Pacing, Spotlight, and Flow)

### Objectives
- Open and close a session strongly.
- Pace a session: cut, transition, and keep energy up.
- Share the spotlight so every player gets a moment.
- Narrate results that push the story forward.

### Lecture

Everything so far was preparation. Now you run it, and running is a distinct skill: managing attention, energy, and time in real time, for several people at once.

**Open strong, every time.** Begin each session with a quick **recap** (do it yourself, or better, ask a player to recap; it gets everyone back in the world and tells you what they remember) and then your **strong start** (Day 8): drop straight into a scene with momentum. Never open a session by asking "so, what do you want to do?" into silence. Open with something happening.

**Pacing is the master skill of running live.** Pacing is controlling the *rate* at which interesting things happen. The tools:

- **Cut to the action.** Skip the boring parts out loud: "You spend the afternoon asking around; by evening, two things are clear..." Don't play out travel, shopping, or downtime beat by beat unless something's at stake.
- **Vary the rhythm.** Alternate intensity: a tense fight, then a quiet social scene to breathe, then exploration with a slow build. A session that's all combat exhausts; all talk drifts. Watch the table's energy and change gears when it flags.
- **Read the room.** Bored faces mean cut or escalate. Leaning in means slow down and let the scene breathe. The players' attention is your tachometer.
- **Use a clock when energy sags.** If a scene stalls, introduce time pressure or a complication (the patrol returns, the tide rises). Pressure restarts a dead scene.

**Spotlight management.** In a group, attention is a limited resource, and your job is to share it fairly. Quiet players get crowded out by loud ones unless you deliberately turn to them: "Kessa, you've been watching the crowd, what do you do?" Make sure each pillar gives different characters their moment (the face shines in social, the rogue in exploration, the fighter in combat), and actively pass the spotlight to anyone who's gone quiet. A player who never gets a moment stops coming back.

**Narrate results that move forward.** When the dice land, your narration should change the situation, not just report pass/fail. A failed lockpick isn't "nothing happens"; it's "the pick snaps, and you hear boots on the stair." A success isn't just "you persuade her"; it's "she sighs, glances at the door, and lowers her voice." Every result should hand the players a new situation to react to. This keeps the loop spinning.

**End on a hook.** Close the session at a moment of tension or a fresh question, not after the loose ends are tidied: the door creaks open to reveal..., the letter names someone they trust, the horizon fills with smoke. A session that ends on a hook is a session everyone's eager to continue. Then do a 60-second wrap: confirm where they are, hand out any advancement, and maybe ask "best moment tonight?" to end on warmth.

> The difference between a session that flies by and one that drags is almost never the prep; it's the pacing. Learn to cut. Most new DMs play out everything and bore the table; experienced DMs skip ruthlessly to the parts that matter and the same adventure suddenly sings. When in doubt, cut to the next interesting decision.

### Examples

**Example 15.1: Cutting vs. playing it all.** Players want to buy supplies, travel two days, and reach the keep. The slow way: roleplay each shop, each night's camp, each travel roll. The fast way: "You gear up (anyone want anything specific?), and after two uneventful days the keep's broken tower comes into view at dusk, with smoke rising from inside. What do you do?" Thirty seconds, and you're in the next real scene.

**Example 15.2: Passing the spotlight.** The party's bard has dominated a negotiation for ten minutes. The DM turns: "While Finn talks, Dura, you're closest to the guard, what's your read on him? And Pike, you've slipped toward the back office, what are you doing?" Two quiet players just got a moment, and the scene got richer.

### Lab

1. **(10 min)** Write a strong recap-and-open for an imagined session 4: a one-paragraph recap and a strong-start scene with momentum.
2. **(15 min)** Practice cutting: take three "boring stretches" (a week of downtime, a long sea voyage, a day of shopping) and narrate each into the next interesting moment in two or three sentences.
3. **(10 min)** Rewrite five flat result-narrations ("you fail," "you succeed," "nothing happens," "you hit it," "you pick the lock") into versions that change the situation.
4. **(10 min)** Write three spotlight-passing lines you can reuse to bring quiet players in.
5. ★ **[needs a table]** Run a 30-minute scene and afterward note where it dragged and who didn't get a moment. **Solo version:** narrate a scene aloud and time it; mark every place you'd cut on a second pass.

### Self-check
1. How should you open a session, and why never open with silence?
2. Name three pacing tools and what "read the room" means.
3. What is spotlight management, and why does it matter for retention?
4. What makes a result-narration good rather than flat?
5. Why end a session on a hook, and what's in the 60-second wrap?

# Day 16: Rulings, Not Rules

### Objectives
- Make a fair, fast ruling on something the rules don't cover.
- Balance the "rule of cool" against fairness and consistency.
- Handle abilities and spells you don't know without stalling.
- Avoid the common 2024 rules pitfalls.

### Lecture

No rulebook covers everything a player will try, and stopping to look up the ones it does cover kills momentum. The core DM skill, behind the screen, is **making rulings**: fair decisions, made fast, that keep the game moving.

**The ruling procedure** (fast enough to do live):

```
1. What is the player actually trying to do, and why? (Ask if unclear.)
2. Is it auto-yes or auto-no? (Day 2.) If so, narrate it; done.
3. If uncertain: which ability/skill fits, and what's a fair DC? (Default 15.)
4. State the stakes (success and failure), then roll.
5. Narrate the result so it changes the situation.
6. Move on. Note anything to double-check later; do not stop to look it up now.
```

**Rulings over rules, but be consistent.** A ruling you make once becomes a precedent your table will expect. So make rulings you're willing to repeat. Consistency is what makes a ruling feel fair rather than arbitrary: if shoving someone into the fire worked for the fighter last week, it works for the enemy this week. When you do get a rule wrong (you will), own it: "I ran that wrong last time; here's the actual rule, we'll use it going forward." Players respect a DM who's honest far more than one who pretends to be infallible.

**The rule of cool, used well.** "Rule of cool" means sometimes you let an awesome idea succeed (or attempt) even if the rules are fuzzy or slightly against it, because it makes a great moment. It's a real tool. The guardrails:

- It should cost a roll, not be free; let them *attempt* the cool thing with a fair (maybe hard) check and real stakes.
- It must apply to enemies too. If the player can swing from the chandelier, so can the villain.
- It shouldn't trivialize a class feature someone built their character around (don't let an Athletics check do what a specific spell does, or that player's choices stop mattering).

Cool *and* fair. The fairness is what keeps the cool from curdling into favoritism.

**Handling things you don't know.** A player casts a spell you've never read, or uses a subclass feature you don't recognize. You do not have to know every rule.

- Ask the player to read you the ability; trust but verify, and let it work as written unless it's clearly broken.
- If a true edge case will take more than 20 seconds to resolve, **make a quick ruling now and look it up at the break.** Tell the table that's what you're doing.
- Keep the [Field Guide](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md) screen handy for the common stuff (conditions, DCs, actions) so the *frequent* lookups are instant.

**Common 2024 pitfalls to rule cleanly:**

- **Calling for too many rolls.** The most common new-DM error. Most actions are auto-yes (Day 2). Roll only for uncertain, interesting outcomes.
- **Forgetting "say yes."** When players propose something reasonable, the default is yes, not "roll to see if the world cooperates."
- **Letting one Influence roll decide a whole social scene.** Roleplay first; the roll resolves a real attempt (Day 4).
- **Forgetting monster reactions and that bonus actions require a source** (Day 5).
- **Forgetting the new actions exist** (Influence/Study/Search/Utilize/Magic); using them gives players clear verbs and you cleaner adjudication.

> The players are not at your table to watch you read a rulebook. They are there to do cool things and find out what happens. A confident, fair ruling that keeps the game moving is almost always better than a "correct" interpretation that stops it cold. You can always fix a ruling next session; you can't get back a dead twenty minutes.

### Examples

**Example 16.1: A clean ruling.** Player: "I throw my wineglass in his face and draw my sword while he's blinded." Not in the rules exactly. Ruling: "Sleight of Hand or an attack-style Dexterity check, DC 13; on a hit he's effectively blinded against your next attack (advantage), on a miss you've wasted your action drawing and splashing. Roll it." Two seconds, fair, and the player feels clever. And if a goblin does the same next week, it works the same way.

**Example 16.2: Rule of cool with guardrails.** A barbarian at 1 HP wants to leap off a balcony onto the dragon's neck. You don't say "you can't"; you say "Acrobatics, DC 18, and if you make it you're riding the dragon with advantage on your attacks, if you miss you take fall damage and land prone in its reach. Worth it?" Cool, costly, and the dragon could try the same kind of stunt on them.

### Lab

1. **(15 min)** Make the ruling, fast, for five out-of-the-box player actions (e.g., "I tip the bookshelf onto the cultists," "I try to talk the ghost into possessing me on purpose," "I freeze the lock with my waterskin and shatter it"). For each: ability, DC, stakes, in under 15 seconds.
2. **(10 min)** Take one "rule of cool" request and write how you'd allow it with a roll, real stakes, and a note on how an enemy could use the same move.
3. **(10 min)** Write your personal "ruling procedure" card (the six steps above, in your own words) for your screen.
4. **(10 min)** List the five common pitfalls and write one sentence on how you'll personally avoid each.
5. ★ Recall a rule you're unsure about, make a ruling now, then look up the real rule and compare; note whether your ruling was close enough to keep using in a pinch.

### Self-check
1. State the ruling procedure in order.
2. Why must rulings be consistent, and how do you handle getting a rule wrong?
3. What are the guardrails on the rule of cool?
4. What do you do when a player uses an ability you don't know?
5. Name three common adjudication pitfalls and the fix for each.

# Day 17: The Table and Its People

### Objectives
- Run a session zero that sets up a healthy table.
- Use safety tools so the game stays fun for everyone.
- Handle the common problem situations at a table.
- Deal with the real-world logistics that actually kill campaigns.

### Lecture

The rules are the easy part. The hard, and most important, part of being a DM is the people. A campaign lives or dies on whether the table is a place people want to return to.

**Session zero: the most valuable session you'll run.** Before the campaign starts, gather the players (no dice) and agree on the foundations. A good session zero covers:

- **The pitch and tone.** You present your three-sentence premise (Day 11) and the tone (gritty? heroic? comedic? horror?) so everyone builds characters that fit *this* game.
- **Expectations.** How often you'll play, how long, where, and what "showing up" means. Mismatched expectations (one player wants deep roleplay, another wants a tactical dungeon crawl) are easier to reconcile now than mid-campaign.
- **Characters and bonds.** Build characters together so they have reasons to be a party and hooks you can use. A party of strangers with no connection is a campaign on hard mode.
- **Lines and veils (safety).** Agree on content that's off the table entirely (**lines**) and content that happens but "off-screen" (**veils**). This isn't about being fragile; it's about everyone knowing the boundaries so they can relax and take risks inside them.

Project 1 is your session-zero packet; this is where it pays off.

**Safety tools** keep the game fun under pressure. They are standard, lightweight, and used at countless tables (see the TTRPG Safety Toolkit in [Field Guide Part 6](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#part-6-curated-free-resources-verified-real)):

- **Lines and veils** (above), set in session zero and adjustable any time.
- **The X-card:** anyone can tap a card (or say "X") to pull back from content that's making them uncomfortable, no explanation required. You smoothly steer away. It costs nothing and makes everyone safer to be bold.
- **Open the door:** make it normal that anyone can step out for a minute. A table where people feel safe is a table where people take the dramatic risks that make D&D great.

**Common problem situations and how to handle them:**

| Situation | Handle it by |
|---|---|
| One player dominates the spotlight | Actively turn to quiet players (Day 15); talk to the dominant player privately, kindly |
| A player is disruptive or rude | Address it directly outside the game; the table's comfort outranks any one player |
| Players going "off the rails" | That's not a problem, that's the game (Day 19); follow them, your prep is portable |
| Rules-lawyering mid-combat | Make the ruling, offer to check at the break, move on (Day 16) |
| Player-vs-player conflict | Distinguish character conflict (can be fun, if all consent) from player conflict (stop it) |
| Someone's clearly not having fun | Ask them, privately, what they want more of; adjust |

The throughline: **most table problems are people problems, and people problems are solved by talking to people, kindly and directly, usually away from the table.** No rules patch fixes a social issue.

**The logistics that actually kill campaigns.** Be honest: most campaigns don't end in a dramatic finale; they end because scheduling collapsed. Defend against it:

- **Pick a realistic cadence** and protect it (weekly, biweekly, monthly; consistency beats frequency).
- **Decide your "go" rule:** do you play if one person can't make it? Most healthy tables play with three of five present rather than cancel; a campaign that needs perfect attendance dies fast.
- **Keep sessions self-contained enough** that a missed session isn't catastrophic, and have a plan for absent characters (they're "in the back," a quick recap catches them up).
- **End on time.** Respecting people's time is why they come back.

> You will spend more energy as a DM on the social and logistical layer than on rules or prep, and that's correct. A mediocre adventure at a warm, well-run table beats a brilliant one at a tense or flaky table every time. Protect the people and the schedule, and the rest is just play.

### Examples

**Example 17.1: A line in action.** In session zero a player quietly notes that harm to children is a hard line for them. Later, your prepped cult plot involved abducted children. You adjust off-screen: the cult abducts the town's *bell-ringers* instead. The plot loses nothing; the player stays comfortable and engaged. That's the whole point of asking up front.

**Example 17.2: The scheduling save.** A five-person table keeps canceling because someone's always out. The DM sets a "three or more, we play" rule and a quick way to sideline absent characters. Suddenly they're playing twice as often, and the campaign survives. Nothing about the rules changed; the logistics did.

### Lab

1. **(15 min)** Write your session-zero agenda: the pitch, the tone, the expectations you'll set, and how you'll build characters together.
2. **(10 min)** Draft how you'll introduce safety tools (lines/veils and the X-card) in one short, non-awkward paragraph you'd actually say.
3. **(10 min)** For three problem situations from the table above, write what you'd actually say or do.
4. **(10 min)** Decide your real cadence and your "go" rule, and write your plan for absent players.
5. ★ **[needs a table]** If you have your group, run a real 30-minute session zero. **Solo version:** record yourself delivering the pitch and the safety-tools intro as if to players.

### Self-check
1. What does a session zero cover, and why is it so valuable?
2. Define lines and veils and the X-card; what are safety tools *for*?
3. What's the throughline for solving table problems?
4. Why is "going off the rails" not actually a problem?
5. What real-world factor kills most campaigns, and how do you defend against it?

# Day 18: The Campaign Over Time (Arcs, Leveling, and Escalation)

### Objectives
- Structure a campaign as an arc across the tiers of play.
- Pace leveling with milestone or XP.
- Escalate threats with recurring villains, clocks, and fronts.
- Keep continuity with recaps and notes.

### Lecture

A campaign is not a long adventure; it's a series of adventures with a throughline, escalating over many sessions. Running one well is a different skill from running a single session.

**The shape of a campaign: the tiers of play.** D&D's twenty levels fall into four tiers (see [Field Guide Part 2](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#treasure-2024-structure)), and a campaign's stakes should grow with them:

```
Tier 1 (1-4):   Local heroes. Save the village, clear the mine, stop the bandits.
Tier 2 (5-10):  Regional heroes. Powers notice you; bastions appear; threats span a region.
Tier 3 (11-16): Masters of the realm. Nations and planes are at stake.
Tier 4 (17-20): Legends. The world or the multiverse hangs in the balance.
```

Most campaigns live in tiers 1–2 and that's great; many never go higher and end satisfyingly. Plan your campaign's *spine* (the central conflict and its escalation) but only in broad strokes, because the players will reshape it. Think "the flood-god cult grows from a local nuisance to a regional catastrophe" not "in session 14 they will fight the high priest."

**Leveling, paced.** Pick milestone or XP (Day 12). Whichever you choose, **pace leveling to the story**, not the calendar: level the party after a meaningful accomplishment, ideally on a high note. Early levels can go fast (levels 1–3 are fragile and not very distinct; many DMs speed through them); the middle tiers are where most campaigns settle in. Don't level so fast that powers never get used, or so slow that play stagnates.

**Escalation: making the stakes climb.** A campaign feels alive when threats *grow*:

- **Recurring villains.** A villain the party meets, fails to stop, and meets again is worth ten one-off monsters. Let villains escape (sometimes), learn from the party, and come back stronger and angrier. The best final boss is one the players already hate from three sessions ago.
- **Clocks and fronts.** You built faction clocks on Day 12. A **front** (from the Apocalypse World tradition; see [Field Guide Part 6](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#part-6-curated-free-resources-verified-real)) is a larger looming threat made of several clocks: the doom that advances if the party does nothing. Running two or three fronts at once, sometimes in conflict, gives the campaign pressure and texture without a scripted plot. Advance them in downtime and between sessions so the world is always moving.
- **Foreshadowing and payoff.** Plant things early (a symbol, a name, a prophecy) and pay them off later. Even if you didn't know what the symbol meant when you introduced it, you can decide later and look like a genius. Players cannot tell foresight from improvisation, only consistency.

**Continuity: don't trust your memory.** Across months you will forget names, promises, and threads. Defend with:

- **A session recap** at the start of each game (have a player do it).
- **Running notes:** an NPC list, a faction-clock tracker, and a "loose threads" list you mine for hooks. (Your [one-page template](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#the-one-page-session-template) has slots for clocks and loose ends for exactly this.)
- **Reincorporation:** bring back earlier NPCs, places, and choices. A world that remembers what the party did feels real and rewards their attention.

**Retconning gracefully.** Sometimes you'll contradict yourself or realize a plan doesn't work. Small fixes: just quietly correct it. Bigger ones: be honest with the table ("let's say that NPC's name was actually X"). Don't let a fear of imperfection freeze you; a living campaign has seams, and players forgive them when the game is fun.

> The campaigns players remember for years are not the ones with the most intricate plots; they're the ones where their choices visibly changed the world and the threats grew to match them. Escalate the stakes, let villains recur, advance the clocks, and pay off what you planted. The throughline is the feeling that this story is *theirs* and it's *going somewhere*.

### Examples

**Example 18.1: A recurring villain earns the finale.** The cult's lieutenant escapes the party in session 3 (you let her flee at low HP rather than die as a mook). She returns in session 7 having learned their tactics, having taken something they care about. By the time they corner her in session 12, the table *hates* her, and beating her means something. A one-shot boss could never carry that weight.

**Example 18.2: Fronts advancing offscreen.** Front A (the flood-cult) and Front B (a noble house exploiting the chaos) both have clocks. The party spends three sessions on the cult and ignores the nobles; when they surface, the noble house has consolidated power, raised prices, and bought the guard. The world moved while the party was busy, which makes it feel real and forces hard prioritization.

### Lab

1. **(15 min)** Sketch your campaign's spine across tiers 1–2: the central conflict and how it escalates from local to regional, in broad strokes (not a session list).
2. **(10 min)** Choose your leveling method and write your pacing plan (when the party hits levels 2, 3, 4, 5, and what accomplishment triggers each).
3. **(10 min)** Design one recurring villain: who they are, why they'll keep escaping/returning, and how they grow over three encounters.
4. **(10 min)** Build two fronts (each a small bundle of clocks) and write the next tick of each.
5. ★ Set up your continuity system: start an NPC list, a clock tracker, and a loose-threads list for your campaign.

### Self-check
1. What are the four tiers of play, and how should stakes change across them?
2. How should you pace leveling, regardless of method?
3. Why is a recurring villain worth more than a one-off, and how do you make one?
4. What is a front, and how do clocks and fronts create pressure?
5. Name three continuity tools and what "reincorporation" means.

# Day 19: Improvisation and the Living World

### Objectives
- Stay calm and keep running when players ignore your prep.
- Use improv tools to invent on the spot.
- Reincorporate established details so improvisation feels planned.
- Let the world react to what the players actually do.

### Lecture

This is the skill new DMs fear most and experienced DMs rely on most: **improvisation.** The players will always, eventually, do the thing you didn't prep. The good news is that everything in this course has been quietly preparing you to improvise, and the better news is that improvising well is mostly a few learnable habits, not raw talent.

**Why prep makes improv easy.** If you prepped a *situation* (Day 8), not a plot, then "going off the rails" isn't going off anything; the situation simply responds wherever they go. Your portable clues drop wherever they look. Your NPC stock and stat-block reskins (Day 12) mean any character you need already exists. Your random tables ([Field Guide Part 5](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#part-5-random-tables-the-improv-safety-net)) catch you when you blank on a name or a complication. Improv is not making everything from nothing; it's recombining the parts you already have.

**The core improv moves:**

- **"Yes, and" / "Yes, but."** Accept the player's contribution and build on it. "Is there a back exit?" Don't default to no; "Yes, and it's chained shut" or "Yes, but it opens onto the watch-house yard." Saying yes keeps players' agency alive; the "and/but" keeps stakes. Reserve a flat "no" for things that would break the fiction.
- **Steal and reskin.** The tavern you didn't prep is a tavern you did prep, renamed. The NPC they cornered is a stock NPC with a new face. Players never know.
- **Borrow their ideas.** When players speculate ("I bet the mayor is behind this!"), consider making them right (or interestingly half-right). Their theories are free plot, and a theory confirmed feels like brilliant foreshadowing on your part.
- **Default to the interesting option.** When you must invent, pick the choice that creates the most play: the door isn't locked, it's *trapped*; the merchant doesn't just sell rope, he *recognizes the party's quarry*.
- **Buy time gracefully.** "Give me a second" is allowed. So is "you take a short rest?" while you think. So is asking the players a question ("what does your character know about this place?") and using their answer.

**Reincorporation: the secret that makes improv feel authored.** When you improvise, reach *backward* for something already established rather than inventing something new. The stranger in the alley is the smuggler from session 2. The symbol on the door is the cult's mark. The poison is the same one that killed the NPC's brother. Pulling threads forward makes a world feel tight and planned even when you're making it up live. This is the single highest-value improv habit; train it.

**Reactive worldbuilding.** When players ask about something you never built, you have two good options: invent it on the spot (and write it down so it's canon now) or, occasionally, turn it back to them ("you tell me, what have you heard about the Drowned Court?") and build on what they say. Either way, the world grows in the direction the players are curious about, which is exactly the direction it should grow.

> The fear is that improvising means the game becomes random or incoherent. The opposite is true when you reincorporate: good improvisation reaches into what's already there and ties it tighter. You are not inventing a new world every time a player surprises you; you are revealing more of the one you already have. Trust your prep, say yes, and pull threads forward.

### Examples

**Example 19.1: Off the rails is just more game.** You prepped a dungeon under the chapel. The players decide the priest is suspicious and tail him through town instead. No problem: the situation (priest is the cult contact) responds. You reskin a stock NPC as the contact he meets, drop a portable clue (the meeting place is the dungeon entrance), and the players have led *themselves* to your dungeon by a road you didn't plan. Your prep wasn't wasted; it was waiting.

**Example 19.2: Reincorporation in real time.** A player asks who's pounding on the inn door at midnight. You blank, then reach backward: it's the frightened dockworker from session 1, the one they helped, now bleeding and begging for help. Instantly the moment has history and weight, and it cost you nothing but a callback. Inventing a brand-new stranger would have been forgettable.

### Lab

1. **(15 min)** Improv drill: have a friend (or a random generator) throw you ten "the players ask about X" prompts; answer each in one or two sentences, out loud, in under ten seconds, defaulting to the interesting option.
2. **(10 min)** Practice "yes, and / yes, but": take five player requests you'd be tempted to refuse and turn each into a "yes, and/but" with stakes.
3. **(10 min)** Reincorporation reps: list five established details from your campaign so far, then write how each could return as the answer to an improvised moment.
4. **(10 min)** Take a prepped scene and write what you'd do if the players completely skipped it; show how the situation still responds.
5. ★ Run a "no prep" five-minute scene aloud using only your random tables and reincorporation. Notice how much you already have to work with.

### Self-check
1. Why does prepping situations (not plots) make improvisation easy?
2. Explain "yes, and / yes, but" and when a flat "no" is still right.
3. What is reincorporation, and why is it the highest-value improv habit?
4. What are two good ways to handle a question about something you never built?
5. Why is "off the rails" usually not a problem for a well-prepped DM?

# Day 21: Final Exam + Capstone (Run Session 1)

The last day has two parts: prove the knowledge, then do the thing.

1. **(60 min)** Take the **Final Exam** in [QUIZZES.md](QUIZZES.md), closed book, and grade it against [ANSWER-KEYS.md](ANSWER-KEYS.md). It is cumulative across all three weeks. 90% is the bar.

Use the loop, adjudicate fast, run all three pillars, manage the spotlight, make rulings, end on a hook.
   - **Solo version (only if you truly cannot assemble a table):** run Session 1 solo by voicing both sides and narrating aloud end to end, or recruit a single willing player. You lose the spotlight-and-group dynamics, but you still practice the core loop, adjudication, and improvisation.
3. **(20 min) The debrief, where the real learning is.** Right after running, write a cold self-assessment against the [rubric](RUBRIC.md): where did the game flow, where did it stall, who got the spotlight, which rulings felt fair, what you'd cut, what you'd prep differently. If you ran for real players, ask them one question: "what was your favorite moment, and what would you want more of?" Then write your next session's strong start while it's fresh.

**Where to go from here.** You have run a game. That sentence is the whole point of this course, and most people who want to DM never get there. From here, mastery is just repetition with attention: keep the [DRILLS.md](DRILLS.md) warm-ups in rotation, run the next session, then the next, and use the debrief every time. Carry the [Field Guide](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md) to the table until you don't need it. Open the doors this course deliberately left closed (deeper monster and spell mastery, homebrew subsystems, the published campaigns, a setting's lore) now that you know what you're building on and why. A campaign is a story with no final exam; it ends when the table decides it does. Keep the chair warm, and keep saying yes.
