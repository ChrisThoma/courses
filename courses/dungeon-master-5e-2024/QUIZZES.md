# Quizzes and Final Exam: DM-101

Rules: **closed book.** Write your answers on paper, then grade with [ANSWER-KEYS.md](ANSWER-KEYS.md). Passing = 90%. These test understanding and judgment, not trivia; most answers are a sentence or two of reasoning, and the applied sections want you to actually make the call you'd make at the table.

---

## Quiz 1: How the Game Runs *(Day 6 · 40 points · ~45 min)*

### Part A: The role, the loop, the pillars (10 pts, 1 each)
1. State the DM's job in one sentence.
2. Name the three roles a DM switches between, and what each does.
3. What are the four steps of the core gameplay loop?
4. Name the three pillars of play and how each is mostly resolved.
5. What does it mean to be "a fan of the characters," and why does it matter?
6. What is the social contract, and where do you establish it?
7. Why are you neither the players' adversary nor their servant?
8. Name three things you actually need to start running on the 2024 rules.
9. Do you need to buy all three 2024 core books to begin? Why or why not?
10. What's wrong with thinking of the DM as "the person who controls the story"?

### Part B: Adjudication (10 pts, 1 each)
1. State "say yes, or roll the dice." When do you actually call for a roll?
2. Name the three kinds of d20 Test and the question each answers.
3. What's your default DC, and name one thing that shifts it up and one that shifts it down.
4. Why should you state the stakes before the roll? Give a strong stakes statement.
5. What does advantage do mechanically, and why is it your favorite tool?
6. How do you compute a passive score, and why use Passive Perception instead of asking for rolls?
7. What's the difference between the Search action and the Study action?
8. A player wants to recall lore about a symbol. Which 2024 action and ability?
9. Why is reaching for the rulebook mid-game usually the wrong move?
10. Give one example of an action you would resolve with no roll at all.

### Part C: The pillars in practice (12 pts, 1 each)
1. What is a "scene," and why think in scenes rather than steps?
2. Give the three-sentence-room structure.
3. Why is tracking time (and light) a tension tool rather than bookkeeping?
4. Name the three parts that make a trap fair. What does "telegraph" mean?
5. How should most exploration scenes end?
6. What three things make an NPC instantly playable?
7. Name the three attitudes and how each affects an Influence attempt.
8. Why roleplay before rolling in a social scene?
9. What's better play than passing a single Influence check?
10. What is the action economy, and why does it make a lone big monster weak?
11. State the 2024 exhaustion effect per level, and what happens at level 6.
12. Name two ways to keep combat fast and two ways to make it about more than hit points.

### Part D: Applied (8 pts): make the call
For 1–4, in one line each, decide "auto-yes / auto-no / roll," and if roll, name the ability, a DC, and the stakes (success and failure):
1. (2) A trained sailor climbs the ship's rigging in calm weather.
2. (2) A rogue picks a good lock while a patrol approaches in two rounds.
3. (2) A commoner tries to leap a 40-foot chasm to escape.
4. (1) Improvise an NPC in one line: name, one want, attitude, voice tag.
5. (1) Take this flat result and rewrite it so the situation changes: "You fail to pick the lock."

## Quiz 2: The Craft *(Day 13 · 40 points · ~45 min)*

### Part A: Prep (10 pts, 1 each)
1. What's the difference between prepping a plot and prepping a situation, and why does situation-prep matter?
2. What is a strong start, and why never open in a quiet tavern?
3. Why are secrets and clues the highest-leverage prep?
4. What makes a clue "portable," and why does that keep a session from stalling?
5. List four items from the one-hour prep checklist.
6. Name three things you should *not* prep.
7. What is the test of good prep (it is not "did it all happen")?
8. Why fit the prep on one page?
9. Where do fantastic locations come from, and how much detail do you write for each?
10. How do you reuse prep when players go a direction you didn't plan?

### Part B: Encounters (10 pts, 1 each)
1. State the four steps of building an encounter with the 2024 XP budget.
2. Is there an encounter multiplier in the 2024 rules? How is it different from 2014?
3. Build it: a Moderate fight for four level-3 characters. What's the XP budget? (Show the math.)
4. Why does a single big solo monster often underperform its budget?
5. How do you fix a solo boss so the action economy doesn't trivialize it?
6. Why is a swarm of weak monsters often deadlier than its XP suggests?
7. Name three design elements that make a fight more than a math problem.
8. What's the difference between Low, Moderate, and High difficulty in feel?
9. Give two fair ways to adjust a fight mid-combat without fudging dice.
10. Why is fudging the dice to "win" or "save" the party corrosive to the table?

### Part C: Adventures, worlds, NPCs, rewards (10 pts, 1 each)
1. Contrast location-based and event-based adventures.
2. List the five beats of the five-room dungeon.
3. State the three-clue rule and the problem it solves.
4. What makes a hook strong rather than hollow?
5. Contrast top-down and bottom-up worldbuilding; which suits a first campaign?
6. What goes in a home-base starter kit?
7. What's on a faction sheet, and what is a clock for?
8. Why do clocks make a world feel alive?
9. Name the three reward currencies, and how do you pace magic items?
10. Why tie a reward to the fiction? Give an example.

### Part D: Applied (10 pts): build it
1. (4) Build a Moderate encounter for **five level-5 characters**: state the per-character budget, the total, and a plausible monster mix that fits (you may use round XP values), plus one terrain feature and one objective beyond "kill them."
2. (3) Write a mystery's solution in one line, then three portable clues that point to it, each reachable two ways.
3. (3) Build one faction: want, method, face, and a three-step clock.

## Final Exam *(Day 21 · 60 points · ~60 min)*

### Part A: Foundations recall (10 pts, 1 each)
1. The DM's job in one sentence.
2. The four steps of the core loop.
3. The three pillars and how each is resolved.
4. "Say yes, or roll the dice": when do you roll?
5. Your default DC and the full DC ladder (the six tiers and their numbers).
6. The three d20 Tests.
7. Why state stakes before the roll?
8. The three NPC attitudes.
9. The 2024 exhaustion rule (per level and at level 6).
10. Why be a fan of the characters?

### Part B: The craft (10 pts, 1 each)
1. Plot vs. situation prep.
2. The one-hour prep checklist (name at least five items).
3. The three-clue rule.
4. The four-step encounter budget method.
5. Is there a 2024 encounter multiplier?
6. The five-room-dungeon beats.
7. Bottom-up vs. top-down worldbuilding.
8. What a faction clock is and why it matters.
9. The three reward currencies.
10. Milestone vs. XP leveling: one advantage of each.

### Part C: Running and sustaining (12 pts, 1 each)
1. How should you open a session?
2. Three pacing tools.
3. What is spotlight management, and why does it affect player retention?
4. The ruling procedure (the steps, in order).
5. The guardrails on the "rule of cool."
6. What do you do when a player uses an ability you don't know?
7. What does a session zero cover?
8. Name two safety tools and what they're for.
9. The four tiers of play and how stakes change across them.
10. Why is a recurring villain worth more than a one-off?
11. What is reincorporation, and why is it the highest-value improv habit?
12. What real-world factor kills most campaigns, and one defense against it?

### Part D: 2024 rules specifics (8 pts, 1 each)
1. Name five formalized 2024 actions this course emphasizes and what each is for.
2. Name the four conditions you should know cold and one effect of each.
3. Cover: name the three degrees and their AC benefit.
4. What does each weapon mastery do: Topple, Vex, Push, Slow?
5. How do death saving throws work (successes/failures to stable or dead)?
6. At what level do bastions become available, and what does a Bastion Turn represent?
7. How does attunement throttle magic items?
8. What are the two ways treasure is delivered (the 2024 structure), and what's in a hoard?

### Part E: Integrated DM problem (20 pts)
You are running Session 4. The party (four characters, level 4) was supposed to investigate the chapel where you prepped a five-room dungeon. Instead, they decide the town priest is suspicious and spend the session tailing him through the market. Meanwhile, the Ashen Hand cult's clock is at step 1 (they've abducted the bell-ringer), and a player at the table has gone quiet for 40 minutes.

1. (3) The players ignored your prepped dungeon. Explain why this is not a problem, and how your Week 2 prep choices let the session keep working.
2. (3) The priest *is* the cult contact. The party tails him to a midnight meeting. Improvise the scene: who does he meet (reskin what?), and how do you reincorporate something already established?
3. (3) A player says, "I want to pickpocket the priest's letter during the crowd crush." Make the full ruling: roll or not, ability, DC, and stakes.
4. (3) Bring the quiet player back in: write the line you'd say and why it works.
5. (3) The party confronts the priest and tries to talk him down. He's Hostile. Walk through how Influence works here and what could shift his attitude.
6. (3) The cult clock is ticking. Describe how you advance it this session (or next) so the world visibly changes, and why that matters.
7. (2) End the session. Write a closing hook that makes everyone want to come back.
