# Field Guide: The DM's Screen & Toolkit

This is the reference you keep open. **Read Parts 1 and 2 before Day 1** so the quick-screen is in your head; read **Part 3 on the Day 14 rest day**; pull from Parts 4–6 all course long and all campaign long. Everything here is built on the **2024 rules** (Player's Handbook, Dungeon Master's Guide, Monster Manual). Page-exact values change between printings; the numbers below match the published 2024 books and the free 2024 rules. Where 2024 differs from the older 2014 rules, it's flagged.

# Part 1: The DM's Screen (rules quick-reference)

## The core mechanic: the d20 Test

Almost everything resolves with one move: roll a d20, add the relevant modifier, compare to a target number. There are three kinds of **d20 Test**:

| Test | You set | Beat |
|---|---|---|
| **Ability check** | A Difficulty Class (DC) | Roll + ability mod (+ proficiency if proficient) ≥ DC |
| **Saving throw** | A DC (from a spell, trap, monster) | Roll + ability mod (+ proficiency if proficient) ≥ DC |
| **Attack roll** | The target's Armor Class (AC) | Roll + attack bonus ≥ AC |

> In 2024, "d20 Test" is the umbrella term for all three. It matters because effects like **Exhaustion** and the **Bless/Bane** family modify *all d20 Tests* at once.

## Difficulty Class (DC): the number you'll set most

| Task difficulty | DC |
|---|---|
| Very easy | 5 |
| Easy | 10 |
| Medium | 15 |
| Hard | 20 |
| Very hard | 25 |
| Nearly impossible | 30 |

Default to **15** when unsure ("medium"), then nudge for circumstances. Most checks at a normal table land between 10 and 20.

## Advantage and disadvantage

Roll **2d20, take the higher** (advantage) or **the lower** (disadvantage). They do not stack (one instance is enough) and they cancel: any amount of advantage plus any amount of disadvantage equals a normal roll. Hand out advantage freely as a reward for clever play; it's worth roughly +5 and costs you nothing to track.

## Passive scores (no roll)

A passive check = **10 + all modifiers** (advantage adds +5, disadvantage subtracts 5). Use **Passive Perception** as the standing "do they notice?" number so you're not constantly calling for Perception rolls and tipping off the table that something's there. Each character's sheet lists it.

## The actions on a turn (2024 set)

On their turn a creature gets a **move**, one **action**, and possibly a **bonus action** (only if something grants one) and one **reaction** (used on someone else's turn, e.g., an opportunity attack). The standard actions:

| Action | What it does |
|---|---|
| **Attack** | Make one (or more) attacks |
| **Dash** | Extra movement equal to your Speed |
| **Disengage** | Your movement doesn't provoke opportunity attacks |
| **Dodge** | Attacks against you have disadvantage; you have advantage on Dex saves |
| **Help** | Give an ally advantage on a check or an attack |
| **Hide** | Make a DC 15 Dexterity (Stealth) check (while obscured or behind cover); on a success you gain the **Invisible** condition until you're found, make noise, or attack |
| **Influence** *(new naming)* | Persuade/deceive/intimidate a creature into doing something; if a roll is needed, DC is usually 15 or the creature's Intelligence score, whichever is higher; attitude affects advantage/disadvantage |
| **Magic** *(new)* | Cast a spell or use a magic action/item |
| **Ready** | Prepare an action to trigger on a condition |
| **Search** | Actively discern something that isn't obvious with Wisdom (Insight, Medicine, Perception, or Survival) |
| **Study** *(new)* | Recall or piece together knowledge with Intelligence (Arcana, History, Investigation, Nature, Religion) |
| **Utilize** *(new)* | Use a non-weapon object (pull a lever, use tools, manipulate gear) |

> 2024 renamed and formalized several actions so players have clear verbs. **Influence** folds the old "social rolls" into one action; **Search** and **Study** split the old catch-all "perceive vs. recall" cleanly; **Utilize** and **Magic** name what used to be informal. You'll use these names at the table.

## Conditions (2024): the 15

Blinded, Charmed, Deafened, **Exhaustion**, Frightened, Grappled, Incapacitated, Invisible, Paralyzed, Petrified, Poisoned, Prone, Restrained, Stunned, Unconscious. Keep the one-line effects handy; the four you'll reach for most:

- **Prone:** disadvantage on attacks; melee attackers against it have advantage, ranged have disadvantage; costs half your movement to stand.
- **Grappled:** Speed 0; disadvantage on attacks against anyone but the grappler.
- **Frightened:** disadvantage on checks and attacks while the source is in sight; can't willingly move closer.
- **Poisoned:** disadvantage on attack rolls and ability checks.

## Exhaustion (2024, simplified)

A single track, 6 levels. **Each level: −2 to all d20 Tests and −5 ft Speed.** They stack (level 3 = −6 to everything). **Level 6 = death.** A long rest removes **one** level. This is harsher early and gentler late than the old 2014 table, and it's easy to run: just subtract.

## Cover

| Cover | Benefit |
|---|---|
| Half (low wall, another creature) | +2 AC and Dex saves |
| Three-quarters (arrow slit, tree trunk) | +5 AC and Dex saves |
| Total | Can't be targeted directly |

## Light and vision (exploration's hidden clock)

- **Bright light:** see normally. **Dim light:** lightly obscured, Perception checks relying on sight have disadvantage. **Darkness:** heavily obscured, effectively Blinded for sight.
- **Darkvision:** see in dim light as bright, and in darkness as dim (in shades of gray) out to its range. Most non-human ancestries have 60 ft.
- A torch/lantern sheds bright light ~20 ft and dim ~20 ft beyond. Track it; running out of light changes everything.

## Travel pace (overland)

| Pace | Per hour | Per day | Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fast | 4 miles | 30 miles | −5 to passive Perception (you miss things) |
| Normal | 3 miles | 24 miles | none |
| Slow | 2 miles | 18 miles | can use Stealth |

## Resting

- **Short rest:** ~1 hour; spend Hit Dice to heal. Several class features recharge here.
- **Long rest:** ~8 hours; regain all HP, all spent Hit Dice, and most resources; removes one Exhaustion level. A creature can't benefit from more than one long rest in any 24-hour period. How easily the party can rest is one of your biggest pacing levers (see Day 9).

## Death and dying

At 0 HP a PC is Unconscious and makes a **death saving throw** each turn (no modifier): 10+ succeeds, under 10 fails. **Three successes = stable; three failures = dead.** A natural 1 = two failures; a natural 20 = regain 1 HP. Any damage while at 0 HP = one automatic failure (a melee hit from within 5 ft = a critical = two failures). Healing of any amount brings them back to consciousness.

# Part 2: The 2024 Encounter & Reward Toolkit

## Encounter XP budget (per character)

This is the 2024 method, and it is simpler than 2014: there is **no "encounter multiplier."** Pick a difficulty, read the per-character budget for the party's level, **multiply by the number of characters**, then spend that XP on monsters by adding up each monster's XP value (listed in its stat block). Spend as close to the budget as you can without going over.

| Party level | Low | Moderate | High |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 50 | 75 | 100 |
| 2 | 100 | 150 | 200 |
| 3 | 150 | 225 | 400 |
| 4 | 250 | 375 | 500 |
| 5 | 500 | 750 | 1,100 |
| 6 | 600 | 1,000 | 1,400 |
| 7 | 750 | 1,300 | 1,700 |
| 8 | 1,000 | 1,700 | 2,100 |
| 9 | 1,300 | 2,000 | 2,600 |
| 10 | 1,600 | 2,300 | 3,100 |
| 11 | 1,900 | 2,900 | 4,100 |
| 12 | 2,200 | 3,700 | 4,700 |
| 13 | 2,600 | 4,200 | 5,400 |
| 14 | 2,900 | 4,900 | 6,200 |
| 15 | 3,300 | 5,400 | 7,800 |
| 16 | 3,800 | 6,100 | 9,800 |
| 17 | 4,500 | 7,200 | 11,700 |
| 18 | 5,000 | 8,700 | 14,200 |
| 19 | 5,500 | 10,700 | 17,200 |
| 20 | 6,400 | 13,200 | 22,000 |

**Reading the difficulties:** *Low* is a manageable fight that drains some resources. *Moderate* is a real fight with some risk. *High* is dangerous and could go badly. As a rough bridge from the older terms: the old "medium" is about the new Low, the old "hard" about Moderate, and the old "deadly" about High.

> The budget is a *starting* estimate, not physics. It ignores the single biggest real-world factor: **action economy** (number of turns per side) and how fresh the party is. Eight weak monsters and one big monster can have the same XP and play nothing alike. Use the budget to get in the ballpark, then trust your eyes (Day 9).

## Rewards: XP vs. milestone

You choose how the party levels:

- **XP leveling:** award monster/encounter XP; characters level when they hit the threshold. More bookkeeping; rewards combat.
- **Milestone leveling:** characters level when the *story* reaches a marker you set (cleared the dungeon, finished the chapter). Less bookkeeping; most modern tables use it. Recommended for your first campaign.

## Treasure (2024 structure)

Treasure comes in two forms, scaled to the party's tier of play:

- **Individual treasure:** coins (and sometimes a small valuable) carried by a creature.
- **Treasure hoards:** the big finds. A hoard = a pile of **coins** + possibly **gemstones or art objects** (fixed values you can hand out as cash) + possibly **magic items**. The DMG gives roll-or-average tables organized by party tier so you can generate a hoard fast.

The four tiers of play to scale rewards (and challenge) to:

| Tier | Levels | Feel |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | 1–4 | Local heroes; a few hundred gold is a fortune |
| 2 | 5–10 | Regional heroes; first real magic items and bastions |
| 3 | 11–16 | Masters of the realm; powerful items, planar stakes |
| 4 | 17–20 | Legends; world- and multiverse-shaking |

## Magic items: rarity, attunement, and pacing

| Rarity | Typical earliest tier |
|---|---|
| Common | 1 |
| Uncommon | 1–2 |
| Rare | 2–3 |
| Very rare | 3 |
| Legendary | 4 |
| Artifact | special, story-defining |

**Attunement** gates the strong items: a character can be attuned to at most **three** items at once, and attuning takes a short rest. This is your throttle. The 2024 DMG lists 400+ items. Give items that say something about where they were found and that a character will be excited to use; a +1 sword is fine, but a sword that whispers the name of its last owner is a plot. Err toward giving *more* interesting items and fewer boring numerical ones.

# Part 3: Bastions, Downtime & the Long Campaign *(read Day 14)*

## Downtime

Between adventures, characters spend in-world days on personal projects: crafting, training, research, carousing, running a business, recovering. Downtime is how time passes believably, how the world advances offscreen (your faction clocks tick during it), and how players pursue goals combat can't satisfy. Keep a simple **campaign calendar** so "two weeks pass" actually means something.

## Bastions (a 2024 addition)

Bastions are the official **stronghold/downtime system**, available to characters at **level 5**. A player's bastion is a base (a tower, a tavern, a ship, a hidden grove) made of **special facilities**, and it advances on a **Bastion Turn**, which represents about **7 days** of game time, typically resolved once per real session or chapter.

- A bastion starts with **two special facilities at level 5**, gaining more as the character levels (a 3rd and 4th around level 9, a 5th around 13, a 6th around 17).
- On a Bastion Turn the player issues **orders** to facilities. The orders are: **Craft, Empower, Harvest, Maintain, Recruit, Research, Trade.** Facilities produce things over time: a Garden grows useful plants, a Workshop crafts gear, a Barracks houses defenders, a Library enables research, and so on (the DMG lists around 29 special facilities).
- **Bastion Events:** between turns, roll for the occasional event so the base isn't just a vending machine: an attack, a windfall, a visitor, a discovery. This is what makes a bastion feel alive and gives you hooks.

You do not need bastions to run a great campaign, and you should not bolt them on before level 5. But they are a clean answer to "what do we do between adventures?" and they give players a stake in the world they're fighting for. Introduce them when the party settles somewhere worth defending.

```
NAME:            (steal from the name table, Part 5)
```

# Part 5: Random tables (the improv safety net)

When a player asks the name of a shopkeeper you never invented, do not freeze. Roll, or just grab the next unused one.

**Names (d12):** 1 Bram  2 Ysolde  3 Garrick  4 Pell  5 Mireille  6 Doran  7 Sefa  8 Otho  9 Lin  10 Ravella  11 Kost  12 Tamsin

**NPC quirk (d8):** 1 won't make eye contact  2 over-friendly, too fast  3 always eating  4 speaks in proverbs  5 terrified of something offscreen  6 name-drops the powerful  7 deeply bored by you  8 hiding an injury

**What does this NPC want (d8):** 1 money owed  2 a person found  3 to be left alone  4 revenge on a rival  5 to look important  6 protection from a threat  7 an object recovered  8 information you have

**Plot hook (d8):** 1 a plea for help  2 a job for pay  3 a debt called in  4 a disappearance  5 a theft  6 a strange omen  7 a map to somewhere  8 a rival beats them to it

**Complication mid-scene (d8):** 1 reinforcements arrive  2 the wrong person shows up  3 a timer starts (fire, flood, alarm)  4 a betrayal surfaces  5 the objective was a decoy  6 an innocent is in danger  7 the environment turns (collapse, storm)  8 it was a trap

**Room dressing (d8):** 1 something is broken  2 a smell out of place  3 signs someone left in a hurry  4 a sound with no source  5 evidence of a struggle  6 something valuable in plain sight (too easy?)  7 a previous explorer's remains  8 it's colder/warmer than it should be

**"Yes, and the cost is..." (d6):** 1 it takes longer than you hoped  2 it makes noise  3 you break your tool  4 someone notices  5 it works, but only once  6 you learn it was the wrong target
