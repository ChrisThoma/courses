# DM-101 Syllabus: Dungeon Mastering (Running D&D 5e, 2024 Rules)

## Course description

A three-week intensive in running Dungeons & Dragons for a new Dungeon Master, built on the 2024 revision of 5th Edition. Students learn the DM's role and the core gameplay loop; how to adjudicate any player action with the d20 and the 2024 action set; how to run the three pillars of play (exploration, social interaction, and combat) so each is fast and fun; how to design adventures and build balanced encounters with the 2024 encounter math; how to build only as much world, NPC, and faction material as a table actually uses; how to hand out treasure, magic items, bastions, and downtime as a working reward economy; and how to run a session live and steer a campaign across an arc and many sessions. By the end, students can prep a session in about an hour, make fair rulings on the fly, improvise when players go off-script, and run a real, playtested first session of a campaign of their own design.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this course you will be able to:

1. **State and perform the DM's job:** run the core loop (describe → players act → adjudicate → narrate), balance referee/narrator/fan roles, and uphold the table's social contract and safety tools.
2. **Adjudicate fairly and fast:** decide when a roll is needed, set a defensible DC, apply advantage/disadvantage and passive scores, and run the 2024 actions (Influence, Study, Search, Utilize, Magic) without stalling.
3. **Run the three pillars:** narrate and pace exploration, voice and motivate NPCs in social scenes, and run combat with the 2024 action economy, conditions, exhaustion, and weapon mastery.
4. **Prep efficiently:** prepare a session in about an hour using a scene-and-secrets method, with a strong start, clues, and only the material you'll use.
5. **Build encounters and adventures:** use the 2024 XP-budget system to build encounters of a chosen difficulty, design location- and event-based adventures, and reward the party with treasure, magic items, bastions, and downtime appropriate to their tier.
6. **Build and sustain a campaign:** create a premise, world, cast, and factions sized for play; run a session live with good pacing and spotlight balance; make rulings instead of stopping for rules; and carry an arc across many sessions with clocks, fronts, and recaps.

| 5 | Running combat: initiative, the 2024 action economy, conditions, exhaustion, weapon mastery, and making fights fun |

| 7 | Rest / review + Field Guide deep-read (build your DM screen) |

## Daily structure (~60–90 min)

| Block | Time | Activity |
|---|---|---|
| Warm-up | 5–10 min | A quick DM drill from `DRILLS.md` (improv a name, describe a room, make a ruling). Train the reflex before you read. |
| Lecture | 25–35 min | Read the lecture; check every example against how you'd actually say it at the table |
| Lab | 25–45 min | The day's hands-on prep, narration, or run-it exercise (solo version where you have no table) |
| Self-check | 5–10 min | Answer the self-check questions from memory |

Grade yourself against `RUBRIC.md`. **Mastery threshold: 90%.** If you score below 90% on a quiz, re-study that week's material on the rest day before moving on; the course is cumulative, and a DM who is shaky on adjudication (Week 1) will be lost building encounters (Week 2) and helpless running them (Week 3).

## Course policies (yes, even self-taught courses need them)

- **Say it out loud.** This is a spoken craft. Reading a technique silently does almost nothing; narrating it to the room (or a recorder) does almost everything. Every Lab has you talking.
- **Closed-book quizzes.** At the table you cannot pause to look up whether to call for a check. The quizzes find the gaps now.
- **Do the [needs a table] tasks for real as soon as you can.** Each has a solo version so you never stall, but live players are where the skill forms. Start recruiting a group in Week 1.
- **Rulings over rules, but know the rules you're ruling on.** "Rule of cool" is a tool, not an excuse to never learn how a check works. Learn the default, then bend it on purpose.
- **The fun of the table is the win condition.** Not your plot, not a perfect rules call, not "winning" against the players. When a policy and the table's fun conflict, the table wins.
- **Falling behind:** use the rest day. If you're more than a day behind, compress by skipping the optional exercises (marked with a star), never by skipping lectures or quizzes.
