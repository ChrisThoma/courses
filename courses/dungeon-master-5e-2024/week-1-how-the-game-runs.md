# Week 1: How the Game Runs (The Role, the Loop, Adjudication, and the Three Pillars)

> **The thesis of this week:** before you build a world, learn to run the game. A Dungeon Master is not a novelist with captive readers; you are a referee, a narrator, and the players' biggest fan, turning a simple loop (you describe, they act, you adjudicate, you narrate the result) into a living story. Master that loop and the three kinds of scene it runs through (exploration, social, combat) and you can sit down behind a published adventure tonight and run a good game. Everything in Weeks 2 and 3 is built on this.

---

# Day 1: What a DM Actually Does

### Objectives
- State the DM's real job in one sentence and name the three roles you switch between.
- Run the core gameplay loop in your head and out loud.
- Name the three pillars of play and the social contract that holds a table together.
- Know what you actually need to start (and what you don't) on the 2024 ruleset.

### Lecture

**The job, in one sentence: you present a world, adjudicate what the players try to do in it, and narrate the results, so that everyone at the table (including you) has a good time.** That last clause is the whole game. You are not trying to beat the players. You are not protecting a story you wrote. You are the host of an improvised adventure whose hero is *the party*, not you.

You do this by switching, moment to moment, between **three roles**:

- **The referee.** You know (or look up) the rules and apply them fairly and consistently. When a player wants to do something, you decide whether it just happens, whether it needs a roll, and what the stakes are. Fairness is the contract: players will accept almost any outcome if they trust the rolls are real.
- **The narrator.** You describe the world: what the characters see, hear, and smell, what the NPCs say, what happens when the dice land. You set tone and pace. You are the players' only window into the fiction.
- **The fan.** This is the role new DMs forget and great DMs never do. You *want* the characters to be cool. You lean in when a player tries something bold. You are rooting for them, and you build situations that let them shine. A DM who is a fan of the characters is a DM people come back to.

**The core gameplay loop.** Strip away everything and D&D is a conversation with a shape:

```
1. DM describes the situation         "The iron door is barred from the other side. You hear chanting beyond it."
2. Players say what they do            "I press my ear to the door to make out the words."
3. DM decides + adjudicates            (Is it automatic? A roll? What's the DC and the stakes?)
4. DM narrates the result              "You catch a name, repeated: 'Vol... Vol-something.' Then the chanting stops."
   -> back to 1
```

That is the entire engine. A four-hour session is this loop a few hundred times. Your job for the rest of Week 1 is to learn to run each step well: to describe vividly (Day 3), to prompt and listen, to adjudicate fast and fair (Day 2), and to narrate results that push the story forward.

**The three pillars of play.** Every scene is mostly one of three kinds, and each plays differently:

| Pillar | What happens | Resolved mostly by |
|---|---|---|
| **Exploration** | Moving through and interacting with the world: travel, searching, traps, puzzles, the dungeon | Description + ability checks |
| **Social interaction** | Talking: NPCs, negotiation, intrigue, information | Roleplay + the Influence action |
| **Combat** | Fighting: the structured, turn-based subsystem | Initiative + the action economy |

A healthy campaign uses all three. A table that only fights gets shallow; a table that never fights loses the stakes that make the rest matter. You'll learn to run each this week.

**The social contract.** D&D is a group activity built on trust. Before the dice, agree (out loud, in a "session zero," which is Project 1) on the basics: what kind of game this is, what's in and out of bounds, how often you'll meet, and how you'll treat each other. The contract is what lets you take risks in the fiction safely. We'll build yours formally on Day 17; for now, just internalize that **the table is a shared space and the fun is shared property.**

**What you need to start (2024 edition).** Less than the marketing implies:

- **The rules.** The free 2024 rules are enough to start, and this course plus the linked references cover the DM-facing pieces you need for the labs; you do not need to buy all three books to begin (see [Field Guide Part 6](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#part-6-curated-free-resources-verified-real)). The three core books are the 2024 **Player's Handbook** (Sept 2024), **Dungeon Master's Guide** (Nov 2024), and **Monster Manual** (Feb 2025).
- **Dice.** One set of polyhedral dice (you'll mostly throw a d20). Players bring their own; have spares.
- **Something to track initiative and notes.** Paper and pencil is plenty.
- **Players.** Three to five is the sweet spot. Start recruiting now (Day 17 covers how); you'll want them for the Day 21 capstone.
- **Your DM screen.** Build it this week from [Field Guide Part 1](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#part-1-the-dm-s-screen-rules-quick-reference).

> The single most important mindset shift for a new DM: you are not the players' adversary and you are not their servant. You are the impartial host of a world that says "yes" to clever ideas and "roll for it" to risky ones. Hold that and most mistakes self-correct.

### Examples

**Example 1.1: The loop, slowed down.** Players enter a tavern. *Describe:* "It's loud, low-ceilinged, smells of spilled ale and wet dog. A one-eyed woman behind the bar clocks you the second you walk in." *They act:* "I go order a drink and ask if she's heard about the missing caravan." *Adjudicate:* This is social; she has no reason to lie, so no roll for the basic facts; you decide she'll trade gossip for a coin. *Narrate:* "She slides you a tankard. 'Caravan? Aye. Third one this season off the Mire Road. Guards found nothing. People are scared.'" The loop turned once and the adventure moved.

**Example 1.2: Being the fan.** A player at 2 HP says, "I leap onto the troll's back and try to drive my dagger into the base of its skull." A timid DM says "uh, roll, I guess." A DM who is a fan says: "Hell yes. That's an Athletics check to mount it, then an attack with advantage from up there. If you pull this off it's going to be incredible." Same rules, completely different table. You are allowed to be excited.

### Lab

1. **(10 min)** Write the DM's one-sentence job from memory, then list the three roles and the three pillars. No peeking.
2. **(15 min)** Run the loop out loud, solo. Invent a simple situation (a locked chest in an abandoned mill), play a player ("I check it for traps"), then adjudicate and narrate. Do five turns of the loop aloud. It will feel silly. Do it anyway; the muscle is verbal.
3. **(10 min)** Take any scene from a movie or book and label which pillar it is and how you'd open it as a DM in one or two sentences of description.
4. **(10 min)** Read [Field Guide Part 1](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#part-1-the-dm-s-screen-rules-quick-reference) (the screen). Don't memorize; just know what's there.
5. ★ Text three people today to gauge interest in playing. You need a table by Day 21.

### Self-check
1. What is the DM's job in one sentence, and what are the three roles?
2. Name the four steps of the core gameplay loop.
3. What are the three pillars, and how is each mostly resolved?
4. What does it mean to be "a fan of the characters," and why does it matter?
5. What do you actually need to start running on the 2024 rules?

# Day 2: Adjudication and the d20 (When to Roll, and What DC)

### Objectives
- Decide quickly whether an action needs a roll at all.
- Choose the right d20 Test and set a defensible DC.
- Apply advantage/disadvantage and passive scores correctly.
- Run the 2024 actions (Influence, Study, Search, Utilize, Magic) without stalling.

### Lecture

Adjudication is the skill that separates a game that flows from a game that stalls every two minutes. It is mostly one decision made over and over: **a player says they do something; what happens?**

**First question: does this even need a roll?** New DMs call for rolls constantly, which is a mistake. The guiding principle, often phrased **"say yes, or roll the dice":**

- If the action would obviously **succeed** (a trained climber goes up a ladder, anyone opens an unlocked door), just **say yes** and narrate it. No roll.
- If it would obviously **fail** (a commoner tries to leap a 40-foot chasm), say so, and offer a different approach. No roll.
- **Roll only when the outcome is uncertain *and* the result is interesting either way.** A roll should have real stakes on both sides. If a failure just means "you try again," don't roll; it wastes time and breaks tension.

**Second question: which d20 Test?** (See [Field Guide Part 1](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#the-core-mechanic-the-d20-test).)

- **Ability check** for "can they do this thing?" (climb, persuade, recall, sneak). You pick the ability and skill and set the DC.
- **Saving throw** for "can they resist this thing happening to them?" (dodge the trap, shake the charm). The effect tells you the DC.
- **Attack roll** for "do they hit?" Against the target's AC.

**Third question: what's the DC?** Default to the table in [Field Guide Part 1](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#difficulty-class-dc-the-number-you-ll-set-most): 5/10/15/20/25/30 for very easy through nearly impossible. When in doubt, **15**. Adjust for the fiction: good tools, a clever approach, or help lowers it (or grants advantage); bad conditions, time pressure, or a slapdash plan raises it. Set the DC *before* the roll, and ideally say the stakes out loud first.

**Fourth, and most overlooked: set the stakes before the dice leave the hand.** "Roll Athletics" is weak. "Roll Athletics; on a hit you scramble up before the guard rounds the corner, on a miss you make it but knock loose a stone and he hears it" is strong. The players now care about the roll because they know what rides on it. This one habit will transform your table.

**Advantage and disadvantage are your favorite tool.** Instead of fiddling with DCs, reward smart play with **advantage** (roll 2d20, keep the higher) and punish reckless play or bad conditions with **disadvantage**. It's fast, it's intuitive, and players love earning it. (See [Field Guide Part 1](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#advantage-and-disadvantage).)

**Passive scores keep secrets and save time.** Rather than calling "everyone roll Perception" (which screams "something is here"), compare the situation's DC to each character's **Passive Perception** (10 + their modifiers). If their passive beats the DC, they notice; if not, they don't, and nobody's the wiser. Use passives for standing awareness and ongoing tasks.

**The 2024 actions, in plain terms.** The 2024 rules gave players clear verbs (see [Field Guide Part 1](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#the-actions-on-a-turn-2024-set)). The ones that change how you adjudicate:

- **Influence** is the social action: a player tries to persuade, deceive, or intimidate an NPC into doing something. First decide whether the NPC is willing, unwilling, or hesitant. If a roll is needed, the DC is usually **15 or the NPC's Intelligence score, whichever is higher**, and attitude affects the roll: Friendly grants advantage, Hostile imposes disadvantage. Crucially, players should *roleplay the attempt first*; the roll resolves a real attempt, it doesn't replace it.
- **Search** is actively discerning something that isn't obvious with **Wisdom** (Insight, Medicine, Perception, or Survival). **Study** is recalling or piecing together knowledge with **Intelligence** (Arcana, History, Investigation, Nature, Religion). Keeping these straight stops the muddle: are they *sensing or reading the immediate situation* (Search) or *reasoning through information* (Study)?
- **Utilize** is using a non-weapon object; **Magic** is casting a spell or using a magic effect. You'll rarely adjudicate these beyond "yes, that works."

> The fastest way to stall a game is to reach for the rulebook. The fastest way to keep it moving is to make a fair call, say it with confidence, and offer to revisit it after the session. A consistent ruling made in two seconds beats a "correct" ruling made in two minutes, every time. We'll go deeper on this on Day 16.

### Examples

**Example 2.1: To roll or not to roll.** "I search the obviously empty broom closet." Nothing is there and finding nothing isn't interesting: **say no roll**, "It's just brooms." "I study the murdered scholar's notes for anything that explains the symbol." Uncertain and interesting: **roll Intelligence (Investigation)**, DC 15, and set stakes: success finds the hidden journal, failure finds it but also knocks a vial that shatters and alerts the house.

**Example 2.2: Setting the DC and stakes.** A rogue wants to pick a lock while the patrol approaches. Lock quality says DC 15; the time pressure doesn't raise the DC, it sets the *stakes*: "DC 15 Thieves' Tools. Make it and you're through before they arrive. Miss it and you're through, but the next patrol catches you here, mid-room." Now the roll matters.

**Example 2.3: Search vs. Study.** "What do I know about the cult's symbol?" That's **Study** (Intelligence, Religion), recalling lore. "I look around the altar for the symbol." That's **Search** (Wisdom, Perception). "I compare the symbol to the scholar's cipher notes." That's **Study** (Intelligence, Investigation). Same symbol, different actions, different checks.

### Lab

1. **(10 min)** Make the call: for each, say "auto-success," "auto-fail," or "roll (which check, what DC, what stakes)." (a) Climb a knotted rope while unharried. (b) Recall the name of the duke's heir. (c) Jump a 10-ft gap being chased. (d) Convince a hostile guard to abandon his post for nothing. (e) Spot a tripwire while moving at a fast pace.
2. **(15 min)** Take ten random actions (make them up or steal them from a movie) and, out loud, set the check, DC, and stakes for each in under ten seconds apiece. Speed is the point.
3. **(10 min)** Practice the stakes habit: rewrite three weak prompts ("roll Perception," "roll Persuasion," "roll Athletics") into strong ones that state success and failure outcomes.
4. **(10 min)** Compute three passive scores: a character with +3 Perception (passive?), the same with advantage (passive?), with disadvantage (passive?).
5. ★ Read a one-page published encounter and mark every place you'd call for a roll and every place you wouldn't.

### Self-check
1. State "say yes, or roll the dice." When do you actually roll?
2. What are the three d20 Tests and what question does each answer?
3. What's your default DC, and what shifts it up or down?
4. Why set stakes before the roll, and what's a strong stakes statement?
5. What's the difference between the Search action and the Study action?

# Day 3: Running Exploration (Scenes, Pacing, and the World)

### Objectives
- Frame an exploration scene with a hook and a decision.
- Describe a space economically using the senses.
- Manage time, travel, light, and the things that make exploration feel real.
- Run traps and hazards fairly.

### Lecture

Exploration is the connective tissue of the game: moving through dungeons, towns, and wilderness, and interacting with what's there. Done badly it's a slog of "you walk down a 10-foot corridor... now another one." Done well it's tense, atmospheric, and full of meaningful choices.

**Think in scenes, not steps.** A scene is a unit of attention: one location, one situation, with a reason to be there and a decision to make. Don't narrate every footstep; cut between scenes like a film. "After an hour picking through the bog, you reach the witch's stilt-house" is one sentence that skips a boring hour and lands you in the next scene that matters.

**Describe with the senses, briefly.** A reliable structure for a new space is the **three-sentence room**: one sentence for the overall impression, one for the most interesting feature, one for an exit or a hook. Lead with what grabs attention. Engage more than sight: the smell of rot, the drip of water, the wrongness of total silence. Then **stop talking and ask "what do you do?"** New DMs over-describe; the players' curiosity should pull the details out of you.

```
THREE-SENTENCE ROOM (example)
1. Impression:  "The chapel is long abandoned, pews rotted to splinters, moonlight falling through a collapsed roof."
2. Feature:     "On the cracked altar sits a single unlit candle that hasn't melted at all, as if placed yesterday."
3. Hook/exit:   "Behind the altar, a narrow stair spirals down into the dark. What do you do?"
```

**Manage the clock.** Time is a resource you control, and it creates tension. Track it loosely but track it: torches burn out (see [Field Guide Part 1](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#light-and-vision-exploration-s-hidden-clock)), the ritual completes at midnight, the reinforcements arrive in an hour. **The threat of time passing is what makes choices in exploration matter.** Without a clock, players will search every drawer in every room forever, because why not?

**Travel.** Don't roleplay a three-day march hour by hour. Decide what's at stake on the journey (random encounters? supplies? navigation?), narrate the texture, and resolve it in a few beats. Use the travel pace table ([Field Guide Part 1](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#travel-pace-overland)) when distance and timing matter; otherwise just say how long it took and what they noticed.

**Light and vision** are a pillar most new DMs ignore, and they're free tension. Who has darkvision? Who's carrying the torch? A party that loses its light in a dungeon is suddenly playing a different, scarier game. Track it.

**Traps and hazards, run fair.** A trap is a puzzle, not a "gotcha." The fair pattern: there is a **tell** (something observable that hints at it), a **trigger**, and an **effect.** Reward players who investigate; a trap that's undetectable and instantly lethal just feels like the DM cheating. Use Passive Perception for whether they notice the tell without searching, an active Search to find it deliberately, and a saving throw or check to avoid/disarm it once engaged. Always telegraph: the scorch marks on the floor, the too-clean pressure plate, the skeleton with a crossbow bolt through it.

> The most common exploration mistake is narrating *at* the players instead of *with* them: long monologues of description they didn't ask for, with no decision at the end. Every scene should end with a question, usually "what do you do?", and a real choice behind it. If a scene has no decision, cut it.

### Examples

**Example 3.1: Cutting vs. crawling.** Crawling: "You go north 30 feet. There's a door. You open it. Empty room, 20 by 20. There's a door on the far wall..." Cutting: "The first few rooms are picked clean, just dust and old rat nests. It's the fourth door that stops you: it's banded in cold iron and warm to the touch. What do you do?" Same dungeon, but the second respects everyone's time and points at a decision.

**Example 3.2: A telegraphed trap.** The hallway floor is clean stone except a 5-foot section dusted with fine grit, and the walls there have a row of pencil-width holes. Passive Perception 13+ notices the mismatch. A player who Searches finds the pressure plate; Thieves' Tools (DC 15) jams it; failing, or just walking on it, triggers a dart volley (Dex save DC 13). The trap is beatable by attention, which makes triggering it a real failure, not bad luck.

### Lab

1. **(15 min)** Write three "three-sentence rooms" for a haunted lighthouse: the entry, a working room, and the lamp chamber at the top. Read them aloud; cut any sentence the players didn't need.
2. **(10 min)** Design one fair trap with a tell, trigger, and effect, and write exactly how a player would detect, avoid, and disarm it.
3. **(10 min)** Practice cutting: narrate a three-day overland journey from town to a mountain ruin in four sentences, including one thing that happened on the way.
4. **(10 min) [needs a table]** Run one exploration scene for your players and end it on a decision. **Solo version:** run it aloud for yourself, playing a curious player who asks two questions, and answer them in character as the world.
5. ★ Add a clock to a dungeon you sketch: what's counting down, and what happens when it hits zero?

### Self-check
1. What is a "scene," and why think in scenes instead of steps?
2. Give the three-sentence room structure.
3. Why is tracking time (and light) a tension tool, not bookkeeping?
4. What three parts make a trap fair, and what does "telegraph" mean?
5. How should most exploration scenes end?

# Day 4: Running Social Interaction (NPCs, Motivation, and the Influence Action)

### Objectives
- Bring an NPC to life fast with a want, an attitude, and a voice.
- Run the 2024 Influence action and the attitude framework.
- Roleplay first and roll second, so social scenes have real stakes.
- Improvise an NPC you never planned.

### Lecture

Social interaction is where many tables have their best moments, and where new DMs feel most exposed, because there's no monster sheet to hide behind. Good news: a vivid NPC needs almost nothing.

**Three things make an NPC playable** (see the [NPC quick-build](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#npc-quick-build-run-cold-in-15-seconds)):

1. **A want.** One concrete thing this NPC is trying to get. The want drives every line they speak. A guard who *wants to get off shift and see his daughter* plays completely differently from one who *wants a bribe*, even saying similar words.
2. **An attitude** toward the party: **Friendly, Indifferent, or Hostile.** This sets how hard they are to move and is the dial the Influence action turns.
3. **A voice tag.** One memorable quirk so the players can tell them apart: a slow drawl, clipped military speech, a nervous laugh, never finishing sentences, nautical metaphors. You do not need a trained actor's range; you need one distinct handle per NPC.

**The Influence action and attitude.** When a player tries to talk an NPC into something, that's the **Influence** action. First decide whether the NPC is willing, unwilling, or hesitant:

| Attitude | Disposition | Influence is... |
|---|---|---|
| **Friendly** | Wants to help, within reason | Many reasonable asks just succeed; if a roll is needed, the character has advantage |
| **Indifferent** | No strong feeling | The default for strangers; if hesitant, roll normally |
| **Hostile** | Opposed, suspicious, or angry | Many big asks are impossible until the attitude shifts; if a roll is possible, the character has disadvantage |

For a hesitant NPC, the Influence DC is usually **15 or the NPC's Intelligence score, whichever is higher**. The size of the ask still matters because it can make the NPC willing, hesitant, or unwilling in the first place. A Friendly innkeeper telling you the road is dangerous: no roll, she just tells you. A Hostile prisoner revealing his employer: maybe impossible without leverage first; with leverage, it's a roll at disadvantage.

**Roleplay first, roll second.** The roll resolves a *real attempt*, it does not replace it. Let the player make their case in character (or describe it), *then* call for the Influence check if the outcome is uncertain. A great argument can make the NPC willing, shift attitude, or justify advantage; a bad or offensive one can make the NPC unwilling or impose disadvantage. This is what keeps social play from collapsing into "I roll Persuasion" every time. The dice serve the conversation, not the reverse.

**Attitudes can change, and that's the game.** The deepest social play isn't passing one check; it's *shifting* a Hostile NPC to Indifferent, or an Indifferent one to Friendly, by appealing to their want, offering something they value, or proving trustworthy. Reward players who figure out what an NPC actually wants. That's better play than any die roll.

**Improvising an NPC you never planned.** Players will talk to the random blacksmith you mentioned in passing. Don't panic. Grab a name from the [name table](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#part-5-random-tables-the-improv-safety-net), pick one physical detail, give them one want and one attitude, and pick a voice tag. Five seconds, and you have a character. If you're truly stuck, the NPC is busy, brusque, and points the party at someone who knows more.

> The biggest social-scene mistake is making the NPC a vending machine: players insert the right words or a high roll, plot falls out. Instead, give every NPC a want of their own. When NPCs pursue their own goals, conversations become negotiations, and negotiations have stakes. The party should sometimes leave a conversation having *given* something to get what they came for.

### Examples

**Example 4.1: Same words, different want.** The party asks a dockworker about smugglers. Want = *protect his crew (who do a little smuggling)*: he's evasive, deflects, lies a little (Insight to catch it). Want = *settle a score with the crew that undercut him*: he's eager, oversells, and may exaggerate. You decided the want in one second, and the whole scene followed from it.

**Example 4.2: Influence with attitude.** A Hostile cult guard won't be talked into letting the party pass (impossible at his attitude for that ask). But they notice he keeps glancing at a holy symbol he clearly resents wearing. They appeal to that (he was coerced into the cult), shifting him toward Indifferent, and *now* an Influence check to look the other way is fair: DC 15 or his Intelligence score, whichever is higher. They changed the situation before they made the ask. That's mastery.

### Lab

1. **(15 min)** Build five NPCs with the quick-build (name, one look, one want, attitude, voice tag). Say one line of dialogue for each *in their voice* out loud.
2. **(10 min)** Take one NPC and run them at all three attitudes toward the same request. Notice how the scene changes.
3. **(10 min)** Practice "roleplay then roll": write a player's persuasion attempt, then decide whether it lowers the DC, raises it, or grants advantage, and why.
4. **(10 min) [needs a table]** Run a 90-second negotiation with a friend playing a PC. **Solo version:** play both sides aloud: the PC makes the case, the NPC responds from their want, and you call the Influence check and resolve it.
5. ★ Pick three real people you know and reduce each to a single voice tag you could imitate. Voice tags are everywhere once you look.

### Self-check
1. What three things make an NPC instantly playable?
2. What are the three attitudes, and how does each affect Influence?
3. Why roleplay before rolling, and how can a good argument change the roll?
4. What's better play than passing a single Influence check?
5. How do you improvise an NPC you never prepared, in five seconds?

# Day 5: Running Combat (Initiative, the Action Economy, and Making Fights Fun)

### Objectives
- Run a combat round cleanly: initiative, turns, the action economy.
- Use the 2024 conditions, exhaustion, and weapon mastery correctly as the DM.
- Give monsters simple, believable tactics.
- Keep combat fast and make it about more than hit points.

### Lecture

Combat is the one pillar with a full rules subsystem, which is why new DMs both over-rely on it and run it slowly. Your goals: run it **fairly**, run it **fast**, and make it **interesting** beyond two sides whittling down hit points.

**The structure of combat:**

```
1. Initiative   Everyone rolls 1d20 + Dex mod. Highest goes first; descend the order. (Monsters can roll once as a group.)
2. Rounds       The order repeats. One round = ~6 seconds of game time; everyone acts once per round.
3. A turn       On your turn you get: MOVE + one ACTION (+ a BONUS ACTION if you have one). Plus one REACTION between turns.
```

**The action economy is the heart of combat.** Each side's power is roughly "number of meaningful actions per round." This is why a single big monster against four PCs is often a pushover (it gets one turn; they get four) and why a swarm of weak monsters can be deadly (lots of turns, lots of dice). Keep this in mind both when you build fights (Day 9) and when you run them: an opponent that can rob the party of actions (with conditions, control, terrain) punches far above its hit points.

**The actions you'll narrate** are in [Field Guide Part 1](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#the-actions-on-a-turn-2024-set). The two new-DM stumbles: forgetting **reactions** (opportunity attacks when an enemy leaves a melee reach; readied actions) and forgetting **bonus actions** are only available if a feature grants one. Track the monsters' reactions; a player walking past a guard should learn that leaving its reach has a cost.

**2024 specifics you run as the DM:**

- **Conditions** (the 15; see [Field Guide Part 1](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#conditions-2024-the-15)). Most monster abilities impose one. Know the four common ones cold (Prone, Grappled, Frightened, Poisoned) and look the rest up without shame.
- **Exhaustion** is now dead simple: −2 to all d20 Tests and −5 ft Speed *per level*, 6 = death (see [Field Guide Part 1](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#exhaustion-2024-simplified)). You'll apply it from certain monsters, hazards, and the occasional forced march.
- **Weapon mastery** is a 2024 feature your *players* will use; you should know what it does because it changes the battlefield. The eight properties (Cleave, Graze, Nick, Push, Sap, Slow, Topple, Vex) let martial characters do things like knock a creature **Prone** (Topple), **push** it back (Push), reduce its Speed (Slow), or set up **advantage** (Vex). When a player's weapon has a mastery, let them use it; it's a big part of why martials feel good in 2024. (See [Field Guide Part 6](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#part-6-curated-free-resources-verified-real) for the full reference.)

**Give monsters tactics, lightly.** A monster that just shuffles forward and full-attacks the nearest PC is boring and easy. You don't need a chess engine; you need one idea per monster: the archers stay back and target the squishy wizard, the brute shoves people off the ledge, the cult leader hangs back and buffs, the wolves try to surround and knock prone. A single tactic makes a monster feel alive and the fight a puzzle.

**Run it fast.** The cardinal sin of combat is dead air. Techniques:

- **Telegraph the next turn:** while one player acts, tell the next "you're up after this, start thinking." Slow turns kill momentum.
- **Track HP simply:** don't announce exact numbers; describe states ("bloodied and staggering," "barely a scratch").
- **Roll monster attacks in the open or fast:** don't agonize. Hit, miss, next.
- **Use the environment:** a fight on a flat empty room is dull; a fight on a collapsing bridge over a chasm with a ticking ritual is alive. Add terrain, hazards, and objectives.

**Make combat about more than killing.** The best fights have an **objective** beyond "reduce enemy HP to 0": protect the hostage, hold the door for three rounds, reach the lever before the room floods, drive them off rather than slaughter them. Objectives create decisions, and decisions are where fun lives. We build encounters like this on Day 9.

> Most combats that "drag" don't have too many hit points; they have too few decisions. If every turn is "I attack the same orc again," the problem is the encounter, not the players. Terrain, objectives, and smart monster tactics fix more boredom than any rules tweak.

### Examples

**Example 5.1: One round, run well.** Initiative is set. The DM says, "Mira, you're up, and Kel you're on deck." Mira's player acts. Then the DM takes the **wolf** turn with a tactic: "The wolf darts past the fighter (the fighter gets an opportunity attack, that's a reaction) to reach the wounded ranger, bites, and uses pack tactics for advantage; on a hit it tries to knock you Prone." Fast, tactical, and every word moved the fight. Kel is already deciding because he was warned.

**Example 5.2: An objective changes everything.** Four PCs versus six cultists is a slog if the goal is "kill all six." Make the goal "the cultists are 3 rounds from completing a ritual at the far altar." Now players must choose: fight through, or sprint past and disrupt the ritual, risking the opportunity attacks. Same monsters, same XP, totally different (and better) fight.

### Lab

1. **(10 min)** Run one round of combat solo, out loud, with three monsters against an imagined party: roll initiative, take each monster's turn with a one-line tactic, and use one condition or weapon mastery in play.
2. **(10 min)** For five common low-level monsters (say goblin, wolf, skeleton, bandit, giant spider), write a one-sentence tactic for each.
3. **(10 min)** Take a boring fight ("4 orcs in an empty room") and add terrain, a hazard, and an objective to make it interesting. Write the new setup.
4. **(10 min)** Practice fast HP narration: write the phrases you'll use for a monster at full, half, and near-death without stating numbers.
5. ★ **[needs a table]** Run a single combat encounter for friends and time it; note where it dragged. **Solo version:** run both sides of a 3-monster fight to the end and note your own slow points.

### Self-check
1. Walk through the structure of combat: initiative, round, turn.
2. What is the action economy, and why does it make one big monster weak and a swarm dangerous?
3. Name the four conditions to know cold, and state the 2024 exhaustion effect per level.
4. What do weapon masteries like Topple and Vex let a player do?
5. Name two ways to keep combat fast and two ways to make it about more than hit points.
