# Projects: DM-101

Projects are where understanding becomes a campaign you can actually run. Each has a deliverable and a grading rubric. Grade yourself honestly; "I basically did it" is a 70, not a 95. The point of these is not to please a grader (there is none but you) but to walk into Day 21 with a real, playable game.

All four are cumulative: Project 1 sets up your table, Project 2 builds the adventure you'll run, Project 3 builds the campaign behind it, and the Final Capstone is you running Session 1 of all of it. Do them in order and the capstone assembles almost by itself.

> A note on players: Projects 1–3 are prep and can be done entirely solo. The Capstone is best with real players; start recruiting in Week 1 (see Day 17). If you genuinely can't field a table by Day 21, each table-dependent piece has a solo path so you can still finish and pass.

---

## Project 1: Session Zero & Table Foundation *(Day 6 · 15% of grade)*

**Goal:** establish the human foundation a campaign needs before any dice are rolled. A great game on a shaky table fails; this is the table.

### Deliverables
1. **The pitch:** your three-sentence campaign premise (setting/tone, central situation, what the party does), written to be read aloud to players. (You'll refine the world in Project 3; a rough premise is fine now.)
2. **Tone and content frame:** name the tone (heroic / gritty / comedic / horror / mixed) and what the campaign is and isn't about, so players build fitting characters.
3. **Expectations agreement:** cadence (how often, how long, where), your "go" rule for absent players, and what you ask of players (show up, be present, share the spotlight).
4. **Safety tools:** your **lines** (off the table entirely) and **veils** (happen off-screen), plus how you'll introduce the X-card. Write the short paragraph you'd actually say to the table.
5. **Character integration plan:** how you'll build characters together so the party has reasons to be a party and you have hooks (the questions you'll ask each player about wants, bonds, and fears).
6. **Table charter (one paragraph):** the social contract in your own words: what kind of table this is and how you'll treat each other.

### Rubric (15 pts)
| Pts | Criterion |
|---|---|
| 3 | Pitch is clear, pitchable, and states tone, situation, and the party's role |
| 3 | Expectations (cadence, "go" rule, mutual asks) are concrete and realistic |
| 4 | Safety tools (lines/veils + X-card intro) are present and you've written what you'd actually say |
| 3 | Character integration plan generates bonds and hooks, not a party of strangers |
| 2 | Table charter states a healthy, specific social contract |

**Pass bar:** you could sit five people down tonight, read this, and everyone would know what game they're building characters for and how the table will treat each other.

## Project 2: A Prepped, Playable Adventure *(Day 13 · 15% of grade)*

**Goal:** prove you can prep a session that's runnable, structured, and survives contact with players, using Week 2's craft. This is the adventure you'll likely run as (or inside) Session 1.

### Deliverables
1. **The one-page prep** (use the [template](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#the-one-page-session-template)) for a one-to-two-session adventure set at your home base, containing: a **strong start**, **3–5 potential scenes**, **~10 portable secrets/clues**, **2–3 fantastic locations**, **2–3 NPCs** (quick-build), encounters, and rewards.
2. **Structure:** state whether the adventure is location-based or event-based (or both), and show the skeleton: a five-room-dungeon beat list, or an event timeline with the factions' moves.
3. **Clue redundancy:** for the one conclusion the players most need to reach, show **three** clues, each reachable at least two ways (the three-clue rule).
4. **At least one budgeted encounter:** name the party (level and size), the difficulty (Low/Moderate/High), the [XP budget](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#encounter-xp-budget-per-character) math, and the monsters that fill it. Add terrain and an objective beyond "kill them."
5. **Rewards tied to the fiction:** a tier-appropriate treasure or magic item, plus at least one reward that doubles as a clue or hook. State your leveling choice (milestone or XP) and the trigger.
6. **NPCs from stock / reskins:** for any NPC who might fight, name the stat block you'd reskin.

### Rubric (15 pts)
| Pts | Criterion |
|---|---|
| 4 | One-page prep is complete: strong start, scenes, ~10 clues, locations, NPCs, rewards |
| 3 | Structure is clear and the clues are portable (3 clues, each two ways) |
| 4 | Encounter is correctly budgeted and designed (terrain + objective), not an empty-room slog |
| 2 | Rewards are tier-appropriate and at least one ties to the fiction |
| 2 | NPCs are runnable cold (wants/attitudes/voices) and reskins named |

**Pass bar:** you could hand this page to *yourself* a month from now, sit down cold, and run a good session from it without freezing, even if the players go a direction you didn't expect.

## Project 3: Campaign Framework *(Day 20 · 15% of grade)*

**Goal:** build the living campaign behind the adventure: the world, cast, factions, and arc that turn one session into a story worth returning to for months.

### Deliverables
1. **Premise and tone** (refined from Project 1): the three-sentence pitch, sharpened.
2. **Home base:** the starter kit from Day 11: name and one-line identity, 3–5 key locations, 3–5 NPCs with wants, the looming problem, and three cheap "lived-in" details (one of which foreshadows the premise).
3. **Factions (at least two):** each on a [faction sheet](SUPPLEMENT-dm-toolkit.md#faction-sheet): want, method, asset, weakness, face, and a three-step clock. Their goals should be capable of conflict.
4. **The campaign spine:** the central conflict and how it escalates across tiers 1–2, in broad strokes (not a session-by-session script). State the stakes at "local" and at "regional."
5. **Leveling plan:** milestone or XP, with the pacing (what gets the party to levels 2–5 and roughly when).
6. **One recurring villain + a front:** a villain who will recur and grow over three encounters, and at least one **front** (a bundle of clocks) with its next tick written.
7. **Continuity system:** show your starting NPC list, clock tracker, and loose-threads list.

### Rubric (15 pts)
| Pts | Criterion |
|---|---|
| 3 | Premise + home base are vivid, sized for play, and generate hooks |
| 4 | Factions have wants, methods, faces, and live clocks capable of conflict |
| 3 | Campaign spine escalates believably across tiers 1–2 (situation, not railroad) |
| 3 | Recurring villain grows over encounters; a front with a next tick exists |
| 2 | Leveling plan and continuity system are concrete and usable |

**Pass bar:** the framework runs *itself* a little. If the party did nothing, the clocks would still advance and the world would still change, and you'd never be short of a hook.

## Final Capstone: Run Session 1 *(Days 20–21 · 20% of grade)*

**Goal:** the only test that matters. Prep and run the first real session of your campaign. This is the Domains 9–10 rubric test, and it's the whole reason for the course.

### Specification
1. **Prep** (Day 20): a one-page prep for Session 1 of your Project-3 campaign (a strong start, 3–5 scenes, ten portable clues, NPCs, a budgeted encounter, a reward). It may reuse or adapt Project 2.
2. **Session zero** (if not already done): run your Project-1 session zero with your players (or deliver it solo to a recorder).
3. **Run it** [needs a table]: run the session for real players. Across the session you must demonstrably: run the **core loop**, **adjudicate** with appropriate rolls and DCs (not too many, not too few), run **at least two of the three pillars**, **manage the spotlight** so everyone acts, make **at least two rulings** on things your prep didn't cover, and **end on a hook**.
   - **Solo version (only if you truly cannot field a table):** run Session 1 end to end by voicing both sides aloud, or with one recruited player. You forfeit the spotlight-and-group criteria but must still demonstrate the loop, adjudication, two pillars, rulings, and a closing hook.
4. **The debrief (the actual deliverable):** immediately after, a written cold self-assessment against the [rubric](RUBRIC.md): where the game flowed and stalled, who got the spotlight, which rulings felt fair, what you'd cut, and your next session's strong start. If you ran for real players, include their answer to "favorite moment, and what do you want more of?"

### Rubric (20 pts)
| Pts | Criterion |
|---|---|
| 4 | Session 1 prep is complete and runnable (strong start, scenes, portable clues, encounter, reward) |
| 5 | You ran the core loop and adjudicated fairly and at the right rate (rolls had stakes; the game kept moving) |
| 4 | At least two pillars ran, and spotlight was shared (table version) / loop and pillars sustained solo |
| 4 | You made fair rulings on the unexpected and improvised without stalling; ended on a hook |
| 3 | The debrief is honest, specific, and produces a concrete next step (the next strong start) |

**Master's bar:** 18+/20, *and* the players ask when the next session is. If you ran solo, the master's bar is that you could now run this for real players with confidence, and your debrief names exactly what you'll do better next time. Either way, you have done the thing: you are a Dungeon Master. The rest is sessions.
