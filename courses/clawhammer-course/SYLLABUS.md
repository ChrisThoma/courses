# Syllabus — MUS-17: Clawhammer Banjo

## Course description

A three-week intensive in clawhammer (frailing) banjo for the absolute beginner who wants to *play*, not just read about it. Students learn the clawhammer striking hand from the ground up — the strike, the thumb, and the bum-ditty groove — then add the fretting-hand ornaments (hammer-ons, pull-offs, slides) and drop-thumb that turn rhythm into melody. Along the way students build a working repertoire of old-time tunes, learn to pick up new tunes by ear, develop genuine rhythmic drive, and study where this music comes from. By the end, students can sit down and play four tunes with a steady, danceable groove and possess a repeatable method for learning the rest of the tradition on their own.

This is an **applied-music (studio) course**: the bulk of the work is playing, every day, with the instrument in your hands.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this course you will be able to:

1. **Tune and hold the banjo** correctly (gDGBD and two alternate old-time tunings) and form a relaxed, sustainable clawhammer striking-hand position.
2. **Play the bum-ditty** — the core clawhammer pattern (strike, brush, thumb) — cleanly, evenly, and at a steady tempo with a metronome.
3. **Fret and change** the core chords (G, C, D7) in time without stopping the groove.
4. **Execute the three left-hand ornaments** — hammer-on, pull-off, and slide — cleanly and in rhythm.
5. **Drop-thumb** to sound melody notes on the inside strings, and combine it with the bum-ditty.
6. **Play four old-time tunes** (Boil Them Cabbage Down, Cripple Creek, Old Joe Clark, Shady Grove) with correct melody, ornaments, and a steady groove.
7. **Learn a new tune by ear** using a repeatable method: get it in your head, find the melody, fit the frailing frame, add ornaments.
8. **Play with drive** — a danceable, in-time groove — and provide simple banjo backup behind a melody or a singer.
9. **Re-tune to and play in** Double C and Sawmill (mountain-minor) tunings, and explain when and why old-time players use them.
10. **Place the music in its history** — the banjo's African origins, the minstrel era, the Appalachian old-time tradition, and the Round Peak style — and use that context to find players and tunes you love.

## Schedule overview

### Week 1 — The Frailing Hand
| Day | Topic |
|---|---|
| 1 | Meet the banjo: parts, tuning (gDGBD), posture, and the African-to-Appalachian story |
| 2 | The strike: a clean down-stroke with the back of the nail |
| 3 | The thumb and the 5th-string drone ("bum… ty") |
| 4 | The **bum-ditty** — strike, brush, thumb — the engine of clawhammer |
| 5 | The fretting hand: G, C, D7 chords and changing them inside the groove |
| 6 | First tune: Boil Them Cabbage Down (frailed chords + sing) — then **Quiz 1** |
| 7 | Woodshed & jam day + Project 1 (The Steady Bum-Ditty) |

### Week 2 — Melody & Drive
| Day | Topic |
|---|---|
| 8 | Hammer-ons and pull-offs (left-hand ornaments) |
| 9 | Slides, and the signature old-time licks |
| 10 | **Drop-thumb** — sounding melody on the inside strings |
| 11 | Fitting a melody into the frailing frame: Cripple Creek |
| 12 | Drive, timing, the "potato" kickoff, and playing with a metronome |
| 13 | Old Joe Clark (a tune with a flat-7 "twang") — then **Quiz 2** |
| 14 | Woodshed & jam day + Project 2 (A Tune With Ornaments) |

### Week 3 — Repertoire & Voice
| Day | Topic |
|---|---|
| 15 | Alternate tunings: Double C (gCGCD) and Sawmill / mountain-minor (gDGCD) |
| 16 | A modal tune: Shady Grove (in Sawmill) |
| 17 | Playing by ear: a repeatable method for learning any tune |
| 18 | Backup, singing while you play, brush styles & Round Peak drive |
| 19 | Making it your own: variation, tone, and a personal sound |
| 20 | Final repertoire workshop + Project 3 (A Polished Tune) |
| 21 | **Final exam** + Final "House Concert" recording due |

## Daily structure (~45–75 minutes)

| Block | Time | Activity |
|---|---|---|
| Tune & warm up | 5 min | Tune the banjo; the day's quick drill from the Practice Menu (hand position, bum-ditty reps) |
| Listen | 5 min | Play a recording of the day's/week's tune — get the sound in your ear |
| Lecture | 10–15 min | Read the lecture; study each tab example with the banjo in hand |
| Play (slow) | 20–40 min | The day's practice, **always slow and in time** with a metronome |
| Review | 5 min | Answer the self-check from memory; note what to fix tomorrow |

Grade yourself against `RUBRIC.md`. **Mastery threshold: 90%.** The honest grading here is in the *playing*: record yourself and listen back ruthlessly — a metronome and a recording don't lie about your timing. If a project's groove isn't steady, it isn't done, no matter how many notes are right.

## Course policies (yes, even a self-taught playing course needs them)

- **Slow and in time beats fast and sloppy. Always.** If you can't play it cleanly slow, you can't play it. Speed is a *result* of accuracy, never a substitute. The metronome is not optional.
- **Listen before you play.** You may not "learn" a tune you haven't heard. Get it in your ear first; your hands chase your ear.
- **Practice short and daily, not long and rarely.** Twenty focused minutes a day beats three hours on Sunday. The neural wiring this builds needs sleep between reps.
- **Record yourself weekly.** It's the most uncomfortable and most useful thing you will do. You hear your real timing and tone, not the flattering version in your head.
- **Loose hands.** Tension is the enemy of both tone and speed, and the cause of most injuries. If your forearm aches, you're squeezing — stop and reset.
- **Closed-book quizzes.** The point is to find gaps, not to feel good.
- **Falling behind:** use the woodshed day to catch up. If you're more than a day behind, slow down rather than skip — drop the ★ optional tunes/variations first, never the core technique days. This course is cumulative: a shaky bum-ditty in Week 1 wrecks everything in Weeks 2 and 3.
