# Week 2: Melody & Drive

> **The thesis of this week:** a melody is just *which string you strike for the "bum."* Last week the bum was whatever-was-handy; this week it becomes a chosen melody note, dressed up with the three old-time ornaments (hammer-on, pull-off, slide) and, the big one, **drop-thumb**, which lets the thumb sound melody notes instead of always the drone. By Friday you'll be playing Cripple Creek and Old Joe Clark with real drive. The non-negotiable from Week 1 carries over and gets *more* important: **slow, even, in time.** A flashy ornament that breaks your groove is worse than no ornament.

Tab notation reminder (full key in the [Field Guide](SUPPLEMENT-tab-tunings-and-resources.md)): under the staff, **I** = index strike, **B** = brush, **T** = thumb. Between fret numbers, **h** = hammer-on, **p** = pull-off, **/** = slide up, **\\** = slide down.

# Day 8: Hammer-ons & Pull-offs

### Objectives
- Sound a hammer-on and a pull-off cleanly with the fretting hand alone.
- Land each one *inside* a bum-ditty without breaking the groove.

### Listen (3 min)
Put on any clawhammer tune and listen for little *slurred* pairs of notes, two pitches from one strike, one sliding into the next. Those liquid, vocal-sounding moves are hammer-ons, pull-offs, and slides. They're what make the banjo *talk* instead of just *plink*.

### Lecture

So far every note has been *struck* by the right hand. The three ornaments let the **left hand make notes on its own**, which (a) adds melody notes you don't have time to strike, and (b) gives old-time its singing, slurry feel.

**Hammer-on (`h`): two notes, one strike.**
- Strike a string (open, or a fretted note).
- While it's still ringing, **slam a left-hand finger down** onto a *higher* fret on that same string. The impact sounds the new, higher note, no second strike.
- Example, open 3rd string hammered to the 2nd fret:

```
 3 G |---0h2-----|
        I
```
Strike the open 3rd string (G), then hammer your finger onto the 2nd fret (A). One strike, two notes (G→A).

**Pull-off (`p`): the reverse.**
- Fret a note and strike it.
- **Pull the fretting finger off sideways** (a little downward flick, not straight up) so it plucks the string as it leaves, sounding the *lower* note underneath (open or a lower fret).

```
 3 G |---2p0-----|
        I
```
Fret the 2nd fret of the 3rd string and strike it (A), then pull off to sound the open string (G). One strike, two notes (A→G).

**Keys to clean ornaments:**
- **Hammer with authority:** a firm, fast finger-drop makes the note ring; a timid one thuds. It's a quick *snap*, not a press.
- **Pull off sideways**, catching the string like a tiny pluck, not lifting straight off (which just mutes it).
- **Stay relaxed:** power comes from a quick motion, not a death grip.

**Putting it in the groove:** the ornament happens *on the "bum"*: instead of a plain struck melody note, you strike-and-hammer (or strike-and-pull). The brush and thumb that follow are unchanged. So the bum-ditty becomes: **(strike+hammer) – brush – thumb.** Same groove, fancier bum.

```
        BUM(h)        DI      TY
 1 D |---------------0--------------|
 2 B |---------------0--------------|
 3 G |---0h2---------0--------------|
 4 D |-----------------------------|
 5 g |-----------------------0------|
        I             B       T
```

### Play
1. **(10 min) Bare ornaments.** Hammer-on open→2nd fret on the 3rd string, 15 times, until the hammered note rings clearly. Then pull-off 2nd→open on the 3rd string, 15 times, until the pulled note rings.
2. **(10 min) On other strings.** Do hammer-ons and pull-offs on the 4th and 2nd strings too (e.g., 4th string 0h2, 2nd string 0h2). Find where your fingers are weak and drill there.
3. **(15 min) In the groove.** Bum-ditty on G at ~50 bpm, but replace the "bum" with a strike+hammer (0h2 on the 3rd string) every measure. Keep the di-ty and the tempo dead steady. Then do a version with a pull-off bum.

### Daily drill
**Ring-it-out, 3 min:** one hammer-on and one pull-off, alternating, each must *ring clearly* (record and check). Any that thuds is a rep; reset.

### Self-check
1. In a hammer-on, what makes the second (higher) note sound?
2. In a pull-off, which direction do you move the finger, and why not straight up?
3. How many right-hand strikes does an ornamented pair of notes take?
4. Where in the bum-ditty does the ornament usually happen?
5. What's the difference between a firm hammer and a timid one, sound-wise?

# Day 9: Slides & the Signature Licks

### Objectives
- Execute a slide cleanly, keeping the note ringing across the move.
- Learn the iconic old-time 2→4 slide ("the Cripple Creek lick").

### Listen (3 min)
Listen to **Cripple Creek** (Field Guide §5 for where). Listen specifically for a *swooping* note that glides upward; that's a slide, and Cripple Creek's most famous lick is built on one. Get the whole tune in your ear; you'll start it in two days.

### Lecture

**Slide (`/` up, `\` down): one strike, the pitch glides.**
- Fret a note and strike it.
- **Keep the string pressed down and slide the finger** up (or down) the neck to another fret, *without lifting.* The pitch glides continuously to the new note, which keeps ringing.

The signature old-time move is the **2→4 slide on the 3rd string**, the heart of countless tunes including Cripple Creek:

```
 3 G |---2/4-----|
        I
```
Fret the 2nd fret of the 3rd string, strike it (A), and slide up to the 4th fret (B), letting it ring the whole way. Say it out loud: *"shloop."* That little swoop is one of the most recognizable sounds in old-time banjo.

**Keys to a clean slide:**
- **Maintain pressure** the whole way; let up and the note dies mid-slide.
- **Move from the arm/hand**, smoothly; the finger stays planted.
- **Land on the target fret** in time: a slide is still rhythmic; it arrives *on a beat*, not whenever.

**Hammer vs. slide, same notes, different feel:** you can get from the 2nd to the 4th fret with a slide (2/4, a smooth swoop) *or* by striking the 2nd and hammering... actually you'd hammer 2h4 only if you re-strike; more often it's the slide for that swoop. Old-time players choose by *feel*: slides are smooth and vocal, hammers are punchy. Having both in your hands is the point.

**In the groove:** like the other ornaments, the slide lives on the "bum." Strike-and-slide, then brush, then thumb:

```
        BUM(slide)    DI      TY
 1 D |---------------0--------------|
 2 B |---------------0--------------|
 3 G |---2/4---------0--------------|
 4 D |-----------------------------|
 5 g |-----------------------0------|
        I             B       T
```

### Play
1. **(10 min) The 2→4 slide.** On the 3rd string, strike the 2nd fret and slide to the 4th, ringing all the way, 20 times. Then try sliding *down* 4\2. Make the swoop clear.
2. **(10 min) Slides on other strings/frets.** Try a 2/4 slide on the 4th string, and a 0... (you can't slide from open; start fretted). Explore a few. Notice how a slide always needs a fretted starting note.
3. **(15 min) Slide in the groove.** Bum-ditty at ~50 bpm with a 2/4 slide on the 3rd string as the "bum" each measure. Then alternate: one measure with a slide-bum, one with a hammer-bum, one with a pull-off-bum. Keep time rock-steady.

### Daily drill
**Ornament roulette, 4 min:** in a slow bum-ditty, randomly choose hammer / pull / slide for each measure's bum. The groove must not flinch. Trains ornaments as *choices* layered on a steady frame.

### Self-check
1. What must you maintain throughout a slide for the note to keep ringing?
2. Can you slide starting from an open (unfretted) string? Why or why not?
3. What is the famous 2→4 slide usually played on (which string), and what tune is it the signature of?
4. Feel-wise, how does a slide differ from a hammer-on between the same two notes?
5. Where in the bum-ditty does a slide live?

# Day 10: Drop-Thumb

### Objectives
- Understand what drop-thumb is and why it's the key to melodic clawhammer.
- Drop the thumb onto an inside string to sound a melody note, in time.

### Listen (3 min)
Listen to a melodic clawhammer player (Cripple Creek, or search "drop thumb clawhammer"). Hear how *busy* and melodic the low/middle register gets; that's the thumb leaving its drone-string home and grabbing melody notes on the inside strings. That's today.

### Lecture

Until now the thumb has played only the **5th string** (the "ty" drone). **Drop-thumb** (a.k.a. double-thumbing) is when the thumb **"drops" down to an inside string** (the 4th, 3rd, or 2nd) to sound a *melody* note instead of the drone. This roughly doubles how many melody notes you can fit, and it's what separates plain frailing from full melodic clawhammer.

**The motion:**
- Same relaxed thumb, but instead of landing on the 5th string, it reaches over and **strikes-down on an inside string** (say the 2nd or 3rd), sounding a fretted or open note there.
- Everything else about the bum-ditty stays the same; you're just *changing the "ty" note* from the drone to a chosen melody note.

A simple drop-thumb pattern: the "ty" drops from the 5th string to the 2nd string:

```
        BUM         DI      TY(drop)
 1 D |---0----------0--------------------|
 2 B |--------------0--------0-----------|   <- thumb drops to the 2nd string here
 3 G |--------------0--------------------|
 4 D |----------------------------------|
 5 g |----------------------------------|
        I           B        T
```

**The classic drill, "double thumbing" / the drop-thumb roll:** alternate a normal thumb (5th string) and a dropped thumb (an inside string). For example, strike, brush, thumb-5th, then strike, brush, thumb-2nd, or the tighter pattern of thumbing two strings in a row. Start by just getting the thumb to *reliably find* the 2nd string and sound it cleanly.

**Why it's hard (and the fix):** the thumb has been trained for a week to only know the 5th string. Re-teaching it to find an inside string takes slow, deliberate reps. **Fix:** drop the metronome to a crawl and watch your thumb land on the target string. Accuracy first; speed is months away and that's fine.

> **This is a "Level 3 takes a while" skill.** Do not expect fluid drop-thumb this week; expect to *start* it. It keeps developing for a long time, and that's normal. Today you plant the seed.

### Play
1. **(10 min) Find the string.** Slowly, just have the thumb drop onto the open 2nd string and sound it cleanly, 20 times, then the 3rd string. No bum-ditty yet; just train the thumb to find inside strings.
2. **(15 min) Drop-thumb in the pattern.** At a *very* slow tempo, play: strike 1st string, brush, **thumb the 2nd string** (instead of the 5th). Repeat. Then alternate measures: one with thumb-on-5th, one with thumb-on-2nd.
3. **(10 min) Double-thumb roll.** Try a continuous strike–brush–thumb(5th)–strike–brush–thumb(2nd) loop. Slow. Messy is expected. Clean-and-slow is the win.

### Daily drill
**Thumb-target, 3 min:** call an inside string (2nd or 3rd); drop the thumb to sound it cleanly. Reset, call another. Pure thumb-accuracy training.

### Self-check
1. What is drop-thumb: what does the thumb do differently?
2. Which strings can the thumb "drop" to?
3. In the bum-ditty, which part (bum/di/ty) does drop-thumb change?
4. Why is drop-thumb hard at first, specifically?
5. Should you expect fluid drop-thumb by the end of this week?

# Day 11: Fitting a Melody Into the Frame (Cripple Creek)

### Objectives
- Understand how a fiddle-tune melody maps onto the frailing frame.
- Learn Cripple Creek using the recording + the signature licks + linked tab.

### Listen (5 min, required)
Listen to **Cripple Creek** at least three times (Field Guide §5: Banjo Hangout, clawhammerbanjo.net, or "Cripple Creek clawhammer slow" on YouTube). Sing or hum the A part ("goin' up Cripple Creek…") and the higher B part. **Do not put hands on the banjo until you can hum both parts.** This is the method, not a suggestion.

### Lecture

**Cripple Creek is the universal first clawhammer tune:** three chords (G, C, D7), two parts (A and B), and it's built from exactly the moves you now own: struck melody notes for the "bum," the 2→4 slide, hammer-ons, pull-offs, and a bit of drop-thumb. This is the day all of Week 2 pays off.

**How a tune sits on the frame.** A fiddle tune is a *melody*. To frail it, you make the **melody notes the "bum"** (the struck note on the beat), and the **brush + thumb fill the spaces between melody notes** with rhythm and drone. So "learning a tune" really means: *figure out the melody notes, decide which string/fret each lives on, and strike those for the bums while keeping the di-ty rolling.* Ornaments (slides/hammers/pulls) connect melody notes that are close together.

**Cripple Creek's structure (in G, gDGBD):**
- **A part** (the lower, "goin' up Cripple Creek" part): melody mostly on the 3rd and 2nd strings, low in pitch, repeated.
- **B part** (the higher answer): melody climbs onto the 2nd and 1st strings.
- Form: **AABB** (play A twice, B twice), then repeat the whole thing as many times as you like; old-time tunes loop forever for dancing.

**The signature lick** (the thing that makes Cripple Creek *sound* like Cripple Creek) is the **2→4 slide on the 3rd string** you drilled on Day 9, used as a melody bum:

```
        BUM(slide)    DI      TY          BUM         DI      TY
 1 D |---------------0--------------------0----------0------------|
 2 B |---------------0--------------------------------0----------|
 3 G |---2/4---------0--------------------0----------0-----------|
 4 D |----------------------------------------------------------|
 5 g |-----------------------0--------------------------------0--|
        I             B       T           I           B        T
```

That swooping slide, dropped into the steady frame, is the soul of the tune.

**Your method for learning the whole tune (this is the transferable skill):**
1. **Hum it** (done, required listening).
2. **Pull up a tab** for a *simple* clawhammer Cripple Creek (Field Guide §5 links: Banjo Hangout / clawhammerbanjo.net / banjoskills all have free beginner versions). Use the tab to find *which string and fret* each melody bum lives on.
3. **Learn it one phrase at a time**, slowly, hands matching the tune in your ear. Get the A part solid before touching the B part.
4. **Keep the di-ty steady** even while the bums are still wobbly; groove first.
5. **Wean off the tab** as soon as you can; let your ear lead.

> **Why we point you to external tab here instead of printing a full transcription:** old-time has dozens of equally "correct" versions of every tune, and the *right* one for you is the one whose recording you love. The skill this course builds is **learning a tune from ear + tab**, not reciting one fixed arrangement. Treat the linked tab as a map, the recording as the territory.

### Play
1. **(10 min) The signature lick.** Play the 2/4-slide bum-ditty figure above until it's smooth and in time.
2. **(20 min) A part.** Using your chosen tab + the recording, learn the A part one phrase at a time, dead slow. Loop it. Aim to play the A part twice through with the groove intact (flubbed bums okay; steady di-ty required).
3. **(10 min) Hum the B part** and find its first couple of melody notes on the 1st/2nd strings. You'll finish B on the woodshed day; no rush.

### Daily drill
**Phrase-looping, 4 min:** take one 2-bar phrase of Cripple Creek and loop it slowly until it's clean, then move the loop to the next phrase. "Chunking" is how tunes actually get learned.

### Self-check
1. In frailing a fiddle tune, which part of the bum-ditty carries the melody note?
2. What is Cripple Creek's signature lick, and which Day-9 move is it?
3. What does "AABB" mean for playing the tune?
4. What are the five steps of the tune-learning method?
5. Why does this course link out to tab instead of printing one fixed version?

# Day 12: Drive, Timing & the Kickoff

### Objectives
- Understand "drive": the danceable groove that is the point of old-time.
- Use a metronome to build steady time and start fixing rush/drag.
- Learn a simple "potato" kickoff to start a tune.

### Listen (3 min)
Listen to a *driving* old-time banjo player; Round Peak players like **Tommy Jarrell** or **Kyle Creed** (Field Guide §6) are the gold standard. Feel how the groove pulls *forward* and makes you want to move. That forward pull is **drive**, and it's a rhythmic feel, not a speed.

### Lecture

You can play every note right and still sound bad if the *time* is bad. And you can play a half-dozen notes with great time and sound wonderful. **In old-time, time and drive matter more than anything else.** Old-time is **dance music**; its job is to make bodies move. That demands a steady, propulsive groove.

**Drive** is the sense of forward momentum in the groove. It comes from:
- **Rock-steady tempo:** no rushing the easy parts or dragging the hard parts. This is 90% of it.
- **A strong, consistent "bum"** landing squarely on the beat; the downbeat is the engine.
- **The "drive" of the di-ty placement:** Round Peak players push the rhythm with a slightly snappy, syncopated feel, but you earn that only *after* dead-steady time is automatic. **First: even. Then: drive.**

**The metronome is how you build it.** Beginners think a metronome is for speed; it's really a mirror for your time. Play a tune you know (Boil Them Cabbage Down, the Cripple Creek A part) with the click and listen for where *you* and the *click* disagree; those spots are where you rush or drag (almost always: rushing the easy bits and the di-ty, dragging the hard chord change). Slow down until you and the click are one.

**The kickoff, "the potato."** Old-time tunes traditionally start with a short rhythmic count-in so everyone comes in together, often called a "**potato, potato**" (it's the rhythm of those words): basically two bum-ditties of pickup that set the tempo, then the tune begins. Learn to give yourself a steady two-beat kickoff before launching a tune; it sets your tempo and stops the flailing, uncertain starts beginners are prone to.

> **The discipline that makes you good:** practice *at a tempo you can play cleanly*, not your ragged maximum. Every rep at your sloppy top speed teaches your hands to be sloppy. Every rep clean and in time teaches them to be clean. Speed arrives on its own when the cleanliness is automatic.

### Play
1. **(10 min) Find your honest tempo.** Take Cripple Creek's A part and a metronome. Find the *fastest* tempo where you and the click never disagree. That, not your flailing top speed, is your real tempo today. Write it down; you'll watch it climb over weeks.
2. **(15 min) Steady-time reps.** Play your A part at that tempo for several minutes, locked to the click. When it's effortless, nudge up 2–4 bpm. Never jump to where it falls apart.
3. **(10 min) Kickoff practice.** Give yourself a steady two-bum-ditty "potato" kickoff and launch into Boil Them Cabbage Down or Cripple Creek, several times, so starting in tempo becomes automatic.

### Daily drill
**Click-disagreement hunt, 4 min:** play a known tune with the metronome and *notice* every spot you and the click part ways; loop that spot slowly until you agree. The most valuable practice you can do.

### Self-check
1. What is "drive," and is it the same as speed?
2. What is a metronome *really* for, beyond setting tempo?
3. Where do beginners most commonly rush, and where do they drag?
4. What is a "potato" kickoff?
5. Why is practicing at your ragged top speed counterproductive?

# Day 13: Old Joe Clark (the flat-7 twang) + Quiz 2

### Objectives
- Learn Old Joe Clark and hear its characteristic "flat-7" Mixolydian sound.
- Take Quiz 2.

### Listen (5 min, required)
Listen to **Old Joe Clark** several times until you can hum both parts. Listen for one note that sounds a little "off" in a *good*, twangy way (a flatted seventh) that gives the tune its mountain flavor. (Field Guide §5 for sources.)

### Lecture

**Old Joe Clark** is one of the most-played old-time tunes ever, and a perfect Week-2 capstone: it's a two-part AABB tune you can frail with everything you now own, and it introduces a new *sound*.

**The flat-7 / Mixolydian flavor (no theory rabbit hole, just the sound):** most tunes you know are plain "major." Old Joe Clark has a **lowered ("flat") 7th note**, which gives it that slightly bluesy, ancient, twangy character instead of a bright sunny major sound. You don't need the theory; you need to *hear* it and let your fingers find that note (it sits naturally in the tune's shape). The takeaway: old-time isn't all vanilla major; many of its best tunes have this modal twang, and your *ear* is what catches it.

> **A note on key:** Old Joe Clark is traditionally played in **A**. As a beginner in gDGBD you can (a) learn it in G-shaped positions to keep it easy, or (b) later use a **capo at the 2nd fret** (and spike/retune the 5th string up) to play your G shapes but sound in A so you can join others. For now, learn the melody and groove in whatever position your chosen tab uses; matching the jam's key comes later.

**Learn it with the same method as Cripple Creek:** hum it → get a simple tab (Field Guide §5) → chunk it phrase by phrase, slow → keep the di-ty steady → wean off the tab. You've done this once; it's faster the second time. That *acceleration* (each new tune coming quicker than the last) is exactly the skill the course is building.

**Bonus, hear the family resemblance:** notice Old Joe Clark, Cripple Creek, and most old-time tunes share the same skeleton: AABB form, a low part and a high part, built on G/C/D, frailed on the bum-ditty frame. Once you internalize the frame, tunes start feeling like variations on something you already know, because they are.

### Play
1. **(10 min) Warm up + hear the flat-7.** Play Cripple Creek to warm up, then noodle until you find Old Joe Clark's twangy note by ear against the recording.
2. **(20 min) Old Joe Clark, A part.** With tab + recording, learn the A part slowly, phrase by phrase. Loop it. Groove first.
3. **(5 min) Start the B part:** just get its opening phrase. Finish on the woodshed day.

### 📝 Quiz 2
Take **Quiz 2** now (see `QUIZZES.md`), closed-book. It covers the ornaments, drop-thumb, the tune-learning method, drive/timing, and Week-2 concepts. Grade against `ANSWER-KEYS.md`. Below 90% → re-study tomorrow.

### Self-check
1. What form (letter pattern) is Old Joe Clark, like most old-time tunes?
2. What gives Old Joe Clark its twangy, slightly bluesy sound (in plain terms, no theory)?
3. What key is Old Joe Clark traditionally in, and how might you eventually match it on a gDGBD banjo?
4. What skeleton do Cripple Creek, Old Joe Clark, and most old-time tunes share?
5. Why does learning your *second* tune go faster than your first?

The capstone is recording a short "house concert" of the tunes you can really play.
