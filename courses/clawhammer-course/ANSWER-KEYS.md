# Answer Keys: MUS-17

Grade honestly. For "name N things" questions, award partial credit per correct item. For the **play** parts, grade against the stated standard; the bar is *steady, in time, and clean*, not "fast" or "sounds nice while I rush." Record yourself if you're unsure; the tape doesn't lie.

## Quiz 1: The Frailing Hand

### Part A
1. Nearest the **floor** (the highest of the four long strings).
2. It's the **short** string (peg sticks out around the 5th fret) and it's a high **drone**, played by the thumb.
3. **g – D – G – B – D** (5th to 1st).
4. **G major.**
5. A **drum / drumhead** (a stretched membrane that resonates).
6. Because strings and the head drift out of tune; playing out of tune trains your ear to accept wrong pitches.
7. The **akonting** (West African Jola lute).
8. Any of: a **downstroke** playing technique; a short high **drone string** played with the thumb; gourd/skin construction.

### Part B
1. **Strike down.**
2. The **back of the fingernail.**
3. **Index or middle** finger.
4. The **5th string** (the drone).
5. About **1–2 inches up the neck from the bridge**, over the head.
6. Any of: plucking up with the fingertip flesh; a tense/locked wrist; hitting extra strings; too-short nail.
7. A **loose wrist/forearm** (not a flicking finger).
8. Bum = the **strike**; ty = the **thumb** (on the 5th string).

### Part C
1. **Strike, brush, thumb** ("bum-di-ty").
2. Strike = **I** (index/middle), brush = **B**, thumb = **T**.
3. **One continuous** downward roll.
4. The **"bum" (strike)** lands on the beat.
5. **Rushing the "di-ty"** so it crowds the next "bum" (a galloping, uneven rhythm).
6. **Slow down, use a metronome, and let the di-ty breathe** (deliberately delay it; aim for even).
7. **Two.**
8. A **metronome** (plus recording yourself and listening back).

### Part D
1. **G** (all open strings).
2. Any correct C-shape note, e.g., **2nd string / 1st fret = C** (or 1st string/2nd fret, 4th string/2nd fret).
3. **Just behind the fret** (on the bridge side of the fret wire).
4. Use the **least pressure that makes the note ring clean**: no extra squeezing.
5. **Low on the back of the neck**, roughly behind the first finger (not wrapped over the top).
6. **Keep frailing the bum-ditty in time**: never stop the groove to fix the left hand.
7. **G, C, and D7.**
8. You must **hear the tune** (be able to hum it) before trying to play it; your hands chase your ear.

### Part E
1. **4 pts:** even spacing of every bum, di-ty not crowding, steady against the click. Deduct for any lurch/wobble.
2. **4 pts:** all chords ring clean, changes happen without stopping or hitching the groove. Deduct for dead strings or broken time.

## Quiz 2: Melody & Drive

### Part A
1. **Slamming a left-hand finger down** onto a higher fret while the string still rings (the impact sounds the note).
2. **Sideways / a little downward** (a tiny pluck as the finger leaves).
3. Lifting straight up just **mutes** the string instead of sounding the lower note.
4. **One.**
5. **Pressure on the string** the whole way (keep it pressed down as you move).
6. **No.** There's no fretted note to keep pressed and glide; a slide needs a fretted starting note.
7. The **3rd string** (2→4).
8. **Cripple Creek.**
9. On the **"bum"** (the struck melody note).
10. A slide is **smooth/vocal/swooping**; a hammer is **punchy/percussive**: same notes, different feel.

### Part B
1. It **drops to an inside string (4th/3rd/2nd) to sound a melody note** instead of the 5th-string drone.
2. The **4th, 3rd, or 2nd** strings.
3. The **"ty" (thumb)** part.
4. The thumb has been trained for a week to find **only the 5th string**; re-teaching it to find inside strings takes slow reps.
5. **Months.** Expect only to *begin* it this course; that's normal.
6. **Sound more melody notes** (roughly doubling them), turning a strum into a melody.

### Part C
1. The **"bum" (struck note)** carries the melody.
2. Play the **A part twice, then the B part twice** (then loop the whole thing).
3. (1) Hum/get it in your head; (2) get a simple tab to find string/fret of each note; (3) chunk it phrase by phrase, slow; (4) keep the di-ty steady over flubbed bums; (5) wean off the tab, let the ear lead.
4. **Hum it** (get the whole melody in your head).
5. **AABB** (a low part and a high part, looped).
6. A **flatted (lowered) 7th** note: a modal/Mixolydian twang (no theory needed; you hear it).
7. **A.**
8. You already own the **frame and technique**, and the second tune is built from the same pieces; only the melody is new.

### Part D
1. **Forward momentum / a danceable propulsive feel.** **No**, not the same as speed.
2. A **mirror for your timing**: it reveals exactly where you rush or drag.
3. The **easy parts** (and the di-ty).
4. The **hard parts** (e.g., a tricky chord change or ornament).
5. A short **rhythmic count-in** (two bum-ditties of pickup) that sets the tempo before the tune starts.
6. Every rep at sloppy top speed **trains your hands to be sloppy**; clean reps train clean playing; speed comes from accuracy.
7. **Dance music**; the groove's job is to make people move / keep the dance steady.
8. **Even time first**, then drive.

### Part E
1. **4 pts:** each ornament clearly audible and on the beat; the di-ty/groove never breaks.
2. **4 pts:** melody recognizable as the tune; steady time both times through.

## Final Exam

### Part A
1. **Down.** 2. **Thumb.** 3. **Strike, brush, thumb** (bum-di-ty). 4. **g D G B D.** 5. **G major.** 6. **Raise the 2nd string B→C.** 7. **Cripple Creek.** 8. **Play A twice, then B twice.** 9. **Even time.** 10. **The akonting.**

### Part B
1. **(2)** One continuous downward roll: **strike** one string (I), **brush** across 2–3 strings (B), **thumb** the 5th-string drone (T).
2. **(2)** Hammer-on = hammer a finger onto a *higher* fret to sound it; pull-off = pull a finger off to sound a *lower* note. **Each takes one strike** for two notes.
3. **(2)** Strike a fretted note and slide the finger to another fret **keeping pressure on the string** so the note glides and keeps ringing.
4. **(2)** The thumb **drops to an inside string to play a melody note** (instead of the drone); it lets you sound **more melody notes** than plain frailing.
5. **(2)** **Rushing the di-ty** so it crowds the next bum; fix = **slow down with a metronome and let the di-ty breathe**.
6. **(2)** Any two: plucking up with flesh (→ strike down with the nail); tense wrist (→ stay loose); hitting extra strings (→ slow down and aim); short nail (→ keep it a bit long and filed).

### Part C
1. **Raise the 2nd string B→C.**
2. Any two: open drones ring in the new key; different easy melody notes fall under the fingers; it changes the *mood*/sound.
3. **Sawmill (gDGCD).**
4. **Lower the 4th string D→C and raise the 2nd string B→C.**
5. **Return to gDGBD.**
6. **Boil Them Cabbage Down, Cripple Creek, Old Joe Clark, Shady Grove.**
7. **Shady Grove** (Sawmill).
8. Modal = darker / more open / haunted / "ancient"; major = brighter / sunnier.
9. Any two: Cluck Old Hen, Little Sadie, Wayfaring Stranger, Last Chance, etc.
10. It's **passed person to person by listening/ear**, not primarily from written notation.

### Part D
1. **Get it solidly in your head; hum it.**
2. **Is the next note higher or lower (and by how much)?**
3. Small phrases are learnable/memorable; the whole tune at once overwhelms; chunking works.
4. Make the **melody notes the "bums"** and roll the **di-ty** around them.
5. You're **training a brand-new skill/muscle**; the first is always slow, the tenth is fast.
6. Old-time has **many equally valid versions**; the skill is learning from ear+tab, and the right version is the recording *you* love, not one fixed transcription.
7. **Backup** = playing **chords and groove** (not the melody note-for-note) to support a singer/other instrument and keep the dance steady.
8. Get the **banjo part on autopilot first**, then add the voice on top.

### Part E
1. **(1)** Forward momentum; from steady tempo + a strong on-beat "bum" (and the Round Peak syncopated push, earned after steady time).
2. **(1)** Rush the easy parts, drag the hard parts.
3. **(1)** Any three: ornament choices; drop-thumb fills/note substitutions; dynamics/accents; texture (more/less brush); varied repeats.
4. **(1)** Near the bridge = brighter/sharper; over the neck = softer/plunkier.
5. **(1)** From **thousands of small choices over time** (favored ornaments, drive, tone); **no, you can't force it**; you cultivate it by listening and choosing.
6. **(1)** A **driving, syncopated, relentlessly danceable** groove (heavy drop-thumb/brush).
7. **(1)** Any two: **Tommy Jarrell, Fred Cockerham, Kyle Creed.**
8. **(1)** West African lutes (akonting) → brought by enslaved Africans → spread (through the minstrel era) → rooted in Appalachian old-time dance music.
9. **(1)** The **first banjo method book (1855)**; it already notates the downstroke "stroke style", documenting clawhammer's early form and the African-rooted technique's transmission.
10. **(1)** **Play with other people** (find a slow jam); old-time is social music.
11. **(1)** **Slippery Hill** (slippery-hill.com), or Banjo Hangout/field recordings.
12. **(1)** To **enjoy making music and keep getting better on your own** (not to play tunes "correctly").

### Part F
1. **(3)** Full tune AABB from memory, steady danceable groove, melody accurate. Deduct for stops, rushing, or broken time.
2. **(2)** Successful retune to Sawmill + a recognizable Shady Grove phrase.
3. **(3)** The three notes are correct for the chosen melody and were found **by ear** (no tab); credit the ear leading the hands even if slow.
