# Week 3: Repertoire & Voice

> **The thesis of this week:** technique is now mostly in place; this week makes you *independent.* You'll open up the alternate tunings old-time lives in (so the spooky modal tunes are available to you), build a real method for learning tunes **by ear** (so you never *need* tab again), learn to **back up** a song and a singer, and start developing the thing no course can hand you: **your own sound.** The graduation requirement is simple and real: a short recording of yourself playing tunes you can actually play, with a groove someone would tap their foot to.

# Day 15: Alternate Tunings

### Objectives
- Re-tune to **Sawmill** (gDGCD) and back, confidently.
- Understand *why* old-time uses alternate tunings instead of just fretting different chords.
- Hear the difference between major (gDGBD) and modal (Sawmill).

### Listen (3 min)
Listen to a Sawmill-tuning tune: **Shady Grove** or **Cluck Old Hen** (Field Guide §5). Notice the darker, more open, slightly haunted quality compared to the bright major sound of Cripple Creek. That mood comes largely from the *tuning.*

### Lecture

Old-time banjo runs on a whole family of tunings, and **players retune constantly** to suit a tune's key and mood. This surprises guitarists, but it's central to the style. Why retune instead of just fretting?

- **Open drone strings ring in the new key.** Each tuning makes a different set of open strings sound *good* together, so the drones and brushes reinforce the tune instead of clashing. A modal tune over gDGBD's bright major drones sounds wrong; in Sawmill it rings true.
- **It changes the available easy melody notes** under your fingers, which shapes how a tune *wants* to be played.
- **It changes the mood:** that's the big one. The tuning is half the sound.

**The two you need (full instructions in [Field Guide §2](SUPPLEMENT-tab-tunings-and-resources.md)):**

- **Sawmill / Mountain Minor / "G modal" (gDGCD):** from gDGBD, **raise the 2nd string B up to C**, one move. This removes the bright major 3rd and gives the open, modal sound for tunes like Shady Grove, Cluck Old Hen, Little Sadie. **This is your priority tuning this week.**
- **Double C (gCGCD):** from gDGBD, **lower the 4th string D→C and raise the 2nd string B→C.** A bright, ringing tuning for many major fiddle tunes (often capoed up to "Double D" to play in D). We touch it lightly.

**The skill is the retuning itself:** doing it with a tuner *and* learning to hear when a string is right. Re-tune slowly, check each string, and **always return to gDGBD** when you're done so you start fresh next time. Re-tuning will feel fiddly today and routine by Friday; that's the goal.

> **Don't fear breaking strings:** raising the 2nd string a half-step (B→C) is a small change and safe. Turn the peg slowly and watch the tuner. If you ever find yourself cranking hard, stop; you've probably overshot an octave.

### Play
1. **(10 min) To Sawmill and back.** Re-tune gDGBD → Sawmill (raise 2nd string B→C) with your tuner, then back to gDGBD. Do it **three times** until the move is familiar.
2. **(10 min) Hear it.** In Sawmill, slowly frail a bum-ditty on the open strings. Listen to that open, modal drone; no bright major 3rd. Compare by ear to gDGBD.
3. **(10 min) Double C tour.** Re-tune to Double C (lower 4th D→C, raise 2nd B→C), frail the open strings to hear its ring, then return to gDGBD. Just exposure today.

### Daily drill
**Retune-and-verify, 4 min:** from gDGBD, go to Sawmill and confirm each string by ear before checking the tuner, building the ear, not just tuner-dependence. Return to gDGBD.

### Self-check
1. What single string change turns gDGBD into Sawmill?
2. Why retune at all instead of just fretting different notes? Give two reasons.
3. What kind of tunes is Sawmill used for?
4. How do you get from gDGBD to Double C?
5. What should you always do with the tuning when you finish practicing?

# Day 16: A Modal Tune (Shady Grove)

### Objectives
- Learn Shady Grove in Sawmill tuning.
- Get comfortable playing *in* an alternate tuning.

### Listen (5 min, required)
Listen to **Shady Grove** several times; there are countless versions (it's one of the most beloved old-time tunes). Hum the melody; feel its lonesome, modal pull. Don't touch the banjo until it's in your head. (Field Guide §5.)

### Lecture

**Shady Grove** is *the* classic alternate-tuning tune for a beginner: a short, haunting, modal melody, usually played in **Sawmill (gDGCD)**, often as both a banjo tune and a sung ballad ("Shady Grove, my little love…"). It's the perfect Week-3 tune: it forces you to play *in* a new tuning, and its modal sound rewards you immediately.

**Playing in a new tuning** means your map changed: the 2nd string is now a C, so melody notes and ornaments sit in slightly different places than your gDGBD reflexes expect. This is good for you: it stops the tuning from being a black box and makes you *think in the strings* again. Use the same tune-learning method:
1. Hum it (required listening, done).
2. Get a **Sawmill-tuning tab** for Shady Grove (Field Guide §5 sources; make sure the tab says gDGCD / Sawmill).
3. Chunk it slowly, phrase by phrase.
4. Steady di-ty over flubbed bums.
5. Wean off tab; let the ear lead: this tune is short and singable, so your ear can carry a lot of it.

**Sing it if you can.** Shady Grove is a song as much as a tune. Frailing a modal tune *and* singing the verse is deeply satisfying and a real old-time skill: the banjo and voice trading the melody.

> **Why a modal tune matters in your education:** a huge, gorgeous chunk of the old-time repertoire is modal/minor: Cluck Old Hen, Little Sadie, Wayfaring Stranger, Last Chance. Today you unlock that whole wing of the tradition. Many players say the modal tunes are where they fell in love with the banjo.

### Play
1. **(5 min) Retune to Sawmill** and warm up with an open-string bum-ditty to settle into the sound.
2. **(25 min) Shady Grove.** Learn it phrase by phrase from your Sawmill tab + the recording, slow. Loop the hard bits. Aim to play the whole (short) tune through, in time, by end of session, even very slowly.
3. **(5 min)** Try singing a line while frailing the changes. Then **retune back to gDGBD** when done.

### Daily drill
**Sawmill noodle, 4 min:** in Sawmill, improvise a slow bum-ditty wandering up and down the strings, just listening to which notes sound "right" in the modal world. Pure ear-training in the new tuning.

### Self-check
1. What tuning is Shady Grove usually played in?
2. Why does playing in a new tuning make you "think in the strings" again?
3. Name two other modal/minor old-time tunes you could explore next.
4. Is Shady Grove a fiddle tune, a song, or both?
5. What do you do with the tuning at the end of the session?

# Day 17: Playing by Ear

### Objectives
- Learn a repeatable method for working out a tune's melody *without* tab.
- Begin trusting your ear as the primary tool.

### Listen (5 min)
Pick a **simple tune you know cold but have never played**: a nursery tune, "Twinkle Twinkle," "Mary Had a Little Lamb," a Christmas carol, *anything* simple and burned into your memory. You'll find it by ear today. Hum it now.

### Lecture

This is the most important skill in the whole course, and the one that makes you free: **finding a melody by ear.** Old-time is an aural tradition; the great players learned tune after tune by *listening and finding it*, and tab is a modern convenience. Tab can show you *one* version; your ear lets you learn *all of them*, and play with people, and eventually create your own variations. Here is a method that works:

**The "hunt-and-peck" method for finding a melody by ear:**
1. **Get the tune solidly in your head:** hum it perfectly first. You can't find what you can't sing.
2. **Find the *first note* on the banjo.** Hum the first note, then hunt around the strings (start near the open 3rd or 2nd string) until a struck note *matches your hum.* That's your starting note.
3. **Go one note at a time.** Hum the next note. Is it *higher or lower* than the last? By a little or a lot? Move up or down a fret/string and check against your hum. When it matches, you've found it. Repeat.
4. **Work in tiny phrases.** Find 3–4 notes, then loop them until they're solid, then find the next few. Don't try to find the whole tune in one pass.
5. **Then add the frame.** Once you have the bare melody notes, make them the "bums" and roll the di-ty around them; now it's a clawhammer arrangement *you* made.

**It is slow and frustrating at first and that is completely normal.** Everyone's first by-ear tune takes forever. The tenth takes minutes. You are training a muscle that has never been used; the only way is reps. The payoff is enormous: independence from tab forever.

**Hearing the chord changes too:** as you get better, you'll start to *feel* when the chord wants to change from G to C to D7; the melody "leans" toward the new chord. Don't force this yet; just notice when a tune sounds like it "wants" to move.

### Play
1. **(10 min) First note hunt.** Take your simple known tune. Hum it, then find just the *first note* on the banjo by matching your hum. Sit with the satisfaction of that.
2. **(20 min) Peck out the melody.** One note at a time (higher or lower?), find the first phrase, loop it, then the next. Get as far as you can. *Slowness is expected.* Resist looking up any tab.
3. **(5 min) Frame it.** Take whatever melody fragment you found and frail it: melody notes as bums, di-ty around them. You just made an arrangement by ear. That's the whole tradition in miniature.

### Daily drill
**Three-note ear-find, 4 min:** hum any short melodic fragment (a doorbell, a jingle) and find its first three notes by ear, no tab. Daily reps here pay off more than any other drill long-term.

### Self-check
1. What's the non-negotiable first step before you can find a tune by ear?
2. When hunting for the next note, what's the key question you ask about it?
3. Why work in tiny phrases rather than the whole tune at once?
4. Once you have the bare melody, how do you turn it into a clawhammer arrangement?
5. Why is being slow and frustrated at first totally expected?

# Day 18: Backup, Singing & Round Peak Drive

### Objectives
- Play simple banjo backup behind a melody or a singer.
- Understand the Round Peak style and where to chase the drive you want.

### Listen (3 min)
Listen to **Tommy Jarrell** or **Fred Cockerham** (Round Peak, Field Guide §6). Hear how the banjo *drives* the fiddle along, not soloing, but laying down a relentless, syncopated groove. That partnership is the engine room of old-time.

### Lecture

**Backup** is what banjo does most of the time in real old-time music: not playing the melody note-for-note, but laying down **chords and groove** so a fiddle, a singer, or another instrument can carry the tune. You already have the core skill: it's exactly the **"frail the changes and sing"** approach from Boil Them Cabbage Down (Day 6), now used behind *any* tune:

- **Know the chord changes** (G/C/D7 covers most beginner tunes), and frail a steady bum-ditty, changing chords where the tune wants them.
- **Lock to the groove**, not to your own ego; backup's whole job is to make the *other* player sound good and keep the dance steady.
- **Vary the texture:** straight bum-ditty, more brushing for a fuller sound, a drop-thumb lick to answer a melodic phrase, the occasional walk-up to the next chord. Subtlety over flash.

**Singing while you play** is the most natural thing in old-time and worth pushing through the awkward phase. Strategy: get the banjo part on total autopilot (a simple backup groove) *first*, then add the voice on top; don't try to learn both at once. Start with tunes whose melody you know best.

**The Round Peak style & finding your drive.** The most influential clawhammer sound comes from **Round Peak**, a community in Surry County, North Carolina, and its masters: **Tommy Jarrell, Fred Cockerham, and Kyle Creed.** Round Peak banjo is prized for its **driving, syncopated, relentlessly danceable groove** and heavy use of drop-thumb and brushing. You don't have to play Round Peak style, but you should **listen to the source players**, because the fastest way to develop drive and feel is to *steep your ears in great players* and let your hands chase that sound. Pick a player whose groove makes you want to move and listen to them constantly. ([What Is The Round Peak Style, Banjo Studio](https://www.banjostudio.com/blogs/blog/what-is-the-round-peak-style-of-clawhammer-banjo-playing))

> **The best thing you can do for your playing after this course:** find a **slow jam**, in person or online, and play with other people. Old-time is a *social* music; it was never meant to be played alone in a room. Backup and drive only fully click when you're locked in with someone else.

### Play
1. **(15 min) Backup reps.** Pick a tune whose chords you know (Boil Them Cabbage Down, Cripple Creek) and play *only backup* (steady bum-ditty through the changes) along with a recording. Lock to *their* time, not yours.
2. **(10 min) Sing + backup.** Frail simple backup and sing a verse of Shady Grove or Boil Them Cabbage Down. Banjo on autopilot, voice on top.
3. **(10 min) Listen actively.** Put on a Round Peak player and *frail along* with their groove, matching their feel as best you can. Steal the drive.

### Daily drill
**Lock-in, 4 min:** play backup along to any old-time recording and try to be perfectly *with* it; when you drift, notice and re-lock. Trains the social-timing skill that matters most.

### Self-check
1. What is backup, and what is the banjo's job in it?
2. What earlier lesson is backup essentially an extension of?
3. What's the strategy for learning to sing and play at once?
4. What is Round Peak, and name two of its master players.
5. What's the single best thing you can do for your playing after this course?

# Day 19: Making It Your Own

### Objectives
- Vary a tune deliberately (ornaments, note choices, dynamics).
- Understand tone and personal sound as something you develop on purpose.

### Listen (3 min)
Find **two different players** playing the *same* tune (e.g., two Cripple Creeks). Notice how different they sound: different ornaments, timing feel, tone, little melodic choices. *Same tune, different voice.* That difference is the art, and it's available to you.

### Lecture

Once a tune is solid, the fun *really* starts: **variation.** Old-time players almost never play a tune the same way twice; they're constantly making small choices that become their personal voice. You have all the tools; now you use them as *expression*, not just execution.

**Ways to vary a tune (try them one at a time):**
- **Ornament choices:** play a melody note plain one time through, then with a slide, then a hammer-on, then a pull-off. Each gives a different feel.
- **Note substitutions / drop-thumb fills:** add a drop-thumb melody note where there was a plain drone; leave one out for space.
- **Dynamics & accents:** hit certain bums harder for a driving accent; ease off others. Old-time has a lot of subtle push-and-pull.
- **Texture:** more brush for fullness, less for clarity; thin it out, then build it back.
- **Tag/variation on the repeat:** play the A part plain the first time, ornamented the second.

**Tone is a choice, not an accident.** Where you strike (closer to the bridge = brighter/sharper, closer to the neck = softer/plunkier), how firm your strike is, the angle of your nail, all shape your sound. Experiment deliberately and notice what you like. The "old-time plunk" many players love comes partly from striking up over the neck on an open-back banjo.

**Your "voice" emerges from thousands of these small choices** made over months and years: which ornaments you favor, how hard you drive, your tone, your tempo. You can't force it, but you *cultivate* it by (a) listening hard to players you love and stealing what moves you, and (b) constantly making little choices instead of playing on autopilot. By now you should have early flickers of preference; lean into them.

> **The goal of this whole course, restated:** to *enjoy making music* and to keep getting better on your own, rather than just to play tunes "correctly." Variation and voice are where banjo stops being homework and becomes *yours.*

### Play
1. **(15 min) One tune, five ways.** Take Cripple Creek (or any solid tune). Play it through five times, each time changing *one* thing: different ornament on a phrase, a drop-thumb fill, harder accents, brighter tone (strike near bridge), then plunkier (strike over the neck). Notice what you like.
2. **(10 min) Tone lab.** Play the same phrase striking near the bridge, in the middle, and up over the neck. Listen to the tonal change. Pick your favorite spot for this tune.
3. **(10 min) Steal a lick.** Listen to a player you admire on a tune you know; find one little thing they do (an ornament, a timing push) and work it into your version.

### Daily drill
**Vary-the-repeat, 4 min:** play any tune AABB and make the *second* A and second B different from the first, even slightly. Trains you out of robotic repetition.

### Self-check
1. Name three ways to vary a tune.
2. How does *where* you strike change your tone?
3. Where does a personal "voice" come from, and can you force it?
4. What two habits cultivate your voice over time?
5. What's the restated goal of this whole course?

# Day 20: Final Repertoire Workshop + Project 3

Today you polish, not learn. Two things:

### 1. Repertoire pass
Play all four core tunes: **Boil Them Cabbage Down, Cripple Creek, Old Joe Clark** (gDGBD) and **Shady Grove** (Sawmill), from memory, in time, AABB where applicable. Note which one is most solid and which needs the most love. For each, do one slow, clean, metronome pass and one "for real" pass at a danceable tempo.

### 2. Project 3: A Polished Tune
Work up **Project 3** (see `PROJECTS.md`): one tune of your choice, polished: correct melody, clean ornaments, steady drive, ideally one personal variation. This is the dress rehearsal for tomorrow's house concert. Record a take, listen back ruthlessly, fix the worst spot, record again.

> **Polishing tip:** find the *single weakest moment* in the tune (usually one chord change or one ornament) and loop just that, slowly, until it's as solid as the rest. Polishing is targeted repair, not playing the whole tune over and over hoping the bad spot fixes itself.

# Day 21: Final Exam + House Concert

### Objectives
- Demonstrate everything: take the final exam and record your final House Concert.

### The Final Exam
Take the **Final Exam** (see `QUIZZES.md`), closed-book, and grade against `ANSWER-KEYS.md`. It covers the whole course: technique, tunes, tunings, the learning method, drive, history. It has written parts *and* required **playing tasks** (recorded), because this is a playing course and the playing is the real exam.

### The Final "House Concert" Recording
This is your graduation (full spec in `PROJECTS.md`). Record a short "house concert": **two to four tunes you can really play**, from memory, in time, with a groove a listener would tap their foot to. Introduce each tune by name (and key/tuning if you switch). It does *not* need to be flawless or fast; it needs to be **musical, steady, and yours.** Coherence and groove beat flash and speed.

> **Why record it:** because a recording is honest, because it's a milestone you'll treasure (your "before" that proves how far three weeks took you), and because performing (even to a phone) is a distinct skill from practicing. Save this file. In six months, re-record the same tunes and hear the difference.

### Where you are, and where to go next
Three weeks ago you'd never held a banjo. Now you have a solid frailing hand, a real groove, ornaments, drop-thumb begun, four tunes, two extra tunings, a method for learning tunes by ear, and the history in your head. That is a genuine foundation, and the hard part is *done.*

**To keep growing (in priority order):**
1. **Play with people.** Find a slow jam (in person or online). This is, by a wide margin, the most important thing. Old-time is social music.
2. **Keep the daily habit**, even 15 minutes. Consistency beats marathon sessions.
3. **Learn tunes by ear**, constantly. Aim for one new tune a week. The repertoire is how you grow.
4. **Listen to source players** (Round Peak and beyond) and steal everything that moves you.
5. **Deepen drop-thumb and drive**, the two long-game skills, over the coming months; they're what separate good from great.

You're a banjo player now. Go find a jam.
