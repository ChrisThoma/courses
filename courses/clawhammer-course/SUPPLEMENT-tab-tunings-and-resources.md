# Field Guide: Tab, Tunings, Chords & Resources (MUS-17)

Read the first three sections (**Your banjo**, **Tuning**, **Reading tab**) *before Day 1*. The rest is reference you'll return to all course.

---

## 1. Your banjo (the 30-second tour)

A 5-string banjo has, from the side you face:

- A round **pot** (the drum) with a **head** (the white plastic or skin membrane) stretched over it; this is what makes the sound.
- A **neck** with metal **frets** crossing it. Pressing a string just *behind* a fret shortens it and raises its pitch.
- Five **strings**. Four run the full length of the neck. The **5th string is the short one**; its tuning peg sticks out of the side of the neck around the 5th fret. It's a high drone string, and it's the banjo's secret weapon.
- A **bridge** (a small wooden piece standing on the head) that the strings cross. If your bridge falls over while changing strings, it stands roughly 3 fingers' width from the tailpiece, but tune by ear/tuner, not by guessing.

**Open-back vs. resonator:** old-time clawhammer is traditionally played on an **open-back** banjo (no wooden bowl on the back) for its softer, "plunkier" tone. A resonator (bluegrass) banjo works fine too; it's just louder and brighter. Either is welcome here.

**Strings are numbered by pitch position, not size:** the **1st string is the bottom one as you hold it** (closest to the floor, highest of the long strings); the **4th string is the top long one** (closest to the ceiling, lowest). The **5th string is the short high drone** at the top. This trips up everyone at first; sit with it for a minute.

## 2. Tuning: gDGBD (open G) and the alternate tunings

The default tuning for this course (and most clawhammer) is **open G, written gDGBD**: read it as the pitch of each string from the **5th string down to the 1st**:

| String | Pitch | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 5th (short drone) | **g** | high G, the drone; lowercase because it's *higher* than the 4th–3rd |
| 4th (top long) | **D** | the lowest string |
| 3rd | **G** | |
| 2nd | **B** | |
| 1st (bottom) | **D** | |

Played open (no fretting), all five strings together sound a **G major chord**, which is why it's called open G.

**How to tune:** use a clip-on tuner or a phone tuner app. Clip it on (or hold the phone near the head), pluck one string at a time, and turn that string's peg until the tuner reads the right letter: **g, D, G, B, D**. Turn slowly; a string at the wrong octave is a common beginner trap (the tuner shows the octave number; aim for the standard banjo octaves: 5th = G4, 4th = D3, 3rd = G3, 2nd = B3, 1st = D4, but if you don't have a reference just match a recording or a tuned-banjo video). **Re-tune every single time you pick up the banjo**: heads and strings drift, and an out-of-tune banjo teaches your ear lies.

**The two alternate tunings in this course (Week 3):**

| Tuning | Strings (5–4–3–2–1) | What you change from gDGBD | Used for |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Double C** | g **C** G **C** D | lower 4th **D→C** and raise 2nd **B→C** | major tunes in C/D; bright, ringing |
| **Sawmill** (a.k.a. Mountain Minor, "G modal") | g D G **C** D | raise the 2nd string **B→C** | modal/minor tunes (Shady Grove, Cluck Old Hen) |

> **Getting to Sawmill from gDGBD is one move:** raise the **2nd string from B up to C** (up one fret's worth of pitch). That single changed note removes the "major 3rd" and gives the spooky, open, modal sound old-time loves. Easy to do and undo; we'll practice it on Day 15.

> **Getting to Double C:** from gDGBD, lower the **4th string D→C** and raise the **2nd string B→C** (so you have two C's, hence "Double C"); the 3rd stays G, 1st stays D, 5th stays g. (Many players then capo or spike up two frets to "Double D" = aDADE for fiddle tunes in D.) We use Double C lightly; Sawmill is the priority.

Re-tune to alternates with your tuner the same way; just aim each string at its new letter. **Always re-tune back to gDGBD** when you're done, by ear or tuner.

## 3. Reading clawhammer tab

Tablature ("tab") is a picture of the strings. **Five lines = the five strings.** Unlike standard music notation, the lines are *strings*, and numbers on them are *frets* (which fret to press), **not pitches**.

```
       (this is an empty tab staff)
 1 D |--------------------------------|   <- 1st string (bottom string, high D)
 2 B |--------------------------------|   <- 2nd string
 3 G |--------------------------------|   <- 3rd string
 4 D |--------------------------------|   <- 4th string (top long string, low D)
 5 g |--------------------------------|   <- 5th string (short drone, high g)
```

- The **top line is the 1st string**, the **bottom line is the 5th string**; i.e., the lines are laid out the way the strings sit when you look down at the banjo in your lap.
- A **number** on a line = press that fret on that string and sound it. **`0` = open** (don't fret, just sound the string).
- Read **left to right** in time, like words.
- Numbers **stacked vertically** = sound those strings together (a brush/strum or a chord).

**Right-hand (striking-hand) letters:** this course writes them *under* the staff so you always know *which* part of the clawhammer motion makes each note:

| Symbol | Means |
|---|---|
| **I** | **Index** (or middle) finger **strike**: a single down-stroke with the back of the nail |
| **B** | **Brush:** drag the back of the nail down across 2–3 strings (the "di") |
| **T** | **Thumb:** sound a string with the thumb (usually the 5th-string drone, the "ty") |

**Left-hand (fretting) ornament symbols**, written between fret numbers:

| Symbol | Means | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **h** | **hammer-on:** strike a lower fret, then "hammer" a finger down to sound a higher fret without striking again | `2h4` (strike 2, hammer to 4) |
| **p** | **pull-off:** sound a fretted note, then pull the finger off (sideways) to sound a lower note | `2p0` (sound 2, pull off to open) |
| **/** | **slide up:** strike a fret, then slide the finger up to a higher fret keeping the note ringing | `2/4` |
| **\\** | **slide down** | `5\2` |

**Putting it together: here is one full bum-ditty measure (the pattern from Day 4) in this notation:**

```
        BUM         DI      TY          BUM         DI      TY
 1 D |---0----------0--------------------0----------0------------|
 2 B |--------------0--------------------------------0-----------|
 3 G |--------------0--------------------------------0-----------|
 4 D |----------------------------------------------------------|
 5 g |-----------------------0--------------------------------0--|
        I           B        T           I           B        T
```

Read that as: **strike** the open 1st string (I) → **brush** strings 1-2-3 (B) → **thumb** the open 5th string (T) → and repeat. That's "bum-ditty bum-ditty," one measure. If you take one picture from this whole course, take this one; you'll have it under your fingers by the end of Week 1.

> **Timing note:** in clawhammer the "**bum**" (strike) is a longer note and the "**di-ty**" (brush-thumb) is the quicker pair that fills the rest of the beat; count it "**1**-and-uh" or say "**bum**-di-ty" out loud with the *bum* slightly stretched. Most beginners rush the di-ty; let it breathe.

## 4. Chord shapes (gDGBD)

Numbers are frets, top to bottom = strings 1→5. `0` = open, `x` = don't worry about it (the 5th string drone is fine to ring on all of these).

```
   G              C              D7             D (full)
1 D | 0          1 D | 2        1 D | 0        1 D | 4
2 B | 0          2 B | 1        2 B | 1        2 B | 3
3 G | 0          3 G | 0        3 G | 2        3 G | 2
4 D | 0          4 D | 2        4 D | 0        4 D | 0
5 g | 0          5 g | 0        5 g | 0        5 g | 0
  (all open)     C major        easy D7        fuller D
```

- **G** = everything open. The friendliest chord in music.
- **C** = 1st string 2nd fret, 2nd string 1st fret, 4th string 2nd fret (3rd string open). Notes C–E–G.
- **D7** = 2nd string 1st fret, 3rd string 2nd fret (1st and 4th open). The everyday old-time "D": easy and it sounds right.
- **D (full)** = 1st string 4th fret, 2nd string 3rd fret, 3rd string 2nd fret (4th string open). Notes D–F#–A. Use it when D7 sounds too "bluesy" for the tune.

Most beginner old-time tunes live on just **G, C, and D7**. Learn those three cold and you can play hundreds of tunes.

## 5. The core repertoire & where to hear it (required listening)

You must **listen to a recording of every tune before and while you learn it.** Below are the four core tunes, their usual key/tuning, and where to find free, trustworthy tab + audio. These sites are the standard free resources of the clawhammer world:

- **Banjo Hangout** (banjohangout.org): enormous free tab archive (search the tune name; filter to "clawhammer"), plus discussion forums. The community standard.
- **Learn Clawhammer Banjo / clawhammerbanjo.net** (Brainjo): excellent free "Clawhammer Core Repertoire" and "Clawhammer Top 10" lesson series with tab and video for exactly these tunes.
- **Tunefox** and **Banjo Skills** (banjoskills.com): free beginner tabs and slow-down video lessons for the standards.
- **Slippery Hill** (slippery-hill.com): archival field recordings of *source* old-time players; this is where you go to hear how the tradition actually sounds.
- **YouTube:** search "[tune name] clawhammer slow" for play-along and slowed-down versions.

| Tune | Key / tuning | Character | Introduced |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Boil Them Cabbage Down** | G / gDGBD | dead-simple I–IV–V, great for chords + singing | Day 6 |
| **Cripple Creek** | G / gDGBD | *the* first clawhammer tune; signature 3rd-string 2→4 slide | Day 11 |
| **Old Joe Clark** | A (playable in G shapes) / gDGBD | Mixolydian "flat-7" twang | Day 13 |
| **Shady Grove** | D modal / **Sawmill** (gDGCD) | haunting, modal; the classic alternate-tuning tune | Day 16 |

## 6. A two-minute history (the long version is woven through the lessons)

- The banjo is **African in origin.** West African gourd-bodied, skin-headed plucked lutes (the **akonting** of the Jola people is the closest living relative) were brought to the Americas by enslaved Africans. The akonting is played with a **downstroke technique** and even has a short **drone "thumb string"**, the direct ancestor of clawhammer and of the 5th string. ([Akonting, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Akonting), [Banjo: A Brief History, Ballad of America](https://balladofamerica.org/banjo/))
- In the **19th-century minstrel era**, white performers (in the deeply racist blackface tradition) learned the downstroke style from Black musicians and popularized the banjo nationally. The **first banjo method book, Briggs' Banjo Instructor (1855)**, already notates the downstroke ("stroke style") that is recognizably clawhammer. This history is ugly and important; the technique we play came through it.
- The style took deep root in the **Appalachian South**, fusing with fiddle music into **old-time**, the dance music of the rural Southeast.
- A 20th-century **folk revival** (Pete Seeger, whose banjo book taught thousands, Mike Seeger, and the rediscovery of source players) carried it forward. The **Round Peak** style of Surry County, North Carolina (**Tommy Jarrell, Fred Cockerham, and Kyle Creed**) became the most influential regional clawhammer sound, prized for its driving, syncopated groove.

You'll meet these people again in the lessons. Knowing the lineage isn't trivia; it tells you whose recordings to chase when you want to get better.

## 7. Gear & maintenance notes (so the banjo doesn't fight you)

- **Fingernail:** clawhammer strikes with the **back of the nail** of the index or middle finger. Keep that nail a little long and filed smooth. If it's weak or splitting, a drop of super-glue or a stick-on nail helps; some players use a metal **fingerpick worn backwards.** A too-short nail = thin, weak tone.
- **Action too high?** If the strings are painfully far from the fretboard and fretting hurts, the banjo's *action* may need lowering (bridge/neck adjustment); worth a setup at a shop. Don't assume the pain is you.
- **Head & bridge:** if the tone is dead or buzzy, check the bridge is upright and in roughly the right spot, and that the head is snug. Beyond that, a once-over setup at a music store is the best $40–60 a beginner banjo can get.
- **Strings:** light or medium banjo strings; change them when they sound dull or won't stay in tune. New strings stretch; re-tune often for the first day.
