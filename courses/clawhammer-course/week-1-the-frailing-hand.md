# Week 1 — The Frailing Hand

> **The thesis of this week:** clawhammer is, at its heart, *one rhythmic motion* of the striking hand — strike down, brush down, catch the drone — repeated until it's as automatic as walking. We call it the **bum-ditty**. Everything else in old-time banjo (melody, ornaments, tunes) is decoration hung on that steady frame. So Week 1 is almost entirely about the **right hand** (the striking hand) and building a groove that doesn't wobble. Get this week solid and the rest of the course is fun. Rush it and everything later collapses. Go slow.

Throughout the course, tab examples use the notation from the [Field Guide](SUPPLEMENT-tab-tunings-and-resources.md) — read its "Reading tab" section before today if you haven't. Letters under the staff are the striking hand: **I** = index strike, **B** = brush, **T** = thumb.

> **A note on "right hand."** This course assumes you fret with your left hand and frail (strike) with your right, like most players. If you're left-handed and playing a lefty banjo, swap "left" and "right" throughout — the music is identical.

# Day 1 — Meet the Banjo

### Objectives
- Identify the parts of the banjo and the (confusing) string numbering.
- Tune to gDGBD with a tuner.
- Sit and hold the banjo in a relaxed, sustainable position.
- Know, in one paragraph, where this instrument comes from.

### Listen (5 min)
Before you play a note, **listen.** Find a recording of a clawhammer banjo player — search "Tommy Jarrell banjo" or "clawhammer banjo old time" — and just listen for two minutes. Notice the *steady, rolling, slightly lazy groove* and the high ringing note (the 5th-string drone) pinging through everything. That sound is your destination.

### Lecture

**The banjo is a drum with strings.** The white head is a drumhead; striking the strings makes them vibrate *and* makes the head resonate, which is why a banjo barks the way it does. (Full part-by-part tour is in the [Field Guide](SUPPLEMENT-tab-tunings-and-resources.md) §1 — read it now if you haven't.)

**The string numbering is backwards from your intuition, so learn it on day one:**
- The **1st string** is the one nearest the *floor* when you hold the banjo — the highest-pitched of the four long strings.
- The **4th string** is nearest the *ceiling* — the lowest.
- The **5th string** is the short one with the peg sticking out of the neck. It's a high drone and it never gets fretted in most beginner playing.

**Tuning — gDGBD ("open G").** From the 5th string down to the 1st: **g, D, G, B, D.** Strummed open, that's a G major chord. Clip on a tuner and bring each string to its letter (Field Guide §2 has the step-by-step and the octave warning). **You will tune every time you play.** An out-of-tune banjo trains your ear to accept wrong notes — non-negotiable.

**Posture (this prevents pain and bad tone):**
- Sit forward on a chair, both feet flat. Rest the pot on your right thigh, neck angled up at maybe 30–45°.
- The banjo should balance against your body so your **left hand is free** — it should never be holding the neck *up*. If the neck dives toward the floor when you let go, shift the banjo or use a strap.
- A **strap is strongly recommended even sitting** — it stabilizes the banjo so both hands can stay loose.
- Right forearm rests lightly on the rim, hand hovering over the head near the bridge — roughly an inch or two up the neck from the bridge is the clawhammer sweet spot for tone.

**The one-paragraph history (the long version is in Field Guide §6 and we'll return to it all course):** the banjo is **African in origin** — descended from West African gourd lutes like the **akonting**, which is played with a *downstroke* and even has a short high *drone string*, exactly like clawhammer and the 5th string. Brought to the Americas by enslaved Africans, popularized (through the racist minstrel era) nationally, and rooted deep in **Appalachian old-time** dance music, it's now played around the world. You are joining a very old conversation.

### Play — today's assignment
1. **(10 min) Tune, and re-tune.** Tune to gDGBD. Detune one string on purpose and tune it back, by ear-and-tuner, three times. Get comfortable with the pegs and which way raises/lowers pitch.
2. **(10 min) Find the strings blind.** Resting your right hand over the head, pluck each string 4→3→2→1 and say its number out loud. Then pluck the 5th-string drone. Do it looking, then *not* looking. The "which string is which" reflex starts now.
3. **(5 min) Sit and settle.** Get the posture comfortable and loose. Strum all the strings once with your thumb and just enjoy that it's a G chord. Roll your shoulders down; unclench your jaw.

### Self-check
1. Which physical string is the "1st string" — nearest the floor or the ceiling?
2. What are the five pitches of gDGBD, from the 5th string down?
3. What chord do the open strings make?
4. What African instrument is the banjo's close relative, and what does it share with clawhammer?
5. Why must you tune every time you play?

# Day 2 — The Strike

### Objectives
- Form the relaxed clawhammer "claw."
- Strike a single string cleanly with the *back of the nail*, moving *down*.
- Get a consistent, round tone on each of the four melody strings.

### Listen (3 min)
Replay yesterday's clawhammer recording. Today, listen for the *individual struck notes* — the "bum" — under the busier brushing. Each one is a single string hit firmly with a nail.

### Lecture

Here is the one idea that separates clawhammer from every other way of playing a stringed instrument: **you do not pluck up. You strike down with the back of your fingernail.**

**Form the claw:**
- Curl your right hand into a loose, relaxed claw — as if holding a small apple or letting your hand hang naturally. Fingers gently curled, not clenched.
- The striking finger is your **index *or* middle finger** (pick one and mostly stick with it — both are traditional; many players use the middle finger because the nail is stronger). The other fingers stay curled out of the way.
- Your **thumb sticks out** to the side, ready to land on the 5th string later. For now it just rides along.

**The strike itself:**
- Move from a relaxed, slightly floppy wrist and a little forearm — *not* by flicking the finger. Think of your hand falling/bouncing down onto the string, like knocking on a low door.
- The **back of the nail** catches the string on the way down and releases. The motion continues *past* the string (you don't stop on it).
- Aim for **one string** cleanly. After striking, your hand naturally comes to rest with the finger having passed through.

**Tone-killers to avoid:**
- **Plucking up** with the fleshy fingertip (guitar habit) — wrong motion, thin sound. Down, with the nail.
- **A tense, locked wrist** — kills tone and your forearm. Stay floppy.
- **Hitting two strings when you mean one** — slow down and aim. Precision first, speed never.

> **Where to strike for tone:** about **1–2 inches up the neck from the bridge**, over the head. Right at the bridge is harsh; up near the fingerboard is soft and plunky (some old-time players love that). Experiment, but start in the middle.

### Play
1. **(10 min) Single strikes.** Strike the open **3rd string (G)** alone, 20 times, slowly, listening for a clean, round note each time. Loose wrist. No rush.
2. **(10 min) String tour.** Strike each open melody string in turn — 4, 3, 2, 1 — cleanly, four times each. The goal is *the same good tone on every string* and hitting only the one you aim at.
3. **(5 min) Nail check.** Notice whether your nail is catching well. If your tone is thin and weak, your nail may be too short or you're using fingertip flesh — adjust (Field Guide §7 on nails).

### Daily drill (start today)
**Clean-strike count, 3 min:** strike one called string ("3rd!") cleanly; if it buzzes or you hit a neighbor, reset. Count how many clean strikes in a row you can get. Loose hand, slow.

### Self-check
1. Which part of the finger strikes the string, and in which direction?
2. Index or middle finger — which do you strike with? (Either is fine; which did you pick?)
3. Where over the head do you strike for a balanced tone?
4. Name two common tone-killers.
5. Should the motion come mainly from a flicking finger or a loose wrist/forearm?

# Day 3 — The Thumb & the Drone

### Objectives
- Sound the 5th-string drone cleanly with the thumb.
- Alternate a strike and a thumb ("bum… ty") in a slow, even pulse.

### Listen (3 min)
Listen again — this time hunt for the **high pinging note** that rings out between the melody notes. That's the open 5th-string **g** drone, played by the thumb. It's the sound of the banjo. By the end of today you'll be making it.

### Lecture

The **5th string** is the short high drone, and the **thumb** is its dedicated player. Together with the strike, the thumb is half of the clawhammer engine.

**The thumb motion:**
- After your striking finger comes down through a melody string, your **thumb drops onto the 5th string** and sounds it with a small downward pluck (the thumb also strikes *down*, with the side/tip — it doesn't pull up much).
- The 5th string lives right at the top, near your thumb's resting position, so the thumb barely has to move.
- Keep it relaxed — the thumb "falls" onto the string as part of the same loose hand motion as the strike.

**"Bum… ty" — the half-pattern:** today we link two events into a slow, even alternation:
- **BUM** = strike a melody string (say the open 3rd string).
- **TY** = thumb sounds the open 5th string.

```
        BUM             TY              BUM             TY
 1 D |----------------------------------------------------------|
 2 B |----------------------------------------------------------|
 3 G |---0-----------------------------0------------------------|
 4 D |----------------------------------------------------------|
 5 g |-----------------0------------------------------0---------|
        I               T               I               T
```

Play **strike (3rd string), thumb (5th string), strike, thumb…** slowly and *evenly*. Count "**bum**-ty, **bum**-ty." Keep the two events the same distance apart in time — a steady pulse. This is the skeleton; tomorrow we fill in the middle.

**The most common mistake:** rushing or crowding the thumb right behind the strike. Leave *equal space.* Use a metronome at a slow tempo (say 60 bpm) and put the **strike on the click**, the **thumb halfway between clicks.**

### Play
1. **(8 min) Bare thumb.** Sound just the open 5th string with the thumb, 20 times, clean and relaxed.
2. **(15 min) Bum-ty.** Strike open 3rd string, then thumb the 5th, over and over, slow and even. With a metronome at ~60: strike on the click, thumb on the "and." Aim for a *machine-steady* alternation for 1 minute straight.
3. **(5 min) Move the bum.** Do the same bum-ty but strike a different melody string each time (4th, then 3rd, then 2nd, then 1st) while the thumb keeps hitting the 5th. Tone stays even; pulse stays steady.

### Daily drill
**Bum-ty metronome hold, 3 min:** at 60 bpm, hold a perfectly even strike-thumb-strike-thumb. Every time the rhythm wobbles or the thumb crowds the strike, that's the rep — reset and continue.

### Self-check
1. Which string does the thumb play, and which finger plays it?
2. Does the thumb pluck up or strike down?
3. In "bum-ty," what's the bum and what's the ty?
4. Where does the thumb fall relative to the metronome click if the strike is on the click?
5. What's the most common timing mistake with the thumb?

# Day 4 — The Bum-Ditty (the engine)

### Objectives
- Add the **brush** between the strike and the thumb to make the full bum-ditty.
- Play a steady, even bum-ditty on the open G chord with a metronome.

### Listen (3 min)
One more focused listen. Now you can hear all three pieces: the struck note (**bum**), the quick downward brush across several strings (**di**), and the thumb drone (**ty**). **Bum-ditty, bum-ditty.** That rolling sound *is* clawhammer.

### Lecture

Today we insert the **brush** between yesterday's strike and thumb. That completes the **bum-ditty** — the single most important pattern in the entire style.

**The three motions, in one smooth roll of a loose hand:**
1. **BUM (strike, "I"):** index/middle nail strikes *one* melody string, down.
2. **DI (brush, "B"):** the same hand keeps moving down and the nail **brushes across 2–3 strings** (a little downward strum) — light and quick.
3. **TY (thumb, "T"):** the thumb sounds the 5th-string drone.

It is *one continuous downward motion of the hand* that happens to catch a single string, then a few strings, then the drone — not three separate jabs. Down… brush… thumb. Loose wrist throughout.

```
        BUM         DI      TY          BUM         DI      TY
 1 D |---0----------0--------------------0----------0------------|
 2 B |--------------0--------------------------------0-----------|
 3 G |--------------0--------------------------------0-----------|
 4 D |----------------------------------------------------------|
 5 g |-----------------------0--------------------------------0--|
        I           B        T           I           B        T
```

(Here the "bum" strikes the open 1st string; the brush catches strings 1-2-3; the thumb hits the 5th. On a G chord every string is open, so it all rings as G.)

**Timing — the heartbeat of old-time:** the **bum** lands *on the beat* and is a touch longer; the **di-ty** is a quicker pair filling the second half of the beat. Count it "**1**-and-uh" or just chant "**BUM**-di-ty" with the *bum* stretched. Two bum-ditties = one measure of typical old-time (in 2/4):

> **1**-di-ty **2**-di-ty | **1**-di-ty **2**-di-ty …

**The beginner failure mode (almost universal):** rushing the "di-ty" so it crowds up against the next "bum," making a lurching, galloping rhythm. **Fix:** slow way down, use a metronome, and deliberately *delay* the di-ty — let it breathe. Even is the goal, not fast.

### Play
1. **(10 min) Air it out.** Without worrying about which strings, just do the *motion* — strike-brush-thumb — slowly, feeling it as one loose downward roll. Watch your hand stay relaxed.
2. **(20 min) Bum-ditty on G.** With a metronome at **50–60 bpm**, play a clean bum-ditty on the open G chord: strike 1st string, brush 1-2-3, thumb 5th. Put the **strike on the click.** Hold it for as long as you can without the rhythm getting lumpy. When it gets lumpy, slow the metronome down, don't speed up.
3. **(5 min) Even-ness audit.** Record 30 seconds on your phone and listen back. Is every "bum" the same distance apart? Is the di-ty crowding? Be honest. This recording habit starts now and never stops.

### Daily drill
**The metronome stare-down, 4 min:** bum-ditty on G at a tempo where you're *perfectly* even — even if that's painfully slow. Hold it. Only nudge the tempo up 2–3 bpm once a full minute is flawless. This is *the* core drill of the course.

### Self-check
1. What are the three parts of the bum-ditty, in order, and which striking-hand part makes each?
2. Is the bum-ditty three separate motions or one continuous one?
3. Which part lands on the beat?
4. What's the near-universal beginner timing mistake, and the fix?
5. How many bum-ditties make a typical old-time measure?

# Day 5 — The Fretting Hand: Chords

### Objectives
- Fret the **G, C, and D7** chords cleanly.
- Change between them without stopping the bum-ditty.

### Listen (3 min)
Listen to **Boil Them Cabbage Down** (tomorrow's tune) — find a version online (Field Guide §5). Notice it only seems to use a few chords and the same melody phrase over and over. That simplicity is why it's everyone's first tune.

### Lecture

So far the left hand has done nothing — open strings only. Today it goes to work. The three chords below cover an enormous chunk of old-time. (Diagrams and notes are in [Field Guide §4](SUPPLEMENT-tab-tunings-and-resources.md).)

- **G** — all strings open. Nothing to fret. Your home base.
- **C** — 1st string/2nd fret, 2nd string/1st fret, 4th string/2nd fret (3rd open).
- **D7** — 2nd string/1st fret, 3rd string/2nd fret (1st and 4th open). The easy everyday "D."

**Fretting-hand fundamentals:**
- Press the string down **just behind the fret** (on the bridge side of the fret wire), not on top of it and not far behind — that gives a clean note with the least effort.
- Press with the **fingertips**, nails short, fingers curled so they don't mute neighboring strings.
- Use the **least pressure that makes the note ring clean.** Squeezing harder is wasted effort and tension. Test by striking each chorded string individually — any buzz or thud means adjust finger placement or curl, not force.
- Keep the **thumb low on the back of the neck** (roughly behind your first finger), not wrapped over the top — it gives your fingers room to curl.

**Changing chords without stopping:** the secret is that the bum-ditty's "bum" gives you a tiny window. Practice the change as a *move*, not a fret-by-fret reconstruction:
- See the **shape** of the next chord and move all fingers toward it together.
- It's okay if the change is rough at first; **keep the right hand frailing in time** even if the left hand isn't fully there yet. **Never stop the groove to fix the left hand.** A slightly muffed chord in time beats a perfect chord that broke the rhythm.

### Play
1. **(10 min) One chord at a time.** Form G (easy), then C, then D7. For each, strike each fretted string individually and make sure it rings clean. Fix any buzz with finger placement, not force.
2. **(10 min) Change in free time.** Slowly: G → C → G → D7 → G, strumming each once with the thumb, no metronome. Just get the moves under your fingers.
3. **(15 min) Change inside the groove.** Metronome ~50 bpm, bum-ditty going. Play 2 bars of G, 2 bars of C, 2 bars of G, 2 bars of D7, repeat. **Keep the right hand steady** through every change, even when the left hand is late. This is the real skill.

### Daily drill
**Chord-flip, 3 min:** call a random chord (G/C/D7); form it as fast as you can with all fingers landing together; strum to check it's clean. Reset, call another. Speed of *forming the shape* is what you're training.

### Self-check
1. Where exactly do you press the string relative to the fret?
2. What's the rule about how hard to press?
3. Where does the left thumb sit?
4. When a chord change is too slow, what do you do with the *right* hand?
5. Which of the three chords requires no fretting at all?

# Day 6 — First Tune: Boil Them Cabbage Down (+ Quiz 1)

### Objectives
- Play your first real tune by frailing its chord changes and singing the melody.
- Take Quiz 1.

### Listen (5 min)
Listen to **Boil Them Cabbage Down** twice through. Sing along until the melody and the words are stuck in your head. You cannot play what you can't hum — get it in your ear *first.*

### Lecture

Here's the good news: your very first tune needs nothing new. **Boil Them Cabbage Down is a I–IV–V tune** — it uses only **G, C, and D7**, the three chords you learned yesterday — and you can play a completely legitimate, fun version by **frailing the chord changes with your steady bum-ditty while you sing the melody.** This is exactly how old-time players back up a song, and it's a real musical skill, not a training-wheels cheat.

**The chord map (one common version, in G):**

```
G                          C              G
Boil them cabbage down, down, bake them hoecakes brown, brown,
G                          D7             G
The only song that I can sing is boil them cabbage down.
```

So your job: **keep a rock-steady bum-ditty going on G, change to C where the words "bake them hoecakes" land, back to G, then D7 on the last line, and home to G.** Sing the tune on top. Let the *singing* carry the melody while the banjo lays down the groove and the chords.

> **Why start here?** Three reasons. (1) It cements chord changes *in time* — the Week-1 capstone skill. (2) It connects the banjo to a *song*, which is the whole point. (3) It's almost impossible to do badly enough not to enjoy it. Tomorrow's project and all of Week 2 build melodic playing on top of this rhythmic foundation.

**If you don't want to sing:** hum it, or just play the changes and *hear* the melody in your head. But try singing — old-time banjo and the human voice grew up together, and nobody at a jam cares if you're a great singer.

### Play
1. **(10 min) Changes alone.** Without singing, play the chord map in time at ~50 bpm: G (2 bars) → C (1 bar) → G (1 bar) → … get the changes landing where the words would.
2. **(15 min) Add the voice.** Now sing (or hum) while you frail the changes. Start dead slow. If the singing throws off your hands, slow down until both can coexist. This split-attention is the skill.
3. **(10 min) Whole tune, twice through.** Play and sing the whole thing start to finish, twice, without stopping — even if it's rough. Getting *through* a tune is its own skill.

### 📝 Quiz 1
Take **Quiz 1** now (see `QUIZZES.md`), closed-book. Grade against `ANSWER-KEYS.md`. It covers the banjo, tuning, the strike, the thumb, the bum-ditty, and chords — Week 1's whole technical core. If you score below 90%, re-study the weak spot on tomorrow's woodshed day.

### Self-check
1. What three chords does Boil Them Cabbage Down use?
2. What does "I–IV–V" mean in plain terms for this tune (which three chords in G)?
3. When a tune's melody is in your *voice*, what is the banjo's job in this version?
4. Why is "frailing the changes and singing" a real skill and not cheating?
5. What must you always do *before* trying to learn a tune?

# Day 7 — Woodshed & Jam Day + Project 1

Today is consolidation, not new material. "Woodshedding" is the old musicians' word for shutting yourself away to drill something until it's solid. Three things:

### 1. Review the week
Reread the self-checks for Days 1–6 and answer every one from memory. Anything you fumble, reread that lecture's relevant paragraph and play the example. The two load-bearing skills are the **even bum-ditty (Day 4)** and **changing chords without stopping (Day 5)** — spend the most time there.

### 2. The "metronome ladder" drill (15 min)
Bum-ditty on G. Find the fastest tempo at which it's still *perfectly even* (record and check). Then drop 10 bpm and hold it flawlessly for two minutes — building the groove into your body at a tempo you own. Resist the urge to play at your ragged top speed; that just rehearses raggedness.

### 3. Project 1 — The Steady Bum-Ditty
Begin **Project 1** (see `PROJECTS.md`). It asks you to record a steady bum-ditty through a chord progression in time — the proof that Week 1's foundation is solid. It's due end of Day 7 (or carry into the Week-2 woodshed day if you genuinely need it — but don't let it slide; everything ahead stands on it).

### Looking ahead
Week 1 was the **engine**: a steady frailing hand, a groove, and chords. Right now your banjo can lay down rhythm and back a song. **Week 2 is melody and drive** — the left-hand ornaments (hammer-ons, pull-offs, slides) and **drop-thumb** that turn that groove into actual *tunes* like Cripple Creek. From here on, the bum-ditty should fade into reflex so your attention is free for making melodies sing.
