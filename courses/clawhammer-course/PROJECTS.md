# Projects: MUS-17

Projects are where technique turns into *tunes you can actually play.* Each has a recorded deliverable and a grading rubric. **Record everything** (a phone is fine) and grade yourself by *listening back*, which is far more honest than judging while you play. "I basically played it" is a 70, not a 95. The standard everywhere is **steady, in time, and musical**, not fast or flawless.

> **Why recordings?** Because a metronome and a microphone don't flatter you. Your timing in your head is always better than your timing on tape. Listening back is the fastest way to improve, and the recordings become a priceless record of how far you've come.

---

## Project 1: The Steady Bum-Ditty *(Day 7 · 10% of grade)*

**Goal:** prove the Week-1 foundation is solid: a clean, even, in-time bum-ditty with chord changes that don't break the groove. This is the Domain 1–3 test.

### Deliverable
A single recording (1–2 minutes) of you playing, **with a metronome audible or clearly steady**:
1. **16 bars of bum-ditty on a G chord** at a tempo where it's *perfectly even* (slow is fine; even is the point).
2. **The chord progression** of Boil Them Cabbage Down (G – C – G – D7 – G), frailed in time with a steady bum-ditty, twice through. Singing/humming the melody is encouraged but not required.

### Rubric (10 pts)
| Pts | Criterion |
|---|---|
| 3 | Bum-ditty is *even*: every "bum" equally spaced, the di-ty not crowding (listen back!) |
| 3 | Tempo is steady against the metronome: no rushing or dragging |
| 2 | Chords ring clean (no dead/buzzing strings) |
| 2 | Chord changes happen *without stopping or hitching* the groove |

**Pass bar:** a stranger could tap their foot to it the whole way through without your time tripping them up.

## Project 2: A Tune With Ornaments *(Day 14 · 15% of grade)*

**Goal:** demonstrate real melodic clawhammer: a tune played through with the three ornaments, in time. This is the Domain 4 + 6–7 test.

### Deliverable
A recording (1–2 minutes) of **Cripple Creek or Old Joe Clark**, played from the music or memory:
1. **The full tune, AABB**, melody recognizable, played all the way through without stopping (slow tempo is fine).
2. **At least one clearly audible** hammer-on, **one** pull-off, and **one** slide, each landing *in time* (the 2→4 slide counts).
3. **A steady groove underneath:** the di-ty keeps rolling even where the melody bums are imperfect.
4. **A spoken intro:** say the tune's name and key before you play (a tiny performance rep).

### Rubric (15 pts)
| Pts | Criterion |
|---|---|
| 4 | The melody is recognizable as the tune, both A and B parts |
| 3 | A clean, audible hammer-on, pull-off, and slide, each in time |
| 4 | Groove/timing steady throughout: di-ty doesn't break for the ornaments |
| 2 | You get all the way through AABB without stopping |
| 2 | Tone is reasonably clean (strikes ring, few buzzes) + spoken intro present |

**Pass bar:** someone who knows the tune would recognize it and not wince at the timing.

## Project 3: A Polished Tune *(Day 20 · 15% of grade)*

**Goal:** one tune taken from "I can get through it" to "I can *play* it": clean, confident, with drive and a touch of personal voice. This is the Domain 4–7 test at a higher bar.

### Deliverable
A recording of **one tune of your choice** from the course repertoire (or another you've learned), polished:
1. **Played from memory**, AABB, at a **danceable tempo** you can sustain cleanly (your honest tempo, not your ragged top speed).
2. **Clean ornaments and chord changes:** the rough spots from Project 2 repaired.
3. **Steady drive:** a groove with forward momentum, ideally checked against a metronome or recording.
4. **At least one deliberate personal variation:** an ornament choice, a drop-thumb fill, a dynamic accent, or a varied repeat (Day 19).
5. **A one-line written note:** what you chose to vary and why.

### Rubric (15 pts)
| Pts | Criterion |
|---|---|
| 4 | Played confidently from memory, all the way through, AABB |
| 4 | Steady drive at a sustainable, danceable tempo |
| 3 | Ornaments and chord changes are clean and in time |
| 2 | A genuine personal variation is present and intentional |
| 2 | Tone and overall musicality (it sounds *good*, not just correct) |

**Pass bar:** you'd be willing to play it for a friend in the room.

## Final Project: The "House Concert" Recording *(Day 21 · 25% of grade)*

**Goal:** the capstone. A short performance of the tunes you can really play, proving you ended the course an actual banjo *player.* This is the graduation requirement and the Domain 6–7 (+ 9) test.

### Specification
1. **Two to four tunes**, played from memory, that you can genuinely play, including **at least one in an alternate tuning** (Shady Grove in Sawmill counts) to show tuning range. Coherence and groove beat quantity; three solid tunes beat four shaky ones.
2. **A spoken intro to each tune:** name it, and state the key/tuning if you switch. Retuning on the recording is fine and realistic; narrate it ("now I'll retune to Sawmill for Shady Grove").
3. **Steady, danceable groove on every tune:** the non-negotiable. A listener should be able to tap a foot start to finish.
4. **Played at *your* honest tempo:** musical and clean, not rushed. Flash and speed earn nothing here; steadiness and feel earn everything.
5. **One short written reflection (½ page):** what came easiest, what's still hardest, which player/recording you want to learn from next, and where you'll find people to play with.

### Rubric (25 pts)
| Pts | Criterion |
|---|---|
| 6 | Steady, danceable groove across *all* tunes (the core skill) |
| 5 | Tunes played accurately and confidently from memory |
| 4 | At least one tune in an alternate tuning, executed cleanly |
| 4 | Clean technique throughout: ornaments, changes, tone |
| 3 | Musicality and personal feel: it sounds like *playing*, not reciting |
| 3 | Spoken intros present + written reflection thoughtful and honest |

**Graduation bar:** 23+/25, **and** a listener who knows nothing about banjo would enjoy listening to it and tap their foot the whole way through. If you hit that, you didn't just finish a course; you became a banjo player. Save the file; it's your "before."
