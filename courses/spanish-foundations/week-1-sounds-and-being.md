# Week 1: Sounds, People & the Verb To Be

> **The thesis of this week:** before you can build sentences, you need two things solid. First, the **sounds**, so that what you say is understood and you can read any word aloud correctly. Second, the verb **to be**, which in Spanish is two different verbs, ser and estar, and which you need for almost every basic sentence about who and what and where things are. Week 1 is mostly mouth and ear, plus your first real grammar. Go slow, say everything out loud, and write everything by hand.

Throughout the course, new words appear with a quick pronunciation hint in quotes and noun gender shown by the article (el or la). Read the pronunciation and stress sections of the [Reference file](SUPPLEMENT-reference-and-vocabulary.md) before today if you have not.

# Day 1: The Sounds of Spanish

### Objectives
- Pronounce the five Spanish vowels purely and consistently.
- Apply the three stress rules to read any word aloud correctly.
- Pronounce the consonants that differ from English.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Find any short clip of spoken Spanish (a news clip, a song, a learner video) and listen for one minute, just for the music of it. Notice the crisp, even vowels and the rolled r. Then say these five vowels aloud, slowly, ten times each: **a, e, i, o, u** ("ah, eh, ee, oh, oo"). Keep them short and pure. This is the foundation of every word you will ever say.

### Lecture

Here is the good news that makes Spanish easier than English to read: **the spelling tells you the sound, every time.** Once you learn the rules, you never have to guess how a written word is pronounced. English has "though, through, tough, cough." Spanish has none of that.

**The vowels are the whole game.** Each of the five has exactly one sound and it never changes (Reference section 1). The most common foreign-accent mistake is letting an English glide sneak in. In English, "no" drifts toward "no-w." In Spanish, **no** is a single pure "noh" that stops cleanly. Same with **e**: it is "eh," not "ay." Drill the vowels until they are automatic and half your accent problem is already solved.

**A handful of consonants differ from English** and they are worth memorizing today (full table in Reference section 1):
- **h** is always silent. *hola* is "OH-lah."
- **j**, and **g** before e or i, are a harsh h from the throat. *jefe* (boss) is "HEH-feh."
- **ñ** is "ny" as in canyon. *español* is "es-pah-NYOL."
- **ll** is a y sound in most of Latin America. *llamar* is "yah-MAR."
- **rr** is a trilled, rolled r. A single **r** between vowels is a quick tap, like the tt in the American "butter." Do not worry if the trill takes weeks; it does for everyone.
- **v** sounds the same as **b**. *vivir* (to live) starts like "bee-BEER."

**Stress: three rules and you are done** (Reference section 2). Words ending in a vowel, n, or s are stressed on the second-to-last syllable. Words ending in any other consonant are stressed on the last syllable. A written accent mark overrides both and tells you exactly where to stress. The accent is not optional decoration; it is a pronunciation instruction, and sometimes the only difference between two words.

### Write by hand (15 min)
1. Copy the five-vowel table from the Reference, with an example word for each, three times. Say each vowel and word aloud as you write it.
2. Write these ten words, and over each one draw a small mark above the stressed syllable after you apply the rules: casa, hablar, español, teléfono, gracias, ciudad, mujer, también, trabajo, café.
3. Copy the three stress rules in your own handwriting once.

### Speak and record (10 min)
Read these words aloud, slowly and clearly, then record yourself reading the list once through: hola, gracias, español, mujer, trabajo, jefe, llamar, perro, pero, ciudad, teléfono, buenos días. Listen back. Are your vowels pure and short? Is the stress on the syllable the rules predict?

### Self-check
1. What are the five vowel sounds, in order?
2. What sound does the letter h make?
3. Where is the stress in a word ending in a vowel, n, or s?
4. What does a written accent mark tell you?
5. Which two letters sound the same in Spanish?

# Day 2: Greetings, Introductions & tú vs. usted

### Objectives
- Greet someone, introduce yourself, and exchange courtesies.
- Choose correctly between tú and usted.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say the survival phrases 1 through 8 from Reference section 7 out loud, twice each, matching the pronunciation hints. These are whole chunks. You do not need to understand their grammar yet to use them.

### Lecture

You can have a real, polite interaction today with memorized chunks, before you can build a single sentence from scratch. That is not cheating; it is how every traveler and every child starts.

**The core greetings** (Reference section 6): *Hola* (hi), *Buenos días* (good morning), *Buenas tardes* (good afternoon), *Buenas noches* (good evening or good night). Note that buenas tardes and buenas noches use the plural feminine because día is masculine but tarde and noche are feminine; just memorize them as set phrases for now.

**Introducing yourself.** The everyday way uses a reflexive verb you will study later, but as a chunk it is simple:
- *Me llamo Ana.* literally "I call myself Ana," meaning "My name is Ana."
- *¿Cómo te llamas?* "What is your name?" (informal)
- *¿Cómo se llama?* "What is your name?" (formal)
- *Mucho gusto.* "Nice to meet you."

**Asking how someone is.** *¿Cómo estás?* (informal) or *¿Cómo está?* (formal). A normal answer: *Bien, gracias. ¿Y tú?* or *¿Y usted?*

**The tú / usted choice is real and it matters.** Spanish has two words for "you" when speaking to one person, and choosing the right one is a social act, not a grammar technicality:
- **tú** is informal. Use it with friends, family, children, peers, and anyone who has invited you to.
- **usted** is formal and respectful. Use it with strangers, elders, customers, officials, your boss, and in any professional or first-time situation.

The safe default with someone new, especially in a work or service setting, is **usted.** It is hard to give offense by being too polite, and easy to give offense by being too familiar too soon. People will often invite you to switch to tú; let them lead. The verb forms differ between the two, so this choice changes how you conjugate, which is one more reason to lock it in now.

> **Regional note:** in much of Latin America, especially Argentina, Uruguay, and parts of Central America, you will hear **vos** instead of tú, with its own verb forms. You do not need to produce it, but do not be confused when you hear it. tú will be understood everywhere.

### Write by hand (15 min)
1. Copy this short dialogue twice, by hand, including the upside-down question marks and the accents:

> A: Buenos días. ¿Cómo se llama usted?
> B: Me llamo Carlos. ¿Y usted?
> A: Me llamo Ana. Mucho gusto.
> B: Mucho gusto, Ana.

2. Then rewrite the same dialogue in the informal register (te llamas, y tú).
3. Write four greetings appropriate to four times of day or situations.

### Speak and record (10 min)
Record yourself doing the full introduction dialogue, playing both parts, once formally and once informally. Then introduce your real self aloud: greet, give your name, ask the other person's name, and say nice to meet you.

### Self-check
1. Give the four time-of-day greetings.
2. How do you say "My name is..." and "Nice to meet you"?
3. What is the difference between tú and usted?
4. Which one is the safe default with a stranger or in a work setting?
5. What is vos, and do you need to produce it?

# Day 3: Noun Gender, Articles & Plurals

### Objectives
- Understand that every noun has a gender.
- Use the definite and indefinite articles correctly.
- Form plurals.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say these noun-with-article pairs aloud, twice each: el hombre, la mujer, el trabajo, la casa, el día, la noche, el amigo, la amiga. Notice you are saying the little word (el or la) every time. That is the habit to build.

### Lecture

**Every Spanish noun is either masculine or feminine.** This is grammatical gender, not biology. *La mesa* (the table) is feminine; *el día* (the day) is masculine. There is no deep reason a table is feminine; it simply is, and you learn the gender as part of the word.

**The rule of thumb, and its exceptions:**
- Most nouns ending in **-o** are masculine: el trabajo, el amigo, el dinero.
- Most nouns ending in **-a** are feminine: la casa, la mesa, la palabra.
- Other endings vary and must be memorized: la noche (feminine), el coche (masculine), la ciudad (feminine), el día (masculine, a famous exception).

Because of these exceptions, the iron rule of this course is: **never learn a noun by itself. Always learn it with its article.** Learn "la noche," not "noche." Learn "el día," not "día." The gender is part of the word, and getting it wrong throws off every adjective and article attached to it later.

**The articles.** Spanish has four forms of "the" and four of "a/an," because they must match the noun in gender and number:

| | masculine | feminine |
|---|---|---|
| "the" singular | **el** | **la** |
| "the" plural | **los** | **las** |
| "a/an" | **un** | **una** |
| "some" (plural) | **unos** | **unas** |

So: el amigo, los amigos, un amigo, unos amigos; la amiga, las amigas, una amiga, unas amigas.

**Making plurals:**
- Add **-s** to a noun ending in a vowel: casa to casas, hombre to hombres.
- Add **-es** to a noun ending in a consonant: mujer to mujeres, ciudad to ciudades, señor to señores.
- The article goes plural too: el día to los días, la mujer to las mujeres.

### Write by hand (20 min)
1. Copy the article table once.
2. For each of these twelve nouns, write it three times: once with the correct definite article (el/la), once made plural with the plural article (los/las), and once with the indefinite article (un/una). Nouns: casa, hombre, mujer, trabajo, amigo, amiga, día, noche, ciudad, mesa, teléfono, palabra. This is your first big handwriting drill. Say each one aloud as you write it.

### Speak and record (10 min)
Point at ten objects around your room. For each, say it aloud with the article ("la mesa, el teléfono..."). Guess the gender from the ending, then check the vocabulary list. Record yourself naming all ten.

### Self-check
1. What are the two genders, and what usually ends a masculine noun and a feminine noun?
2. Why must you always learn a noun with its article?
3. Give the four forms of "the."
4. How do you make a plural for a noun ending in a vowel? Ending in a consonant?
5. What gender is "día," and why is it notable?

# Day 4: Subject Pronouns and the Verb ser

### Objectives
- Know the subject pronouns.
- Conjugate ser in the present tense.
- Use ser to say who and what someone or something is.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say the full conjugation of ser aloud, twice: soy, eres, es, somos, sois, son. Then say these aloud: Soy Ana. Soy de México. Es mi amigo. Somos estudiantes.

### Lecture

**Subject pronouns** (the full table is in Reference section 3): yo (I), tú (you informal), usted (you formal), él (he), ella (she), nosotros (we), ustedes (you all), ellos/ellas (they). Remember that **usted uses the same verb form as él/ella**, and ustedes the same as ellos/ellas. That is the one thing that trips people up early.

**Spanish drops the pronoun most of the time.** Because the verb ending already tells you who is acting, you usually do not say "yo." "Soy de México" already means "I am from Mexico." Use the pronoun only for emphasis or to clear up ambiguity.

**ser is one of the two verbs for "to be."** Today is ser; tomorrow is estar. ser is irregular, so you memorize it as a chunk:

| yo soy | I am |
| tú eres | you are (informal) |
| él/ella/usted es | he/she is, you are (formal) |
| nosotros somos | we are |
| vosotros sois | you all are (Spain) |
| ellos/ellas/ustedes son | they are, you all are |

**Use ser for things that define what something fundamentally is.** A useful way to remember ser's jobs is identity, origin, and characteristics that belong to the thing itself:
- **Identity and naming:** *Soy Ana. Es mi jefe.* (I am Ana. He is my boss.)
- **Origin and nationality:** *Soy de Colombia. Ella es mexicana.* (I am from Colombia. She is Mexican.)
- **Profession:** *Somos profesores. Él es médico.* (We are teachers. He is a doctor.) Note Spanish does not use "a" here: not "es un médico" in the plain statement, just "es médico."
- **Inherent characteristics:** *La casa es grande. El trabajo es difícil.* (The house is big. The work is difficult.)
- **Time and dates:** *Son las tres. Hoy es lunes.* (It is three o'clock. Today is Monday.)

### Write by hand (20 min)
1. Handwrite the full ser conjugation table three times, saying each form aloud.
2. Copy these six sentences once each, then translate four sentences of your own into Spanish using ser (who you are, where you are from, what your job is, a characteristic of something):
   - Soy de Estados Unidos.
   - Tú eres mi amigo.
   - Ella es la jefa.
   - Nosotros somos compañeros de trabajo.
   - El teléfono es nuevo.
   - Hoy es martes.

### Speak and record (10 min)
Record yourself saying five true sentences about your real life with ser: your name, your origin, your job, and two characteristics of people or things around you.

### Self-check
1. Conjugate ser for all six persons from memory.
2. Which pronoun shares its verb form with él and ella?
3. Why does Spanish usually drop the subject pronoun?
4. Name three jobs that ser does (when you use it).
5. How do you say "She is a teacher" (note the missing "a")?

# Day 5: The Verb estar, and ser vs. estar

### Objectives
- Conjugate estar in the present tense.
- Use estar for location and condition.
- Choose correctly between ser and estar.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say estar aloud twice: estoy, estás, está, estamos, estáis, están. Then say: Estoy bien. ¿Dónde estás? La casa está aquí. Estamos cansados.

### Lecture

Spanish splits "to be" into two verbs, and choosing between them is one of the first real skills of the language. Yesterday was **ser**, for what something fundamentally is. Today is **estar**, for states and locations.

**estar conjugation** (note the accents on every form except estamos):

| yo estoy | tú estás | él/ella/usted está |
| nosotros estamos | vosotros estáis | ellos/ellas/ustedes están |

**estar has two main jobs:**
- **Location, where something is:** *Estoy en casa. ¿Dónde está el baño? El jefe está en la oficina.* (I am at home. Where is the bathroom? The boss is in the office.)
- **Condition or temporary state, how something is right now:** *Estoy cansado. ¿Cómo estás? La puerta está abierta. Estamos ocupados.* (I am tired. How are you? The door is open. We are busy.)

**The choice, in one line:** use **ser** for what something *is* (identity, origin, profession, inherent traits), and **estar** for where it *is* and how it *is right now* (location and condition). A common memory aid is that estar covers **Position and Condition**, and ser covers **Description, Origin, profession, Time** and other permanent identity.

The classic contrast pairs make it click:
- *Ella es bonita* (she is pretty, a characteristic) vs. *Ella está bonita hoy* (she looks pretty today, a state).
- *Él es aburrido* (he is boring, a trait) vs. *Él está aburrido* (he is bored, a state).
- *El café es bueno* (coffee is good, in general) vs. *El café está bueno* (this coffee tastes good, right now).

For where someone is **from**, you use ser (*soy de Perú*), but for where someone or something **is located**, you use estar (*estoy en Perú*). Origin is identity; location is position.

### Write by hand (20 min)
1. Handwrite the full estar conjugation three times, saying each form aloud, and put the accent marks in.
2. For each sentence, decide ser or estar, then write the full correct sentence by hand:
   - Yo ___ de México. (origin)
   - El libro ___ en la mesa. (location)
   - Nosotros ___ cansados. (condition)
   - Ella ___ médica. (profession)
   - ¿Cómo ___ tú? (condition)
   - La casa ___ grande. (characteristic)

### Speak and record (10 min)
Record yourself saying: where you are right now, how you are feeling, where two objects are located, and one sentence using ser and one using estar about the same thing to feel the contrast.

### Self-check
1. Conjugate estar for all six persons.
2. What are estar's two main jobs?
3. State the one-line rule for choosing ser vs. estar.
4. "I am from Mexico" vs. "I am in Mexico": which verb for each?
5. Explain the difference between "es aburrido" and "está aburrido."

# Day 6: Numbers, Telling Time, and Quiz 1

### Objectives
- Count from 0 to 100.
- Tell the time.
- Take Quiz 1.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Count aloud from 1 to 20, twice. Then say the tens: diez, veinte, treinta, cuarenta, cincuenta, sesenta, setenta, ochenta, noventa, cien.

### Lecture

**Numbers 0 to 100** are in Reference section 5. A few points worth saying out loud:
- 0 to 15 are their own words. 16 to 19 are written as one word built on "ten and...": dieciséis, diecisiete, dieciocho, diecinueve.
- The twenties are one word: veintiuno, veintidós, veintitrés.
- From 30 on, you join tens and units with **y**: treinta y uno, cuarenta y cinco, noventa y nueve.
- **uno** shortens to **un** before a masculine noun (un amigo, veintiún años) and becomes **una** before a feminine noun (una mesa).

**Telling time** uses ser, plus a quirk: it is singular for one o'clock and plural for everything else, because you are really saying "it is one hour" or "it is X hours."
- *¿Qué hora es?* (What time is it?)
- *Es la una.* (It is one o'clock.) Singular, with "la."
- *Son las dos. Son las tres.* (It is two/three o'clock.) Plural, with "las."
- *Son las cuatro y media.* (4:30, "and half.")
- *Son las cinco y cuarto.* (5:15, "and quarter.")
- *Son las seis y diez.* (6:10, "and ten.")
- *Son las siete menos cuarto.* (6:45, "seven minus a quarter.")
- To say at what time something happens, use **a**: *El trabajo empieza a las ocho.* (Work starts at eight.)
- Add *de la mañana* (a.m.), *de la tarde* (afternoon), or *de la noche* (night) to be clear.

### Write by hand (15 min)
1. Write the numbers 0 to 20 in words, once.
2. Write these numbers in words: 21, 35, 48, 60, 77, 99, 100.
3. Write out five clock times in full Spanish sentences (for example, 1:00, 2:30, 4:15, 7:45, 9:10).

### Quiz 1
Take **Quiz 1** now (see `QUIZZES.md`), closed-book. Grade against `ANSWER-KEYS.md`. It covers pronunciation and stress, greetings and tú/usted, nouns and articles, ser and estar, and numbers and time, the whole Week 1 core. If you score below 90%, re-study the weak spot on tomorrow's review day.

### Self-check
1. Count from 11 to 20 from memory.
2. How do you join tens and units, as in 45?
3. Why is it "es la una" but "son las dos"?
4. How do you say 4:30 and 6:45?
5. How do you say "at eight o'clock"?
