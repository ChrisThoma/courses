# Week 2: Regular Verbs & Sentence Structure

> **The thesis of this week:** verbs are the engine of every sentence, and Spanish verbs follow patterns you can learn once and apply forever. This week you learn to conjugate the three regular verb families (-ar, -er, -ir) in the present tense, then add the tools that turn single verbs into real conversation: questions, negation, adjectives that agree, and the two must-have verbs tener (to have) and ir (to go). By Sunday you can say what you, and anyone else, do, want, have, and are going to do. Conjugate out loud and by hand every single day; this is the week that repetition pays off most.

New verbs are shown with their full conjugation in the [Reference file](SUPPLEMENT-reference-and-vocabulary.md) section 4. Keep it open.

# Day 8: Regular -ar Verbs and Word Order

### Objectives
- Conjugate any regular -ar verb in the present tense.
- Build a basic Spanish sentence in the right order.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say the conjugation of hablar aloud, twice: hablo, hablas, habla, hablamos, habláis, hablan. Then say: Hablo español. ¿Hablas inglés? Ella trabaja aquí. Nosotros necesitamos ayuda.

### Lecture

Most Spanish verbs are regular, which means they follow a fixed pattern. To conjugate a regular **-ar verb**, drop the -ar and add the ending for the person:

| | hablar (to speak) |
|---|---|
| yo | habl**o** |
| tú | habl**as** |
| él/ella/usted | habl**a** |
| nosotros | habl**amos** |
| vosotros | habl**áis** |
| ellos/ellas/ustedes | habl**an** |

That is it. The same six endings (-o, -as, -a, -amos, -áis, -an) work for thousands of -ar verbs. Learn the pattern once and you can use every -ar verb you ever meet. High-frequency -ar verbs to drill this week: **trabajar** (to work), **necesitar** (to need), **ayudar** (to help), **hablar** (to speak), **llamar** (to call), **estudiar** (to study), **comprar** (to buy), **escuchar** (to listen), **mirar** (to look at), **esperar** (to wait or to hope).

**Spanish present tense covers three English meanings.** *Hablo español* can mean "I speak Spanish," "I am speaking Spanish," or "I do speak Spanish," depending on context. Spanish does not need a separate "am ...ing" form for most situations. That makes early speaking simpler than it is in English.

**Word order.** A basic statement runs **subject, verb, object**, much like English, but the subject is usually dropped:
- *(Yo) necesito ayuda.* (I need help.)
- *María trabaja en una oficina.* (María works in an office.)
- *Nosotros estudiamos español.* (We study Spanish.)

The big difference comes later with object pronouns and adjective placement; for now, build subject + verb + the rest, and remember you can leave the subject pronoun out.

### Write by hand (20 min)
1. Handwrite the full present-tense conjugation of **trabajar** and **necesitar**, three times each, saying each form aloud.
2. Write one true sentence with each of these verbs about your real life: hablar, trabajar, estudiar, necesitar, ayudar.

### Speak and record (10 min)
Record yourself conjugating hablar out loud from memory, then saying five sentences about what you and people you know do, using five different -ar verbs.

### Self-check
1. What are the six present-tense endings for -ar verbs?
2. How do you conjugate "to work" for "we"?
3. What three English meanings can "hablo" carry?
4. What is the basic Spanish word order for a statement?
5. Why can you usually leave out the subject pronoun?

# Day 9: Regular -er and -ir Verbs

### Objectives
- Conjugate regular -er and -ir verbs.
- Notice exactly where the two patterns differ.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say comer and vivir aloud, twice each: como, comes, come, comemos, coméis, comen; vivo, vives, vive, vivimos, vivís, viven. Then: Como en casa. ¿Dónde vives? Ella vive en Texas. Nosotros comemos a la una.

### Lecture

The other two verb families are **-er** and **-ir**. Drop the ending and add:

| | comer (to eat) | vivir (to live) |
|---|---|---|
| yo | com**o** | viv**o** |
| tú | com**es** | viv**es** |
| él/ella/usted | com**e** | viv**e** |
| nosotros | com**emos** | viv**imos** |
| vosotros | com**éis** | viv**ís** |
| ellos/ellas/ustedes | com**en** | viv**en** |

Here is the helpful shortcut: **-er and -ir verbs are identical except for the nosotros and vosotros forms.** -er has -emos and -éis; -ir has -imos and -ís. Everywhere else they match. And notice the **yo** form ends in -o for all three families (hablo, como, vivo), so a third of the work is already familiar.

High-frequency -er and -ir verbs to drill: **comer** (to eat), **beber** (to drink), **leer** (to read), **aprender** (to learn), **vender** (to sell), **comprender** (to understand); **vivir** (to live), **escribir** (to write), **abrir** (to open), **recibir** (to receive), **decidir** (to decide).

You now have all three regular patterns. Between them they cover the large majority of Spanish verbs. The irregular ones (next: tener and ir) are common enough that you memorize them individually, but the regular machine you just learned does most of the lifting forever.

### Write by hand (20 min)
1. Handwrite the full conjugation of **comer** and **vivir**, three times each, out loud.
2. Write one true sentence each with comer, beber, leer, vivir, escribir.
3. Mixed drill: write the correct form for each: nosotros (comer), tú (vivir), ella (aprender), yo (escribir), ustedes (leer), él (beber).

### Speak and record (10 min)
Record yourself saying eight sentences mixing -ar, -er, and -ir verbs about your daily life: what you eat, drink, read, write, where you live, what you study, and so on.

### Self-check
1. Give the -er and -ir endings for all six persons.
2. Where exactly do the -er and -ir patterns differ?
3. What ending does the yo form take for all three verb families?
4. Conjugate "to write" for "I" and for "they."
5. Name two -er verbs and two -ir verbs.

# Day 10: Asking Questions

### Objectives
- Form yes/no questions.
- Use the main question words.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say each question word aloud with an example: ¿Qué? (¿Qué necesitas?), ¿Quién? (¿Quién es?), ¿Dónde? (¿Dónde vives?), ¿Cuándo? (¿Cuándo trabajas?), ¿Por qué? (¿Por qué?), ¿Cómo? (¿Cómo estás?), ¿Cuánto? (¿Cuánto cuesta?).

### Lecture

Questions in Spanish are wonderfully simple compared with English, because you do not need a helper word like "do."

**Yes/no questions** are made just by changing your intonation, or by inverting the subject and verb. No "do" is needed:
- Statement: *Tú hablas español.*
- Question: *¿Hablas español?* or *¿Hablas tú español?*

Your voice rises at the end, exactly as in English. And remember the opening **¿** so the reader knows a question is coming.

**Question words** (Reference section 6). Each starts the question and carries a written accent that distinguishes it from the same word used in other ways:

| Word | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| ¿Qué? | What? | ¿Qué necesita usted? |
| ¿Quién? / ¿Quiénes? | Who? (sing./plural) | ¿Quién es el jefe? |
| ¿Dónde? | Where? | ¿Dónde está la oficina? |
| ¿Adónde? | (to) Where? | ¿Adónde vas? |
| ¿Cuándo? | When? | ¿Cuándo trabajas? |
| ¿Por qué? | Why? | ¿Por qué necesitas ayuda? |
| ¿Cómo? | How? | ¿Cómo se llama? |
| ¿Cuánto/a/os/as? | How much/many? | ¿Cuánto cuesta? ¿Cuántos años tienes? |
| ¿Cuál? / ¿Cuáles? | Which? | ¿Cuál es tu teléfono? |

After a question word, the verb usually comes right next, then the subject if you state it: *¿Dónde trabaja María?* (Where does María work?). Note "por qué" (why, two words with accent) is the question, and "porque" (because, one word, no accent) is the answer. Keep them straight.

### Write by hand (20 min)
1. Copy the question-word table once.
2. Write eight questions of your own, one with each question word, about a coworker or friend.
3. Turn these four statements into yes/no questions in writing: Tú trabajas aquí. / Ella habla inglés. / Ustedes viven en la ciudad. / Él necesita ayuda.

### Speak and record (10 min)
Record yourself asking ten questions out loud that you might really ask someone you work or interact with: their name, where they are from, where something is, when they work, what they need, and so on.

### Self-check
1. How do you turn a statement into a yes/no question?
2. Do you need a word like "do" to ask a question in Spanish?
3. List six question words with their meanings.
4. What is the difference between "por qué" and "porque"?
5. Where does the verb usually go after a question word?

# Day 11: Negation and Adjective Agreement

### Objectives
- Make any sentence negative.
- Make adjectives agree in gender and number, and place them correctly.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say: No hablo francés. No, no trabajo hoy. La casa es grande. Las casas son grandes. El amigo es bueno. Los amigos son buenos.

### Lecture

**Negation is the easiest grammar point in the language: put no before the verb.** That is the whole rule.
- *Hablo español* to *No hablo español.*
- *Ella trabaja aquí* to *Ella no trabaja aquí.*

To answer a yes/no question in the negative, you often say "no" twice, just as English doubles up differently: *¿Trabajas hoy? No, no trabajo hoy.* The first "no" means "no," the second is the grammatical negation before the verb.

Spanish also uses **double negatives**, which are correct and required, not an error: *No necesito nada* (I don't need anything, literally "I don't need nothing"), *No veo a nadie* (I don't see anyone). Keep the "no" before the verb even when another negative word follows.

**Adjective agreement.** Unlike English, Spanish adjectives change to match the noun in **gender** and **number**:
- Adjectives ending in **-o** have four forms: bueno, buena, buenos, buenas. *el amigo bueno, la amiga buena, los amigos buenos, las amigas buenas.*
- Adjectives ending in **-e** or a consonant usually do not change for gender, only for number: grande, grandes; fácil, fáciles. *el trabajo grande, la casa grande, los trabajos grandes.*

**Placement.** The normal position of a descriptive adjective is **after** the noun, the opposite of English:
- *una casa grande* (a big house), *un coche nuevo* (a new car), *una mujer inteligente* (an intelligent woman).
- A few common adjectives can go before the noun for nuance, and quantity words (mucho, poco, otro) go before, but the safe default you should drill is **noun then adjective.**

### Write by hand (20 min)
1. Make these six sentences negative, written out: Hablo francés. / Ella trabaja los domingos. / Tenemos tiempo. / Ellos viven aquí. / Necesito el coche.
2. Write each noun phrase in all four agreement forms (masc. sing., fem. sing., masc. plural, fem. plural) using the adjective shown: amigo (bueno); casa (pequeño); coche (nuevo).
3. Write four noun-plus-adjective phrases of your own, adjective correctly placed and agreeing.

### Speak and record (10 min)
Record yourself describing four people or things with correctly agreeing adjectives, then saying four negative sentences about what you do not do or do not have.

### Self-check
1. Where do you put "no" to make a sentence negative?
2. Are double negatives correct in Spanish? Give an example.
3. How many forms does an adjective ending in -o have? Name them for "bueno."
4. Where does a descriptive adjective normally go, relative to the noun?
5. Make "una casa" plus "grande" agree, then make it plural.

# Day 12: tener (to have) and Its Expressions

### Objectives
- Conjugate tener.
- Use tener for possession, for age, and in fixed expressions.
- Use tener que plus an infinitive for obligation.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say tener aloud, twice: tengo, tienes, tiene, tenemos, tenéis, tienen. Then: Tengo dos hermanos. ¿Cuántos años tienes? Tengo hambre. Tengo que trabajar.

### Lecture

**tener (to have)** is irregular and constant, so memorize it now (Reference section 4):

| yo tengo | tú tienes | él/ella/usted tiene |
| nosotros tenemos | vosotros tenéis | ellos/ellas/ustedes tienen |

**Its basic job is possession:** *Tengo un coche. Tenemos tiempo. ¿Tienes el teléfono?*

**But Spanish uses tener where English uses "to be," and these are extremely common:**
- **Age:** *Tengo treinta años* (I am thirty, literally "I have thirty years"). *¿Cuántos años tienes?* (How old are you?)
- **Physical states:** tener hambre (to be hungry), tener sed (to be thirsty), tener frío (to be cold), tener calor (to be hot), tener sueño (to be sleepy), tener miedo (to be afraid), tener prisa (to be in a hurry).
  - *Tengo hambre. ¿Tienes frío? Ella tiene prisa.*
- **tener razón** (to be right): *Tienes razón.*

**tener que plus an infinitive means "to have to" do something,** and it is one of the most useful patterns in everyday speech:
- *Tengo que trabajar.* (I have to work.)
- *Tenemos que hablar con el cliente.* (We have to speak with the client.)
- *Tienes que esperar un momento.* (You have to wait a moment.)

The infinitive (the unconjugated -ar/-er/-ir form) follows "tener que" unchanged. Only tener is conjugated.

### Write by hand (20 min)
1. Handwrite the full conjugation of tener three times.
2. Write a sentence for each: your age, one thing you possess, one tener expression about how you feel right now, and two "tengo que..." sentences about what you have to do today.
3. Translate: "She has two children." / "We are hungry." / "Do you (informal) have time?" / "They have to work."

### Speak and record (10 min)
Record yourself saying your age, three things you have, two physical states using tener, and three things you have to do today with tener que.

### Self-check
1. Conjugate tener for all six persons.
2. How do you say your age in Spanish, literally?
3. Give three tener expressions for physical states.
4. What does "tener que" plus an infinitive mean?
5. In "Tengo que trabajar," which word is conjugated and which stays in the infinitive?

# Day 13: ir (to go), the Near Future, and Quiz 2

### Objectives
- Conjugate ir.
- Use ir a plus an infinitive to talk about the future.
- Use the contractions al and del.
- Take Quiz 2.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say ir aloud, twice: voy, vas, va, vamos, vais, van. Then: Voy a casa. ¿Adónde vas? Vamos a trabajar. Ella va a llamar al cliente.

### Lecture

**ir (to go)** is short and very irregular, so memorize it whole:

| yo voy | tú vas | él/ella/usted va |
| nosotros vamos | vosotros vais | ellos/ellas/ustedes van |

**ir is normally followed by "a" to show destination:** *Voy a la oficina. Vamos al trabajo.*

**The contraction rule:** when **a** is followed by **el**, they combine into **al**. When **de** is followed by **el**, they combine into **del**. These two contractions are mandatory:
- a + el = **al**: *Voy al trabajo.* (not "a el")
- de + el = **del**: *Es el coche del jefe.* (not "de el")
- a + la, a + los, a + las do **not** contract: *Voy a la casa, a los amigos.*

**ir a plus an infinitive is how you talk about the near future,** and it is far more common in speech than the formal future tense (which you meet in SPAN-20):
- *Voy a trabajar mañana.* (I am going to work tomorrow.)
- *Vamos a comer.* (We are going to eat.) This also doubles as "Let's eat.")
- *Ella va a llamar al cliente.* (She is going to call the client.)

The pattern is: conjugated **ir** + **a** + the **infinitive**. Only ir changes; the second verb stays in its dictionary form. This single structure lets you talk about all your plans without learning a new tense.

### Write by hand (15 min)
1. Handwrite the conjugation of ir three times.
2. Write five "going to" sentences about your real plans for tomorrow using ir a plus an infinitive.
3. Practice the contraction: rewrite correctly: voy a + el banco; el libro de + el profesor; vamos a + la oficina; el coche de + el jefe.

### Quiz 2
Take **Quiz 2** now (see `QUIZZES.md`), closed-book. Grade against `ANSWER-KEYS.md`. It covers all three regular verb families, questions, negation, adjective agreement, tener, and ir, the whole Week 2 core. If you score below 90%, re-study the weak spot on tomorrow's review day.

### Self-check
1. Conjugate ir for all six persons.
2. What word normally follows ir to show destination?
3. Give the two mandatory contractions and when they apply.
4. How do you say "I am going to work tomorrow"?
5. In "Vamos a comer," which verb is conjugated and which is the infinitive?
