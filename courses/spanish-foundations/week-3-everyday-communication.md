# Week 3: Everyday Communication

> **The thesis of this week:** you have the grammar engine. This week you make it sound like a real person talking. You add the irregular and stem-changing verbs you reach for in every conversation (querer, poder, hacer), the gustar pattern for saying what you like, the language of time, dates, weather, and plans, and the practical, polite phrasing of everyday and on-the-job interaction. By the end you can hold a genuine short conversation and handle the situations that come up when you need to communicate with someone in Spanish regularly. # Day 15: Stem-Changing and Key Irregular Verbs

### Objectives
- Conjugate stem-changing verbs (the "boot" pattern).
- Use querer, poder, and hacer.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say querer and poder aloud: quiero, quieres, quiere, queremos, queréis, quieren; puedo, puedes, puede, podemos, podéis, pueden. Then: Quiero un café. ¿Puedes ayudarme? Ella hace el trabajo.

### Lecture

Some very common verbs are **stem-changing**: the vowel in the stem changes in every form except **nosotros** and **vosotros**. Because those two forms keep the original stem and the other four change, the changed forms make a boot shape on the conjugation grid. There is nothing to do but learn the pattern and the common verbs.

**e to ie** verbs, like **querer** (to want):

| quiero | quieres | quiere |
| queremos | queréis | quieren |

Notice queremos and queréis keep the e. Other e to ie verbs: **entender** (to understand), **pensar** (to think), **empezar** (to start), **preferir** (to prefer), **venir** (to come, also irregular in the yo form: vengo).

**o to ue** verbs, like **poder** (to be able, can):

| puedo | puedes | puede |
| podemos | podéis | pueden |

Other o to ue verbs: **dormir** (to sleep), **volver** (to return), **encontrar** (to find), **costar** (to cost, used mostly in third person: cuesta, cuestan).

**poder and querer are followed by an infinitive** to say what you want or can do:
- *Quiero hablar con el jefe.* (I want to speak with the boss.)
- *¿Puede esperar un momento?* (Can you wait a moment?)
- *No puedo trabajar mañana.* (I can't work tomorrow.)

**hacer (to do, to make)** is irregular only in the yo form (**hago**); the rest is regular -er: haces, hace, hacemos, hacéis, hacen. It is everywhere: *Hago mi trabajo. ¿Qué haces? Hace calor* (it is hot, in weather, covered Day 17).

### Write by hand (20 min)
1. Handwrite the full conjugations of querer, poder, and hacer, twice each, marking the boot for the two stem-changers (circle the four changed forms).
2. Write five sentences with querer or poder plus an infinitive about what you want or can/cannot do.
3. Write the yo and él forms of: pensar, dormir, volver, entender, empezar.

### Speak and record (10 min)
Record yourself saying: three things you want to do, two things you can and cannot do, and what you do (hacer) at work or at home.

### Self-check
1. In a stem-changing verb, which two forms do NOT change?
2. Conjugate querer (e to ie) fully.
3. Conjugate poder (o to ue) fully.
4. What follows poder and querer to say what you can or want to do?
5. What is irregular about hacer, and in which form only?

# Day 16: gustar and Saying What You Like

### Objectives
- Use the gustar pattern correctly.
- Express likes and dislikes for things and activities.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say: Me gusta el café. Me gustan los libros. ¿Te gusta el trabajo? No me gusta esperar. Nos gusta hablar español.

### Lecture

**gustar does not work like the English "to like," and trying to translate word for word will break it.** gustar literally means "to be pleasing." So "I like coffee" is really "Coffee is pleasing to me": *Me gusta el café.* The thing you like is the subject, and you are the receiver.

This means two things change from English:
1. You use a small **indirect object pronoun** to say to whom it is pleasing:

| me gusta | it is pleasing to me (I like it) |
| te gusta | to you (informal) |
| le gusta | to him/her/you formal |
| nos gusta | to us |
| os gusta | to you all (Spain) |
| les gusta | to them/you all |

2. The verb is **gusta** (singular) or **gustan** (plural) depending on **the thing liked**, not on you:
- One thing, or an activity: **gusta**. *Me gusta el café. Me gusta trabajar aquí.* (With one or more verbs in the infinitive, always use gusta: *Me gusta leer y escribir.*)
- More than one thing: **gustan**. *Me gustan los libros. ¿Te gustan los gatos?*

To say you do **not** like something, put **no** before the pronoun: *No me gusta el frío. No me gustan los lunes.*

To emphasize or clarify who, you can add **a** plus the person at the front: *A mí me gusta el café. A ella le gusta el trabajo. A mi jefe le gustan los detalles.* The "a..." part is clarification; the pronoun (me, le...) is still required.

A few useful relatives that work the same way: **encantar** (to love something: *Me encanta el español*), **interesar** (to interest), **importar** (to matter), and **doler** (to hurt: *Me duele la cabeza*, my head hurts).

### Write by hand (20 min)
1. Copy the me/te/le/nos/les chart once.
2. Write six sentences: three things you like (mix singular and plural so you practice gusta vs. gustan) and three you do not like.
3. Write two sentences about what someone else likes using "A ... le gusta(n)..."

### Speak and record (10 min)
Record yourself saying five things you like and three you dislike, then ask three "¿Te gusta...?" or "¿Le gusta...?" questions you might ask a real person.

### Self-check
1. What does gustar literally mean?
2. When do you use gusta, and when gustan?
3. Which form do you use with an activity, like "to read"?
4. How do you make it negative?
5. What does the "a mí / a ella" part add, and is the pronoun still needed?

# Day 17: Days, Dates, Weather & Making Plans

### Objectives
- Say the days, months, and dates.
- Describe the weather.
- Make a simple plan with someone.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say the days: lunes, martes, miércoles, jueves, viernes, sábado, domingo. Then: Hoy es lunes. Hace frío. Hace sol. Vamos a comer el viernes.

### Lecture

**The days of the week** (not capitalized in Spanish): lunes, martes, miércoles, jueves, viernes, sábado, domingo. The Spanish week starts on Monday. To say "on Monday" you use **el**: *Trabajo el lunes* (I work on Monday). For "on Mondays" in general, use **los**: *No trabajo los domingos.*

**The months** (also not capitalized): enero, febrero, marzo, abril, mayo, junio, julio, agosto, septiembre, octubre, noviembre, diciembre.

**Dates** use the pattern: el + number + de + month. *Hoy es el quince de marzo.* (Today is March 15th.) Spanish uses plain numbers for dates, not "fifteenth," except the first, which is often **primero**: *el primero de enero.* The full pattern: *¿Cuál es la fecha? Es el diez de octubre.*

**Weather mostly uses hacer** ("it makes..."), plus a couple with estar and hay:
- *Hace calor.* (It is hot.) *Hace frío.* (It is cold.) *Hace sol.* (It is sunny.) *Hace viento.* (It is windy.) *Hace buen tiempo / mal tiempo.* (The weather is good / bad.)
- *Está nublado.* (It is cloudy.) *Llueve.* (It is raining, from llover.) *Nieva.* (It is snowing, from nevar.)
- *¿Qué tiempo hace?* (What is the weather like?)

**Making a plan** combines tools you already have: ir a plus an infinitive, plus a day or time:
- *¿Vamos a comer el viernes?* (Shall we eat on Friday?)
- *¿Puedes el lunes a las dos?* (Can you on Monday at two?)
- *Voy a llamar mañana.* (I am going to call tomorrow.)
- *Nos vemos el jueves.* (See you Thursday.)

### Write by hand (20 min)
1. Write the seven days and the twelve months once each.
2. Write today's date and three other dates in full Spanish sentences.
3. Write four weather sentences for four different conditions.
4. Write a three-line plan-making exchange (propose a day and time, respond).

### Speak and record (10 min)
Record yourself saying today's day and date, the current weather, and a short proposal to meet someone on a specific day and time.

### Self-check
1. What day does the Spanish week start on, and are days capitalized?
2. How do you say "on Mondays" (in general)?
3. Give the date pattern, and what is special about the first of the month.
4. Which verb does most weather use? Give three examples.
5. How would you propose meeting someone on Friday at three?

# Day 18: Practical & Workplace Spanish

### Objectives
- Use the high-frequency vocabulary and phrases of real everyday and on-the-job communication.
- Make polite requests and check understanding.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say survival phrases 9 through 25 from Reference section 7 aloud, twice each. These are the backbone of getting through a real interaction.

### Lecture

This is the day the course points squarely at communicating with people you deal with regularly. Most real interaction is built from a small set of moves: greeting, requesting, confirming, clarifying, and thanking. You already have the grammar; today you assemble the practical phrasing.

**Polite requests.** The softest, most reliable request frame is a question with poder, plus an infinitive:
- *¿Puede ayudarme, por favor?* (Can you help me, please?)
- *¿Puede repetir, por favor?* (Can you repeat, please?)
- *¿Puede esperar un momento?* (Can you wait a moment?)
- *Necesito... / Quiero..., por favor.* (I need... / I want..., please.) Adding por favor keeps "quiero" from sounding blunt.

**Checking and repairing understanding** is the most important skill when you are still learning, and asking for it is normal and welcome, not embarrassing:
- *No entiendo.* (I don't understand.)
- *Más despacio, por favor.* (Slower, please.)
- *¿Cómo se dice ... en español?* (How do you say ... in Spanish?)
- *¿Qué significa ...?* (What does ... mean?)
- *¿Puede escribirlo?* (Can you write it down?)
- *Un momento, por favor.* (One moment, please.)

**Practical vocabulary worth drilling** (built on the Reference lists): el trabajo (work), la oficina (office), el cliente (client), el jefe / la jefa (boss), el compañero / la compañera (coworker), la reunión (meeting), la pregunta (question), la respuesta (answer), el problema (problem, masculine despite the -a), la ayuda (help), el horario (schedule), hoy / mañana / la semana (today / tomorrow / the week).

**A short workplace exchange you can build right now:**

> A: Buenos días. ¿Cómo está?
> B: Bien, gracias. ¿En qué puedo ayudar?
> A: Necesito hablar con el jefe, por favor.
> B: Un momento. ¿Cómo se llama usted?
> A: Me llamo... Gracias por su ayuda.

### Write by hand (20 min)
1. Copy survival phrases 9 through 25 once each, by hand, with all accents and question marks.
2. Write a six-line dialogue set in a situation where you actually need Spanish (a request, a clarification, a thank-you), using usted throughout.

### Speak and record (10 min)
Record yourself running through the six-line workplace exchange above, both parts, then your own dialogue from the writing task. Aim for clear, polite usted forms.

### Self-check
1. What is the softest frame for a polite request?
2. Why is "por favor" important after "quiero"?
3. Give three phrases for repairing understanding.
4. What gender is "el problema," and why is it a trap?
5. How do you ask "How do you say ... in Spanish?"

# Day 19: Simple Commands and Connectors

### Objectives
- Form and use the basic affirmative usted command.
- Use connectors to link ideas into longer, smoother speech.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Say: Espere un momento, por favor. Pase. Hable más despacio. Then connectors: y, pero, porque, también, entonces, por eso.

### Lecture

You do not need the full command system yet (that is a SPAN-20 topic), but a few **polite usted commands** are so useful in real interaction that they are worth a clean introduction now.

**To form a regular affirmative usted command, take the yo form, drop the -o, and add the opposite vowel:** -ar verbs take **-e**, and -er/-ir verbs take **-a**.
- hablar to hablo to **hable** (speak): *Hable más despacio.*
- esperar to espero to **espere** (wait): *Espere un momento.*
- comer to como to **coma** (eat): *Coma, por favor.*
- escribir to escribo to **escriba** (write): *Escriba aquí, por favor.*

A few high-frequency commands are irregular and you simply learn them: **pase** (come in / go ahead), **siga** (continue / go on), **disculpe** (excuse me / pardon), **perdone** (forgive me), **dígame** (tell me / go ahead). Adding **por favor** keeps any command warm and polite. For "let's...," you already have it: **vamos a** plus an infinitive (*Vamos a empezar*, let's start).

**Connectors turn choppy sentences into real speech.** A beginner who strings ideas with these sounds far more fluent than one who stops after each short sentence:
- **y** (and), **o** (or), **pero** (but), **porque** (because), **también** (also), **además** (besides), **entonces** (so / then), **por eso** (that is why), **primero / luego / después** (first / then / afterward), **por ejemplo** (for example).
- *Trabajo aquí, pero vivo en otra ciudad, entonces necesito el coche.* (I work here, but I live in another city, so I need the car.)

### Write by hand (20 min)
1. Form the usted command of: hablar, esperar, mirar, comer, escribir, abrir, and write each in a short polite sentence with por favor.
2. Write three longer sentences that each use at least two connectors to link ideas.

### Speak and record (10 min)
Record yourself giving five polite commands you might really need, then saying three connected, multi-clause sentences about your work or week using connectors.

### Self-check
1. How do you form a regular affirmative usted command from the yo form?
2. Give the usted command of hablar and of comer.
3. Name three irregular high-frequency commands.
4. How do you say "let's start"?
5. Name five connectors and what they do.

# Day 20: Putting It Together & Project 3

### Objectives
- Combine everything into a sustained, real conversation.
- Begin Project 3.

### Listen and say (5 min)
Read the model conversation below aloud twice, playing both roles, before you study it.

### Lecture

Everything in this course exists to let you do what happens below: a normal exchange, built entirely from tools you now have. Read it, then notice which day each piece came from.

> A: Buenas tardes. ¿Cómo está usted?
> B: Muy bien, gracias. ¿Y usted?
> A: Bien. Me llamo Laura. Trabajo aquí los lunes y los miércoles.
> B: Mucho gusto, Laura. Soy Daniel. ¿De dónde es usted?
> A: Soy de Chicago, pero ahora vivo aquí. ¿Puedo hacerle una pregunta?
> B: Claro. Dígame.
> A: ¿A qué hora empieza la reunión mañana? Tengo que llamar a un cliente primero.
> B: Empieza a las nueve. ¿Necesita ayuda con algo?
> A: No, gracias. Solo quiero estar preparada. Me gusta llegar temprano.
> B: Muy bien. Entonces, nos vemos mañana a las nueve.
> A: Perfecto. Gracias por su ayuda. Que tenga un buen día.

Read it again and label, in your notebook, where each structure comes from: ser and estar, present-tense verbs, tener que, ir a / nos vemos, gustar, a polite command (dígame), question words, time, and connectors. That single conversation touches almost every day of the course. Once you can point to each piece, you can see that what you have is a working system, not a pile of separate facts.

**Strategies when you get stuck mid-conversation**, which you will:
- **Buy time:** *Un momento... / Pues... / A ver...* (Let's see...)
- **Ask for help:** *¿Cómo se dice...? / No sé la palabra.*
- **Talk around the word (circumlocution):** if you do not know "stapler," say *la cosa para los papeles* (the thing for papers). Describing your way to the meaning is a real skill and keeps you talking.
- **Slow them down:** *Más despacio, por favor.* People are almost always glad to help someone making the effort.

### Write by hand (15 min)
Write a new ten-line conversation of your own, by hand, set in a situation you actually face, using at least: one ser and one estar, two different present-tense verbs, one tener que, one ir a or plan, one gustar, and two connectors. Put in all accents and question marks.

### Project 3
Begin **Project 3: A Real Exchange** (see `PROJECTS.md`): you will record yourself performing a realistic conversation (both parts, or with a partner if you have one), and turn in the handwritten script. Due with the final materials.

### Self-check
1. Pick three lines of the model conversation and name which day's grammar each uses.
2. Give two phrases for buying time when you are stuck.
3. What is circumlocution, and why is it useful?
4. How do you politely ask someone to slow down?
5. What is the value of writing the conversation by hand before recording it?

### Where to go next
You now have a correct foundation: the sounds, the present tense, the core verbs, and the ability to hold a simple conversation. To keep it growing:
- **Use it weekly with a real person.** Nothing else comes close. Find one Spanish speaker you can exchange even five minutes with regularly, and use the survival phrases to keep yourself in the conversation.
- **Keep the daily habit.** Ten minutes of spaced-repetition review and one out-loud conversation with yourself beats long, rare cramming.
- **Move on to SPAN-20: Intermediate Conversation & Grammar**, which adds the past tenses, object pronouns, commands in full, the future and conditional, and the subjunctive, the structures that take you from simple present-tense sentences to talking about anything, in any time frame.
