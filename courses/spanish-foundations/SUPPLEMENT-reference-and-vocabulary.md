## 1. The sounds of Spanish

Spanish spelling is almost perfectly regular. Once you know the rules below, you can read any written word aloud correctly. There are no silent surprises the way English has them.

### The five vowels (the most important thing on this page)

Each Spanish vowel has **one** clear, short, pure sound. It does not glide or change the way English vowels do. Keep them crisp and the same every time.

| Vowel | Sound | Like the | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **a** | "ah" | a in *father* | casa (house) |
| **e** | "eh" | e in *bet* | mesa (table) |
| **i** | "ee" | ee in *see* | sí (yes) |
| **o** | "oh" | o in *or*, short | hola (hello) |
| **u** | "oo" | oo in *food* | tú (you) |

The single most common beginner mistake is letting English creep in and turning these into gliding vowels (for example saying the *o* in "hola" like the English "oh-w"). Keep them pure and short.

### Consonants that differ from English

| Letter(s) | Sound | Notes | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **h** | silent | always silent | hola = "OH-lah" |
| **j** | harsh h | like clearing your throat | jugar = "hoo-GAR" |
| **g** before e, i | harsh h | same as j | gente = "HEN-teh" |
| **g** before a, o, u | hard g | as in *go* | gato = "GAH-toh" |
| **gu** before e, i | hard g | the u is silent | guitarra |
| **ñ** | "ny" | as in *canyon* | español, mañana |
| **ll** | "y" | a y sound in most of Latin America | llamar = "yah-MAR" |
| **y** | "y" | as a consonant; alone it means "and" | yo, ya |
| **rr** | trilled r | a rolled r; practice it slowly | perro (dog) |
| **r** | tapped r | a single quick tap, like the *tt* in American *butter*; trilled at the start of a word | pero (but), rojo |
| **v** | like b | Spanish v and b sound the same | vivir |
| **z** | "s" | an s sound in Latin America (a *th* in most of Spain) | zapato |
| **c** before e, i | "s" | an s sound in Latin America (a *th* in Spain) | cena, cinco |
| **c** before a, o, u | hard k | as in *cat* | casa |
| **qu** | hard k | the u is silent | queso = "KEH-soh" |

Everything else (b, d, f, k, l, m, n, p, s, t, plus the vowels) is close enough to English to start with.

## 2. Stress and written accents

Knowing where to put the stress is what makes you sound Spanish rather than foreign. There are only three rules, and they cover every word.

1. If a word ends in a **vowel, n, or s**, stress the **second-to-last** syllable.
   - casa = CA-sa, hablan = HA-blan, lunes = LU-nes
2. If a word ends in **any other consonant**, stress the **last** syllable.
   - hablar = ha-BLAR, ciudad = ciu-DAD, español = es-pa-ÑOL
3. If a word breaks rules 1 and 2, it carries a **written accent mark** that tells you exactly which syllable to stress. The accent always wins.
   - café = ca-FÉ, teléfono = te-LÉ-fo-no, también = tam-BIÉN

So the written accent is not decoration. It is a pronunciation instruction, and in some words it is the only thing separating two different words (for example **tú** "you" vs **tu** "your", or **él** "he" vs **el** "the"). When you handwrite Spanish in this course, **a missing accent is a spelling error**, marked wrong, every time.

Typing accents: on a phone, hold the vowel key and slide to the accented version. On a computer, switch to the Spanish or International keyboard, or learn your system's accent shortcut. The ñ and the accented vowels (á é í ó ú) and the opening question and exclamation marks (¿ ¡) all matter.

### The upside-down marks

Spanish opens questions and exclamations with an upside-down mark and closes them with the normal one:

> ¿Cómo estás? (How are you?)  ¡Hola! (Hi!)

They tell the reader a question or exclamation is coming. Use both, every time, in your handwritten work.

## 3. Subject pronouns

| Person | Spanish | English |
|---|---|---|
| 1st singular | **yo** | I |
| 2nd singular informal | **tú** | you (one person, familiar) |
| 2nd singular formal | **usted** | you (one person, polite) |
| 3rd singular | **él / ella** | he / she |
| 1st plural | **nosotros / nosotras** | we |
| 2nd plural (Spain) | **vosotros / vosotras** | you all (familiar, Spain) |
| 2nd / 3rd plural | **ustedes** | you all |
| 3rd plural | **ellos / ellas** | they |

Notes:
- **usted** and **ustedes** use the same verb forms as él/ella and ellos/ellas. They are "you" in meaning but "he/she/they" in grammar.
- **vosotros** is used mainly in Spain. In Latin America, **ustedes** covers all "you all." This course teaches the full set but drills the Latin American usage, so vosotros forms are shown for completeness and not required on quizzes.
- Spanish usually **drops the subject pronoun** because the verb ending already shows who is doing the action. "Hablo" already means "I speak." Use the pronoun for emphasis or to remove ambiguity.

## 4. Present-tense verb tables

### Regular verbs (the pattern)

Drop the -ar, -er, or -ir ending of the infinitive and add the endings below.

| | -ar: hablar (to speak) | -er: comer (to eat) | -ir: vivir (to live) |
|---|---|---|---|
| yo | habl**o** | com**o** | viv**o** |
| tú | habl**as** | com**es** | viv**es** |
| él/ella/usted | habl**a** | com**e** | viv**e** |
| nosotros | habl**amos** | com**emos** | viv**imos** |
| vosotros | habl**áis** | com**éis** | viv**ís** |
| ellos/ellas/ustedes | habl**an** | com**en** | viv**en** |

Notice that -er and -ir verbs differ only in the nosotros and vosotros forms.

### ser and estar (the two "to be" verbs)

| | ser (to be) | estar (to be) |
|---|---|---|
| yo | soy | estoy |
| tú | eres | estás |
| él/ella/usted | es | está |
| nosotros | somos | estamos |
| vosotros | sois | estáis |
| ellos/ellas/ustedes | son | están |

### Other high-frequency irregular verbs

| | tener (to have) | ir (to go) | hacer (to do/make) | querer (to want) | poder (to be able) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| yo | tengo | voy | hago | quiero | puedo |
| tú | tienes | vas | haces | quieres | puedes |
| él/ella/usted | tiene | va | hace | quiere | puede |
| nosotros | tenemos | vamos | hacemos | queremos | podemos |
| vosotros | tenéis | vais | hacéis | queréis | podéis |
| ellos/ellas/ustedes | tienen | van | hacen | quieren | pueden |

querer and poder are **stem-changing verbs**: the e becomes ie (querer) and the o becomes ue (poder) in every form except nosotros and vosotros. That boot-shaped pattern is covered on Day 15.

## 5. Numbers 0 to 100

0 cero, 1 uno, 2 dos, 3 tres, 4 cuatro, 5 cinco, 6 seis, 7 siete, 8 ocho, 9 nueve, 10 diez,
11 once, 12 doce, 13 trece, 14 catorce, 15 quince, 16 dieciséis, 17 diecisiete, 18 dieciocho, 19 diecinueve, 20 veinte,
21 veintiuno, 22 veintidós, 30 treinta, 31 treinta y uno, 40 cuarenta, 50 cincuenta, 60 sesenta, 70 setenta, 80 ochenta, 90 noventa, 100 cien.

From 31 on, the tens and units are joined with **y** (treinta y uno, cuarenta y dos). The teens and twenties are written as one word (dieciséis, veintidós).

## 6. Core vocabulary lists

Drill these with the spaced-repetition system in the [Practice Menu](DRILLS.md). Each noun is shown with its article so you learn its gender at the same time as the word. Never learn a noun without its gender.

### Greetings and courtesy
hola (hi), buenos días (good morning), buenas tardes (good afternoon), buenas noches (good evening/night), adiós (goodbye), hasta luego (see you later), por favor (please), gracias (thank you), de nada (you're welcome), perdón (sorry/excuse me), sí (yes), no (no), señor (sir/Mr.), señora (ma'am/Mrs.), señorita (miss).

### People and family
el hombre (man), la mujer (woman), el niño / la niña (boy/girl), el amigo / la amiga (friend), la familia (family), el padre (father), la madre (mother), el hijo / la hija (son/daughter), el hermano / la hermana (brother/sister), el jefe / la jefa (boss), el compañero / la compañera (coworker/companion), el cliente / la clienta (client).

### Common nouns
la casa (house), el trabajo (work/job), la comida (food/meal), el agua (water), el día (day), la noche (night), la cosa (thing), el tiempo (time/weather), el dinero (money), la palabra (word), el teléfono (phone), el coche / el carro (car), la calle (street), la puerta (door), la mesa (table), la silla (chair).

### High-frequency verbs (infinitives)
ser (to be), estar (to be), tener (to have), hacer (to do/make), ir (to go), hablar (to speak), comer (to eat), vivir (to live), trabajar (to work), querer (to want), poder (to be able), necesitar (to need), gustar (to be pleasing), ayudar (to help), entender (to understand), saber (to know a fact), conocer (to know a person), ver (to see), venir (to come), dar (to give).

### Useful adjectives (shown in masculine singular)
bueno (good), malo (bad), grande (big), pequeño (small), nuevo (new), viejo (old), bonito (pretty), feo (ugly), fácil (easy), difícil (difficult), contento (happy), cansado (tired), ocupado (busy), enfermo (sick), abierto (open), cerrado (closed).

### Question words
¿qué? (what), ¿quién? (who), ¿dónde? (where), ¿cuándo? (when), ¿por qué? (why), ¿cómo? (how), ¿cuánto? (how much), ¿cuál? (which).

### Glue words (small words that hold sentences together)
y (and), o (or), pero (but), porque (because), también (also), muy (very), mucho (a lot), un poco (a little), aquí (here), allí (there), ahora (now), hoy (today), mañana (tomorrow), con (with), sin (without), para (for/to), de (of/from), en (in/on/at), a (to/at).

## 7. The 30 survival phrases

Memorize these as whole chunks. They get you through a real interaction even before you can build the grammar yourself. Say each one aloud ten times this week.

1. Hola, ¿cómo está? (Hi, how are you? formal)
2. ¿Cómo estás? (How are you? informal)
3. Bien, gracias. ¿Y usted? (Fine, thanks. And you?)
4. Me llamo... (My name is...)
5. ¿Cómo se llama? (What is your name? formal)
6. Mucho gusto. (Nice to meet you.)
7. ¿De dónde es usted? (Where are you from?)
8. Soy de... (I am from...)
9. No entiendo. (I don't understand.)
10. ¿Puede repetir, por favor? (Can you repeat, please?)
11. Más despacio, por favor. (Slower, please.)
12. ¿Cómo se dice... en español? (How do you say... in Spanish?)
13. ¿Qué significa...? (What does... mean?)
14. No sé. (I don't know.)
15. No hablo mucho español todavía. (I don't speak much Spanish yet.)
16. ¿Habla inglés? (Do you speak English?)
17. Necesito ayuda. (I need help.)
18. ¿Me puede ayudar? (Can you help me?)
19. ¿Dónde está...? (Where is...?)
20. ¿Cuánto cuesta? (How much does it cost?)
21. Quiero... / Necesito... (I want... / I need...)
22. ¿Puede esperar un momento? (Can you wait a moment?)
23. Un momento, por favor. (One moment, please.)
24. Está bien. (It's fine. / Okay.)
25. Gracias por su ayuda. (Thank you for your help.)
26. Lo siento. (I'm sorry.)
27. Con permiso. (Excuse me, to pass by.)
28. ¿Está claro? / ¿Entiende? (Is it clear? / Do you understand?)
29. Hasta mañana. (See you tomorrow.)
30. Que tenga un buen día. (Have a good day. formal)
