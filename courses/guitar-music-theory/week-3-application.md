# Day 15 — Minor Keys: Three Flavors of Dark

### Objectives
- Build natural, harmonic, and melodic minor from any root.
- Know relative vs. parallel minor cold, and use the relative trick for instant key signatures.
- Derive the diatonic chords of a minor key, and explain the V-vs-v choice.

### Lecture

**Natural minor is the major scale started from its 6th degree.** Play C major from A to A (A B C D E F G) and you get **A natural minor**. Same seven notes, different home. That pair is called **relative**: every major key has a relative minor (its 6th degree), sharing its key signature.

```
Relative pairs: C↔Am  G↔Em  D↔Bm  A↔F#m  E↔C#m  F↔Dm  Bb↔Gm  Eb↔Cm
```

As a formula from the root: **natural minor = 1 2 b3 4 5 b6 b7** (W-H-W-W-H-W-W).

**Parallel minor** is a different comparison: same *root*, different scale. C major vs. **C minor** (C D Eb F G Ab Bb). Relative = same notes/different home; parallel = same home/different notes. Both matter: relative for key signatures and fretboard economy (same patterns!), parallel for "borrowing" (Day 19).

**Why three minors?** Natural minor has a problem: its 7th (b7) is a whole step below the tonic, so there's no leading tone, its v chord is *minor*, and the pull home is weak. Composers fixed it:

| Scale | Formula | Fix | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Natural** | 1 2 b3 4 5 b6 b7 | — | weak cadence |
| **Harmonic** | 1 2 b3 4 5 b6 **7** | raise 7 → real **V7** and leading tone | exotic 1½-step gap b6→7 |
| **Melodic** | 1 2 b3 4 5 **6 7** | also raise 6 → smooth melody line | sounds almost major except b3 |

Practical summary for the working guitarist: **think natural minor for riffs and key signatures; raise the 7th when you want a strong V→i cadence.** That's 90% of minor-key practice in rock, pop, folk, and metal.

**Diatonic chords of natural minor** (= the relative major's chords, renumbered from the new home):

```
i    ii°    bIII    iv    v    bVI    bVII
Am   B°     C       Dm    Em   F      G        (A minor)
```

With the harmonic-minor 7th, **v becomes V (or V7)**: Am–Dm–**E7**–Am is the classic minor cadence (flamenco, klezmer, metal, "House of the Rising Sun" territory).

### Examples — play these

**Example 15.1 — Relative pair on one pattern.** Play Day 3's root-6 major pattern at fret 3 (G major). Now loop a low E drone (let the open 6th string ring, or record one) and play the *same pattern* over it, phrasing your lines to land on E, the 6th degree, found inside the pattern at string 5 fret 7 and string 4 fret 2. Same notes, but it *is* E minor now. Home is a decision made by the harmony, not a pattern.

**Example 15.2 — A natural minor, open position:**

```
e|--------------------------0--
B|-----------------0--1--3-----
G|-----------0--2--------------
D|-----0--2--3-----------------
A|--0--2--3--------------------
E|------------------------------
   A  B  C  D  E  F  G  A ...
   1  2  b3 4  5  b6 b7 1
```

**Example 15.3 — The three minors from A, on the A string** (hear the differences):

```
Natural:   A|--0--2--3--5--7--8--10--12--
Harmonic:  A|--0--2--3--5--7--8--11--12--   (G→G#: hear the "snake-charmer" gap)
Melodic:   A|--0--2--3--5--7--9--11--12--   (F→F# and G→G#: almost major)
```

**Example 15.4 — Why we raise the 7th.** Play Am–Dm–Em–Am (all natural minor: soft, modal). Now Am–Dm–**E7**–Am. The E7 (with its G#) yanks you home. One borrowed semitone, total change of gravity.

### Exercises (Lab)

1. **(10 min)** Instant relative-minor drill: relative minor of D? F? E? Bb? A? Relative *major* of C#m? Gm? Bm? (Answer from the circle of fifths — the minor shares the major's signature.)
2. **(15 min)** Spell on paper, then play on one string: E natural minor · E harmonic minor · D natural minor · B harmonic minor · G melodic minor.
3. **(15 min)** Write the diatonic chords of E minor and of D minor (natural). Play each family. Then swap v→V7 and play i–iv–V7–i in both keys.
4. **(15 min)** Composition: write an 8-bar minor-key progression that uses bVII and bVI (the "Stairway"/"All Along the Watchtower" flavor: i–bVII–bVI...), ending with a V7→i cadence. Label everything; play it.
5. ★ Ear: have natural vs. harmonic minor scales played in random order; identify by the 7th degree.

### Self-check
1. Relative minor of Eb major? Key signature of F# minor?
2. Natural minor formula in degrees?
3. Why does harmonic minor exist? What single note does it change?
4. Diatonic chord built on b7 in A natural minor?

# Day 16 — Pentatonics and the Blues

### Objectives
- Build major and minor pentatonic scales; know their relationship to the full scales and to each other.
- Play minor pentatonic across the neck (all five boxes, with Box 1 mastered).
- Understand the 12-bar blues form and why blues breaks the diatonic rules beautifully.

### Lecture

**Pentatonic = the seven-note scale with the friction removed.** Delete the two half-step-creating notes and every remaining note sounds good over every chord of the key:

- **Major pentatonic = 1 2 3 5 6** (major scale minus 4 and 7)
- **Minor pentatonic = 1 b3 4 5 b7** (natural minor minus 2 and b6)

They're relatives, just like their parent scales: **A minor pentatonic (A C D E G) = C major pentatonic (C D E G A)**, the same five notes with a different home. One physical pattern, two musical meanings, depending on the chord underneath.

**Box 1, the most-played scale shape in guitar history** (A minor pentatonic, fret 5):

```
e|--5--8--
B|--5--8--
G|--5--7--
D|--5--7--
A|--5--7--
E|--5--8--
```

Root markers: string 6 fret 5, string 4 fret 7, string 1 fret 5. The other four boxes are this same five-note loop entered at each of its notes, and they connect like CAGED shapes (in fact each pentatonic box nests inside a CAGED shape).

**The blues scale** adds one outlaw note: **1 b3 4 b5 5 b7**. That added b5 (the "blue note") works as a passing sting between 4 and 5, almost never sat on.

**The 12-bar blues**, the most important form in American music:

```
| I7  | I7  | I7  | I7  |
| IV7 | IV7 | I7  | I7  |
| V7  | IV7 | I7  | V7  |   (last bar = "turnaround")
```

In A: A7 ×4 / D7 D7 A7 A7 / E7 D7 A7 E7.

**Here's the theory scandal:** Day 10 told you dominant 7ths occur diatonically *only* on V, yet blues puts a 7 on **every** chord, and melodies use the *minor* pentatonic over *major*-quality chords (b3 against a natural 3!). Far from an error, that clash **is the blues**. Lesson: the diatonic system is a default, not a law. Blues established a parallel tradition with its own rules, and rock inherited both.

**Soloing rules of thumb** (the distilled, practical version):
- Minor pentatonic of the key works over the whole 12-bar: the universal donor.
- Major pentatonic of the key works too (brighter, country-flavored), but avoid leaning on it over the IV7.
- The pros mix both and target **chord tones** of the chord of the moment (your Day 12 CAGED skill).

### Examples — play these

**Example 16.1 — One pattern, two souls.** Loop a low A drone; play Box 1 at fret 5 (A minor pentatonic, dark). Now loop a C drone; play the *exact same frets* (C major pentatonic, bright). Nothing changed but home.

**Example 16.2 — Box 1 + blue note (A blues scale):**

```
e|--5--8-----
B|--5--8-----
G|--5--7--8--   ← the b5 (Eb) at G string fret 8
D|--5--7-----
A|--5--6--7--   ← b5 again (Eb) at A string fret 6
E|--5--8-----
```

Play 4–b5–5 (D–Eb–E) as a slide; that gesture is half of blues vocabulary.

**Example 16.3 — The 12-bar in A.** Comp it with 7th-chord grips from Day 10 (A7 root-6 fret 5, D7 root-5 fret 5, E7 root-5 fret 7 — or open A7/D7/E7). Count bars out loud; the *form* must become bodily knowledge.

**Example 16.4 — Call and response.** Record (or loop) Example 16.3. Solo using only Box 1 with this constraint: 2-bar phrase, 2 bars of space, repeat. Phrasing beats note choice, today and forever.

### Exercises (Lab)

1. **(10 min)** Spell on paper: E minor pentatonic · G major pentatonic · B minor pentatonic · F major pentatonic. State each one's relative twin.
2. **(15 min)** Box 1 in four keys: Em (open/12), Gm (3), Am (5), Cm (8). Roots called out loud.
3. **(15 min)** Learn Box 2 (the next box up the neck from Box 1: frets 7–10 in A minor pent.) and practice crossing between Box 1 ↔ 2 mid-phrase.
4. **(20 min)** Play a full 12-bar blues in A *alone*: comp a chorus, solo a chorus (Example 16.4 constraint), comp, solo. Then move the whole thing to E.
5. ★ Target-tone drill: over the 12-bar, end every phrase on the *root of the current chord* (A over A7, D over D7, E over E7). This is the doorway to "playing the changes."

### Self-check
1. Which two degrees are removed to make each pentatonic, and why those?
2. A minor pentatonic and which major pentatonic are the same notes?
3. What's the added note in the blues scale, and how is it used?
4. Write out the 12-bar form in Roman numerals from memory.

# Day 17 — Modes, Demystified

### Objectives
- Understand modes as "the major scale with a different home" AND as standalone formulas.
- Know each mode's character note and a real-song reference.
- Play Dorian and Mixolydian over drones until you *hear* mode-ness.

### Lecture

**You already did this twice** (relative minor on Day 15, pentatonic twins on Day 16). Modes are the full generalization: take one major scale and let *each* of its seven notes be home. Seven homes, seven moods:

| Mode | Start on degree | Formula vs. major | Character note | Sound / reference |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Ionian** | 1 | — (it *is* major) | — | plain major |
| **Dorian** | 2 | 1 2 b3 4 5 **6** b7 | natural 6 in a minor scale | cool minor: "So What," "Oye Como Va," "Scarborough Fair" |
| **Phrygian** | 3 | 1 **b2** b3 4 5 b6 b7 | b2 | Spanish/metal darkness |
| **Lydian** | 4 | 1 2 3 **#4** 5 6 7 | #4 | floating major: *The Simpsons* theme, film scores |
| **Mixolydian** | 5 | 1 2 3 4 5 6 **b7** | b7 in a major scale | rock/folk major: "Norwegian Wood," "Royals" (verse), "Sweet Home Alabama" (famously debated D Mixolydian) |
| **Aeolian** | 6 | 1 2 b3 4 5 b6 b7 | b6 | = natural minor |
| **Locrian** | 7 | 1 b2 b3 4 **b5** b6 b7 | b5 | unstable; theoretical curiosity (fits over m7b5) |

Memory aid for the brightness spectrum, brightest → darkest: **Lydian, Ionian, Mixolydian, Dorian, Aeolian, Phrygian, Locrian** — each step flats exactly one more note.

**The two ways to think (you need both):**
1. **Derivative:** "D Dorian = the C major pattern, with D as home." Cheap to compute; great for the fretboard (you already know the patterns).
2. **Parallel:** "D Dorian = D natural minor with a raised 6." This is the one that lets you *hear* what makes the mode itself.

**The crucial caveat that saves you from the classic modal trap:** a mode only exists if the *harmony/drone* establishes its home. Playing C major scale shapes over a C chord is Ionian no matter where on the neck you play, and no matter which note you start the run on. Mode is decided by **what's underneath**, not by your starting fret. Practice modes over drones or vamps, never in silence.

**What guitarists actually use:** Dorian (minor funk/rock jams; think Santana, "Evil Ways" vamps), Mixolydian (dominant-chord rock and blues-major sounds), Aeolian and Ionian (already yours), Lydian and Phrygian as spices. Locrian: know why it's unstable (its "tonic" triad is diminished), then move on.

### Examples — play these

**Example 17.1 — One pattern, three homes.** Use the open-position C-major note pool. Loop these drones (record or let a low string ring) and play the pool over each:
- C drone → Ionian (sunny)
- D drone → Dorian (cool, funky minor)
- G drone → Mixolydian (rock major)

Land phrases on the drone's note. Same frets every time; three different worlds.

**Example 17.2 — Parallel comparison, the ear-opener.** Over an A drone:
- A Aeolian: A B C D E **F** G
- A Dorian: A B C D E **F#** G

One note, F vs F#: play both and *feel* Dorian's cool lift. Then:
- A Ionian: A B C# D E F# **G#**
- A Mixolydian: A B C# D E F# **G**

That G-natural is the entire sound of rock 'n' roll major.

**Example 17.3 — Modal vamps.** Two-chord loops that lock in a mode (record and jam):
- Dorian: **Am7 → D7** (over and over — "Oye Como Va")
- Mixolydian: **A → G/A** (or just A7 vamp)
- Lydian: **C → D/C** (C Lydian)

### Exercises (Lab)

1. **(10 min)** Derivative math: which major scale gives you E Dorian? A Mixolydian? F# Phrygian? Bb Lydian? (Answer pattern: "X mode of Y major.")
2. **(15 min)** Parallel spelling: spell D Dorian, E Mixolydian, G Lydian, B Phrygian from their formulas.
3. **(20 min)** Drone lab: A Dorian and A Mixolydian over an A drone, 5+ minutes each, deliberately featuring the character note in every phrase.
4. **(15 min)** Record the Dorian vamp (Am7–D7); jam over it with A Dorian. Then jam A *Aeolian* over the same vamp and hear the F clash against the D7's F#. That clash is why Dorian is "correct" here.
5. ★ Identify the mode: a song's notes are all natural, but every phrase resolves to D and the harmony vamps on Dm. (Answer it, then check Day 17's table.)

### Self-check
1. Mixolydian's formula and character note?
2. Two ways to define G Dorian?
3. Why doesn't starting a C-major run on E automatically make it Phrygian?
4. Which mode would you reach for over a one-chord funk vamp on Em7?

# Day 18 — Ear Training and Song Analysis

### Objectives
- Consolidate interval/chord-quality recognition into a repeatable practice system.
- Transcribe a real chord progression by ear, start to finish.
- Analyze three real songs with Roman numerals.

### Lecture

**Theory without ears is bookkeeping.** Today formalizes the ear work you've been doing in the margins all course.

**The recognition stack**, trained in this order because each layer supports the next:
1. **Intervals** (Day 2 song cues → eventually raw recognition). Melodic first, then harmonic (both notes at once).
2. **Chord quality:** maj / min / dim / aug, then maj7 / dom7 / m7 / m7b5.
3. **Scale degree hearing:** over a key drone, identify which degree a note is. (This is the skill that makes melodies transcribable: "do–mi–re…" feelings, even if you never use solfège names.)
4. **Progression hearing:** track the *bass* + the *function*: "home… away… tension… home." Most pop progressions are 4 chords from the Day 9 family; you're choosing from ~7 suspects, not infinity.

**The transcription algorithm** (use it in today's lab):
1. Find the key: locate the "home" chord by ear; hunt its root on string 6 or 5.
2. Decide major or minor home.
3. For each chord change: catch the **bass note** first (sing it, find it on string 6/5), then decide quality (maj/min), then assign the numeral.
4. Sanity-check against the diatonic family. A chord outside the family is a *finding*, not a failure. Name it (bVII? IV borrowed? V7-of-something? Day 19 covers these).

**Analysis = transcription + explanation.** A complete analysis of a song's section states: key · numerals · cadence type at each phrase end · anything non-diatonic, with its likely explanation. That's it. You now have every tool this requires.

### Examples

**Example 18.1 — Worked analysis: "House of the Rising Sun" (verse).**
Chords: Am–C–D–F–Am–C–E(7)–E(7) … Am–C–D–F–Am–**E7**–Am.
- Home is clearly Am → A minor.
- Numerals: i–bIII–IV–bVI–i–bIII–V7.
- Findings: the **IV is major** (D, not Dm — a Dorian flavor: that's the raised 6th, F#, inside D) and **V7 is major** (harmonic-minor move, Day 15). Cadence: authentic, V7→i.
- One sentence: *A-minor song using the Dorian IV for color and the harmonic-minor V7 for its cadence.* You can now write sentences like this. That's mastery talking.

**Example 18.2 — Degree hearing warm-up.** Loop a G drone. Play G major scale slowly; sing degree numbers. Then play random scale tones and name the degree by feel: 1 and 5 feel like pillars, 7 leans hard into 1, 4 leans down to 3.

### Exercises (Lab) — today is all lab

1. **(15 min)** Interval ear sprint: 20 random intervals (self-played eyes-closed or via any ear-training app), melodic, within an octave. Log your score; you'll re-test on Day 21.
2. **(10 min)** Chord-quality sprint: 16 random qualities from a fixed root. Log score.
3. **(15 min)** Degree hearing: Example 18.2 in G, then in E.
4. **(45 min)** Transcribe by ear, with the algorithm: one simple song you love (pick something strummy with ≤5 chords — pop, folk, country, classic rock). Write key + numerals. *Then* check published chords and reconcile every difference.
5. **(20 min)** Quick-analyze two more songs *from published chords* (no ear work): assign key, numerals, cadences, findings. These two + the transcription are the seed of Project 3.

### Daily drill (final form — keep this after the course ends)
10 min: 3 min note-finding ▸ 2 min interval ear ▸ 2 min chord-quality ear ▸ 3 min "recite the key": random key → signature, diatonic triads, relative minor.

### Self-check
1. What do you listen for *first* when transcribing a chord change?
2. A chord outside the diatonic family — failure or finding?
3. Which scale degrees feel like "pillars" and which feel like "leaners"?

# Day 19 — Beyond Diatonic: The Color Palette

### Objectives
- Build and use sus2/sus4, add9, 6th, slash chords, and extensions (9, 11, 13) without fear.
- Understand borrowed chords (modal interchange) and secondary dominants, the two most common "rule-breakers" in real songs.

### Lecture

You know the seven-chord family. Real songs constantly color outside it, in highly systematic ways. Two small ideas explain almost every "weird chord" you'll ever meet.

**First, the chord-name decoder ring** (all measured from the major scale, as always):

| Symbol | Recipe | Note |
|---|---|---|
| **sus4 / sus2** | replace the 3rd with 4 or 2 | no 3rd → neither major nor minor; "suspended," wants resolution (Dsus4→D, the most-strummed move in folk) |
| **add9** | triad + 9 (=2, an octave up) | keeps the 3rd (unlike sus2) |
| **6** | triad + 6 | C6 = C E G A; mellow ending chord |
| **9, 11, 13** | 7th chord + the extension (and implicitly those below it) | C9 = C7 + D. Quality of the *7th* carries through: Cmaj9 vs C9 vs Cm9 |
| **slash (C/E)** | chord / bass note | "C over E" — usually an inversion (Day 8!) or a pedal bass. Walks bass lines: C → G/B → Am |

Guitar reality check: with 6 strings you can't play every note of a 13th chord, and nobody does. **Keep root (or let the bass have it), 3rd, 7th, and the color note; drop the 5th first.** The 3rd and 7th define the chord; everything else is seasoning.

**Idea 1 — Borrowed chords (modal interchange):** a song in a major key may *borrow* any chord from the **parallel minor** (Day 15 paying off). The big four in C major:

| Borrowed | From C minor | Classic sound |
|---|---|---|
| **bVII** (Bb) | ✓ | classic rock: I–bVII–IV ("Sweet Home Alabama" region, "Hey Jude" outro) |
| **iv** (Fm) | ✓ | the heart-tug: IV→iv→I ("Creep," countless ballads) |
| **bVI** (Ab) | ✓ | epic lift: bVI–bVII–I (the "Mario ending" cadence) |
| **bIII** (Eb) | ✓ | bold rock slab |

**Idea 2 — Secondary dominants:** any diatonic chord can be preceded by *its own* V7, as if it were briefly the tonic. Notation: **V7/x** ("five-seven of x"). In C: A7→Dm is **V7/ii**; D7→G is **V7/V**; E7→Am is **V7/vi** (you already met this inside "House of the Rising Sun"!). The recipe: want to highlight chord x? Play the dominant 7th rooted a P5 above x first. The temporary leading tone creates a mini-cadence into x.

These two ideas + diatonic harmony cover the harmonic content of the overwhelming majority of popular music. (What's left is mostly local applications of the same dominant logic: tritone subs, diminished passing chords, true modulation. Chase them after the course.)

### Examples — play these

**Example 19.1 — The sus cycle:** D → Dsus4 → D → Dsus2 → D (frets: x-x-0-2-3-2 → ...-3-3 → ...-3-2 → ...-3-0 → back). The 3rd leaves, aches, returns.

**Example 19.2 — Slash-chord bass walk:** C → G/B → Am → Am/G → F (x-3-2-0-1-0 → x-2-0-0-0-3 → x-0-2-2-1-0 → 3-x-2-2-1-0 → 1-3-3-2-1-1). The bass walks C-B-A-G-F. This is "Landslide"/"Dust in the Wind" country.

**Example 19.3 — The borrowed-iv tearjerker:** C → F → **Fm** → C. Find Fm as fret-1 barre or x-x-3-1-1-1. One semitone (A→Ab) carries the entire emotion.

**Example 19.4 — Secondary dominant chain:** C → **A7** → Dm → **D7** → G7 → C  (I → V7/ii → ii → V7/V → V7 → I). Each dominant tips you into the next chord like dominoes.

**Example 19.5 — Extended-chord flavor:** play Dm7–G7–Cmaj7 (Day 10), then re-play as Dm9–G13–Cmaj9: Dm9 = x-5-3-5-5-x · G13 = 3-x-3-4-5-x · Cmaj9 = x-3-2-4-3-x. Same function, richer paint.

### Exercises (Lab)

1. **(10 min)** Decode on paper, then play any voicing: Asus4 · Gadd9 · E6 · Am9 · D/F# · Cmaj13 (which notes would you actually keep?).
2. **(15 min)** Borrowing lab: write a verse progression in G major that uses one borrowed chord (your choice of bVII=F, iv=Cm, bVI=Eb). Play it; describe the borrowed chord's effect in one sentence.
3. **(15 min)** Secondary-dominant lab: take I–vi–ii–V in C (C–Am–Dm–G7) and supercharge it: C–**E7**–Am–**A7**–Dm–**D7**–G7–C. Play both versions. Label every insertion as V7/x.
4. **(15 min)** Find them in the wild: revisit your Day 18 analyses; hunt for any sus, slash, borrowed, or secondary-dominant moments you couldn't name before. Name them now.
5. ★ Re-voice Example 19.4 on strings 1–4 only with minimal motion (voice-leading capstone).

### Self-check
1. sus2 vs add9 — what's the actual difference?
2. Where does a borrowed chord come from? Name the big four in a major key.
3. Recipe for a secondary dominant targeting chord x?
4. Which two chord tones are unskippable in an extended chord, and why?
