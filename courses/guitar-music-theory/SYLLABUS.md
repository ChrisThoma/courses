# Syllabus — MUS-21G: Applied Music Theory for Guitar

## Course description

A three-week intensive in practical music theory for guitarists. Students learn the fretboard as a system of intervals, master scale and chord construction, analyze real songs using Roman numeral analysis, and apply harmony through composition projects. By the end of the course students can name any note on the neck, build any common chord or scale from scratch in any key, explain why a progression works, and write original music with intent.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this course you will be able to:

1. **Name any note** on any string, any fret, within ~3 seconds.
2. **Construct and play** the major scale, three minor scales, major/minor pentatonics, the blues scale, and all seven modes — in any key, in multiple positions.
3. **Build any chord** (major, minor, diminished, augmented, sus, all 7th types, common extensions) from its formula, and find at least two voicings for it on the neck.
4. **Analyze** the key and chord progression of a song using Roman numerals, and identify common devices (cadences, borrowed chords, secondary dominants, key changes).
5. **Hear** the difference between intervals, chord qualities, and the most common progressions.
6. **Compose** an original chord progression and melody, and defend every choice in theoretical terms.

## Daily structure (~2 hours)

| Block | Time | Activity |
|---|---|---|

Grade yourself against `RUBRIC.md`. **Mastery threshold: 90%.** If you score below 90% on a quiz, re-study that week's material on the rest day before moving on — the course is cumulative and gaps compound.

## Course policies (yes, even self-taught courses need them)

- **No passive reading.** If a lesson shows tab or a diagram, you play it before moving on.
- **Closed-book quizzes.** The point is to find gaps, not to feel good.
- **Daily drills are non-negotiable.** Ten minutes a day beats two hours on Sunday.
- **Falling behind:** use the rest day. If you're more than a day behind, compress by skipping the optional exercises (marked ★), never by skipping lectures or quizzes.
